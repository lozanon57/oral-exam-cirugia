*Block D · Benign Digestive Surgery · PGY1-2*

*Guidelines: Tokyo Guidelines 2018 · WSES Acute Cholecystitis 2020 · SAGES-AHPBA Bile Duct Injury Management 2025 · ESGE 2019*


## Biliary Anatomy & Physiology

The biliary system transports bile from hepatocytes to the duodenum. The liver produces **500–1000 mL of bile per day**, composed of bile salts (67%), phospholipids (22%), cholesterol (4%), bilirubin, water, and electrolytes. Bile salts emulsify dietary fats and facilitate absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).

The **extrahepatic biliary tree** consists of the right and left hepatic ducts, which join at the **hepatic confluence (hilar plate)** to form the common hepatic duct (CHD). The cystic duct joins the CHD to form the **common bile duct (CBD)**, which courses posterior to the first part of the duodenum, passes through the head of the pancreas, and drains into the second part of the duodenum via the **ampulla of Vater**, controlled by the **sphincter of Oddi**. The normal CBD diameter is ≤6 mm; after cholecystectomy it may dilate to 10 mm without pathological significance.

**Calot's triangle** is bounded superiorly by the inferior surface of the liver, medially by the common hepatic duct, and inferiorly by the cystic duct. The **cystic artery** (usually a branch of the right hepatic artery) runs within this triangle. Safe dissection in Calot's triangle is the cornerstone of cholecystectomy.

> **Critical View of Safety (CVS) — Strasberg Definition** — Three criteria must ALL be met before clipping: (1) Hepatocystic triangle is cleared of fat and fibrous tissue. (2) The lowest third of the gallbladder is separated from the liver bed. (3) Only two structures are seen entering the gallbladder — the cystic duct and cystic artery. The CVS is the most effective strategy to prevent bile duct injury during laparoscopic cholecystectomy.

*Anatomical Variants of the Biliary Tree — Clinical Relevance*
Variant | Frequency | Risk | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Aberrant right posterior sectoral duct draining into CHD | 2–4% | Inadvertent ligation → right posterior sector ischemia/biloma | IOC or MRCP pre-op; CVS confirmation
Cystic duct joining right hepatic duct | 1–2% | Right hepatic duct misidentified as cystic duct | Always trace cystic duct to gallbladder junction
Mirizzi syndrome Type I (external CBD compression by cystic duct stone) | 0.1–0.7% | Obstructive jaundice, misidentified anatomy | MRCP; subtotal cholecystectomy or open approach
Mirizzi syndrome Type II (cholecystocholedochal fistula <33% CBD circumference) | 0.1–0.7% | CBD injury if aggressive dissection | Subtotal cholecystectomy + T-tube or hepaticojejunostomy
Low union of cystic duct (parallel to CBD) | 15–20% | Cystic duct clip may encroach on CBD | Dissect cystic duct close to gallbladder; IOC
Accessory hepatic duct (duct of Luschka) | 30% minor ducts | Bile leak post-cholecystectomy | Clip or suture any visible duct in gallbladder fossa

> **Mirizzi Syndrome** — Mirizzi syndrome is external compression of the CBD by a large stone impacted in Hartmann's pouch or cystic duct. Type I: external compression only. Type II–IV: progressive erosion into the CBD (cholecystocholedochal fistula) involving <33%, 33–66%, and >66% of CBD circumference respectively. Type V adds cholecystoenteric fistula. Pre-operative MRCP is mandatory. Laparoscopic approach carries high CBD injury risk in Types II–IV; open or subtotal cholecystectomy preferred.


## Acute Cholecystitis — Diagnosis & Tokyo TG18 Severity Grading

Acute cholecystitis results from obstruction of the cystic duct, most commonly by a gallstone (95% calculous; 5% acalculous). Obstruction leads to bile stasis, mucosal inflammation, bacterial invasion (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus), and progressive transmural inflammation. **Gangrenous cholecystitis** (15%) and **emphysematous cholecystitis** (1%, associated with Clostridium perfringens) represent severe forms with high perforation risk.

*Tokyo Guidelines TG18 — Diagnostic Criteria for Acute Cholecystitis*
Category | Criteria
--- | ---
A. Local signs of inflammation | Murphy's sign OR right upper quadrant mass/pain/tenderness
B. Systemic signs of inflammation | Fever >38°C OR elevated CRP OR elevated WBC >10,000/μL
C. Imaging findings | Ultrasound/CT/MRI: thickened gallbladder wall (>4mm), pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy's sign, gallstones
Suspected diagnosis | One criterion from A + one from B
Definite diagnosis | One from A + one from B + one from C

*Tokyo TG18 — Severity Grading of Acute Cholecystitis*
Grade | Definition | Management
--- | --- | ---
Grade I (Mild) | Does not meet Grade II/III criteria; acute cholecystitis in a healthy patient with mild local inflammation | Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy within 72h (preferred)
Grade II (Moderate) | WBC >18,000; palpable tender mass RUQ; duration >72h; marked local inflammation (gangrenous, pericholecystic abscess, hepatic abscess, biliary peritonitis, emphysematous) | Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy OR delayed (6–8 wk) after conservative treatment if high surgical risk
Grade III (Severe) | Organ dysfunction: cardiovascular (hypotension requiring vasopressors), neurological (altered consciousness), respiratory (PaO2/FiO2 <300), renal (oliguria, Cr >2.0), hepatic (PT-INR >1.5), hematological (platelets <100,000) | Urgent organ support; percutaneous cholecystostomy in high surgical risk; early cholecystectomy if surgical risk acceptable

**Ultrasound** is the first-line imaging modality (sensitivity 88%, specificity 80%): gallstones, wall thickening (>4 mm), pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy's sign. **CT abdomen/pelvis** (IV contrast) is indicated when US is non-diagnostic or complications are suspected (perforation, abscess, emphysematous cholecystitis — showing intramural gas). **HIDA scan (hepatobiliary iminodiacetic acid scintigraphy)** has sensitivity 97%, specificity 90% for acute cholecystitis; non-filling of gallbladder at 4h = positive. Reserved for equivocal cases.

> **Acalculous Cholecystitis** — Occurs in critically ill patients (ICU, post-major surgery, burns, total parenteral nutrition, trauma). Pathophysiology: bile stasis + ischemia. Higher rate of gangrene and perforation than calculous cholecystitis. US shows wall thickening, sludge, pericholecystic fluid without stones. Management: percutaneous cholecystostomy is preferred in unstable patients; cholecystectomy when patient stabilizes.


## Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy — Technique & Complications

Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (LC) is the gold standard for symptomatic gallstone disease. The standard **4-port technique** uses: 10–12 mm umbilical port (camera), 5 mm epigastric port (liver retraction), 5 mm right subcostal working port, and 5 mm lateral right subcostal port (fundus retraction). The **critical view of safety (CVS)** must be achieved and documented photographically before any structure is clipped or divided.

> **SAGES Multi-Society CVS Guidelines (Current) + SAGES-AHPBA BDI 2025** — **CVS (current multi-society standard):** (1) CVS must be achieved and documented in every cholecystectomy. (2) When CVS cannot be safely achieved: subtotal cholecystectomy is the preferred bail-out (not top-down dissection); alternatively IOC or fluorescent cholangiography when anatomy is uncertain. (3) Photo documentation of CVS recommended as standard of care. **SAGES-AHPBA Bile Duct Injury Management Guideline 2025 (PMID 41266841):** For major bile duct injuries requiring definitive repair — **delayed repair >6 weeks is preferred over early repair <6 weeks** (conditional recommendation based on GRADE evidence synthesis). This is the first joint SAGES-AHPBA evidence-based guideline on BDI management; it formalises what many HPB centres already practiced.

*Subtotal Cholecystectomy — Indications, Techniques & Outcomes*
Item | Details
--- | ---
Indications | Frozen Calot's triangle; severe inflammation/fibrosis; cirrhosis (portal hypertension); Mirizzi syndrome II–IV; gangrenous cholecystitis with posterior wall adherent to liver
Fenestrating technique | Gallbladder opened, stones evacuated, posterior wall left adherent to liver bed; cystic duct orifice closed from inside; drain placed
Reconstituting technique | Gallbladder transected below Hartmann's pouch; anterior wall closed; posterior wall left; cystic duct stump oversewn
Bile leak rate | 15–20% (higher than standard LC); managed with ERCP + stent in most cases
Long-term outcomes | Acceptable; remnant gallbladder can cause recurrent symptoms (5–10%)

**Conversion to open** is not a complication but a sound surgical decision. Rates: elective 2–5%; acute cholecystitis up to 15%. Indications: inability to achieve CVS, uncontrolled bleeding, bowel injury, suspected malignancy. **Dome-down approach**: dissection begins at the fundus and proceeds toward the cystic duct when Calot's triangle anatomy is obscured — reduces CBD injury risk.

**Single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy (SILC)** uses a single umbilical port. Systematic reviews show comparable safety to 4-port LC but longer operating time, higher hernia rate at extraction site (4 vs 1%), and no superiority in pain scores. Not recommended as routine practice; appropriate for highly selected patients in experienced hands.

> **Early vs Delayed Cholecystectomy in Acute Cholecystitis** — Multiple RCTs and meta-analyses confirm early LC (within 72h of symptom onset) is superior to delayed LC (6–8 weeks): shorter total hospital stay, no increase in complications, conversion rate, or bile duct injuries. TG18 recommends early LC for Grade I and II cholecystitis when the surgical team is experienced. For Grade III, percutaneous cholecystostomy is a bridge to definitive surgery.


## Choledocholithiasis — Diagnosis & Management

**Choledocholithiasis** is present in 10–15% of patients with symptomatic cholelithiasis. Stones in the CBD cause biliary obstruction, jaundice, pancreatitis, and cholangitis. Clinical presentation: right upper quadrant pain, jaundice (conjugated hyperbilirubinemia), elevated alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and gamma-GT, with variable elevation of transaminases.

*ESGE/ASGE Risk Stratification for Choledocholithiasis (Pre-operative)*
Risk Category | Criteria | Recommendation
--- | --- | ---
High (>50% probability) | CBD stone on US/CT/MRCP OR bilirubin >4 mg/dL + dilated CBD (>6 mm) OR ascending cholangitis OR gallstone pancreatitis with bilirubin >4 mg/dL | Proceed to ERCP
Intermediate (10–50%) | CBD dilation (>6 mm) on US OR bilirubin 1.8–4 mg/dL OR abnormal liver function tests | EUS or MRCP to confirm; if positive → ERCP
Low (<10%) | None of the above criteria | Proceed to LC; intraoperative cholangiography optional

**MRCP (magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography)** has sensitivity 85–92% and specificity 93–97% for CBD stones; non-invasive, no radiation. **EUS (endoscopic ultrasound)** has sensitivity 97% and specificity 96%; preferred when stone <5 mm (may be missed on MRCP) or when MRCP is inconclusive. EUS can proceed directly to ERCP in the same session.

*Timing Strategies for CBD Stone Management*
Strategy | Approach | Advantages | Disadvantages
--- | --- | --- | ---
Pre-operative ERCP → LC | ERCP clears CBD; LC performed separately (same admission or within days) | Highest CBD clearance rate; standard approach for high-risk patients | Two procedures; risk of post-ERCP pancreatitis (3–5%)
Simultaneous LC + IOC + LCBDE | LC with intraoperative cholangiography; if stones found → laparoscopic common bile duct exploration | Single anesthetic; avoids ERCP; IOC detects unsuspected stones | Requires LCBDE skills; longer OR time
LC + IOC → post-operative ERCP | LC performed; IOC used; positive IOC → ERCP postoperatively (within 24–48h) | Avoids LCBDE learning curve; flexible | Second intervention; retained stone risk
LC without IOC | LC only; CBD stones detected if symptomatic postoperatively | Simplest; acceptable if low risk | Missed stones; not appropriate for intermediate/high risk

> **Intraoperative Cholangiography (IOC) Technique** — IOC is performed via cannulation of the cystic duct after partial transection. Fluoroscopy confirms: (1) free flow of contrast into the duodenum, (2) visualization of right and left hepatic ducts, (3) no filling defects (stones), (4) correct CBD anatomy before clipping. IOC reduces bile duct injury rate by 50–70% in some series. SAGES recommends IOC or fluoroscopic dissection when CVS cannot be achieved.

**Laparoscopic Common Bile Duct Exploration (LCBDE)** achieves CBD clearance in 85–95% of cases. Transcystic approach (for stones <8 mm, favorable cystic duct anatomy): basket or balloon extraction, Dormia basket, or electrohydraulic lithotripsy. Transcholedochal approach (choledochotomy): for larger stones or failed transcystic; requires T-tube closure or primary closure over a biliary stent. Outcomes equivalent to ERCP for single-stage management.


## Bile Duct Injury — Classification, Management & Reconstruction

Bile duct injury (BDI) occurs in 0.3–0.7% of laparoscopic cholecystectomies (vs 0.1–0.2% for open). BDI is associated with significant morbidity, impaired quality of life, long-term biliary strictures, secondary biliary cirrhosis, and medicolegal consequences. The most common mechanism is misidentification of the CBD as the cystic duct (classical laparoscopic injury).

*Strasberg Classification of Bile Duct Injuries*
Class | Injury Type | Management
--- | --- | ---
A | Cystic duct leak or leak from small ducts in liver bed (duct of Luschka) | ERCP + biliary stent ± sphincterotomy; drain if collection
B | Occlusion of aberrant right hepatic duct (no leak) | Often asymptomatic; if bile duct atrophy → observation; if obstruction → hepaticojejunostomy
C | Transection of aberrant right hepatic duct (leak) | ERCP usually fails; surgical repair required; hepaticojejunostomy
D | Lateral injury to CHD/CBD without transection | Primary repair over T-tube if recognized intraoperatively; delayed → hepaticojejunostomy
E1 | Transection of CHD/CBD >2 cm from confluence (Bismuth I) | Hepaticojejunostomy
E2 | Transection <2 cm from confluence (Bismuth II) | Hepaticojejunostomy
E3 | Transection at confluence (Bismuth III) | Hepaticojejunostomy; high technical demand
E4 | Destruction of right/left hepatic duct separation (Bismuth IV) | Extended hepaticojejunostomy; liver resection occasionally needed; refer to HPB centre
E5 | Combined right sectoral duct injury + CHD injury | Refer to specialized HPB centre; complex reconstruction

> **Immediate vs Delayed Repair of Bile Duct Injuries** — If recognized INTRA-OPERATIVELY: immediate repair by an experienced hepatobiliary surgeon is acceptable for simple injuries (primary repair over T-tube for D-type). For complex injuries (E1–E5), do NOT attempt repair — place drains, transfer to HPB centre within 48–72h. If recognized POST-OPERATIVELY: do NOT rush to operate. Control sepsis, drain biloma/peritonitis (PTC or IR drainage), optimize nutrition, allow 6–8 weeks for inflammation to resolve, then perform hepaticojejunostomy (Roux-en-Y). Overall best outcomes achieved at >6 weeks after injury.

*Bismuth Classification of Biliary Strictures*
Type | Level of Stricture
--- | ---
I | Low CHD stricture; >2 cm hepatic duct stump
II | Middle CHD stricture; <2 cm hepatic duct stump
III | Hilar stricture; hepatic duct confluence preserved
IV | Destruction of hilar confluence; right and left ducts separated
V | Involves aberrant right sectoral duct alone or with confluence

**Hepaticojejunostomy (Roux-en-Y)** is the gold standard reconstruction for significant BDI. Key principles: tension-free anastomosis to healthy bile duct mucosa, adequate Roux limb length (≥60 cm), mucosa-to-mucosa apposition with absorbable sutures, no routine stenting for simple injuries. Long-term stricture rate after hepaticojejunostomy: 10–30% requiring re-intervention. Endoscopic or percutaneous balloon dilation before reoperation.


## Acute Cholangitis — Diagnosis & Management

Acute cholangitis results from bile duct obstruction (most commonly choledocholithiasis, followed by malignant stricture, biliary stent occlusion) combined with bacterial infection. Biliary hypertension drives bacteremia and endotoxemia. Most common organisms: E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus, Bacteroides.

*Classic Presentations of Acute Cholangitis*
Triad/Pentad | Components | Significance
--- | --- | ---
Charcot's Triad (1877) | Fever/rigors + Jaundice + RUQ pain | Present in ~70% of acute cholangitis; diagnostic sensitivity 72%
Reynolds' Pentad (1959) | Charcot's triad + Hypotension + Altered mental status (confusion) | Indicates Grade III (severe) cholangitis; high mortality; requires emergency decompression

*Tokyo TG18 — Severity Grading of Acute Cholangitis*
Grade | Criteria | Management
--- | --- | ---
Grade I (Mild) | Does not meet Grade II/III criteria; responds to initial medical treatment | Antibiotics; ERCP within 24–48h; cholecystectomy during same admission if indicated
Grade II (Moderate) | WBC >12,000 or <4,000; fever >39°C; age >75; bilirubin >5 mg/dL; hypoalbuminemia; any two of five | Antibiotics + urgent ERCP (within 24h); ICU if deteriorating
Grade III (Severe) | Organ dysfunction (cardiovascular: systolic BP <90; neurological: altered consciousness; respiratory: PaO2/FiO2 <300; renal: Cr >2.0; hepatic: PT-INR >1.5; hematological: platelets <100,000) | Emergency ERCP (within 12h); ICU; organ support; vasopressors

> **Antibiotic Selection in Acute Cholangitis** — Community-acquired: piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q8h OR ciprofloxacin + metronidazole. Healthcare-associated/severe: carbapenem (meropenem 1g IV q8h) ± vancomycin if MRSA risk. Duration: Grade I — 3–5 days after source control; Grade II/III — 5–7 days. De-escalate based on culture sensitivity results. Biliary decompression (ERCP with stone extraction ± sphincterotomy) is the definitive treatment — antibiotics alone are insufficient.


## Gallstone Pancreatitis — Management & Surgical Timing

Gallstones are the leading cause of acute pancreatitis in the Western world (40–50%). Small stones (<5 mm) and microlithiasis carry the highest risk of transient ampullary obstruction. Pathophysiology: transient pancreatic duct obstruction → acinar cell injury → activation of pancreatic enzymes → auto-digestion → systemic inflammatory response. Severity follows the **Revised Atlanta Classification**: mild (no organ failure, no local complications), moderately severe (transient organ failure <48h or local complications), severe (persistent organ failure >48h).

*ERCP Indications in Gallstone Pancreatitis*
Indication | Timing | Evidence
--- | --- | ---
Concomitant acute cholangitis | Emergency ERCP <12h | Strong; TG18 Grade III cholangitis
Persistent CBD obstruction (jaundice not resolving at 48–72h, worsening LFTs) | Urgent ERCP within 24–72h | Moderate; suggests persistent stone
Severe pancreatitis WITHOUT cholangitis | ERCP does NOT improve outcomes | IMRIE, NIECE trials — no benefit of routine early ERCP in severe pancreatitis without cholangitis
Mild pancreatitis without jaundice/cholangitis | ERCP NOT indicated; LC clears stones | Strong evidence; perform same-admission LC

> **WSES Classification — Gallstone Pancreatitis Severity** — The World Society of Emergency Surgery classifies gallstone pancreatitis as: Mild (Revised Atlanta mild; APACHE II <8; BISAP <2; Ranson <3) — same-admission LC. Moderate (transient organ failure or local complications) — LC same admission if patient recovers clinically; consider interval if complications ongoing. Severe (persistent organ failure; pancreatic necrosis) — definitive biliary surgery deferred 6–8 weeks after resolution of acute phase; interval cholecystectomy after full recovery.

**Intraoperative cholangiography (IOC)** is recommended during same-admission LC for gallstone pancreatitis to confirm CBD clearance. If stones found on IOC: transcystic stone extraction or post-operative ERCP. **MRCP** pre-operatively is indicated if persistent jaundice or dilated CBD suggests ongoing CBD obstruction. Same-admission ERCP + sphincterotomy can serve as a definitive bridge to LC in patients deemed too high-risk for immediate surgery.


## Percutaneous Cholecystostomy & Special Situations

**Percutaneous cholecystostomy (PC)** is a temporizing procedure for acute cholecystitis in high surgical risk patients (Tokyo Grade III; ASA IV–V; APACHE II >12). Under US or CT guidance, a catheter is placed into the gallbladder via transhepatic (preferred — reduces bile leak) or transperitoneal route. Success in achieving clinical improvement: 85–90%. Bridging interval cholecystectomy performed when patient medically optimized (typically 4–8 weeks). Tube removal: after cholangiogram confirms cystic duct patency (usually 4–6 weeks).

> **When NOT to Perform Interval Cholecystectomy After PC** — Some Grade III patients (very elderly, multiple comorbidities, poor functional status) carry prohibitive surgical risk even after optimization. In these cases, PC can serve as a definitive treatment: tube removed at 6 weeks if cystic duct is patent (stone passed). Long-term recurrence of cholecystitis in non-operative patients: 20–40% at 5 years. Shared decision-making is essential.

*Bile Leak Management Algorithm*
Bile Leak Type | Strasberg Class | Initial Management | Definitive Treatment
--- | --- | --- | ---
Cystic duct stump leak (low pressure) | A | ERCP + sphincterotomy ± biliary stent (7–10 Fr, 6–8 weeks) | Stent removal after leak sealed (confirmed fluoroscopically)
Duct of Luschka leak | A | ERCP + sphincterotomy ± biliary stent | Same as above; drain pericholecystic collection if present
CBD lateral injury (small) | D | ERCP + stent; drain if biloma | Delayed repair if ERCP fails; hepaticojejunostomy
Major BDI (complete transection) | E1–E5 | Control sepsis; PTC drainage; refer HPB specialist | Roux-en-Y hepaticojejunostomy at 6–8 weeks
