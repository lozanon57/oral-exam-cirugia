# Perioperative Care, Anaesthesia & ERAS — Preoperative Assessment, Perioperative Medicine & Enhanced Recovery

*Block G · Perioperative & Minimally Invasive · PGY1-2*

*Guidelines: ERAS Society Guidelines 2023–2024 (Colorectal, Liver, Pancreas, Bariatric) · NICE NG45 Perioperative Care · ESC/ESA Non-cardiac Surgery Guidelines 2022 · NICE NG89 VTE Prevention*


## Preoperative Assessment

Systematic preoperative assessment identifies modifiable risk factors, guides optimisation, and determines anaesthetic strategy. The ASA Physical Status Classification is the most widely used perioperative risk tool:

ASA I: Normal healthy patient — no functional limitation, no organic or psychiatric disease.
ASA II: Mild systemic disease — e.g., well-controlled DM, hypertension, BMI 30–40, active smoker, mild lung disease.
ASA III: Severe systemic disease — e.g., poorly controlled DM/HTN, COPD, morbid obesity (BMI >40), active hepatitis, alcohol dependence, implanted pacemaker, history of MI >3 months ago.
ASA IV: Severe systemic disease that is a constant threat to life — e.g., MI or stroke <3 months, ongoing cardiac ischaemia, severe valve dysfunction, sepsis, DIC, ARF/ESRD.
ASA V: Moribund patient — not expected to survive without the operation (ruptured AAA, massive trauma, fulminant hepatic failure).
ASA VI: Brain-dead patient — organ donation.
The 'E' suffix denotes emergency surgery (e.g., ASA IIIe).

Functional capacity: measured in METs (Metabolic Equivalent of Tasks). Climbing a flight of stairs = 4 METs (adequate). Vigorous sports = >10 METs. <4 METs = poor functional capacity → consider further testing. CPET (cardiopulmonary exercise testing) provides VO2max (peak oxygen consumption): VO2max ≥15 mL/kg/min = low risk; 10–14.9 = moderate; <10 = high risk for major surgery.

Cardiac risk: Lee Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI — Lee 1999) identifies 6 risk factors: (1) Ischaemic heart disease (history of MI, positive stress test, angina, nitrate use, Q waves on ECG); (2) Heart failure (pulmonary oedema, S3, orthopnoea, CXR findings); (3) Cerebrovascular disease (TIA/stroke history); (4) Diabetes mellitus on insulin; (5) Preoperative creatinine >170 µmol/L; (6) High-risk surgery (intraperitoneal, intrathoracic, suprainguinal vascular). RCRI 0 = 0.4% MACE risk; RCRI 1 = 1%; RCRI 2 = 2.4%; RCRI ≥3 = 5.4%. RCRI ≥3 with poor functional capacity → consider CPET or non-invasive cardiac testing before elective high-risk surgery.

Airway assessment: Mallampati classification (I–IV), LEMON (Look externally, Evaluate 3-3-2 rule, Mallampati, Obstruction, Neck mobility). Grade III/IV Mallampati = anticipated difficult airway → awake fibreoptic intubation plan, notify anaesthetic team, senior involvement.

Pulmonary risk: Canet score (ARISCAT) predicts postoperative pulmonary complications (PPC — atelectasis, pneumonia, respiratory failure). High Caprini/ARISCAT risk → prehabilitation, inspiratory muscle training, smoking cessation (minimum 4–8 weeks pre-op).

> **Active Cardiac Conditions — Postpone Elective Surgery** — ESC/ESA 2022 guidelines: postpone elective non-cardiac surgery in the presence of ACTIVE cardiac conditions: (1) Unstable angina or recent ACS (<60 days); (2) Decompensated heart failure (NYHA IV); (3) Severe symptomatic valvular disease — especially severe aortic stenosis (AS, mean gradient >40 mmHg or AVA <1 cm²); (4) Significant arrhythmias (complete heart block, symptomatic VT, uncontrolled AF with fast ventricular response). These patients require cardiac optimisation/intervention before elective surgery. Emergency surgery proceeds with highest-level monitoring.


## Perioperative Cardiac Management

Antiplatelet management: aspirin (100 mg daily) — continue perioperatively for most surgical patients (reduces MACE risk from thromboembolic events; modest increase in bleeding risk accepted). Exceptions: intracranial, posterior eye, intramedullary spinal surgery (high consequences of bleeding). P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel): stop 5–7 days before surgery to allow platelet function recovery. Exception: bare metal stent (BMS) in last 6 weeks or drug-eluting stent (DES) in last 12 months → delay elective surgery; continue DAPT (dual antiplatelet). Emergency surgery on DAPT: accept increased bleeding risk; transfuse platelets if life-threatening haemorrhage.

Anticoagulation management: warfarin — stop 5 days pre-op, check INR (<1.5 before surgery); bridging heparin (LMWH or UFH) in high-risk patients (mechanical heart valves, AF with high stroke risk — CHADS-VASc ≥3, recent DVT/PE <3 months). The BRIDGE trial changed practice for AF patients.

> **DOAC Perioperative Management** — Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs — rivaroxaban, apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban): stop 24–48 hours before low-bleed-risk surgery; 48–72 hours before high-bleed-risk surgery (or longer for dabigatran if CrCl <50 mL/min — 96h). No bridging with LMWH required for DOACs (no rebound hypercoagulability). Resume 24–48h post-op when haemostasis is secure. Check renal function before stopping dabigatran (renal clearance — prolonged action if CrCl <30).


## ERAS — Enhanced Recovery After Surgery

Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) is a multimodal, evidence-based perioperative care pathway that reduces complications, hospital stay, and readmission while improving patient experience and functional recovery. ERAS Society has published guidelines for colonic, rectal, liver, pancreatic, bariatric, thoracic, and emergency surgery. Core ERAS elements (colorectal pathway as the paradigm):

PREOPERATIVE elements: (1) Patient education and counselling — set realistic expectations, explain the ERAS journey. (2) Optimise medical comorbidities — anaemia correction, diabetic control, smoking/alcohol cessation (minimum 4–8 weeks). (3) Prehabilitation — exercise, nutrition, psychological preparation; reduces postoperative complications. (4) Carbohydrate loading — complex carbohydrate drink (CHO2 — Nutricia PreOp, 400 mL the evening before and 200 mL 2 hours before surgery) in non-diabetic patients without delayed gastric emptying. Reduces insulin resistance, attenuates catabolic response. (5) Avoid prolonged fasting — clear fluids up to 2 hours before GA, solids up to 6 hours. Standard fasting: nil solids 6h; clear fluids 2h before GA. (6) Avoid bowel preparation for colonic surgery — mechanical bowel preparation (MBP) alone increases surgical site infection; oral antibiotics + MBP (Nichols-Condon prep) may reduce SSI for colonic surgery; ERAS generally advises against routine MBP.

INTRAOPERATIVE elements: (1) Minimally invasive approach (laparoscopic or robotic) where feasible. (2) Goal-directed fluid therapy (GDFT) — use oesophageal Doppler or arterial waveform analysis to optimise stroke volume and avoid over-hydration. (3) Normothermia — forced-air warming, warm IV fluids, temperature monitoring. (4) Short-acting anaesthetic agents — desflurane, propofol TIVA, dexmedetomidine. (5) Regional anaesthesia — epidural (open surgery) or TAP block (laparoscopic surgery). (6) Avoid long-acting opioids intraoperatively.

POSTOPERATIVE elements: (1) Early removal of urinary catheter (Day 1 for colon, Day 3–4 for rectal/pelvic surgery). (2) Early mobilisation — out of bed on Day 1 (target 2 hours out of bed on Day 1). (3) Early oral nutrition — normal diet within 4 hours of surgery for laparoscopic colectomy. (4) Multimodal analgesia — scheduled paracetamol, NSAIDs, regional block; minimise or avoid systemic opioids. (5) Avoid/minimise IV fluids post-op — encourage oral intake; stop IV fluids when oral >50% of needs. (6) Drain removal — avoid routine pelvic drains; remove if inserted by Day 3. (7) VTE prophylaxis — LMWH from Day 1 post-op.

> **ERAS 2023–2024 Key Updates** — **ERAS compliance** directly correlates with outcomes — each 10% increase reduces major complication rate by ~4% and LOS by ~1 day; centres achieving >70% compliance have significantly better outcomes than partial adopters. **2023–2024 specific updates:** (1) **MBP + oral antibiotics (MBP+OAB)** for elective colorectal surgery is now Grade A — replaces the previous 'avoid prep' recommendation. Regimen: PEG-based MBP + neomycin 1g + metronidazole 500mg at 19h, 18h, and 9h before surgery; significantly reduces SSI and anastomotic leak. (2) **Pasireotide** (somatostatin analogue) for high-risk pancreatic anastomosis (soft texture, small duct) is Grade A for POPF reduction — 900 mcg SC twice daily × 7 days from day of surgery; superior to standard octreotide. (3) **Tranexamic acid (TXA)** 1g IV at induction routinely recommended for colorectal surgery (Grade B) to reduce blood loss. (4) **Prehabilitation** (exercise + nutrition + psychological support ≥4 weeks) is Grade B for high-risk patients (ASA III, frail, major resection).


## Fluid Management

Perioperative fluid management requires understanding of physiology, fluid types, and clinical monitoring. The traditional 4-2-1 rule for maintenance IV fluids: 4 mL/kg/h for the first 10 kg + 2 mL/kg/h for 10–20 kg + 1 mL/kg/h for >20 kg. Example: 70 kg patient = (4×10) + (2×10) + (1×50) = 110 mL/h. However, this formula was derived for paediatric patients and overestimates adult requirements in the postoperative period.

Goal-directed fluid therapy (GDFT): uses dynamic flow parameters (oesophageal Doppler — stroke volume/SV, corrected flow time/FTc; or arterial waveform analysis — pulse pressure variation/PPV, stroke volume variation/SVV) to guide fluid administration intraoperatively. Principle: give a fluid challenge (200–250 mL colloid or 500 mL crystalloid) and measure response (SV increase >10% = preload-responsive; give more fluid). Aim to 'ride the Frank-Starling curve' — avoid both hypovolaemia and hypervolaemia. SVV >13% (or PPV >13%) in mechanically ventilated patients = preload-responsive. Goal: SV optimisation during surgery, avoiding excess crystalloid.

Crystaloid choice: 0.9% saline ('normal saline') — hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis risk with large volumes (150 mEq/L chloride, vs plasma 98–106 mEq/L). Mechanism: excess chloride causes renal vasoconstriction, tubuloglomerular feedback, and HCO3− wasting → hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis. Balanced solutions — Hartmann's (Ringer's lactate), PlasmaLyte — have lower chloride content (approximately plasma levels) and contain buffers (lactate in Hartmann's, gluconate/acetate in PlasmaLyte). The SMART trial (2018) and SALT-ED trial (2018) demonstrated that balanced crystalloids reduced major adverse kidney events (MAKE-30) compared to 0.9% saline in critically ill and non-critically ill patients. ERAS Society recommends balanced crystalloids over 0.9% saline for perioperative use.

> **0.9% Saline — Hyperchloraemic Acidosis** — Large-volume 0.9% saline (>2 L) → hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis (pH ↓, Cl− ↑, HCO3− ↓, normal anion gap). Mechanism: chloride load causes bicarbonate wasting and proximal tubular acidosis. Clinical consequences: impaired renal function (renal artery vasoconstriction), reduced urine output, misinterpreted as ongoing metabolic problem. Clinical tip: in a post-operative patient with unexplained metabolic acidosis and large 0.9% saline infusion, check chloride — if >115 mmol/L consider hyperchloraemic acidosis and switch to balanced solution.


## Pain Management & Regional Anaesthesia

Multimodal analgesia is the cornerstone of ERAS-compliant pain management — combining analgesics with different mechanisms to provide additive/synergistic pain control while minimising opioid dose and side effects. WHO analgesic ladder (originally for cancer pain, applied perioperatively): Step 1 (mild pain): non-opioid (paracetamol, NSAIDs, adjuvants); Step 2 (moderate pain): weak opioid (tramadol, codeine) + non-opioid; Step 3 (severe pain): strong opioid (morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl) + non-opioid. Modern surgical anaesthesia targets Step 1 multimodal analgesia to AVOID Step 3 where possible.

Core multimodal regimen for major abdominal surgery: (1) Paracetamol 1g IV/oral QDS (scheduled — not PRN); (2) NSAID (ibuprofen, diclofenac, ketorolac) — if no contraindication (renal function, anastomotic risk); (3) Dexamethasone 8 mg IV intraoperatively (anti-emetic + anti-inflammatory); (4) Regional anaesthesia; (5) Low-dose ketamine (0.1–0.3 mg/kg bolus ± infusion) — NMDA antagonist, reduces central sensitisation (opioid-sparing); (6) PRN opioid for breakthrough only.

Regional anaesthesia techniques: Epidural analgesia: thoracic epidural catheter (T6–T10) with local anaesthetic ± opioid infusion — gold standard for open abdominal surgery. Reduces pain scores, reduces pulmonary complications (better cough), reduces ILEUS (sympatholytic effect promotes gut motility), reduces post-op cardiac events. Contraindications: patient refusal, coagulopathy (INR>1.5, platelets<80), local infection, raised ICP. Timing with LMWH: wait ≥12 hours after prophylactic LMWH dose before epidural insertion/removal; wait ≥24h after therapeutic dose.

TAP (Transversus Abdominis Plane) block: inject LA between internal oblique and transversus abdominis under USS guidance — blocks T7–L1 somatic afferents. Provides good analgesia for laparoscopic surgery (lateral approach = flank; subcostal = epigastrium). Does NOT provide visceral analgesia (unlike epidural). Duration: 12–24h. Adjuncts (liposomal bupivacaine, Exparel) extend duration to 72h.

Paravertebral block (PVB): unilateral somatic block — ideal for breast, thoracic, unilateral abdominal surgery. Rectus sheath block: midline operations. PROSPECT (Procedure-Specific Postoperative Pain Management) guidelines: laparoscopic colectomy → TAP block + multimodal NSAID/paracetamol; open colectomy → thoracic epidural + paracetamol/NSAID.

> **NSAIDs and Anastomotic Leak — The Controversy** — NSAIDs (cyclooxygenase inhibitors) are integral to ERAS multimodal analgesia. Concern: COX inhibition reduces prostaglandin-mediated collagen synthesis and mucosal repair, potentially impairing anastomotic healing. Epidemiological studies (Klein et al.) reported association between NSAID use and anastomotic leak. However, ERAS Society current position (2023): evidence is insufficient to recommend routine NSAID avoidance; risks must be weighed against opioid-related ileus and complications. In high-risk anastomoses (low rectal, hostile pelvis, poor bowel quality): consider limiting NSAID duration to 48–72h. Ketorolac is most frequently implicated — avoid prolonged use.


## Venous Thromboembolism Prevention

VTE (DVT and PE) is a major cause of preventable surgical mortality. The Caprini Risk Assessment Model (RAM) assigns points for individual patient and procedural risk factors to stratify VTE risk and guide prophylaxis intensity.

Caprini Score categories: Low risk (score 0–1): ambulation only, no pharmacological prophylaxis for most surgical patients. Moderate risk (score 2): LMWH or UFH or mechanical prophylaxis. High risk (score 3–4): LMWH + mechanical (IPC + TED stockings). Very high risk (score ≥5): LMWH + mechanical; consider extended prophylaxis.

NICE NG89 (2018): recommend LMWH prophylaxis for all surgical patients with estimated hospital stay >3 days and/or having abdominal/thoracic/pelvic surgery OR age >60 OR prior VTE OR cancer diagnosis. Mechanical prophylaxis (anti-embolism stockings — TED; intermittent pneumatic compression — IPC) reduces DVT risk by ~50% — recommended for all surgical inpatients regardless of bleeding risk.

LMWH timing: first dose 12 hours BEFORE or 12 hours AFTER surgery for prophylactic dose — varies by centre. For neuroaxial anaesthesia (epidural/spinal): minimum 12 hours between last prophylactic LMWH dose and neuraxial procedure/catheter removal; minimum 24 hours between last therapeutic dose and neuraxial procedure.

Renal dose adjustment: enoxaparin — reduce to once-daily if CrCl 15–29 mL/min; avoid if CrCl <15 mL/min. Dalteparin: use anti-Xa monitoring if CrCl <30 mL/min. UFH (unfractionated heparin) does not require dose adjustment for renal impairment.

Extended VTE prophylaxis for cancer surgery: standard in-hospital LMWH prophylaxis is insufficient for cancer patients after major abdominal/pelvic surgery. Evidence: ENOXACAN II trial (5 weeks enoxaparin after major abdominal surgery for cancer — reduced VTE from 12% to 4.8%) and FAME trial (low molecular weight heparin 4 weeks vs 1 week post-cancer surgery — significant VTE reduction). ERAS guidelines and NICE NG89: extend LMWH prophylaxis to 28 days after major cancer surgery involving the abdomen or pelvis.

> **Extended VTE Prophylaxis — Cancer Surgery** — NICE NG89 and ERAS Society: 28 days of LMWH prophylaxis after major abdominal/pelvic surgery for cancer. Extended prophylaxis is self-administered by patients at home after discharge. Ensure adequate patient education, prescription on discharge, and monitoring for bleeding complications. Consider extended prophylaxis for all cancer patients undergoing laparotomy/major laparoscopic procedures.


## Enhanced Recovery & Complications Recognition

ERAS compliance monitoring: institutional ERAS programs require systematic audit of element compliance per patient. The ISREC (International Study Group of Rectal Cancer) anastomotic leak definition: radiological or clinical evidence of disruption of the bowel anastomotic line, with or without drainage of gas/fluid from the pelvis. Grading A (clinical, managed conservatively), B (intervention without laparotomy), C (laparotomy required).

Clavien-Dindo classification: the standard for reporting surgical complications across all specialties (Dindo et al., 2004). Grade I: deviation from normal post-op course not requiring treatment. Grade II: requiring pharmacological treatment (IV antibiotics, blood transfusion). Grade IIIa: requiring surgical/endoscopic/radiological intervention WITHOUT general anaesthesia. Grade IIIb: requiring intervention UNDER general anaesthesia. Grade IVa: single organ failure (ICU-level care). Grade IVb: multi-organ failure (ICU-level). Grade V: death. The suffix 'd' indicates the complication is still present at discharge (e.g., Grade IIId).

Failure to Rescue (FTR): a quality indicator measuring the proportion of patients who die following a major complication. FTR highlights that the focus should be on recognising and responding rapidly to deterioration — not just preventing initial complications. High-volume centres with consultant-led ward rounds, ERAS nurse coordinators, and early warning systems have lower FTR rates. The SORT (Surgical Outcome Risk Tool) and POSSUM tools estimate individual patient operative mortality risk.

National Early Warning Score (NEWS2): used in the UK for early detection of patient deterioration. Parameters: respiratory rate, SpO2, supplemental O2, systolic BP, pulse rate, level of consciousness (ACVPU), temperature. NEWS2 ≥5 = urgent clinical review; ≥7 = emergency assessment, consider ICU referral. In surgical patients, NEWS2 triggers should prompt consideration of anastomotic leak, sepsis, PE, and haemorrhage. Early recognition and escalation ('failure to rescue') is associated with improved surgical mortality.

> **Failure to Rescue — Definition and Importance** — Failure to Rescue (FTR) = death following a recognised major complication. High FTR rates indicate deficiencies in complication recognition or response — not necessarily in preventing the complication itself. Key factors that reduce FTR: (1) NEWS2-triggered escalation protocols; (2) Consultant review of deteriorating patients within 1 hour; (3) Clear escalation pathways to ICU/HDU; (4) ERAS nurse coordinators for daily post-op monitoring; (5) Structured handover (SBAR — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation). Reducing FTR is a major focus of surgical quality improvement programs.
