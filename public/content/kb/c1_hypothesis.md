# Hypothesis Generation & Research Question — Academic Surgery — From Clinical Observation to Testable Hypothesis

*Block C · Academic Surgery · PGY1+*

*Guidelines: Hulley's Designing Clinical Research 4th ed. · EQUATOR Network 2023 · Declaration of Helsinki 2013 · ICH E6(R2) GCP*


## What Makes a Good Research Question

The research question is the cornerstone of every study. A poorly formulated question cannot be salvaged by sophisticated methodology, large sample sizes, or advanced statistical analysis. Conversely, a precisely defined, clinically meaningful question — even if addressed by a modest single-centre cohort — makes a genuine contribution to knowledge. The discipline of formulating a good research question before designing any study is the most important habit a surgical investigator can develop.

The FINER criteria, proposed by Hulley and colleagues, provide a systematic framework for evaluating research questions. Each criterion addresses a distinct dimension of question quality: **F**easible (can this study actually be done?), **I**nteresting (does the answer matter?), **N**ovel (is the question unanswered?), **E**thical (can it be studied without unacceptable harm?), **R**elevant (will the answer change clinical practice or guide future research?). A research question must satisfy all five criteria — failure on any single dimension is usually fatal to the study.

*FINER Criteria — Detailed Assessment Framework*
Criterion | Key Questions | Common Failure Mode | Surgical Example
--- | --- | --- | ---
Feasible | Adequate subjects? Technical capability? Budget? Timeline? | Overestimating accrual — the commonest grant-failure mode | Power calculation shows 800 patients needed; your centre does 40/year
Interesting | Will surgeons, patients, or guidelines act on this answer? | Incremental questions with no practice impact | 5th RCT comparing two equivalent mesh types
Novel | Has this been answered? Is a meta-analysis already definitive? | Replicating existing evidence without added value | RCT of antibiotic prophylaxis in bowel resection — settled
Ethical | Could harm to participants outweigh benefit? Equipoise present? | No clinical equipoise — one arm known inferior | RCT of no-repair vs repair of rectal perforation — no equipoise
Relevant | Will a positive OR negative result change something? | Proxy outcome with no clinical translation | Measuring cytokine levels with no patient-outcome link

> **Clinical Application** — You observe that your centre's anastomotic leak rate after low anterior resection in diabetic patients seems higher than the published literature. Before designing a study, apply FINER: **F** — your centre performs 80 LAR/year, 20–25% in diabetics (feasible for pilot); **I** — anastomotic leak doubles reoperation and stoma rates, hugely impactful; **N** — existing data are inconsistent, no prospective study specifically in T2DM with modern glycaemic targets; **E** — observational study, no experimental risk; **R** — if confirmed, optimise glycaemic targets pre-operatively and change patient counselling. All five criteria passed — proceed to PICO formulation.

The **novelty** criterion deserves particular attention in the era of systematic reviews and meta-analyses. Before formulating a new research question, a systematic literature search is mandatory — not a convenient Google Scholar search but a formal MEDLINE/EMBASE/Cochrane search with a defined strategy. If a high-quality systematic review already exists on the question, consider: (1) whether your question is more specific (different population, intervention, or outcome subgroup); (2) whether practice has changed since the review's search date; (3) whether there is genuine equipoise about the existing evidence. Reproducing established findings without additive value is not research — it is publication waste.

> **Common Pitfall** — The most common error in novelty assessment is **insufficient literature search**. A study designed without finding the existing evidence does not become novel retroactively — peer reviewers will identify the prior work and reject the manuscript. Conduct a structured systematic search of MEDLINE (PubMed), EMBASE, and Cochrane Database **before** writing the protocol, not before submission.


## The PICO Framework

PICO — Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome — is the structured template for translating a clinical question into a researchable format. Originally developed for systematic review question framing (Richardson, 1995), PICO has become the universal language of clinical research question formulation across study designs. A correctly formulated PICO statement constrains the study scope, defines inclusion/exclusion criteria, specifies the primary outcome, and — importantly — makes the research question directly auditable against prior literature.

**Population (P):** Describes the specific patient group of interest with sufficient precision to determine eligibility. Overly broad populations dilute the study signal; overly narrow populations limit generalisability. The P should specify: diagnosis, severity/stage, demographic constraints relevant to the question (age, sex), relevant comorbidities, and treatment context (primary vs recurrent, elective vs emergency). Example: 'Adult patients (≥18 years) with resectable T2-T3 rectal adenocarcinoma without distant metastases undergoing elective curative resection at a high-volume centre.'

**Intervention (I):** The treatment, procedure, diagnostic test, or exposure being studied. The I must be specified with sufficient technical detail to ensure reproducibility. For surgical trials this is particularly challenging: 'laparoscopic anterior resection' encompasses a wide range of techniques, surgeon experience levels, and institutional protocols. The CONSORT extension for surgical trials requires specification of procedure complexity, learning curve, and surgeon credentialling. Example: 'Laparoscopic total mesorectal excision (TME) performed by a surgeon with ≥50 laparoscopic TME cases in a centre performing ≥20 TME/year.'

**Comparison (C):** The control or comparator against which the intervention is measured. In RCTs this is the control arm; in observational studies it is the reference group. The C must represent the genuine clinical alternative — not a deliberately weakened comparator (biased design). 'Placebo' is appropriate when no active treatment exists; 'standard care' must be precisely defined as it varies geographically and temporally. In some questions, the comparison is absence of the intervention (pre-post designs) or a historical control — both of which carry significant methodological limitations.

**Outcome (O):** The endpoint that will be measured. Outcome specification is the most consequential PICO element because it determines sample size, statistical analysis, and the clinical meaningfulness of results. Outcomes are classified as: **Primary** (the single pre-specified endpoint powering the study), **Secondary** (pre-specified additional endpoints, hypothesis-generating), and **Exploratory** (post-hoc, hypothesis-generating only). Patient-centred outcomes (overall survival, quality of life, functional recovery) are preferred over surrogate endpoints (tumour markers, imaging response) when the question concerns patient benefit.

> **Board Pearl — PICO in Surgical Research** — Surgical studies frequently use **composite outcomes** (e.g., 'major morbidity = Clavien-Dindo ≥3') as primary endpoints, combining events that may have different clinical significance. Always scrutinise composite endpoints: if the composite is driven primarily by minor components, the apparent treatment effect may not translate to clinically meaningful benefit. Decomposing composite outcomes in sensitivity analyses is good analytical practice.

**Case:** You are a PGY3 surgical resident who has noticed that patients undergoing emergency colostomy reversal (Hartmann's reversal) at your centre have a higher anastomotic leak rate when the reversal is performed within 3 months of the original operation compared to those operated after 6 months. You want to design a study to test this observation formally.


## From Clinical Observation to Hypothesis

The research loop describes the iterative process by which clinical observation generates hypotheses, hypotheses generate studies, studies generate evidence, and evidence changes practice — which in turn generates new observations. Entry into this loop requires disciplined translation of informal clinical impression into a formally stated hypothesis. The gap between observation ('it seems like...') and hypothesis ('we hypothesise that...') is bridged by structured questioning, systematic literature review, and critical appraisal of existing evidence.

Clinical observation is subject to cognitive biases that systematically distort informal data collection. **Availability bias** causes recent or memorable cases to be over-represented in informal recall. **Confirmation bias** filters new observations through pre-existing beliefs. **Ascertainment bias** occurs when outcomes are observed more carefully in one group than another (e.g., more imaging in high-risk patients). Formal research methodology exists precisely to control these biases through pre-specification, blinding, and randomisation. The first step in converting observation to hypothesis is acknowledging that informal clinical impressions are hypothesis-generating only — not hypothesis-confirming.

> **Common Pitfall — Availability Bias** — The most memorable cases — complications, unusual presentations, treatment failures — are disproportionately represented in informal clinical memory. A surgeon who recently managed three anastomotic leaks in diabetic patients may genuinely believe the leak rate in diabetics is higher at their centre, when in reality the cumulative data do not show a difference. **Formal audit or registry analysis is the minimum standard** before designing a hypothesis-driven study.

The progression from observation to hypothesis follows a logical sequence. First, the observation is described in neutral, non-causal language: 'Among our patients, anastomotic leak appears more frequent in diabetics.' Second, a causal mechanism is proposed based on biological plausibility: 'Hyperglycaemia impairs neutrophil function, collagen synthesis, and angiogenesis — all critical for anastomotic healing.' Third, the hypothesis is formalised as a testable statement: 'In patients undergoing low anterior resection, perioperative hyperglycaemia (mean glucose >10 mmol/L) is associated with increased anastomotic leak rate.' Fourth, the hypothesis is matched to an appropriate study design and powered accordingly.

> **Board Pearl — Biological Plausibility** — A hypothesis without a plausible biological mechanism is a statistical artefact waiting to be disproven. Before formalising a hypothesis, articulate **why** the association should exist mechanistically. Reviewers and grant committees will ask. If you cannot explain the biology, reconsider whether the observed association is spurious.


## Types of Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a precisely stated proposition about a relationship between variables that can be tested by empirical data. Three types of hypothesis are relevant to clinical research: the null hypothesis, the alternative hypothesis, and the directional hypothesis. Understanding their logical relationship is fundamental to interpreting p-values, confidence intervals, and statistical test results.

The **null hypothesis (H₀)** states that there is no difference, no association, or no effect between the groups being compared. H₀ is the default position — the assumption that existing practice is adequate and any observed difference is due to chance. Example: 'There is no difference in anastomotic leak rate between diabetic and non-diabetic patients undergoing low anterior resection (H₀: leak rate_diabetic = leak rate_non-diabetic).' The study is designed to gather sufficient evidence to reject H₀ at a pre-specified significance threshold (typically α=0.05).

The **alternative hypothesis (H₁)** is the proposition the investigator believes to be true and wishes to demonstrate. H₁ is accepted by default when H₀ is rejected. Example: 'The anastomotic leak rate is different between diabetic and non-diabetic patients.' The alternative hypothesis can be two-tailed (different, in either direction) or one-tailed (specifically higher or lower).

A **directional hypothesis** specifies the expected direction of the difference: 'Anastomotic leak rate is **higher** in diabetic patients than non-diabetic patients.' Directional hypotheses are appropriate when there is strong prior evidence or biological rationale supporting a specific direction. They allow the use of one-tailed statistical tests, which are more powerful but less conservative. One-tailed tests are controversial in clinical research because a result in the unexpected direction is often clinically important and should not be ignored.

*Hypothesis Types — Summary*
Type | Statement form | Statistical test | When to use
--- | --- | --- | ---
Null (H₀) | 'There is no difference between A and B' | Two-tailed (default) | Always stated; what you aim to reject
Alternative (H₁) | 'There is a difference between A and B' | Two-tailed | Standard for most clinical studies
Directional (H₁) | 'A is greater/less than B' | One-tailed | Strong prior evidence of specific direction; use cautiously

> **Common Pitfall — Misinterpreting p-values** — **p < 0.05 does not mean the alternative hypothesis is true.** It means the probability of observing the data (or more extreme data) if H₀ were true is less than 5%. Rejecting H₀ is not the same as proving H₁. A statistically significant result can still be clinically irrelevant (large sample, tiny effect size), methodologically flawed (uncontrolled confounding), or a false positive (type I error). Always interpret p-values alongside effect sizes and confidence intervals.


## Matching Hypothesis to Study Design

The study design is not freely chosen — it is determined by the research question, hypothesis type, available resources, and ethical constraints. Choosing an inappropriate design for a given hypothesis is one of the most consequential errors in surgical research. The hierarchy of evidence reflects the relative ability of different designs to control for bias and confounding — not their absolute quality, since a well-conducted cohort study outperforms a poorly conducted RCT.

*Hypothesis-to-Design Matching*
Research Question Type | Optimal Design | Alternative | Key Limitation
--- | --- | --- | ---
Does intervention A cause outcome B? | RCT | Prospective cohort (if RCT not feasible) | Ethical/practical constraints on randomisation
How common is condition X? | Cross-sectional survey / registry | Systematic review of prevalence studies | Sampling bias, temporal validity
What predicts outcome Y? | Prospective cohort | Retrospective cohort (registry) | Confounding by indication in observational studies
What causes rare disease? | Case-control | Nested case-control (large cohort) | Recall bias, selection of controls
What is the evidence base? | Systematic review + meta-analysis | Narrative review (lower evidence) | Publication bias, heterogeneity
What happens after new procedure? | Case series with structured follow-up | Registry-based audit | No comparison group; hypothesis-generating only

Surgical RCTs face unique challenges that distinguish them from pharmaceutical trials. **Blinding** of the patient is often impossible (scars, different recovery profiles). **Blinding of the surgeon** is definitionally impossible — the surgeon knows what operation they are performing. **Standardisation** of the intervention is difficult — two surgeons performing 'laparoscopic TME' may be doing substantially different operations. The **IDEAL framework** (Idea, Development, Exploration, Assessment, Long-term study) provides a staged approach to evaluating surgical innovations that respects the learning curve and technical evolution inherent to surgical procedures.

> **Clinical Application — Feasibility Studies** — Before committing to a full-scale RCT, most surgical research groups conduct a **feasibility or pilot study** to test: (1) recruitment rate — can you achieve the required sample size?; (2) protocol adherence — will surgeons follow the intervention protocol consistently?; (3) outcome measurement — can the primary outcome be captured reliably?; (4) retention — do patients complete follow-up? A feasibility study should use a pre-specified progression criterion: 'If ≥80% of eligible patients consent and ≥90% complete 6-month follow-up, proceed to full trial.' NIHR and MRC require feasibility data for surgical RCT funding.


## Common Errors in Hypothesis Formulation

The integrity of the research process depends on the hypothesis being formulated **before** data collection and analysis, remaining unchanged through the study, and being reported honestly regardless of the result. Multiple systematic distortions of this process have been documented in the surgical and medical literature, collectively contributing to the 'replication crisis' in biomedical science — the observation that a substantial proportion of published research findings cannot be replicated.

**HARKing — Hypothesising After Results are Known** — occurs when a researcher analyses data exploratorily, identifies a significant association, and then reports it as if it were the pre-specified hypothesis. HARKing is a form of research misconduct because it inflates the type I error rate (false positive rate): with 20 comparisons, one will be significant at p<0.05 by chance alone. When reported as a confirmatory hypothesis, this chance finding appears to be real evidence. HARKing is detectable by comparing protocols (registered on ClinicalTrials.gov or ISRCTN) with published primary outcomes.

**Outcome switching** is a specific form of HARKing in which the primary outcome specified at registration is changed to a secondary or exploratory outcome that happened to reach statistical significance. A landmark analysis of 323 registered RCTs (Chan, JAMA 2004) found that 62% had at least one primary outcome that was either silently changed, introduced, or omitted between registration and publication. Outcome switching is addressed by mandatory prospective registration with primary outcome specification before study initiation.

**P-hacking** (also termed data dredging or significance hunting) involves testing multiple hypotheses or multiple subgroups until a p<0.05 is found, without pre-specifying the comparisons. P-hacking exploits the mathematical property that with enough comparisons, false positives become inevitable. Corrections for multiple comparisons (Bonferroni, Benjamini-Hochberg FDR) are designed to counteract this inflation, but the most robust protection is pre-specification of all comparisons in a statistical analysis plan.

> **Board Pearl — Pre-registration** — All clinical trials should be registered in a publicly accessible registry (ClinicalTrials.gov, ISRCTN, ANZCTR) **before** the first patient is enrolled. Registration requires specification of: primary outcome, secondary outcomes, inclusion/exclusion criteria, sample size, and statistical analysis plan. Journals increasingly require pre-registration as a condition of submission. For observational studies, pre-registration on Open Science Framework (OSF) or similar platforms is best practice.

> **Common Pitfall — Selective Reporting** — Reporting only statistically significant results (or only non-significant results when that supports your argument) is **publication bias** at the study level and **selective reporting** within a study. Both distort the evidence base. Reporting guidelines (CONSORT for RCTs, STROBE for observational studies) mandate reporting of all pre-specified outcomes, including null results. A negative result, if properly powered, is as valuable as a positive one.


## Ethical Framework for Surgical Research — Declaration of Helsinki and GCP

All research involving human participants must be conducted within an ethical framework grounded in respect for persons, beneficence, and justice. The **Declaration of Helsinki** (World Medical Association, 1964; revised most recently in 2013) is the foundational ethical document for medical research. It establishes that: (1) the interests of individual research participants must always take precedence over the interests of science and society; (2) research involving humans must be preceded by adequate laboratory and animal study; (3) every research protocol must be reviewed and approved by an independent Research Ethics Committee (REC) before the study begins; (4) informed consent must be obtained from all participants; (5) results — including negative results — must be published.

**Good Clinical Practice (GCP)** — codified in the International Council for Harmonisation guideline ICH E6(R2) — is the international quality standard for designing, conducting, recording, and reporting clinical trials involving human participants. GCP ensures that trial data are credible and that participants' rights, safety, and wellbeing are protected. GCP requires: a qualified investigator, adequate resources, an approved protocol, documented informed consent, accurate data collection, pharmacovigilance reporting, and sponsor oversight. In the UK, GCP training is mandatory for all staff involved in NHS research. Clinical trials of investigational medicinal products (CTIMPs) require additional MHRA authorisation.

*Declaration of Helsinki — Key Principles for Surgical Research*
Principle | Content | Surgical Application
--- | --- | ---
Paragraph 13 — REC approval | Independent ethics committee must approve all protocols before initiation | Institutional review board (IRB) or REC approval required before first enrolment
Paragraph 25 — Informed consent | Free, informed, documented consent from competent participants | Patient must understand both arms of the RCT including risks of each
Paragraph 26 — Vulnerable populations | Special protections for incapacitated, emergency, paediatric populations | Emergency surgical research requires prospective ethics waiver and proxy consent procedures
Paragraph 27 — Surrogate consent | Legal representative may consent for incapacitated participant if ethics approved | Emergency laparotomy trial — assent from next of kin with deferred consent from patient
Paragraph 34 — Negative results | Authors, editors, sponsors have obligation to publish all results | Negative surgical RCT results must be published, not suppressed
Paragraph 35 — Post-trial access | Participants should have access to beneficial intervention after trial completion | Investigational surgical technique available to control arm if proven superior

> **GCP — Core Responsibilities of the Principal Investigator** — The Principal Investigator (PI) bears ultimate responsibility for the conduct of the trial at their site. GCP mandates that the PI: (1) is medically qualified and competent; (2) has completed GCP training; (3) ensures informed consent is properly obtained and documented; (4) reports serious adverse events (SAEs) to the sponsor within 24 hours; (5) maintains accurate, contemporaneous source data; (6) permits monitoring and audit access; (7) ensures delegation of duties is documented in the delegation log. Non-compliance with GCP can result in invalidation of trial data and regulatory action.

The ethical requirement of **clinical equipoise** is central to surgical research design. Equipoise — genuine uncertainty in the expert medical community about the comparative merits of two treatments — is the ethical justification for randomisation. A surgeon who genuinely believes that one operative approach is superior cannot ethically randomise patients to the inferior arm. Apparent equipoise (a surgeon pretending to be uncertain for research purposes) is unethical. Equipoise can exist at the community level even when individual surgeons have preferences — this 'collective equipoise' justifies multi-surgeon RCTs.

> **Emergency Surgical Research — Special Consent Challenges** — Research in emergency surgical settings (trauma, bowel obstruction, ruptured aneurysm) poses unique ethical challenges because: patients may lack capacity for consent at the time of randomisation; time pressure may prevent adequate discussion; proxies may not be available. Ethics committees may approve **deferred consent** or **waiver of consent** for emergency research, provided: the research cannot be done without the waiver, the risk is minimal relative to the emergency condition, patients are fully informed retrospectively at the earliest opportunity, and they may withdraw data if they choose. CRASH-2 (antifibrinolytic therapy in trauma) used a waiver of consent model approved in 40 countries.


## Grant Application Structure and Funding Bodies

Securing research funding is an essential skill for the academic surgeon. Grant success rates at major funding bodies are typically 10–20%, making an understanding of application structure and reviewer expectations critical. A grant application is essentially a research protocol with a persuasive narrative — it must convince reviewers that the question is important, the team is capable, the methodology is rigorous, and the budget is justified. The structure of most major grant applications follows a common template, though specific requirements vary by funder.

*Major Surgical Research Funding Bodies*
Funder | Country | Typical Programme | Focus Area
--- | --- | --- | ---
NIHR (National Institute for Health and Care Research) | UK | HTA, EME, HS&DR, Research for Patient Benefit | Pragmatic clinical trials; health technology assessment; implementation
MRC (Medical Research Council) | UK | MRC Clinical Trials; MRC/NIHR EME Programme | Discovery science; methodological development; early-phase trials
NIH (National Institutes of Health) | USA | R01 (independent investigator); R21 (exploratory); K-series (career development) | Broad biomedical research; basic science to clinical translation
ASCO (American Society of Clinical Oncology) | USA | ASCO Young Investigator Award; ASCO Career Development Award | Oncology-focused clinical and translational research

Wellcome Trust | UK/International | Wellcome Clinical Research Career Development Fellowship; Investigator Awards | Translational research; global health; mental health
ESMO / EORTC | Europe | EORTC clinical trials; ESMO research grants | European oncology trials; cancer research

A competitive grant application typically contains the following sections: (1) **Lay summary** — a clear, jargon-free explanation of the research for non-specialist reviewers and the public (often posted publicly); (2) **Scientific background** — a critical synthesis of existing evidence demonstrating the gap the study will address; (3) **Research objectives and hypotheses** — precisely formulated FINER-compliant questions; (4) **Methodology** — study design, population, intervention, outcomes, sample size calculation, statistical analysis plan; (5) **Team** — investigator qualifications, track record, and collaborative structure; (6) **Feasibility** — patient access data, pilot data, site capability; (7) **Timeline** — GANTT chart with milestones and deliverables; (8) **Budget** — detailed, justified costs; (9) **Dissemination plan** — publication strategy, patient and public involvement, policy impact; (10) **Ethical considerations** — ethics approval strategy, participant burden, data governance.

> **Board Pearl — Pilot Data for Grant Success** — The single most powerful predictor of grant success is **preliminary data**. Reviewers ask: 'Can this team do this study?' Pilot data demonstrate that: (1) the patient population exists in the numbers claimed; (2) the team can successfully consent and recruit; (3) the outcome can be reliably measured; (4) the intervention is technically deliverable. Even a 20-patient pilot study with appropriate outcomes dramatically increases grant competitiveness. Plan your career as a sequence of pilot → grant → full RCT → systematic review.

> **NIHR Research for Patient Benefit — Practical Tips** — NIHR Research for Patient Benefit (RfPB) is the most accessible UK funder for surgical residents beginning their academic career. Key requirements: (1) the research question must be generated by clinicians and patients together; (2) Patient and Public Involvement (PPI) is mandatory — at least one patient contributor must be named; (3) applications are considered quarterly; (4) maximum award typically £250,000 over 3 years. Success rates approximately 15–20%. Two-stage application: outline (short, often reviewed within the region) then full application if shortlisted.


## Research Misconduct — Fabrication, Falsification, and Plagiarism

Research misconduct encompasses a range of behaviours that undermine the integrity of the scientific record. The three cardinal forms are defined by the US Office of Research Integrity (ORI) and adopted internationally: **Fabrication** — making up data or results and recording or reporting them as if real; **Falsification** — manipulating research materials, equipment, data, or results such that the research is not accurately represented; **Plagiarism** — appropriating another's ideas, processes, results, or words without giving appropriate credit. These three categories — collectively termed FFP — represent the most serious forms of misconduct and are grounds for retraction, institutional investigation, and professional disqualification.

*Forms of Research Misconduct — Definitions and Consequences*
Category | Definition | Surgical Example | Consequence
--- | --- | --- | ---
Fabrication | Inventing data that were not collected | Reporting operative times for procedures not actually performed in the stated protocol | Retraction; dismissal; research funding ban; GMC referral
Falsification | Manipulating or selectively altering data | Removing outlier anastomotic leak cases from database to achieve p<0.05 | Retraction; institutional investigation; may constitute fraud
Plagiarism | Using others' work without attribution | Copying a methods section from a prior paper without citation | Retraction of article; copyright infringement; career damage
Duplicate publication | Publishing the same data in multiple journals | Submitting the same case series to two journals simultaneously | Retraction; journal blacklisting; editor alerts
Authorship misconduct | Gift, ghost, or guest authorship | Adding department chief as co-author who had no study involvement | Journal sanctions; institutional policy violation
Image manipulation | Altering histological or Western blot images | Artificially enhancing contrast on pathology images | Retraction; may be classified as fabrication

Beyond the FFP triad, the research community recognises a spectrum of **questionable research practices (QRPs)** that — while not always rising to the level of formal misconduct — systematically distort the scientific record. These include: HARKing, p-hacking, selective outcome reporting, inadequate data storage, failure to declare conflicts of interest, and gift authorship. QRPs are widespread — surveys suggest 30–70% of researchers have engaged in at least one QRP — and collectively contribute to the replication crisis more than outright fraud, because individual instances are difficult to detect and address.

In the UK, research misconduct is investigated under the **UK Research Integrity Office (UKRIO)** framework and individual institutional policies. The General Medical Council (GMC) requires doctors to be honest in research and to report suspected misconduct. Authors on multi-author papers bear shared responsibility for the integrity of published data — adding your name to a paper without reviewing the underlying data is ethically and professionally hazardous. The Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) provides guidance on managing misconduct at the editorial level and is consulted by most major surgical journals.

> **Authorship Standards — ICMJE Criteria** — The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (ICMJE) defines authorship by four criteria, ALL of which must be met: (1) Substantial contributions to conception or design, OR acquisition, analysis, or interpretation of data; (2) Drafting the work or revising it critically for intellectual content; (3) Final approval of the version to be published; (4) Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work. Anyone who does not meet all four criteria should be listed in Acknowledgements, not as an author. Gift authorship (adding contributors for prestige or reciprocity) and ghost authorship (failing to name actual contributors) are both violations of ICMJE standards.

> **ORCID — Researcher Identity** — ORCID (Open Researcher and Contributor ID) provides a persistent 16-digit identifier for researchers. It distinguishes your publications from similarly named colleagues, connects across databases (PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science), and tracks name changes. Most major funders (NIHR, Wellcome, NIH) now require ORCID registration in grant applications. Most major journals request ORCID at submission. Register at orcid.org — it is free and takes 5 minutes. Include your ORCID in your CV and correspondence.


## Open Science — Pre-registration, Data Sharing, and Reproducibility

Open science encompasses a set of practices designed to make the research process more transparent, reproducible, and accessible. The open science movement emerged partly in response to the replication crisis — the recognition that many published findings could not be independently reproduced. Key components of open science relevant to surgical researchers include: prospective trial registration, pre-registration of study protocols and statistical analysis plans, open access publication, data sharing, code sharing, and open materials.

**Prospective trial registration** in a WHO-approved registry (ClinicalTrials.gov, ISRCTN, ANZCTR) before the first patient is enrolled is now a condition of publication in virtually all major surgical journals and a requirement for ethical approval in most countries. Registration commits the team to the pre-specified primary outcome and statistical analysis plan, making post-hoc outcome switching detectable. The ICMJE requires registration as a prerequisite for journal submission. Studies of observational design can be registered on OSF (Open Science Framework) with a time-stamped protocol.

**Data sharing** — making individual participant data (IPD) available to other researchers under appropriate governance — enables replication of analyses, patient-level meta-analysis, and re-use of costly datasets. The NIH mandates a Data Management and Sharing Plan for all funded research since January 2023. NIHR research data sharing guidance requires that data be shared within 12 months of study completion. Data sharing must respect: patient consent (either specific or broad), GDPR/data protection legislation, anonymisation requirements, and institutional governance. Suitable platforms include UK Data Service, Harvard Dataverse, and Zenodo.

*Open Science Practices — Practical Implementation*
Practice | Tool/Platform | Requirement Level | Benefit
--- | --- | --- | ---
Trial registration | ClinicalTrials.gov; ISRCTN | Mandatory for RCTs (ICMJE) | Prevents HARKing; enables systematic reviews
Protocol pre-registration | OSF; PROSPERO (for systematic reviews) | Best practice for observational studies | Time-stamps hypothesis; reduces publication bias
Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP) | Published in trial registry or as protocol paper | Required by CONSORT, STROBE | Prevents p-hacking; enables replication
Open access publication | PubMed Central; institutional repositories; hybrid journals | Mandated by NIH, NIHR, Wellcome | Maximises readership and impact
Data sharing | UK Data Service; Harvard Dataverse; Zenodo; XNAT | Mandatory for NIH/NIHR funded research | Enables replication; increases citation rate
Code sharing | GitHub; OSF; Zenodo | Increasingly required by journals | Enables computational reproducibility
Registered reports | Journals offering Registered Report format | Voluntary; guarantees publication pre-data | Eliminates publication bias for small studies

> **Reproducibility in Surgical Research** — Reproducibility requires that an independent researcher, given the same data and code, arrives at the same conclusions. Replicability requires that an independent researcher, collecting new data with the same protocol, arrives at the same conclusions. In surgical research, full replication is rarely possible (different surgeons, hospitals, eras), but reproducibility of analysis is achievable. Share: (1) anonymised datasets; (2) all analysis code (R, Stata, SPSS syntax); (3) the statistical analysis plan; (4) the outcome measurement protocol. A reproducible surgical study is one where any statistician given the data arrives at the same numbers.


## The Research Team — Roles, Collaboration, and Conflicts of Interest

Modern surgical research is inherently collaborative. Single-surgeon, single-centre studies — however carefully conducted — have limited generalisability and are insufficient for most policy-changing questions. The contemporary surgical research team spans multiple disciplines: clinical investigators, biostatisticians, qualitative researchers, health economists, trial managers, data managers, patient and public representatives, and methodologists. Understanding the roles within this team and managing the relationships between them is as important as understanding the science itself.

*Surgical Research Team — Roles and Responsibilities*
Role | Responsibilities | When Essential
--- | --- | ---
Chief Investigator (CI) | Overall scientific and regulatory accountability; grant holder; lead author | All funded studies; multicentre trials
Principal Investigator (PI) | Site-level accountability; GCP responsibility; local recruitment | Each participating centre in multicentre study
Biostatistician | Sample size; statistical analysis plan; analysis; reporting | All quantitative studies (mandatory for grants)
Trial Manager / Research Nurse | Day-to-day protocol delivery; consent; data collection; adverse event reporting | All RCTs; complex prospective studies
Data Manager | Database design; data quality; data governance; linkage | Large studies; studies using electronic records
Health Economist | Cost-effectiveness analysis; HTA submissions; budget impact modelling | NIHR HTA studies; trials with economic endpoint

**Conflicts of interest (COI)** arise when a researcher's financial, professional, or personal interests could inappropriately influence their judgement in the conduct, reporting, or interpretation of research. The major categories of COI in surgical research are: **Financial COI** — funding from device or pharmaceutical companies, consultancy fees, equity interests, speaker payments, patent royalties; **Professional COI** — desire to demonstrate superiority of one's own technique, career advancement incentives, institutional prestige; **Personal COI** — prior beliefs, prior publications, personal relationships with authors. All journals require disclosure of COI at submission. All grant applications require disclosure of financial interests. Failure to disclose constitutes misconduct.

Industry-sponsored surgical research presents particular challenges. Device manufacturers may fund RCTs of their products with contract provisions that give the sponsor control over data access, publication decisions, or statistical analysis. The NEJM, JAMA, and Lancet require that academic investigators have independent access to all trial data and the right to publish regardless of results. 'Publication restriction' clauses in industry contracts violate journal policy and should not be signed. The FDAAA 2007 (US) and CTR regulation (EU) require registration and results reporting of industry-sponsored trials within 12 months of study completion.

> **Device Industry Relationships in Surgical Research** — Surgical device companies routinely provide training, equipment loans, and 'educational' funding to surgeons. These arrangements create financial conflicts of interest that should be declared. Specifically: (1) if you receive payment for training surgeons on a device you are then studying, this is a COI that must be declared; (2) if a company funds a registry that you lead, their analysis rights must be contractually limited; (3) proctored surgical cases funded by the device company should not be included in your 'independent' outcomes data. The Association of British Healthcare Industries (ABHI) code of practice and MedTech Europe Code govern industry-healthcare professional relationships.

> **Delphi Consensus Methodology in Surgical Research** — When RCT evidence is absent and expert opinion must be formalised, the **Delphi method** provides a structured approach to reaching consensus. In surgical research, Delphi studies are used to: define core outcome sets (e.g., COMET Initiative — what outcomes should all trials in a domain measure?); establish consensus on case definitions (e.g., anastomotic leak grading); prioritise research questions (e.g., James Lind Alliance Priority Setting Partnerships). Delphi uses iterative anonymous questionnaire rounds (typically 2–3) with controlled feedback between rounds, converging toward consensus defined a priori (e.g., ≥70% agreement). Participant panels should include surgeons, other clinicians, and patients.
