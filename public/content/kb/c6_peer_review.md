## Authorship and Research Integrity

Authorship of a peer-reviewed publication confers both academic credit and ethical responsibility. The credit-responsibility dyad is fundamental: authors receive recognition in their academic record, CV, and grant applications, and in exchange they are accountable for the integrity of the reported work. The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (ICMJE) defines authorship precisely through four criteria, all of which must be satisfied simultaneously by each individual listed as an author. The growing complexity of large-scale, multi-disciplinary, and multi-institutional research has made authorship criteria harder to apply but no less important.

ICMJE authorship requires ALL of the following four criteria: (1) Substantial contributions to conception/design OR acquisition/analysis/interpretation of data; (2) Drafting or critically revising the manuscript for important intellectual content; (3) Final approval of the version to be published; (4) Accountability for all aspects of the work — agreeing to investigate and resolve questions related to accuracy or integrity. Authorship confers credit AND responsibility. It is not earned by: funding the study, providing laboratory space, being head of department, or collecting data without contributing to analysis or writing.

> **Common Pitfall — Ghost and Gift Authorship** — **Ghost authorship**: a person who made substantial intellectual contributions to the work is omitted from the author list — common in industry-sponsored trials where company statisticians do the analysis but do not appear as authors. **Gift authorship**: a person is listed as author who did not meet ICMJE criteria, typically a department head or senior colleague who contributed nothing to the specific study. Both practices violate ICMJE guidelines and are sanctionable by journals. The **Contributor Roles Taxonomy (CRediT)** provides a standardised vocabulary for specifying each author's actual contribution — increasingly required by high-impact journals.

*CRediT Taxonomy — 14 Contributor Roles*
Role | Description | Example in Surgical Research
--- | --- | ---
Conceptualisation | Ideas; formulation of overarching research goals and aims | Identifying the clinical question and hypothesis
Methodology | Design; creation of models and statistical analysis plans | Study design, sample size calculation, statistical plan
Data curation | Annotation, scrubbing, maintaining research data | Database management, data quality checks
Formal analysis | Application of statistical, mathematical, computational, or other analysis methods | Running the statistical analysis
Investigation | Conducting the research process — patient enrolment, procedures | Surgeon performing the intervention
Writing — Original Draft | Preparation of the initial manuscript | Lead author writing the first draft
Writing — Review & Editing | Critical revision of the manuscript for intellectual content | Co-authors revising and providing feedback
Supervision | Oversight and leadership of the research team | Senior author supervision — counts toward authorship only with other contributions
Funding acquisition | Securing financial support | Grant acquisition — insufficient alone for authorship
Project administration | Coordination and management | Research coordination — insufficient alone for authorship


## The Peer Review Process

After submission, a manuscript undergoes editorial screening (scope, plagiarism check via iThenticate, basic quality) before being assigned to an editor who selects 2–3 peer reviewers. Peer review types: **single-blind** (reviewers know authors; authors don't know reviewers — most common), **double-blind** (both anonymous), **open peer review** (identities known; increasingly used by BMJ, PLOS Medicine). Reviewer decisions: accept, minor revision, major revision, reject with resubmit, reject. **Major revision** is the most common response for publishable work — treat it as a positive outcome. The average time from submission to first decision at a top surgical journal is 4–8 weeks; total time from submission to publication is 6–18 months.

*Peer Review Types — Comparison*
Type | Who Knows What | Advantages | Disadvantages | Examples
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Single-blind | Reviewers know authors; authors don't know reviewers | Reduces reviewer retaliation risk; most familiar to reviewers | Reviewer bias toward prestigious institutions; gender bias documented | EJSO, ASO, Colorectal Disease
Double-blind | Neither reviewers nor authors know each other's identity | Reduces prestige bias; protects junior authors | Difficult to achieve in small fields; identifiable by writing style | BJS, Lancet Oncology (optional)
Open peer review | Both identities disclosed; reviews often published | Accountability; civility; transparency; reduces reviewer conflicts of interest | Reviewer reluctance; retaliation risk in competitive fields | BMJ, PLOS Medicine, F1000Research
Post-publication review | Community reviews after publication | Rapid error detection; ongoing quality monitoring | Risk of harassment; uncontrolled format | PubPeer, F1000 Recommends

> **Board Pearl — Responding to Reviewers** — The response-to-reviewers letter is as important as the manuscript revision itself. Structure: (1) thank the editor and reviewers; (2) address every comment, numbered to match the reviewer's numbering; (3) for each change: state what you changed, where (page/line number), and paste the revised text; (4) for disagreement: provide evidence-based counter-argument respectfully — do not simply comply with a reviewer request that would compromise scientific accuracy. A well-structured response letter dramatically increases acceptance rates at revision stage.

**Reviewer conflicts of interest** are a significant and underappreciated source of bias in peer review. A reviewer has a conflict of interest when they have a financial relationship with the authors or their institution, a personal relationship (positive or negative), are direct academic competitors for the same grant funding or priority claim, or have publicly taken a strong position on the paper's topic. Most journals ask editors to avoid assigning competitors as reviewers, but the system relies heavily on self-declaration by editors and reviewers. Authors may suggest reviewers (names considered but not guaranteed) and may exclude specific individuals (exclusions are generally honoured), providing some protection against targeted hostile review.

> **Common Pitfall — Predatory Journals** — Predatory journals exploit the open-access author-pays model without providing legitimate peer review. Red flags: unsolicited email invitations; rapid acceptance (days to 1 week); no verifiable peer review process; journal not indexed in PubMed/MEDLINE, Scopus, or DOAJ; inflated/fabricated impact factor claims; similar names to legitimate journals. Verification tools: **DOAJ** (Directory of Open Access Journals) for legitimate OA journals; **ISSN Portal** to verify journal existence; **Scimago** for indexed journals. The Bohannon sting (Science 2013) revealed widespread acceptance of a fatally flawed paper by 157 journals — demonstrating the scale of the predatory journal problem.

**Predatory conferences** parallel predatory journals. These are conferences that exist primarily to generate revenue through registration fees and publication fees, without providing genuine scientific peer review, quality control of presentations, or substantive academic networking. Red flags for predatory conferences: unsolicited invitation emails with excessive flattery; invitations to multiple, geographically diverse conferences from the same organiser; no verifiable scientific committee; no sponsorship from established professional societies (ESSO, ESSO, ESMO, ASES); abstract acceptance within 24–48 hours; excessive registration fees with no financial support for early-career researchers. Presenting at predatory conferences wastes resources and provides no legitimate academic credit.


## Open Access Publishing — Gold, Green & Diamond Routes

Open Access (OA) refers to the principle that published research should be freely available to any reader, anywhere, without payment or institutional subscription barriers. OA has moved from an academic ideal to a funder and legislative mandate in many countries. The European Research Council (ERC), Horizon Europe, Wellcome Trust, NIH, and Spanish ISCIII all mandate OA publication for funded research. Three primary OA routes exist, each with different cost, speed, and sustainability implications.

*Open Access Routes — Gold, Green, and Diamond*
Route | Definition | Cost to Author | Examples | Compliance with Plan S
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Gold OA | Published version freely available immediately on journal website under CC licence; author pays APC | €500–€9,500 (varies dramatically) | PLOS Medicine, Cancers (MDPI), Nature Communications | Yes — if journal is compliant
Hybrid OA | Subscription journal with per-article OA option; author pays APC to unlock individual article | €2,000–€5,000 per article | Annals of Surgery, BJS, EJSO — most major journals | Yes — if via Transformative Agreement
Green OA | Author self-archives accepted manuscript in institutional or subject repository after embargo period | Free (no APC) | PubMed Central (PMC), Zenodo, DIGITAL.CSIC, institutional repository | Yes — immediate CC-BY deposit to repository
Diamond OA | Published open access; no APC to authors; journal funded by institution, society, or subsidy | Free | PLOS Biology (subsidised); many society journals; emerging model | Yes — ideal but less scalable
Bronze/Gratis | Freely readable on journal website but no reuse rights; no OA licence — not true OA | Free | Many journal websites during COVID emergency access | No — does not meet Plan S

> **Plan S — Mandatory Immediate Open Access** — Plan S is a 2018 initiative by cOAlition S (a consortium of research funders including ERC, Wellcome, many national funders) requiring that all research funded by member funders be published in compliant OA venues by 2021, with no embargo period. Key Plan S requirements: (1) immediate OA (no embargo); (2) CC-BY licence (allows reuse, adaptation, and commercial use with attribution); (3) published in a fully OA journal, in a hybrid journal under a Transformative Agreement, or self-archived in a compliant repository. Spanish ISCIII joined cOAlition S in 2022. If you receive ISCIII funding, your publications must comply. Check compliance via the Journal Checker Tool (journalcheckertool.org).

**Article Processing Charges (APCs)** are the fees charged by journals for Gold or Hybrid OA publication. APCs vary enormously: MDPI journals charge ~€1,000–€2,500; Nature journals charge €9,500–€11,000; Elsevier hybrid charges ~€3,000–€5,000 per article. APCs represent a significant and growing financial burden, particularly for researchers in low- and middle-income countries or without institutional funding. **APC waivers** are available from many publishers on a case-by-case basis: geographic waivers (automatic for authors from World Bank low-income countries), hardship waivers (for unfunded research), and institutional deals (transformative agreements between publishers and institutions that bundle subscription and APC costs). Researchers should explore waiver eligibility before assuming APCs must be paid personally.


## Preprint Servers — medRxiv, bioRxiv, SSRN

Preprints are complete manuscript drafts posted to publicly accessible servers before (or in parallel with) formal peer review and journal submission. Preprint servers provide immediate free dissemination of research findings, allowing the scientific community to access, critique, and build upon results without waiting 6–18 months for the traditional publication process. Preprint use has grown dramatically in biomedical research since 2013 (bioRxiv launch) and especially since 2019 (medRxiv launch and COVID-19 acceleration).

*Major Preprint Servers for Surgical and Medical Research*
Server | Focus | Moderation | Permitted Journals | Citation in Methods
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
medRxiv | Clinical medicine, surgery, epidemiology, public health | Basic screening (no peer review); health/safety alerts for COVID-related | Most medical journals accept prior posting | Cite as preprint with DOI; note 'not peer reviewed'
bioRxiv | Biological sciences, molecular biology, physiology | Basic screening; no peer review | Most biology and some medical journals | Cite as preprint with DOI
SSRN (Elsevier) | Social sciences, economics, law, some clinical | No formal screening — highest risk of predatory use | Variable | Cite with caution; lower quality filter
Research Square | Preprint server; also provides in-review preprints for Springer/Nature journals | Basic check; links to journal review | Springer Nature journals | Authors can share in-review versions
Preprints.org (MDPI) | Broad biomedical and science scope | Basic screening | MDPI journals primarily | Lower prestige; suitable for rapid dissemination

> **Common Pitfall — Citing Preprints as Established Evidence** — Preprints have NOT undergone peer review. Citing a preprint as established evidence — particularly in clinical decision-making or systematic reviews — carries significant risk: preprints may contain errors, methodological flaws, or conclusions that change substantially after peer review. During the COVID-19 pandemic, multiple preprints on treatments were widely cited before peer review revealed serious methodological problems (e.g., the hydroxychloroquine preprints). Best practice: when citing a preprint in a manuscript, always label it clearly as a preprint ('Smith et al., medRxiv 2024, preprint not peer reviewed') and check at submission whether the preprint has since been published in a peer-reviewed journal.

Most major surgical journals now permit submission of manuscripts that have been posted as preprints, provided the preprint is linked in the submission and the journal is informed. ICMJE member journals explicitly allow preprinting as long as the journal is notified. However, some journals (particularly high-impact general journals) have complex policies on preprints and press releases — always verify the specific journal's preprint policy in their Instructions to Authors before posting. After publication of the peer-reviewed paper, authors should update the preprint record with a link to the published version to prevent confusion between the preprint and the final article.


## Post-Publication Correction & Retraction Process

Scientific literature is a living record that must be correctable when errors are identified. Three primary mechanisms exist for post-publication record correction: errata/corrigenda (minor errors), expressions of concern (investigations in progress), and retractions (serious errors invalidating conclusions). The distinction between these mechanisms matters: each has a different implication for the scientific record and for the authors' standing.

*Post-Publication Correction Mechanisms*
Mechanism | When Used | Who Initiates | Effect on Record | COPE Guidance
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Erratum (journal error) | The journal made an error in typesetting, layout, or production not attributable to authors | Journal editors | Correction notice published; original article unchanged | Erratum linked to original article in databases
Corrigendum (author error) | Authors made an error in the submitted content that does not invalidate conclusions (e.g., transposition of values, figure labelling) | Authors via editor | Correction published; original article amended or annotated | Indexed alongside original; correction must be complete
Expression of Concern (EoC) | Serious concerns about integrity raised (misconduct allegation, institutional investigation pending) but facts not yet established | Editor | Notice published; article remains but flagged; readers warned | Used when investigation incomplete; does not prejudge outcome
Retraction | Conclusions are unreliable due to data fabrication, falsification, plagiarism, duplicate publication, or major unresolvable error | Authors or editor | Retraction notice replaces or annotates original; article marked RETRACTED in databases | Authors must cooperate; uncooperative retraction possible; reasons must be stated

> **COPE Retraction Guidelines — Author Obligations** — COPE (Committee on Publication Ethics) guidelines state that authors have an obligation to notify editors immediately when they discover significant errors in their published work. If an error does not invalidate conclusions, a corrigendum should be submitted. If conclusions are affected, authors should proactively request retraction. Journals may retract articles without author consent if authors refuse to cooperate after being notified of serious concerns. Retracted articles remain in PubMed and databases but are clearly marked as retracted — they should not be cited as evidence in subsequent publications. Citing a retracted paper without acknowledging its retraction status is an ethical violation.

The **Retraction Watch** database (retractionwatch.com) tracks retractions across biomedical journals and provides a searchable database of retracted papers. As of 2024, over 45,000 papers have been retracted and indexed. Reasons for retraction include: data fabrication or falsification (most severe — typically career-ending); image manipulation (increasingly detected by AI screening tools such as Proofig); plagiarism (text-matching software iThenticate detects verbatim copying); duplicate publication (same data submitted to multiple journals without disclosure); and honest errors that invalidate conclusions. The time from publication to retraction has decreased significantly as post-publication peer review platforms (PubPeer) and automated image integrity tools have improved.


## Research Impact — Citations, H-index, Altmetrics

Research impact metrics quantify how widely a publication or researcher's work has been engaged with, cited, and disseminated. These metrics are used in grant applications, academic promotion decisions, departmental resource allocation, and journal ranking. Understanding the strengths and limitations of each metric is essential for an academic surgeon — both for interpreting their own track record and for critically evaluating claims made about journal quality or colleague productivity.

*Research Impact Metrics — Definition, Strengths, and Limitations*
Metric | Definition | Calculated By | Strength | Limitation
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Citation count | Total number of times a paper is cited by other papers | Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar | Direct measure of scientific engagement | Includes self-citations and negative citations; field-dependent
Impact Factor (IF) | Average citations per paper published by a journal in the prior 2 years | Clarivate (Web of Science) | Familiar; correlates with readership | Journal-level (not paper-level); manipulable; biased toward review articles
H-index | Author has published H papers each cited at least H times (e.g., H=20 means 20 papers each cited ≥20 times) | Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar | Measures sustained productivity AND impact | Disadvantages early-career researchers; field-dependent; does not credit negative results
CiteScore | Average citations per document over 4 years (broader than IF) | Scopus (Elsevier) | More stable than 2-year IF; includes more document types | Scopus-specific; may favour certain fields
Altmetric score | Composite score of social media mentions, news coverage, policy citations, downloads | Altmetric.com | Measures real-world translation and public engagement | Does not correlate with citation impact; easily gamed; unstable over time
Downloads | Number of times the full text is accessed | Publisher platforms | Rapid early measure of interest | Does not indicate citation; includes automated bots

> **Board Pearl — H-index in Academic Promotion** — The H-index is increasingly used in academic promotion and grant panel decisions. Context is essential: an H-index of 15 in surgical oncology is impressive for a PGY5 resident but modest for a 20-year full professor. Field-adjusted norms exist: surgery (H~10–15 for senior lecturer level); basic science (H~20–25 for equivalent level). The H-index is NOT a linear scale — moving from H=10 to H=11 requires one more paper with 11+ citations, but moving from H=30 to H=31 requires a paper cited 31+ times, which takes years. Google Scholar gives the highest H-index (includes book chapters, conference abstracts); Web of Science the lowest (most restrictive); Scopus intermediate. Always specify the database when reporting your H-index.

**Impact factor manipulation** is a recognised problem in journal publishing. Methods include: encouraging self-citation (editors asking authors to add references to their journal); publishing large numbers of review articles (which accumulate citations rapidly); forming citation cartels between journals; and 'coercive citation' (reviewers requesting that authors add references to their own work). The journal impact factor is calculated by Clarivate based on Web of Science data — a journal's IF can be suppressed by Clarivate if manipulation is detected. Researchers should be aware that submitting to a journal whose reviewers repeatedly request unjustified self-citations is itself an ethics concern.
