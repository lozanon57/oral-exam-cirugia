# Hernia Surgery — General Surgery — Groin, Incisional & Abdominal Wall Reconstruction

*Block B · General Surgery · PGY1-2*

*Guidelines: EHS/HerniaSurge Inguinal Hernia Update 2023 (BJS Open) · EHS Midline Incisional Hernia 2023 (BJS) · EHS Primary Ventral Hernia 2019*


## Anatomy of Groin Hernias

The inguinal region is bounded by the inguinal ligament inferiorly, the rectus abdominis medially, and the iliac crest laterally. The inguinal canal transmits the spermatic cord (male) or round ligament (female) and the ilioinguinal nerve. The internal inguinal ring is a defect in the transversalis fascia at the mid-inguinal point; the external ring is a defect in the external oblique aponeurosis superior to the pubic tubercle. The inferior epigastric vessels serve as the key landmark dividing indirect (lateral) from direct (medial) inguinal hernias. The 'triangle of doom' (bounded medially by the vas deferens, laterally by the gonadal vessels, and inferiorly by the peritoneal fold) contains the external iliac artery and vein — stapling or dissection in this area risks catastrophic vascular injury. The 'triangle of pain' (lateral to the gonadal vessels, inferior to the iliopubic tract) contains the femoral branch of the genitofemoral nerve and the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve — tacks in this area cause chronic neuropathic pain. Cooper's ligament (the pectineal ligament) is the sturdy periosteal condensation on the superior pubic ramus — the medial anchor for mesh in laparoscopic repair and the site for McVay repair (Cooper's ligament repair) for femoral hernias. The iliopubic tract is the thickened inferior margin of the transversalis fascia running from the ASIS to the superior pubic ramus — the inferior limit of safe staple/tack placement in TEP/TAPP.

*Groin Hernia Classification*
Type | Position relative to epigastric vessels | Defect Location | Frequency
--- | --- | --- | ---
Indirect inguinal | Lateral to inferior epigastric vessels | Through internal inguinal ring | 60% of groin hernias
Direct inguinal | Medial to inferior epigastric vessels | Hasselbach's triangle floor (transversalis fascia) | 25%
Femoral | Below inguinal ligament, medial to femoral vein | Femoral canal | 15%; highest strangulation risk

> **Board Pearl — Femoral Hernia** — Femoral hernias have the highest strangulation risk of all groin hernias (~30% present as strangulation) because the femoral ring is narrow and rigid (bounded by inguinal ligament, lacunar ligament, femoral vein, and iliopubic tract). All femoral hernias should be repaired promptly — non-operative management is not appropriate for femoral hernias in fit patients. Femoral hernias are more common in women but inguinal hernias remain the most common groin hernia in women overall.

> **Board Pearl — Genitofemoral Nerve Anatomy** — The genitofemoral nerve (L1-L2) divides into: femoral branch (passes lateral to femoral artery, provides sensation to femoral triangle skin) and genital branch (passes through internal inguinal ring with spermatic cord, provides sensation to scrotum/labium majus and cremasteric muscle). The ilioinguinal nerve (L1) enters the inguinal canal through the internal ring and provides sensation to the upper medial thigh and scrotum/labium majus. Both are at risk in open anterior repair (Lichtenstein) from direct injury or mesh compression. In TEP/TAPP, the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (L2-L3) and the femoral branch of the genitofemoral nerve are at risk from tacks placed lateral to the gonadal vessels below the iliopubic tract (triangle of pain).


## Operative Techniques

*Groin Hernia Repair — Technique Comparison*
Technique | Approach | Anaesthesia | Best Indication | Advantage
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Lichtenstein | Open anterior | LA/GA | High-risk patient; local anaesthesia preference; recurrent after laparoscopic | Lowest learning curve; LA option
TEP (totally extraperitoneal) | Laparoscopic extraperitoneal | GA | Primary bilateral inguinal; unilateral with expertise | No peritoneal entry; faster recovery
TAPP (transabdominal preperitoneal) | Laparoscopic transperitoneal | GA | Recurrent hernia after open; unclear anatomy on TEP; prior pelvic surgery (relative contra-indication) | Better visualisation; flexible
Shouldice | Open tissue repair | LA/GA | Young patient without mesh preference; small direct hernia | No mesh; good results in experienced hands

> **LEVEL Trial (Eker, Lancet 2012) / CHECK trial** — TEP vs Lichtenstein for primary unilateral inguinal hernia: TEP and TAPP achieve lower chronic pain rates (7–10% less) compared to Lichtenstein at 2 years. Lichtenstein has lower learning curve and equivalent recurrence for unilateral primary hernia. EHS guidelines recommend laparoscopic (TEP/TAPP) as preferred approach where expertise exists.


## Laparoscopic Groin Hernia Repair — TEP & TAPP Technical Details

Total extraperitoneal repair (TEP) creates the preperitoneal space without entering the peritoneal cavity. Port placement: 10mm infraumbilical port (camera), two 5mm ports in the midline between umbilicus and pubis. Access to the preperitoneal space: open the anterior rectus sheath on one side, retract the rectus laterally, and enter the preperitoneal plane. Balloon dissection (Spacemaker balloon trocar or finger dissection) creates the working space down to the pubic symphysis. The space of Retzius (between the pubis anteriorly and the bladder posteriorly) is developed medially; the space of Bogros laterally. Key anatomical landmarks: pubic symphysis, Cooper's ligament, inferior epigastric vessels, vas deferens, gonadal vessels, and the peritoneal edge. The mesh (minimum 10×15cm, recommended 12×17cm) covers all three myopectineal orifices (direct, indirect, and femoral defects). Mesh is unrolled flat and secured medially to Cooper's ligament with one or two tacks; no lateral tacks below the iliopubic tract (triangle of pain). TEP contraindications (relative): prior infraumbilical surgery, prostatectomy, pelvic radiation — TAPP preferred. Parietalization of the cord structures (separating the vas and vessels from the peritoneum in the preperitoneal space) is essential for TEP/TAPP — failure to parietalise risks lateral recurrence around the cord.

TAPP (transabdominal preperitoneal repair) uses the peritoneal cavity as the working space and creates the preperitoneal space through a peritoneal incision. Port placement: 10mm umbilical camera port, two 5mm ports in the anterior axillary line bilaterally. The peritoneum is incised 2–3cm above the defect from the medial umbilical ligament to the ASIS. The preperitoneal flap is dissected inferiorly, exposing Cooper's ligament, iliopubic tract, cord structures, and the myopectineal orifice. Mesh (minimum 10×15cm) placed and secured. The peritoneum is closed with continuous suture or tacks. TAPP advantages over TEP: better vision; ability to inspect contralateral side; preferred for recurrent hernias after open repair (posterior approach avoids anterior scar); suitable after prior pelvic surgery with some experience. TAPP disadvantages: peritoneal entry risks visceral injury; requires meticulous peritoneal closure to prevent adhesion formation and bowel entrapment.

*TEP vs TAPP — Comparative Summary*
Feature | TEP | TAPP
--- | --- | ---
Peritoneal entry | No | Yes
Risk of visceral injury | Lower | Higher (bowel, bladder)
Risk of port site hernia | Lower | Higher
Visualisation | More limited | Better
Bilateral hernia repair | Excellent — same working space | Excellent
Prior infraumbilical surgery | Relative contra-indication | Preferred
Learning curve | Longer | Shorter
Preferred recurrent after open | Yes | Yes (either acceptable)

> **Mesh Fixation — Tacks vs Fibrin Glue** — Tack fixation (spiral tacks or absorbable tacks) is effective but risks injury to nerves in the triangle of pain. Fibrin glue fixation has equivalent short-term recurrence rates with significantly lower rates of post-operative pain and chronic neuralgia in randomised trials (TIMELI trial, Fumagalli 2013). EHS 2018 guidelines: for mesh ≥10×15cm, no fixation (large enough to stay in place) or fibrin glue preferred over mechanical tacks in TEP/TAPP to reduce chronic pain. If tacks used, medial fixation to Cooper's ligament only — never below the iliopubic tract laterally.


## Complex Incisional Hernia

Incisional hernias occur in 10–20% of laparotomy wounds. Complex incisional hernias — large defects (>10cm), recurrent hernias, infected mesh, loss of domain — require component separation techniques. Anterior component separation (Ramirez, 1990) divides the external oblique aponeurosis lateral to the rectus sheath bilaterally, gaining 8–10cm of medial advancement per side. Posterior component separation (transversus abdominis release, TAR) provides greater advancement with lower wound complication rates (avoids skin flap devascularisation) and is preferred for complex defects at experienced centres. TAR involves division of the transversus abdominis muscle medial to its lateral border, releasing the posterior rectus sheath and creating a large retromuscular space for sublay mesh placement. The retromuscular space (Rives-Stoppa position) is the gold standard for large midline incisional hernia repair — mesh is placed behind the rectus abdominis muscle but in front of the posterior rectus sheath, providing excellent mesh integration and low recurrence.

> **Board Pearl** — The European Hernia Society (EHS) classification grades incisional hernias by width (W1 <4cm, W2 4–10cm, W3 >10cm) and location (midline M1–M5, lateral L1–L4). Mesh reinforcement is mandatory for W2 and W3 defects — primary suture repair alone has >30% recurrence at 2 years for defects >4cm (PRIMA trial, Muysoms, Hernia 2016).


## Mesh Selection & Fixation Principles

Mesh selection is determined by: (1) contamination grade (clean vs contaminated field), (2) mesh position (onlay, sublay, intraperitoneal IPOM, extraperitoneal), and (3) patient factors (BMI, immunosuppression, diabetes). Synthetic meshes are the most widely used: polypropylene (heavyweight and lightweight), polyester, and PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride). Lightweight polypropylene (40–60g/m²) has demonstrated lower rates of chronic pain and improved compliance compared to heavyweight mesh in randomised trials, with equivalent recurrence at 3–5 years (LEICHT trial). Composite meshes (polypropylene with absorbable anti-adhesion coating on the peritoneal side) are used for intraperitoneal mesh placement (IPOM technique) to prevent visceral adhesions. Biological meshes (porcine/bovine acellular dermal matrix, porcine small intestinal submucosa) are used in contaminated fields (CDC class II–III) where synthetic mesh is contraindicated. They act as scaffolds for tissue ingrowth but do not provide permanent tensile strength — recurrence rates are higher than synthetic mesh (30–50% at 3 years). Biosynthetic meshes (long-lasting absorbable synthetic, e.g., Phasix — poly-4-hydroxybutyrate) bridge the gap between synthetic and biological, providing 12–18 months of structural support in contaminated fields with lower long-term recurrence than biological mesh.

*Mesh Selection by Contamination Grade*
CDC Class | Contamination | Mesh Choice | Alternative
--- | --- | --- | ---
Class I (clean) | No infection, no GI entry | Lightweight polypropylene | Polyester; PVDF
Class II (clean-contaminated) | GI entry, no spillage | Biological or biosynthetic | Heavyweight PP with antibiotic prophylaxis
Class III (contaminated) | Gross spillage, strangulation | Biological mesh or biosynthetic | No mesh: tissue repair only
Class IV (dirty-infected) | Active infection, abscess | Tissue repair only or biological | Delayed mesh placement

> **Mesh Infection — Management Principles** — Mesh infection after hernia repair requires individualised management: (1) Superficial SSI with mesh not involved — wound care + antibiotics; (2) Mesh involved but not eroded — attempt mesh salvage with washout + drain + antibiotics (success rate 30–50% for polypropylene; higher for biological); (3) Mesh erosion into bowel/viscera — mesh explantation mandatory; (4) Chronic sinus or fistula — mesh explantation + reconstruction (delayed with biological mesh or tissue repair). Polypropylene mesh can sometimes be salvaged with aggressive surgical debridement and wound VAC therapy. Polyester and multi-filament meshes cannot be salvaged and require removal. Re-repair after mesh explantation should be delayed 6–12 months and performed with biological or biosynthetic mesh.


## Chronic Groin Pain After Hernia Repair

Chronic inguinodynia (chronic groin pain after hernia repair) is defined as pain persisting >3 months after surgery that affects daily activities. Incidence: 10–15% after Lichtenstein, 5–8% after TEP/TAPP. The International Association for the Study of Pain (IASP) distinguishes neuropathic pain (nerve injury or entrapment — burning, shooting, allodynia, hyperalgesia) from nociceptive pain (mesh traction, scar, recurrence — dull, aching, activity-related). Nerves at risk in open repair: ilioinguinal (most common — medial thigh/scrotal numbness), iliohypogastric, and genital branch of genitofemoral nerve. In laparoscopic repair: lateral femoral cutaneous nerve and femoral branch of genitofemoral nerve (triangle of pain). Risk factors for chronic pain: young age, pre-operative pain, high pain sensitivity, nerve entrapment in mesh/suture, mesh contraction, and recurrence. Management ladder: (1) conservative — physiotherapy, NSAIDs, gabapentinoids; (2) nerve blocks (diagnostic and therapeutic — ilioinguinal/iliohypogastric/genitofemoral injection); (3) surgical exploration — neurectomy (triple neurectomy: ilioinguinal + iliohypogastric + genital branch) ± mesh removal if mesh contraction/migration identified; (4) neuromodulation (spinal cord stimulation) for refractory cases.

> **Board Pearl — Triple Neurectomy for Chronic Inguinodynia** — Triple neurectomy (simultaneous division of ilioinguinal, iliohypogastric, and genital branch of genitofemoral nerve) achieves significant pain reduction in 70–80% of carefully selected patients with chronic neuropathic inguinodynia after open repair. The procedure removes the three nerves at risk regardless of which is identified as the pain generator — avoiding reoperation for incomplete neurectomy. Numbness in the distribution of the divided nerves is an expected sequela and must be discussed pre-operatively.


## Parastomal & Lumbar Hernias

Parastomal hernia occurs in 30–50% of colostomies and 10–30% of ileostomies within 5 years. Repair is challenging with high recurrence rates. Options: (1) relocation of the stoma to a new site (high recurrence at new site); (2) local repair (primary suture or mesh) — high recurrence; (3) keyhole mesh (retromuscular position around the stoma aperture); (4) Sugarbaker technique (mesh placed extraperitoneally with the stoma lateralised under the mesh, preventing intestinal herniation through the stoma aperture). The Sugarbaker technique has the lowest recurrence rate in randomised comparisons (STOMA trial, Brandsma 2017). Prophylactic mesh placement at the time of stoma creation reduces parastomal hernia incidence by 50% at 2 years (PREVENT trial) — keyhole lightweight polypropylene mesh at index operation is recommended when a permanent stoma is planned. Lumbar hernia occurs through the superior lumbar triangle (Grynfeltt-Lesshaft — bounded by the 12th rib, sacrospinalis muscle, and internal oblique) or inferior lumbar triangle (Petit — bounded by the iliac crest, latissimus dorsi, and external oblique). Lumbar hernias are rare (<300 reported cases); secondary lumbar hernias after retroperitoneal surgery (nephrectomy, iliac crest bone graft) are more common. Repair: open retromuscular mesh repair or laparoscopic extraperitoneal mesh.

> **Board Pearl — Spigelian and Obturator Hernias** — Spigelian hernia occurs through the semilunar line (lateral border of rectus abdominis) at the level of the arcuate line. It is typically interparietal (between the external oblique and internal oblique aponeuroses), making it impalpable on examination — most present with pain and intermittent bulge. CT is diagnostic. Repair: open or laparoscopic with mesh. Obturator hernia occurs through the obturator canal and is most common in elderly, thin, multiparous women. Howship-Romberg sign (medial thigh pain exacerbated by hip internal rotation) is pathognomonic. Typically presents as SBO with CT identifying the hernia in the obturator canal. Repair via laparotomy or laparoscopic TAPP.


## Paediatric Hernias & Hydrocele

Paediatric inguinal hernias are virtually always indirect, caused by a patent processus vaginalis (PPV) — failure of peritoneal obliteration along the descent of the testis or round ligament. Incidence: 3–5% of term infants, up to 30% in premature infants. Male:female ratio 6:1. Right-sided predominance (60%) due to later descent of the right testis. All paediatric inguinal hernias require elective repair to prevent incarceration — non-operative management is not appropriate as spontaneous closure does not occur (unlike hydrocele). Operative technique: open herniotomy (ligation of the patent processus vaginalis at the internal ring, without mesh) is the standard in children. Laparoscopic herniotomy is increasingly used and allows inspection of the contralateral side (20–30% contralateral PPV). Hydrocele: a patent processus vaginalis that admits peritoneal fluid but not bowel. Communicating hydrocele (size varies with posture) requires repair; non-communicating hydrocele in children <18 months frequently closes spontaneously and can be observed. Repair at 12–18 months if persistent. Varicocele: dilation of the pampiniform plexus in the spermatic cord — associated with impaired spermatogenesis. Surgical or interventional radiology (embolisation) treatment in adolescents with asymmetric testicular atrophy or oligospermia.

> **Incarcerated Paediatric Inguinal Hernia** — Incarceration occurs in 20% of paediatric inguinal hernias in the first year of life. Manual reduction should be attempted first (after adequate analgesia/sedation) — success rate 70–80%. If reduction is successful, semi-elective repair within 24–48h (not immediate — post-reduction oedema increases operative difficulty). Emergency surgery is required if: reduction fails, necrotic bowel, ovarian torsion (incarcerated ovary in girls — palpate for hard, non-tender lump in labia majora — urgent operation), or strangulation. Testicular ischaemia from cord compression during incarceration occurs in 2–10% — assess testicular viability post-operatively.
