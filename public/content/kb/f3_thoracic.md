## Thoracic Anatomy

A thorough command of thoracic anatomy underpins safe surgical decision-making in both elective and emergency settings. The pleural space is a potential space between the visceral pleura (covering the lung) and the parietal pleura (lining the chest wall, diaphragm, and mediastinum). Normally it contains 5–15 mL of serous fluid. The right lung has three lobes — upper, middle, and lower — separated by the oblique fissure (separates lower lobe from upper and middle) and the horizontal fissure (separates upper from middle). The left lung has two lobes — upper and lower — separated by the oblique fissure; the lingula is the equivalent of the right middle lobe. Accessory fissures are common and visible on CT.

The bronchopulmonary segments are the functional units of the lung, each supplied by a segmental bronchus and segmental artery (10 in the right lung, 8–10 in the left). Knowledge of segmental anatomy is essential for anatomical sublobar resections (segmentectomy). The right lung has 10 segments: S1 (apical), S2 (posterior), S3 (anterior) in the upper lobe; S4 (lateral), S5 (medial) in the middle lobe; S6 (superior), S7 (medial basal), S8 (anterior basal), S9 (lateral basal), S10 (posterior basal) in the lower lobe.

*Mediastinal Compartments — Boundaries and Contents*
Compartment | Boundaries | Normal Contents | Pathological Masses
--- | --- | --- | ---
Anterior (prevascular) | Posterior to sternum, anterior to pericardium/great vessels | Thymus, internal mammary vessels, lymph nodes, fat | Thymoma, teratoma/germ cell tumour, lymphoma (Hodgkin's — '4 Ts': Thymoma, Teratoma, Terrible lymphoma, Thyroid)

> **Board Pearl — Surgical Landmarks for Thoracic Procedures** — Angle of Louis (manubriosternal junction) = level of 2nd costal cartilage = T4/T5 disc = bifurcation of trachea = start of aortic arch. Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (2nd ICS MCL) — insert needle ABOVE the 3rd rib to avoid neurovascular bundle. Chest drain insertion: 5th ICS, mid-axillary line (MAL) — the 'triangle of safety' (bounded anteriorly by lateral border of pectoralis major, laterally by anterior border of latissimus dorsi, inferiorly by the 5th ICS). Never insert below 5th ICS (risk of diaphragm injury and intra-abdominal organ puncture).

> **Intercostal Neurovascular Bundle** — The intercostal neurovascular bundle (vein, artery, nerve — VAN from superior to inferior) runs in the costal groove on the inferior aspect of each rib. Instruments should be inserted OVER the SUPERIOR border of the LOWER rib (e.g., over the 3rd rib when entering the 2nd ICS) to avoid injury to the bundle.


## Pleural Disease

Pneumothorax is classified as primary (no underlying lung disease — typically tall, thin young males; thought to result from rupture of apical subpleural blebs) or secondary (underlying lung disease — COPD, cystic fibrosis, pneumonia, malignancy — much higher morbidity). The British Thoracic Society (BTS) 2023 guidelines stratify management by size and symptoms. Size is measured at the level of the hilum on CXR: >2 cm rim of air = large. For primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP): symptomatic or large → aspiration (16G cannula, 2nd ICS MCL) as first-line; if aspiration fails or secondary pneumothorax → intercostal drain (10–14 Fr small-bore Seldinger). Surgical indications: second ipsilateral pneumothorax, first contralateral, bilateral simultaneous, failure of drain to re-expand lung after 5 days, persistent air leak. Surgical standard: VATS pleurodesis (talc insufflation or mechanical abrasion + bullectomy).

Pleural effusion: Light's criteria (1972) distinguish transudates (all three criteria negative) from exudates (any one positive): (1) pleural protein/serum protein ratio >0.5; (2) pleural LDH/serum LDH ratio >0.6; (3) pleural LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH. Transudates: heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, hypothyroidism. Exudates: malignancy (most common in age >60), pneumonia/empyema, TB, PE, autoimmune. Parapneumonic effusions progress: simple (sterile exudate) → complicated (bacteria, low pH<7.2, low glucose<2.2 mmol/L, high LDH) → empyema (frank pus or positive culture).

*Light's Criteria — Pleural Fluid Analysis*
Criterion | Exudate | Transudate | Clinical Use
--- | --- | --- | ---
Pleural protein / serum protein | >0.5 | ≤0.5 | Most discriminatory single test
Pleural LDH / serum LDH | >0.6 | ≤0.6 | Useful if protein borderline
Pleural LDH vs serum ULN | >2/3 of ULN | ≤2/3 of ULN | Indicates cellular activity
pH (complicated parapneumonic) | <7.2 → drain | N/A | pH<7.2 = drain mandatory
Glucose (complicated parapneumonic) | <2.2 mmol/L → drain | N/A | Correlates with empyema risk
LDH (complicated parapneumonic) | >1000 IU/L → drain | N/A | High LDH = active infection

Mesothelioma: malignant pleural mesothelioma (MPM) is strongly associated with asbestos exposure (latency 30–40 years). Histological subtypes: epithelioid (best prognosis), sarcomatoid (worst), biphasic. Diagnosis requires CT chest (pleural thickening, calcified plaques, effusion) followed by thoracoscopic biopsy (pleural fluid cytology alone insufficient — sensitivity only 30–40%). The MARS2 trial (2023, JAMA Surgery) compared extended pleurectomy/decortication (EPD) versus chemotherapy alone: EPD was associated with inferior overall survival vs no surgery (median OS 19.3 vs 24.8 months, HR 1.54 — surgery caused harm). This reversed previous practice of aggressive surgical resection. Current standard: platinum + pemetrexed chemotherapy ± bevacizumab; immunotherapy (nivolumab + ipilimumab — CheckMate 743) for first-line; pembrolizumab monotherapy in selected cases (IND.227 trial demonstrated benefit in PD-L1+ MPM).

> **MARS2 — Paradigm Shift: Surgery Harms in Mesothelioma** — The MARS2 RCT (2023) demonstrated that extended pleurectomy/decortication (EPD) worsened survival compared to chemotherapy alone for MPM (HR for death 1.54; p=0.03). Extrapleural pneumonectomy (EPP) is no longer recommended. Surgical palliation (pleurodesis, indwelling pleural catheter for effusion) remains appropriate for symptom control.


## Lung Cancer

Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide. Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) accounts for 85% (adenocarcinoma 40%, squamous cell 25–30%, large cell 10%); small cell lung cancer (SCLC) 15%. SCLC is almost always metastatic at diagnosis (limited vs extensive stage) and is managed with chemo-immunotherapy (atezolizumab + carboplatin/etoposide); surgery is rare. NSCLC staging uses the TNM 9th edition (IASLC 2023): T (tumour size/invasion), N (nodal — N0 no nodes, N1 ipsilateral hilar, N2 ipsilateral mediastinal, N3 contralateral or supraclavicular), M (metastasis). Stage groupings: IA–IIB = surgically resectable; IIIA = potentially resectable (selected N2); IIIB-IV = unresectable.

Resectability thresholds: (1) FEV1 predicted postoperative (ppoFEV1) >40% — minimum for lobectomy; >30% with satisfactory DLCO for selected patients; (2) DLCO ppo >40%; (3) VO2max >15 mL/kg/min on CPET = low risk; 10–15 = moderate risk; <10 = high risk/unresectable. Predicted postoperative FEV1 is calculated: ppoFEV1 = preoperative FEV1 × (1 − fraction of functional lung removed). For pneumonectomy: ppoFEV1 >40% and DLCO >40% are the minimum thresholds.

Mediastinal staging is essential before resection in clinically stage I–III NSCLC. CT is inadequate alone (sensitivity 55%, specificity 81%). Endobronchial ultrasound (EBUS) with TBNA is the first-line invasive staging modality — sensitivity 88–93%, specificity 100% for N2/N3 disease. Mediastinoscopy (cervical) remains the gold standard (sensitivity ~90%) and is used when EBUS is negative but clinical suspicion for N2 remains high. EBUS has replaced routine mediastinoscopy at most centres but confirmation mediastinoscopy ('remediastinoscopy' after neoadjuvant) is sometimes required. PET-CT identifies occult metastases and guides biopsy targets.

Neoadjuvant/adjuvant therapies: CheckMate 816 (NEJM 2022): nivolumab + chemotherapy × 3 cycles neoadjuvant for stage IB–IIIA NSCLC — pathological complete response (pCR) 24% vs 2.2% with chemo alone; significantly improved EFS (HR 0.63). This is now standard neoadjuvant for resectable stage II–IIIA. ADAURA trial (NEJM 2020): adjuvant osimertinib (EGFR-mutated, stage IB–IIIA) after complete resection — DFS HR 0.20 (80% risk reduction); 5-year OS benefit demonstrated. Osimertinib 80 mg daily × 3 years is standard of care for EGFR-mutated resected NSCLC.

> **Board Pearl — Key Trials Summary for Lung Cancer** — ADAURA: adjuvant osimertinib for EGFR+ (exon 19 del or L858R) resected NSCLC stages IB–IIIA → 80% DFS benefit. CheckMate 816: neoadjuvant nivolumab + chemo for stage IB–IIIA → 24% pCR rate, improved EFS. VIOLET: VATS lobectomy = better QoL than open, equivalent oncology. JCOG0802: segmentectomy preferred for ≤2cm stage IA NSCLC.


## Oesophageal Surgery (Malignant)

Oesophageal cancer is the 6th leading cause of cancer mortality. Two main histological types: adenocarcinoma (AC — Barrett's oesophagus, obesity, GORD; arising at GOJ/lower oesophagus; dominant in Western countries) and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC — tobacco, alcohol, achalasia; upper/middle oesophagus; dominant in Asia and Africa). Staging requires CT chest/abdomen/pelvis (lymph nodes, distant metastases), EUS (T and N staging — most accurate for depth of invasion), and PET-CT (occult M1 disease detected in 15% altering management). Laparoscopy ± peritoneal washings for GOJ/gastric involvement.

Surgical approaches: (1) Ivor Lewis oesophagectomy (right thoracotomy + laparotomy + intrathoracic anastomosis) — best for mid/lower oesophageal tumours; requires adequate thoracic reach. (2) McKeown (3-stage) oesophagectomy (right thoracotomy + laparotomy + left cervical anastomosis) — best for upper/middle tumours; higher anastomotic leak rate but leaks less morbid (cervical). (3) Transhiatal oesophagectomy (laparotomy + blunt mediastinal dissection + cervical anastomosis, no thoracotomy) — avoids thoracic complications; limited mediastinal lymphadenectomy; higher recurrence in SCC.

Anastomotic leak occurs in 5–15% after oesophagectomy and carries significant mortality (Clavien-Dindo grade IVa). Management depends on leak severity: cervical leaks — open wound, drain, NPO, will usually heal with wound care; intrathoracic leaks — CT-guided drain + endoscopic stent (self-expanding metal stent, SEMS) as primary approach in contained leaks; reoperation for uncontained leaks with sepsis, failed stenting. Covered SEMS with concomitant drainage is now first-line for type II/III intrathoracic leaks.

## Chest Wall & Mediastinal Disease

Chest wall tumours: primary chest wall tumours are uncommon. Benign: osteochondroma (most common benign), fibrous dysplasia, chondroma. Malignant: chondrosarcoma (most common primary malignant), Ewing's sarcoma, solitary plasmacytoma, primitive neuroectodermal tumour (PNET). Metastatic disease (lung, breast, kidney, thyroid) is more common than primary. Resection principles: oncologically adequate margin ≥2 cm from tumour edge; resection of ≥1 entire rib above and below; chest wall reconstruction with prosthetic mesh (Prolene, PTFE, or methyl methacrylate sandwich) for defects >5 cm or involving anterior chest wall.

Sternal reconstruction: required after sternectomy for primary sternal tumours or radiation-induced necrosis. Techniques: titanium mesh/plates + pectoralis major advancement flap; rectus abdominis myocutaneous flap for inferior sternal defects; omentum transposition for infected wounds. Multidisciplinary planning essential.

Thymoma and myasthenia gravis: thymoma is the most common anterior mediastinal tumour in adults. Staged by Masaoka classification: Stage I — complete encapsulation (5-year OS >95%); Stage IIA — microscopic capsular invasion; Stage IIB — macroscopic invasion; Stage III — invasion of adjacent organs; Stage IVA — pleural/pericardial dissemination; Stage IVB — haematogenous metastases. Treatment: complete surgical resection (thymectomy) via median sternotomy or VATS for early-stage. Masaoka III/IVA: multimodal (induction chemo + surgery + radiotherapy). Myasthenia gravis (MG) occurs in 30–40% of thymoma patients. Thymectomy improves MG symptoms in 80% (MGTX trial demonstrated benefit of thymectomy in non-thymomatous MG: improved Quantitative MG Score, reduced immunosuppression). Pre-op MG management: pyridostigmine, immunosuppression, plasmapheresis if severe (myasthenic crisis risk perioperatively).

*Masaoka Staging — Thymoma*
Stage | Pathological Finding | 5-Year OS | Treatment
--- | --- | --- | ---
I | Intact capsule, no invasion | >95% | Complete thymectomy (VATS or open)
IIA | Microscopic transcapsular invasion | ~90% | Complete thymectomy ± adjuvant RT
IIB | Macroscopic invasion of peritumoral fat | ~85% | Complete thymectomy + adjuvant RT

> **Anterior Mediastinal Mass — Airway Risk Before Biopsy** — Any anterior mediastinal mass presenting with symptoms of tracheal/vascular compression (cough, dyspnoea, facial oedema, SVC syndrome) requires CT and echocardiogram to assess airway compression BEFORE biopsy or GA. General anaesthesia in patients with >50% tracheal compression risks complete airway collapse on induction. Preferred approach: tissue diagnosis under local anaesthesia (CT-guided core biopsy, anterior mediastinotomy under LA, or image-guided FNA). If lymphoma is suspected, peripheral node biopsy or bone marrow biopsy may avoid mediastinal intervention entirely.

> **Mediastinal Mass Differential — The 4 Ts** — Anterior mediastinal masses = 4 Ts: Thymoma (most common), Teratoma/Germ Cell Tumour (AFP + beta-HCG — elevated in non-seminomatous GCT), Terrible lymphoma (Hodgkin's most common), Thyroid (retrosternal goitre — moves on swallowing, radioiodine uptake on scan). Middle mediastinum: aortic aneurysm, pericardial/bronchogenic cyst, lymphoma. Posterior mediastinum: neurogenic tumour (posterior in a young patient = schwannoma/neurofibroma until proven otherwise).


## Thoracic Emergencies

Tension pneumothorax is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate clinical diagnosis and treatment — do not wait for CXR. Clinical features: tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side (late sign), absent breath sounds on affected side, hypotension, elevated JVP (raised CVP), tachycardia, hypoxia, haemodynamic collapse. Mechanism: air enters pleural space through a one-way valve mechanism, accumulates under pressure, compresses mediastinum, kinks great veins (IVC/SVC), reduces venous return, causes obstructive shock.

Immediate management: needle decompression — 14G cannula, 2nd ICS, midclavicular line — inserted over superior border of 3rd rib; listen for hiss of air under pressure. This converts tension to simple pneumothorax, buying time. Immediately followed by formal intercostal drain (28–32 Fr) in the triangle of safety (5th ICS, MAL). Do NOT wait for needle decompression confirmation before placing drain if diagnosis is clinically certain.

Massive haemothorax: defined as >1500 mL blood immediately drained or >200 mL/h for 2–4 hours after chest drain insertion. Cause: intercostal vessel injury (usually low-pressure — responds to drainage), pulmonary vessel/hilar injury (high-pressure — requires operative control), subclavian/aortic injury. Management: immediate large-bore chest drain (32–36 Fr), resuscitation, surgical exploration for persistent bleeding. Thoracotomy indications: initial drainage >1500 mL, ongoing loss >200 mL/h × 4h, haemodynamic instability. VATS can achieve haemostasis in selected stable patients.

*Thoracic Emergencies — Rapid Summary*
Emergency | Classic Signs | Immediate Action | Definitive Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Tension pneumothorax | Absent BS, tracheal deviation away, ↑JVP, hypotension, hypoxia | 14G needle — 2nd ICS MCL (over 3rd rib) | Chest drain 28–32 Fr — 5th ICS MAL
Massive haemothorax | >1500 mL drained, shock, absent BS | Large-bore (32–36 Fr) drain, transfusion, MTP | Thoracotomy if ongoing >200 mL/h × 4h
Open pneumothorax (sucking chest wound) | Wound at chest wall communicating with pleural space, paradoxical breathing | 3-sided occlusive dressing (leaves one side open to prevent tension PTX) | Surgical closure of wound + chest drain placed remotely
Cardiac tamponade | Beck's triad: hypotension + muffled heart sounds + ↑JVP; pulsus paradoxus >10mmHg | Pericardiocentesis — subxiphoid approach, 18G needle | Surgical pericardial window (more reliable; open thoracotomy if traumatic)
Bronchopleural fistula | Post-pneumonectomy air leak, empyema, contralateral flooding | Position affected side DOWN, NBM, bronchoscopy | Endoscopic occlusion (fibrin glue/coil) or surgical repair

> **Beck's Triad — Cardiac Tamponade** — Beck's triad: (1) Hypotension — reduced cardiac output; (2) Muffled heart sounds — pericardial fluid attenuates sounds; (3) Elevated JVP (raised CVP) — impaired venous return. Additional sign: pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg fall in systolic BP on inspiration). ECG: low-voltage complexes, electrical alternans (swinging heart). Confirm with FAST/echo (pericardial fluid with RA/RV collapse in diastole). Pericardiocentesis relieves tamponade temporarily; subxiphoid pericardial window under GA is definitive for recurrent/traumatic. Emergency thoracotomy for traumatic tamponade in arrest.

> **Open Chest Wound — 3-Sided Dressing** — Open pneumothorax ('sucking chest wound'): air enters pleural space through chest wall defect on inspiration, equilibrating pleural and atmospheric pressure, collapsing the lung. Apply a 3-sided occlusive dressing (Asherman chest seal or improvised — tape 3 sides, leave inferior side open as flutter valve). This allows air to escape on expiration but prevents entry on inspiration, preventing tension pneumothorax. A separate chest drain must be placed remote from the wound. Do NOT apply a 4-sided dressing — this converts to tension pneumothorax.

Bronchopleural fistula (BPF): abnormal communication between the bronchial tree and pleural space. Most commonly occurs after pneumonectomy (1–3% incidence; right-sided pneumonectomy higher risk due to shorter right main bronchial stump). Risk factors: poor bronchial stump closure technique, residual tumour at margin, post-operative empyema, prolonged mechanical ventilation, diabetes, steroid use, prior radiotherapy. Clinical features: sudden expectoration of large volume of serous/seropurulent fluid (post-pneumonectomy space contents flooding contralateral lung — potentially fatal), falling fluid level on CXR, persistent air leak after lobectomy. Management: immediate — place patient with affected side DOWN to protect contralateral lung, nil by mouth, broad-spectrum antibiotics, bronchoscopy to confirm BPF. Definitive: endoscopic occlusion (fibrin glue, coils, bronchial blockers) for small BPF; re-thoracotomy and surgical repair (mobilising local tissue flap — intercostal muscle or omentum — to reinforce stump) for large BPF.
