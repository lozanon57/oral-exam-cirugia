## Diverticular Disease — Epidemiology & Pathophysiology

**Colonic diverticulosis** refers to the presence of acquired pseudodiverticula (outpouchings of mucosa and submucosa through weaknesses in the muscularis propria, typically at sites where vasa recta penetrate the bowel wall). True diverticula (all bowel wall layers) are rare in the colon. Diverticulosis prevalence increases dramatically with age: <40 years <5%; 60 years ~30%; >80 years ~65%. In Western countries, 80% of diverticula are left-sided (sigmoid colon), where intraluminal pressure is highest (law of Laplace: T = P×r — smaller sigmoid radius generates higher wall tension for same luminal pressure).

**Pathophysiology**: Low dietary fibre → small, firm stools → prolonged colonic transit → segmentation contractions generate high intraluminal pressures → mucosal herniation at vasa recta entry points. Risk factors: low dietary fibre, sedentary lifestyle, obesity (RR 1.78), NSAIDs (RR 1.50), alcohol, smoking, red meat intake. The **microbiome hypothesis** proposes that dysbiosis (reduced Lactobacillus, increased Proteobacteria) promotes chronic low-grade inflammation predisposing to diverticulitis.

*Diverticulosis vs Diverticulitis vs Diverticular Disease*
Term | Definition | Prevalence | Annual Progression Rate
--- | --- | --- | ---
Diverticulosis | Asymptomatic presence of diverticula | 30–65% in >60 yrs | 4% per year develop symptoms
Symptomatic uncomplicated diverticular disease (SUDD) | Recurrent abdominal pain without acute inflammation | ~20% of diverticulosis | Variable
Acute uncomplicated diverticulitis | Acute inflammation without abscess/perforation/fistula/obstruction | ~4% of diverticulosis lifetime | ~20% recurrence at 1 year
Acute complicated diverticulitis | Diverticulitis + abscess/perforation/fistula/obstruction | ~25% of acute diverticulitis episodes | Higher surgical rate

> **Right-sided vs Left-sided Diverticular Disease** — Right-sided diverticulosis (cecum, ascending colon) is more common in Asian populations (Japan, Korea, Taiwan — up to 75% of cases). Right-sided diverticula are often true diverticula. Right-sided diverticulitis can mimic appendicitis and presents with right iliac fossa pain. Right hemicolectomy is the standard surgical treatment (vs sigmoid resection for left-sided disease). Differentiation from appendicitis often requires CT scan.


## Acute Diverticulitis — Classification Systems

CT of the abdomen and pelvis (IV contrast, sensitivity 97%, specificity 99%) is the diagnostic standard for acute diverticulitis, demonstrating pericolic fat stranding, bowel wall thickening (>4 mm), diverticula, abscesses, pneumoperitoneum, or fistulae. Ultrasound is an alternative (sensitivity 92%, specificity 90%) for uncomplicated cases, avoiding radiation — preferred in young women (exclude gynaecological pathology).

*Hinchey Classification of Complicated Diverticulitis*
Stage | CT Finding | Clinical Correlate | Standard Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Stage I | Pericolic abscess (≤5 cm) | Localized peritonitis, tender mass RLQ or LLQ | Antibiotics ± percutaneous drainage if large
Stage II | Distant (pelvic) abscess (>5 cm) | Pelvic pain, tender mass, sepsis | Antibiotics + percutaneous drainage; surgical rescue if fails
Stage III | Purulent peritonitis (generalized — perforated abscess) | Generalized peritonitis, acutely ill, sepsis | Sigmoid resection; Hartmann's OR primary anastomosis + diverting ileostomy
Stage IV | Fecal peritonitis (free perforation) | Acutely ill, hemodynamic instability, frank peritonitis | Emergency Hartmann's procedure; source control priority

*Modified CT-Based Classification (Ambrosetti, Sartelli/WSES)*
Category | CT Features | CRP (mg/L) | WBC (/μL) | Management
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Mild (uncomplicated) | Pericolic fat stranding ± wall thickening; no abscess | <140 | <13,000 | Outpatient antibiotics or no antibiotics (selected patients)
Moderate (complicated — abscess) | Hinchey I or II abscess; contained perforation | 140–200 | 13,000–15,000 | Inpatient antibiotics; percutaneous drainage if abscess ≥4 cm
Severe (complicated — peritonitis) | Hinchey III or IV; free air; fecal peritonitis | >200 | >15,000 | Emergency surgery; Hartmann's or primary anastomosis

> **CRP & WBC as Severity Predictors** — CRP >140–150 mg/L on admission is strongly associated with complicated diverticulitis (abscess or perforation) and predicts failure of conservative management. WBC >13,000/μL adds specificity. Patients with uncomplicated diverticulitis and CRP <50 mg/L can often be managed as outpatients. Serial CRP is useful for monitoring response to antibiotics — failure to decrease by 25% at 48–72h suggests treatment failure or missed complication.

> **Post-Diverticulitis Colonoscopy — Timing** — Colonoscopy should be performed 6–8 weeks after recovery from acute diverticulitis to: (1) exclude colorectal cancer mimicking diverticulitis (found in 1–2% of cases), (2) exclude IBD. Colonoscopy is NOT performed during the acute episode (perforation risk). Patients with complicated diverticulitis (abscess, perforation) have higher cancer miss rate — colonoscopy is mandatory. For uncomplicated diverticulitis with reliable CT and low clinical suspicion for cancer, colonoscopy can be deferred or performed at 8–12 weeks.


## Interventional & Emergency Surgical Management

**Hinchey II (pelvic/distant abscess ≥4 cm)**: percutaneous image-guided drainage (CT or US) + antibiotics is the preferred management over immediate surgery. Drainage achieves resolution in 70–80% of patients, converting emergencies to elective procedures. Failed percutaneous drainage (fever >48h, increasing sepsis) requires surgical intervention. After successful drainage and recovery, elective sigmoid resection is discussed.

*Hartmann's Procedure vs Primary Anastomosis with Diverting Ileostomy — Comparison*
Factor | Hartmann's Procedure | Primary Anastomosis + Diverting Ileostomy
--- | --- | ---
Indication | Hinchey III/IV; hemodynamic instability; immunosuppression; high-risk anastomosis | Hinchey III; hemodynamically stable; experienced surgeon; good nutritional status
Stoma type | End colostomy (sigmoid) | Loop ileostomy (diverting)
Reversal complexity | High (Hartmann's reversal — laparotomy, colorectal anastomosis) | Lower (loop ileostomy reversal — simpler operation)
Permanent stoma risk | ~30–50% | ~10–20%
Anastomotic leak risk | Not applicable | ~6–10% (protected by diverting stoma)
Mortality | Comparable | Comparable in selected patients
LADIES trial conclusion | Higher permanent stoma rate | Preferred when feasible and safe

> **Laparoscopic Peritoneal Lavage for Hinchey III — Negative Evidence** — Three major RCTs (DILALA, SCANDIV, LADIES-LOLA arm) evaluated laparoscopic peritoneal lavage (LPL) vs sigmoid resection for Hinchey III purulent diverticulitis. ALL THREE showed LPL to be INFERIOR: higher rate of reoperation (13–31% LPL vs 0–5% resection), treatment failure, and residual abscess. LPL does NOT address the perforated diverticulum. Conclusion: laparoscopic lavage CANNOT be recommended as standard treatment for Hinchey III — sigmoid resection remains the standard.

## Elective Sigmoid Resection — Indications & Technique

The decision for elective sigmoid resection following acute diverticulitis requires individualized assessment. Historical teaching of 'two episodes = surgery' has been abandoned. Current guidelines (ASCRS 2020, ESCP 2020) base decisions on: symptom severity, quality of life impact, patient fitness, complication risk of recurrence, and patient preference — NOT episode count alone.

*ASCRS 2020 — Indications for Elective Sigmoid Resection*
Indication Category | Details
--- | ---
Complicated diverticulitis (abscess/fistula) | After Hinchey II abscess successfully managed percutaneously; fistula (colovesical, colovaginal); after emergency surgery with Hartmann's — plan Hartmann's reversal
Recurrent diverticulitis with quality of life impairment | Recurrent episodes regardless of number — if significantly impacting quality of life (pain, anxiety, work/social impact); patient-reported outcome measures
High-risk recurrence features | Immunosuppressed patients (transplant, steroids, chemotherapy); CKD; first episode with large abscess; young age (<50 yrs) with high cumulative lifetime risk
Inability to exclude malignancy | If colonoscopy or CT cannot confidently exclude CRC — surgical resection for tissue diagnosis
After emergency surgery (Hartmann's) | Hartmann's reversal 3–6 months after emergency sigmoid resection and recovery

> **Timing of Elective Surgery After Acute Episode** — Elective sigmoid resection should be performed at least 6–8 weeks after the acute episode to allow inflammation to resolve. Operating during or immediately after the acute phase significantly increases anastomotic leak rates, wound complications, and conversion to open. Pre-operative colonoscopy 6–8 weeks post-episode is mandatory to exclude cancer before planning resection.

**Laparoscopic sigmoid resection** is the preferred approach for elective surgery (vs open): lower wound complications, shorter hospital stay (3–4 days vs 5–7 days), faster return to normal activities. The key technical principle: resection must include the entire sigmoid colon to the **peritoneal reflection** (rectosigmoid junction), with the proximal margin in non-inflamed, soft descending colon. Leaving a diseased sigmoid segment results in higher recurrence. The anastomosis is always to the **upper rectum** — colorectal anastomosis, not colocolonic. A properly functioning stapled colorectal anastomosis using a circular stapler (28–29 mm EEA) is the standard.

> **Diverticular Fistulae** — Colovesical fistula (most common, 65%): pneumaturia, fecaluria, recurrent UTI, typically in men (uterus protects in women). CT: air in bladder, thickened sigmoid-bladder interface. Cystoscopy ± sigmoidoscopy. Treatment: sigmoid resection + fistula division + bladder repair ± Foley catheter. Colovaginal fistula (post-hysterectomy women): feculent vaginal discharge. Coloenteric fistula: usually asymptomatic. Colocutaneous fistula: rare, often post-operative.


## Colonic Volvulus — Sigmoid & Caecal

**Colonic volvulus** is axial torsion of the colon on its mesenteric axis causing closed-loop obstruction. Two forms: **sigmoid volvulus** (most common, 60–75% of colonic volvulus) and **caecal volvulus** (25–40%). Together they account for 10–15% of all large bowel obstruction.

*Sigmoid vs Caecal Volvulus — Comparative Features*
Feature | Sigmoid Volvulus | Caecal Volvulus
--- | --- | ---
Pathophysiology | Redundant sigmoid loop twists on mesentery; chronic constipation, psychiatric illness, Chagas disease risk factors | Incomplete caecal fixation to posterior peritoneum; right colon mobilizes and rotates
Demographics | Elderly, institutionalized, neuropsychiatric patients; common in Africa, India | Younger patients (30–60 yrs); post-laparotomy, pregnancy
X-ray appearance | Omega sign (large distended loop, apex in RUQ); 'coffee bean' sign | Ileocaecal bascule; kidney-bean sign pointing LUQ; 'bird of prey' sign on CT
First-line treatment | Rigid sigmoidoscopy or flexible colonoscopic decompression (success 70–90%) | Surgery (right hemicolectomy) — colonoscopic decompression less reliable; higher gangrene risk
Definitive treatment | Elective sigmoid resection after successful decompression (same admission or outpatient) | Right hemicolectomy with primary anastomosis (if viable colon) or ileostomy (if ischemic)
Recurrence without surgery | ~50–90% | ~20%
Emergency surgery indication | Peritonitis, failed decompression, suspected ischemia/gangrene | Signs of ischemia; failed endoscopic reduction; caecal bascule pattern

> **Endoscopic Decompression of Sigmoid Volvulus — Technique** — First-line: rigid proctoscopy or flexible sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy WITHOUT bowel prep (patient prepared with enema only). A soft rubber tube (flatus tube) advanced through the twisted sigmoid under endoscopic guidance. Decompression indicated by passage of flatus and liquid stool. Rectal tube left in situ for 24–48h. Success rate 70–90%. Contraindications: peritonitis, suspected ischemia (bloody mucosa, no mucosal perfusion), hemodynamic instability. After successful decompression: elective sigmoid resection same admission (recurrence rate 50–90% without surgery).


## Ogilvie's Syndrome — Colonic Pseudo-obstruction

**Ogilvie's syndrome (acute colonic pseudo-obstruction, ACPO)** is massive colonic dilation without mechanical obstruction. Pathophysiology: imbalance between sympathetic (inhibitory) and parasympathetic (stimulatory) colonic innervation — excess sympathetic tone → colon dilation. Predisposing factors: post-operative (especially orthopaedic, cardiac surgery), severe medical illness (pneumonia, cardiac failure, renal failure), opioid use, electrolyte disturbances, retroperitoneal haematoma.

**Diagnosis**: plain abdominal X-ray or CT showing massive colonic dilation (typically right colon, caecum most distended) without a transition point. CT with water-soluble contrast enema can confirm absence of mechanical obstruction. **Caecal diameter >12 cm** is associated with high risk of perforation (risk increases substantially above 10–12 cm). Perforation risk: 3% overall; up to 30% if caecal diameter >14 cm.

*Management Algorithm for Ogilvie's Syndrome*
Step | Intervention | Detail
--- | --- | ---
1. Supportive care | NBM, IV fluids, electrolyte correction, stop constipating medications (opioids, anticholinergics) | Monitor caecal diameter on serial X-rays/CT; NG tube suction
2. Neostigmine (first-line pharmacological) | 2 mg IV over 3–5 minutes; cardiac monitoring mandatory; atropine at bedside | Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor → increases parasympathetic tone → colonic decompression. Success rate 60–90%. Response within 30 min (borborygmi, flatus, stool). Contraindicated: mechanical obstruction, HR <60, bronchospasm, recent MI, renal failure
3. Colonoscopic decompression | If neostigmine fails or contraindicated; without full bowel prep | Decompression tube left in situ 24–48h. Success 60–80%. Recurrence 40%. Can repeat neostigmine or colonoscopy.
4. Surgery | Cecostomy or right hemicolectomy if all measures fail OR signs of ischemia/perforation | Rare; highest morbidity in already ill patients

> **Neostigmine Contraindications & Administration** — Neostigmine dose: 2 mg IV over 3–5 min. Mandatory: cardiac monitoring (ECG), atropine 0.6 mg at bedside (for bradycardia/bronchospasm), patient supine. Response: borborygmi within 10 min, flatus/stool within 30 min. CONTRAINDICATIONS: established mechanical obstruction, severe bradycardia (HR <60), active bronchospasm, recent myocardial infarction, pregnancy, renal failure (Cr >3 mg/dL). Second dose may be given after 3 hours if partial response.


## Colonic Angiodysplasia & Lower GI Bleeding

**Lower gastrointestinal bleeding (LGIB)** is defined as bleeding originating distal to the ligament of Treitz. Common causes: diverticular disease (30–40%), colonic angiodysplasia (15–20%), colorectal neoplasia (10%), anorectal disease (hemorrhoids, fissures — most common cause of minor LGIB), ischemic colitis, IBD. **Diverticular bleeding** is the most common cause of significant LGIB: typically painless, bright red or maroon rectal bleeding, often massive; usually self-limiting (80%).

**Colonic angiodysplasia** (arteriovenous malformations, AVMs): acquired, predominantly right colon, >60 years, associated with chronic renal failure, aortic stenosis (Heyde's syndrome), and anticoagulation. Endoscopic appearance: flat, cherry-red, fern-like vascular lesion. Diagnosis: colonoscopy (gold standard); CT angiography if actively bleeding (sensitivity >90% for extravasation >0.3–0.5 mL/min); mesenteric angiography (sensitivity >90% for extravasation >0.5 mL/min).

*LGIB Management Algorithm*
Step | Intervention | Detail
--- | --- | ---
1. Resuscitation | IV access, blood group + crossmatch, IV fluids, blood transfusion if Hb <7–8 | Hemodynamic assessment: HR, BP, postural hypotension; ICU if hemodynamically unstable
2. Colonoscopy | After rapid bowel prep (4L PEG); gold standard — diagnostic and therapeutic | Identifies bleeding source in 70–90%; treatment: adrenaline injection, bipolar coagulation, haemoclip, APC for angiodysplasia
3. CT angiography | If colonoscopy fails or active rapid bleeding (Hb drop >2 g/dL/24h) | Identifies extravasation >0.3 mL/min; guides intervention radiology targeting
4. Interventional radiology | Mesenteric angiography + superselective embolization (coils, polyvinyl alcohol) | Success 80–90% for active bleeding; ischemia risk 5–15%; post-embolization surveillance
5. Surgery | If all above fail; recurrent massive bleeding; ischemia post-embolization | Segmental resection if source localized; blind subtotal colectomy if source unlocalized (last resort — high morbidity)

> **Diverticular Bleeding — Key Facts** — Diverticular bleeding: left-sided diverticula are more common but RIGHT-sided diverticula bleed more frequently (despite lower prevalence). Bleeding is arterial (vasa recta erosion), typically massive, painless, bright red or maroon. Self-limiting in 80%. Recurrence after first bleed: 25% at 1 year. After second bleed: 50% recurrence. Elective sigmoid (or right) colectomy offered after second episode of significant diverticular bleeding. NSAID use doubles bleeding risk.
