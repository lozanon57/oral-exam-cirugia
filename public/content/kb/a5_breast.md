## Breast Conservation vs Mastectomy

Breast-conserving surgery (BCS) — wide local excision (WLE) with clear margins followed by whole-breast radiotherapy — is oncologically equivalent to mastectomy for most stage I-III breast cancers. This equivalence was established by the landmark Milan Trial (Veronesi, NEJM 2002) and NSABP B-06 (Fisher, NEJM 2002) with up to 20-year follow-up. BCS should be offered to all women without absolute contraindications (inability to receive RT, multicentric disease requiring non-achievable margins, patient preference for mastectomy).

*Contraindications to Breast-Conserving Surgery*
Contraindication | Type | Notes
--- | --- | ---
Multicentric disease (≥2 quadrants) | Relative/Absolute | May be overcome by oncoplastic techniques in selected cases
Prior breast irradiation to the same breast | Absolute | Retreatment RT not feasible
Positive margins not achievable | Absolute | After re-excision attempt; anatomy-dependent
Active SLE/scleroderma | Relative | Connective tissue disease increases RT toxicity
Pregnancy (1st/2nd trimester) | Relative | RT deferred; mastectomy may be preferred to avoid RT exposure
BRCA1/2 mutation with patient preference | Not contraindication | Equivalent local recurrence; contralateral risk counselling mandatory

> **ACOSOG Z0011 (Giuliano, JAMA 2011/2017)** — **Population:** 891 women with T1-T2 breast cancer, 1–2 positive sentinel nodes, BCS + whole-breast RT. **Result:** 10-year OS 86.3% SLN alone vs 83.6% ALND (non-inferiority p=0.02); locoregional recurrence equivalent. **Practice change:** Completion axillary lymph node dissection eliminated for this patient group. Patient eligibility: T1-T2, 1-2 positive SLN, BCS planned, whole-breast RT planned, no neoadjuvant therapy, clinically node-negative.

> **Board Pearl — Margin Width + NCCN 2026: SLNB Omission** — **Margin width:** For invasive breast cancer with BCS, the SSO/ASTRO consensus (2014) defines adequate margin as **no ink on tumour** — a 1mm or 2mm margin is not required. For DCIS, a 2mm margin is recommended. **NCCN v2.2026 — SLNB Omission:** SLNB may be omitted for carefully selected patients with **HR+/HER2−, cT1, cN0** undergoing BCS with planned whole-breast RT and endocrine therapy. Based on SOUND (Lancet Oncology 2023), INSEMA, and BOOG 2013-08 trials showing axillary recurrence rates of only 0.4–0.9% at 5 years. Criteria: postmenopausal >50 years, HR+/HER2−, grade 1–2, cT1N0, agreeable to whole-breast RT + endocrine therapy. Does NOT apply to HER2+ or TNBC subtypes or post-NAC patients.


## Sentinel Node Biopsy Technique

Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) is the standard axillary staging procedure for clinically node-negative (cN0) breast cancer. Dual-tracer technique (radioisotope 99mTc-labelled colloid + blue dye or fluorescent tracer) achieves the highest detection rate (>96%) with lowest false-negative rate (<5%). The procedure: peritumoral or periareolar injection of tracer; intraoperative gamma probe guidance to identify the hot node(s); visual identification of blue-stained nodes; ex vivo counting. Minimum 3 sentinel nodes should be retrieved; all hot and/or blue nodes are removed.

> **Common Pitfall — Z0011 Eligibility** — Do not omit ALND outside Z0011 criteria. Z0011 does NOT apply to: mastectomy patients; patients with ≥3 positive SLN; patients who received neoadjuvant chemotherapy; patients not planned for whole-breast RT. Applying Z0011 findings outside these criteria risks inadequate axillary management.


## Molecular Subtypes and Surgical Implications

*Breast Cancer Molecular Subtypes — Surgical Implications*

Luminal A | ER+/PR+ high, HER2-, low Ki-67 | Rarely — endocrine therapy preferred | BCS preferred; Oncotype DX may guide adjuvant chemo
Luminal B | ER+, HER2-, high Ki-67 OR HER2+ | Consider if downstaging needed | SLNB standard; post-NAC restage axilla
HER2+ (non-luminal) | HER2+ ER-/PR- | Yes — dual HER2 blockade + chemo | pCR 50–60%; residual disease → T-DM1

> **Board Pearl — BRCA1/2 and Surgical Decision** — **BRCA1/2 mutations** do not change the indication for BCS vs mastectomy from an oncological standpoint — local recurrence rates are equivalent. However, mutation carriers have a significantly elevated contralateral breast cancer risk (BRCA1: 65%, BRCA2: 45% lifetime) and bilateral risk-reducing mastectomy should be discussed. The surgical decision is driven by patient preference and contralateral risk management, not ipsilateral recurrence risk.


## Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy — Indications & Surgical Planning

Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) is administered before surgery to downstage the primary tumour and axilla, enabling breast-conserving surgery in patients who would otherwise require mastectomy, and to assess tumour chemosensitivity in vivo. In addition, pathological complete response (pCR — no residual invasive cancer in breast or nodes, ypT0/is ypN0) is a validated surrogate for improved long-term survival in HER2+ and TNBC subtypes. Subtypes most likely to benefit from and respond to NAC are: HER2+ (dual HER2 blockade with pertuzumab/trastuzumab + chemotherapy; pCR 40–65%), TNBC (platinum-containing regimens; pCR 35–55%), and large Luminal B (high Ki-67; pCR lower but downstaging meaningful). Luminal A tumours have very low pCR rates and rarely benefit from NAC — neoadjuvant endocrine therapy is an option for elderly/frail patients.

*Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy — Subtypes, Regimens, and Residual Disease Management*
Subtype | NAC Regimen | pCR Rate | Residual Disease Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
HER2+ (non-luminal) | AC × 4 → paclitaxel/trastuzumab/pertuzumab × 4 (NeoSphere protocol) | 50–65% | T-DM1 (ado-trastuzumab emtansine) for 14 cycles (KATHERINE trial)
HER2+ (luminal) | As above ± neoadjuvant AI | 35–50% | T-DM1 for residual; consider CDK4/6 inhibitors for HR+ residual
TNBC | AC × 4 → paclitaxel × 4 ± carboplatin (GeparSixto) | 40–55% with platinum | Capecitabine × 8 cycles (CREATE-X trial); olaparib if BRCAm
Luminal B (HER2-) | Anthracycline + taxane-based | 10–20% | Adjuvant CDK4/6 inhibitor (abemaciclib MONARCH-E); consider extended endocrine therapy

> **Board Pearl — pCR and Survival Surrogate** — pCR (ypT0/is ypN0) predicts improved event-free survival and OS in **HER2+ and TNBC** but is a weaker surrogate in HR+ (Luminal) subtypes. The FDA has accepted pCR as a regulatory endpoint for accelerated approval in these high-proliferative subtypes. For Luminal tumours, residual cancer burden (RCB score) provides better prognostic discrimination than pCR alone.


## Oncoplastic Breast Surgery — Techniques & Indications

Oncoplastic breast surgery (OPS) combines oncological resection with immediate plastic surgical reconstruction techniques to achieve tumour-free margins while preserving acceptable breast shape and volume. OPS has expanded the indications for breast-conserving surgery, enabling resection of larger tumours and challenging locations (central, lower pole, medial) that would otherwise require mastectomy. Two broad categories of oncoplastic techniques exist: volume displacement (rearranging existing breast tissue) and volume replacement (importing tissue from outside the breast).

*Oncoplastic Breast Surgery — Technique Classification and Indications*
Category | Techniques | Best Indication | Tumour:Breast Ratio
--- | --- | --- | ---
Volume Displacement — Level I | Glandular advancement flaps, batwing/hatchet excision, round block (periareolar) | Peripheral tumours; small-medium breasts | <20%
Volume Displacement — Level II | Therapeutic reduction mammoplasty (inverted-T, vertical scar), wise pattern, inferior pedicle | Large-breasted women; central/lower tumours; ptotic breasts | 20–50%
Volume Replacement — Pedicled | Thoracodorsal artery perforator (TAP) flap, lateral intercostal perforator (LICAP) flap | Lateral quadrant defects; thin-breasted women | >20%, moderate volume
Volume Replacement — LD mini-flap | Latissimus dorsi miniflap (without implant) | Upper outer/inner quadrant; partial mastectomy defect | 20–40%

> **Board Pearl — Tumour:Breast Ratio >20%** — A **tumour:breast ratio >20%** is the accepted threshold at which standard WLE is likely to produce an unacceptable cosmetic deformity. At this ratio, oncoplastic level II techniques (reduction mammoplasty, large volume displacement) or volume replacement should be considered. In large-breasted women with macromastia, therapeutic mammoplasty (resection of tumour combined with reduction) provides simultaneous oncological and aesthetic benefit and may offer the opportunity to irradiate a smaller breast volume.


## Immediate Breast Reconstruction — Implant-Based vs Autologous

Immediate breast reconstruction (IBR) at the time of mastectomy offers psychological, oncological, and practical advantages over delayed reconstruction. IBR does not delay adjuvant chemotherapy (typically starting 3–4 weeks post-mastectomy) or radiation (6–8 weeks post-surgery). Two broad approaches exist: implant-based reconstruction (IBR) — the most common technique worldwide — and autologous tissue reconstruction, which uses the patient's own tissue to recreate the breast mound.

*Immediate Breast Reconstruction — Implant-Based vs Autologous Comparison*
Parameter | Implant-Based (Direct/Two-Stage) | DIEP Flap | TRAM Flap | LD Flap ± Implant
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Operative time | 1–2 hours | 6–8 hours (microsurgery) | 4–6 hours | 3–4 hours
Recovery | 2–4 weeks | 4–6 weeks | 4–6 weeks | 3–4 weeks
Donor site morbidity | None | Minimal (muscle-sparing) | Abdominal wall weakness | Back weakness, seroma
Effect of radiation | High: capsular contracture, implant failure (30–50% with RT) | Moderate: fat necrosis but autologous tissue tolerates RT better | Moderate | Moderate
Body habitus requirement | None (requires adequate skin envelope) | Adequate abdominal tissue | Adequate abdominal tissue | Adequate back tissue
Nipple-sparing mastectomy suitability | Excellent (direct-to-implant) | Excellent | Good | Good
Long-term outcome | Implant exchange may be needed; 10yr revision ~30% | Durable; natural feel; ages naturally | Durable | Implant-dependent feel

> **Common Pitfall — Radiation Effect on Reconstruction** — Post-mastectomy radiotherapy (PMRT) significantly increases the risk of implant-based reconstruction failure (capsular contracture, implant rupture, chronic pain, poor cosmesis): incidence 30–50% with PMRT vs 5–15% without. When PMRT is anticipated (4+ positive nodes, T3/T4, close margins), discuss with the patient the option of: (1) delayed reconstruction after radiation; (2) autologous reconstruction (DIEP/TRAM tolerate radiation better); or (3) two-stage TE with planned conversion to autologous tissue after radiation. The tissue expander should remain deflated during radiation and exchanged to permanent implant or autologous reconstruction 3–6 months post-RT.

> **Board Pearl — Nipple-Sparing Mastectomy Criteria** — Nipple-sparing mastectomy (NSM) is oncologically safe when: tumour is >2 cm from the nipple; nipple-areola complex (NAC) is not involved clinically or on MRI; no bloody nipple discharge; no Paget's disease; nipple not inverted due to tumour. Intraoperative frozen section of the retroareolar tissue is mandatory — if positive, the NAC must be excised. NSM is contraindicated in inflammatory breast cancer and when subareolar tumour extension is confirmed.


## Axillary Management After Neoadjuvant Therapy

Axillary management after NAC is complex because standard SLNB in a previously node-positive (cN+) patient has a higher false-negative rate (FNR) than in cN0 patients. Three landmark prospective studies — FALSE trial (Netherlands), ACOSOG Z1071 (US), and SENTINA (Germany) — assessed the accuracy of post-NAC SLNB in initially node-positive patients who converted to ypN0. All three demonstrated FNR of 12–14% with dual-tracer SLNB alone, which exceeds the acceptable threshold of <10%. This FNR can be reduced to <5% by targeted axillary dissection (TAD).

Targeted Axillary Dissection (TAD) is the recommended strategy for ypN0 assessment after NAC in initially node-positive patients. TAD consists of: (1) clip placement in the biopsy-proven positive node at diagnosis; (2) post-NAC seed/wire localisation of the clipped node; (3) surgical excision of the sentinel nodes PLUS the clipped (marked) node, regardless of whether it is a sentinel node. Retrieval of the marked node is confirmed by specimen radiography. Studies show TAD reduces FNR to 2–4%, making axillary dissection avoidable in patients achieving ypN0 with TAD.

> **Board Pearl — ypN0 After NAC: Is ALND Still Needed?** — **ypN0 with TAD (clipped node retrieved + ≥3 SLN removed):** ALND can be safely omitted. Multiple studies demonstrate that patients achieving ypN0 with adequate TAD have similar locoregional recurrence rates with axillary irradiation (regional nodal irradiation) substituting for ALND. **ypN1 (residual nodal disease):** ALND is still required in most cases, as residual axillary disease has significant implications for locoregional control and systemic therapy intensification.


## Cutaneous Melanoma — Pathology, Classification & AJCC 8th Staging

Cutaneous melanoma arises from malignant transformation of melanocytes. Incidence continues to rise — it accounts for only 4% of skin cancers but 80% of skin cancer deaths. Key clinicopathological subtypes include: **superficial spreading melanoma** (SSM, most common, 70%, radial growth phase before vertical invasion); **nodular melanoma** (NM, 15–30%, de novo vertical growth, poor prognosis); **lentigo maligna melanoma** (LMM, 5–10%, chronically sun-exposed skin, often facial, elderly patients, broad radial growth); **acral lentiginous melanoma** (ALM, 5%, palms/soles/subungual, overrepresented in non-white populations, no UV causation); and **desmoplastic melanoma** (perineural invasion, high local recurrence, low sentinel node positivity rate).

The AJCC 8th Edition (2017) staging system for cutaneous melanoma is based on: **T category** (Breslow thickness + ulceration, ± mitotic rate for T1); **N category** (nodal burden: micrometastases detected by SLNB vs macrometastases detected clinically; in-transit/satellite/microsatellite disease); and **M category** (site of distant metastasis and LDH elevation). The **Breslow thickness** — measured from the granular layer of the epidermis to the deepest tumour cell — remains the single most important independent prognostic factor. The **Clark level** (anatomical depth) has been eliminated from the 8th edition staging for T2-T4 melanoma.

*AJCC 8th Edition — T Category Melanoma Staging*
T Category | Breslow Thickness | Ulceration | Notes
--- | --- | --- | ---
T1a | < 0.8 mm | No | Lowest risk group; SLNB generally not indicated
T1b | < 0.8 mm with ulceration OR 0.8–1.0 mm ± ulceration | Yes / Any | SLNB discussion recommended
T2a | 1.01–2.0 mm | No | SLNB recommended
T2b | 1.01–2.0 mm | Yes | SLNB recommended; worse prognosis than T2a
T3a | 2.01–4.0 mm | No | Intermediate-high risk
T3b | 2.01–4.0 mm | Yes | High risk; adjuvant systemic therapy indicated if Stage III
T4a | > 4.0 mm | No | High risk
T4b | > 4.0 mm | Yes | Highest T-category risk; adjuvant therapy mandatory if operable Stage III

*AJCC 8th Edition — N and M Categories*
Category | Definition | Key Point
--- | --- | ---
N1a | 1 clinically occult node (SLNB+) | No in-transit/satellite
N1b | 1 clinically detected node | Macroscopic nodal disease
N1c | No regional nodes, but in-transit/satellite/microsatellite lesion(s) | No nodal involvement
N2a | 2–3 clinically occult nodes | SLNB+ only
N2b | 2–3 clinically detected nodes (any occult) | Macrometastases
N2c | 1 node (any) + in-transit/satellite/microsatellite | Combined nodal + in-transit

> **Board Pearl — Ulceration and Microsatellites** — **Ulceration** upstages T category (T1a→T1b, T2a→T2b, etc.) and is defined as absence of an intact epidermis overlying the melanoma on histology — not macroscopic ulceration. It represents an adverse biological feature independent of Breslow thickness. **Microsatellite lesions** (discontinuous tumour nests >0.05mm separated from the main tumour mass by normal dermis, detected on primary excision specimen) are now classified as N1c/N2c/N3 in AJCC 8th and indicate SLNB followed by systemic therapy consideration, even without nodal disease.

> **Key Prognostic Factors — AJCC 8th Melanoma** — In order of prognostic importance: **(1) Breslow thickness** — most important factor for localised disease; **(2) Ulceration** — independent poor prognostic factor at every T stage; **(3) Sentinel lymph node status** — most important for Stage II vs III distinction; **(4) Number of positive nodes** — N1 vs N2 vs N3; **(5) Mitotic rate** — retained for T1 substaging (T1b includes any mitotic rate in some classifications); **(6) LDH** — elevated LDH in Stage IV is M1c/1d modifier; **(7) BRAF V600 mutation status** — predicts systemic therapy selection, not stage.


## Wide Excision Margins in Melanoma — Evidence & Technique

Wide local excision (WLE) of cutaneous melanoma is performed after initial diagnostic excision biopsy (which should ideally be a narrow-margin complete excisional biopsy of the primary lesion, not punch or shave biopsy, to allow accurate Breslow measurement). The definitive wide excision is performed with specific margins determined by Breslow thickness — these margins represent the result of four landmark randomised controlled trials (WHO Melanoma Trial 1, MSLT-I margin component, UK Melanoma Study Group, Swedish Melanoma Study Group, and Intergroup Melanoma Surgical Trial) that collectively established the current evidence-based margin recommendations.

*NCCN 2025 — Recommended Wide Excision Margins by Breslow Thickness*
Breslow Thickness | Recommended Margin | Evidence Base | Notes
--- | --- | --- | ---
In situ (lentigo maligna) | 0.5–1.0 cm | Expert consensus; staged excision (Mohs) for facial/LMM | Larger margins or staged surgery for lentigo maligna on face
≤ 1.0 mm (T1) | 1 cm | WHO Trial 1; Balch JAMA 2001; UK MGS | 1 cm equivalent to wider margins for thin melanoma
1.01–2.0 mm (T2) | 1–2 cm | UK MGS (Thomas, NEJM 2004) | 1 cm statistically acceptable; 2 cm preferred when anatomically feasible
2.01–4.0 mm (T3) | 2 cm | Intergroup Melanoma Surgical Trial; Gillgren LANCET 2011 | 3 cm NOT superior to 2 cm in randomised trials
> 4.0 mm (T4) | 2 cm | Expert consensus based on trial extrapolation | 3 cm not proven superior; anatomical constraints often limit to 2 cm

The **deep margin** of wide excision should include the deep fascia (fascia of the underlying muscle) but need not remove muscle unless the tumour directly overlies or invades fascial structures. Fascial excision does not improve survival but reduces local recurrence risk at the primary site. For **subungual melanoma**, amputation at the distal interphalangeal joint (DIP) provides adequate margins for digit preservation while achieving local control. For **facial melanoma** or anatomically constrained sites, the desired 1–2 cm margin may not be achievable — staged excision, Mohs micrographic surgery with melanoma-specific staining, or reduced margins with RT may be necessary, but this should be discussed in a multidisciplinary setting.

> **Board Pearl — The 3 cm Margin Is Not Superior** — The **MelMarT (Gillgren, Lancet 2011)** and UK Melanoma Study Group trials definitively showed that **3 cm excision margins are NOT superior to 2 cm** for T3-T4 melanoma in terms of locoregional recurrence, distant recurrence, or overall survival. The 2 cm margin is now the standard maximum recommended margin for melanomas >2 mm Breslow thickness. Wider margins only increase surgical morbidity (need for flaps/grafts) without oncological benefit.

> **Common Pitfall — Initial Biopsy Technique** — Punch biopsy or shave biopsy should NOT be used for suspected melanoma — these techniques may transect the lesion, preventing accurate Breslow thickness measurement and understaging the tumour. The preferred initial biopsy is **narrow-margin excisional biopsy** (1–2 mm margins) of the entire lesion. For large lesions or functionally difficult locations (face, acral), a **full-thickness incisional punch** of the most representative/thickest area is acceptable but suboptimal. Do NOT perform a wide local excision as the initial biopsy — this prevents subsequent SLNB mapping.


## Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy in Melanoma — MSLT-I & MSLT-II

Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) in melanoma provides accurate pathological staging, identifies patients with occult nodal disease who may benefit from regional therapy and systemic adjuvant treatment, and stratifies prognosis. The technique uses intradermal injection of technetium-99m-labelled colloid (and/or blue dye/ICG fluorescence) around the primary melanoma site (not scar) to identify the sentinel node(s) via lymphoscintigraphy and intraoperative gamma probe. Melanoma SLNB should be performed at the time of wide excision — not as a staged procedure if avoidable.

> **MSLT-I (Morton, NEJM 2006 / Lancet Oncol 2014)** — **Population:** 1347 patients with intermediate-thickness (1.2–3.5 mm) or thick (>3.5 mm) melanoma randomised to wide excision + SLNB (with immediate completion CLND if positive) vs wide excision alone (CLND only at clinical nodal relapse). **Results:** 10-year melanoma-specific survival: no significant difference in the overall groups, but among patients with nodal disease, those identified by SLNB had **significantly better 10-year survival** than those identified at clinical relapse (62.1% vs 41.5%). **Practice change:** SLNB provides accurate staging and **early CLND in SLNB+ patients** improves melanoma-specific survival vs delayed CLND at relapse, for intermediate-thickness tumours.

> **MSLT-II (Faries, NEJM 2017)** — **Population:** 1934 patients with positive SLNB randomised to immediate completion lymph node dissection (CLND) vs ultrasound surveillance of the nodal basin. **Results:** Melanoma-specific survival at 3 years: 86% CLND vs 86% surveillance (non-significant difference). **CLND improved:** disease-free survival (68% vs 63%) and regional nodal control. **CLND increased:** lymphedema (24% vs 6%). **Practice change:** CLND after positive SLNB does NOT improve melanoma-specific survival. Ultrasound surveillance of the regional basin is an acceptable and now preferred alternative to CLND in patients with micrometastatic sentinel node disease.

*SLNB Indications in Melanoma — NCCN 2025*
T Category / Breslow | SLNB Recommendation | Notes
--- | --- | ---
T1a (< 0.8 mm, no ulceration) | Not recommended | SLN positivity rate <5%; not cost-effective
T1b (< 0.8 mm + ulceration OR 0.8–1.0 mm) | Discuss / Offer | SLN positivity rate 5–12%; shared decision-making
T2 (1.01–2.0 mm) | Recommended | SLN positivity rate ~15–20%
T3 (2.01–4.0 mm) | Recommended | SLN positivity rate ~30%
T4 (> 4.0 mm) | Recommended | SLN positivity rate 35–40%; adjuvant therapy if positive
Desmoplastic melanoma (pure) | Consider omission | SLN positivity rate <5% even with thick tumours; exception to standard algorithm

> **Board Pearl — What to Do After a Positive SLNB (MSLT-II Era)** — After a positive SLNB in 2025, the recommendation is: **do NOT perform routine completion CLND** — MSLT-II demonstrated no survival benefit and significant lymphedema risk (24%). Instead: (1) **Ultrasound surveillance** of the regional nodal basin every 3–4 months for 2 years; (2) **Adjuvant systemic therapy**: nivolumab, pembrolizumab, or dabrafenib+trametinib (if BRAF+) for Stage III — reduces distant recurrence. CLND is still indicated for: macroscopic extracapsular extension; clinically palpable nodes (Stage IIIB/C); or patient/surgeon preference after counselling. The shift is from 'CLND is standard' to 'surveillance is standard' for occult micrometastatic sentinel node disease.


## Systemic Therapy for Advanced Melanoma — Immunotherapy & Targeted Therapy

The treatment of advanced (Stage III/IV) melanoma has been transformed since 2011 by two therapeutic revolutions: **checkpoint immunotherapy** (anti-CTLA-4 and anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies) and **BRAF-targeted therapy** (BRAF and MEK inhibitors for BRAF V600-mutated melanoma). Approximately **50% of cutaneous melanomas** harbour a BRAF V600 mutation (predominantly V600E, ~80%; V600K, ~15%). BRAF mutation testing is mandatory in all Stage III/IV melanoma before systemic therapy selection. Mucosal and uveal melanomas have significantly lower BRAF mutation rates (<5%) and different molecular landscapes.

*Systemic Therapy for Advanced Melanoma — Key Regimens and Trials*

--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Nivolumab + Ipilimumab | PD-1 + CTLA-4 | CheckMate 067 (Wolchok, NEJM 2022) | 52% vs 44% (nivo) vs 26% (ipi) | First-line standard regardless of BRAF status; 59% grade 3/4 toxicity

Dabrafenib + Trametinib | BRAF V600 + MEK | COMBI-D/V (Long, NEJM 2015) | 34% at 5yr | BRAF+ only; rapid response; resistance develops in ~50% by 1 yr

> **CheckMate 067 — 7-Year Follow-Up (Wolchok, NEJM 2022)** — **Population:** 945 patients with advanced (unresectable Stage III/IV) melanoma randomised to nivolumab + ipilimumab vs nivolumab alone vs ipilimumab alone. **7-year results:** OS: 49% (nivo+ipi) vs 44% (nivo) vs 21% (ipi). Median OS: 72.1 months (nivo+ipi) vs 36.9 months (nivo) vs 19.9 months (ipi). **PD-L1 status:** In PD-L1 ≥1% tumours, nivo+ipi and nivo monotherapy had similar OS. In PD-L1 <1%, nivo+ipi was superior to nivo alone. **Practice change:** Nivolumab+ipilimumab is the preferred first-line standard for most advanced melanoma patients; nivolumab monotherapy acceptable for PD-L1 high or patients unsuitable for combination toxicity.

> **Board Pearl — BRAF+ Advanced Melanoma: Immunotherapy or Targeted Therapy First?** — In BRAF V600-mutated advanced melanoma, both immunotherapy (nivo+ipi) and targeted therapy (dabrafenib+trametinib) achieve ~50% 5-year OS. **Current practice (2025):** No prospective head-to-head trial definitively establishes superiority of one approach as first-line. **Arguments for immunotherapy first:** durable responses (plateau in survival curves); responses to targeted therapy after PD-1 failure are maintained. **Arguments for targeted therapy first:** rapid cytoreduction in high-volume/symptomatic disease; bridging to immunotherapy. Most melanoma centres (ESMO 2023) prefer **first-line immunotherapy** (nivo+ipi) for most BRAF+ patients without urgent need for rapid response, reserving targeted therapy for second line or for patients with high-volume disease requiring rapid control. Sequencing remains an area of active investigation (DREAMseq trial, SECOMBIT trial).

> **In-Transit Melanoma — Regional Therapy Options** — **In-transit metastases** (dermal/subdermal lesions between primary site and regional lymph nodes, more than 2 cm from primary) indicate locoregional disease progression. Management options: **(1) Surgery:** resection if limited/resectable disease. **(2) Intralesional T-VEC** (talimogene laherparepvec — oncolytic HSV-1 virus): approved for unresectable cutaneous/subcutaneous/nodal melanoma; durable response rate 16%; excellent tolerability. **(3) Isolated limb perfusion (ILP)** or **isolated limb infusion (ILI):** hyperthermic melphalan ± TNF-α delivered to the isolated limb via extracorporeal circuit; overall response rate 60–80%; complete response 40–50%; standard for extensive/unresectable in-transit disease of extremities. **(4) Systemic immunotherapy** (pembrolizumab/nivolumab ± ipilimumab): preferred when systemic disease concurrent or systemic therapy appropriate.
