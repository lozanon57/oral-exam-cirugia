# Gastric Cancer — Surgical Oncology — Gastrectomy, D2 Dissection & Perioperative Chemotherapy

*Bloque A · Surgical Oncology · PGY2-3*

*Guías: NCCN Gastric Cancer v2.2025 · ESMO 2024 · JGCA 5th ed.*


## Surgical Anatomy and Lymph Node Stations

The stomach is supplied by five named arteries arising from the coeliac axis: left gastric (dominant supply to lesser curvature), right gastric (lesser curvature from hepatic artery), left gastroepiploic (greater curvature from splenic artery), right gastroepiploic (greater curvature from gastroduodenal artery), and short gastric arteries (fundus from splenic artery). The corresponding lymph node stations are numbered 1–16 per the JGCA 5th edition, forming the anatomical basis for the D-level dissection classification. Understanding this arterial supply is essential for both safe dissection and oncologically adequate lymphadenectomy.

*JGCA Lymph Node Stations — Anatomical Groups and Dissection Levels*
Station | Location | Vascular Reference | Included in D-Level
--- | --- | --- | ---
1 | Right cardial nodes | Left gastric artery at cardia | D1, D1+, D2
2 | Left cardial nodes | Short gastric/left gastroepiploic at cardia | D1+ (total gastrectomy), D2
3 | Lesser curvature nodes | Left and right gastric arteries | D1, D1+, D2
4sa/4sb/4d | Greater curvature nodes | Short gastric, left and right gastroepiploic | D1 (4d), D1+ (4sa/sb), D2
5 | Suprapyloric nodes | Right gastric artery | D1, D1+, D2
6 | Infrapyloric nodes | Right gastroepiploic/gastroduodenal | D1, D1+, D2
7 | Left gastric artery nodes | Along left gastric artery trunk | D2
8a | Common hepatic artery nodes (anterosuperior) | Common hepatic artery | D2
9 | Coeliac axis nodes | At origin of left gastric artery | D2
10 | Splenic hilar nodes | Splenic hilum | D2 (total gastrectomy)
11p/11d | Proximal/distal splenic artery nodes | Along splenic artery | D2
12a | Hepatoduodenal ligament nodes | Along hepatic artery in hepatoduodenal ligament | D2
16 | Para-aortic nodes | Around abdominal aorta | M1 disease — NOT D2

*(Figura: Figure 1. Gastric lymph node stations 1–12 (JGCA 5th edition). D1 dissection removes perigastric stations 1–6. D2 dissection additionally removes stations 7–12 along named vascular structures.)*

> **Board Pearl — D2 Standard** — D2 lymphadenectomy is the standard for curative-intent gastrectomy in all guideline frameworks (NCCN, ESMO, JGCA). The 15-year follow-up of the Dutch D1D2 trial confirmed a survival advantage for D2 over D1 (29% vs 21% cancer-specific survival, p=0.034). A minimum of **16 lymph nodes** must be examined for adequate pathological staging (JGCA); AJCC requires ≥16 for valid N-staging.

> **Station 16 Para-aortic Nodes — Critical Distinction** — Station 16 (para-aortic nodes) is NOT part of D2 dissection — positive para-aortic nodes represent **M1 disease** in the TNM 8th edition classification. The JCOG9501 trial showed that D3 dissection (including para-aortic nodes) conferred no survival benefit over D2 in gastric cancer and significantly increased morbidity. If station 16 nodes are found positive at staging laparoscopy or intraoperative frozen section, curative gastrectomy should be aborted.

> **FLOT4 Trial (Al-Batran, Lancet 2019) — Still Standard in 2025** — **Population:** 716 patients with resectable gastric or GEJ adenocarcinoma, perioperative FLOT (docetaxel/oxaliplatin/leucovorin/5-FU) vs ECF/ECX. **Result:** Median OS 50 vs 35 months (HR 0.77, p=0.012); pCR 16% vs 6%. **Practice change:** FLOT remains the Category 1 Western standard perioperative regimen per NCCN 2025 and ESMO 2024. **2025 update — emerging option**: Nivolumab + FLOT (DANTE/MATTERHORN trials, 2024) shows improved pCR in HER2-negative, PD-L1 CPS ≥5 patients and is listed as Category 2A in NCCN 2025 for selected patients. Not yet universal standard but rapidly evolving.


## Operative Procedures

**Distal gastrectomy** (60–75% gastrectomy) is indicated for antral and body tumours when a 5cm proximal margin from the tumour can be achieved. Reconstruction options: Billroth II (gastrojejunostomy), Roux-en-Y gastrojejunostomy (preferred to prevent bile reflux gastritis). **Total gastrectomy** is required for fundic and diffuse-type tumours (signet ring/linitis plastica) and when adequate proximal margin cannot be achieved with distal gastrectomy. Reconstruction is by oesophagojejunostomy (Roux-en-Y). D2 dissection is performed with both procedures.

*Distal vs Total Gastrectomy — Indications and Key Operative Considerations*
Feature | Distal Gastrectomy | Total Gastrectomy
--- | --- | ---
Indication | Antrum/body tumours — proximal margin achievable | Fundic, diffuse-type, or when 5cm proximal margin not achievable
Reconstruction | Roux-en-Y gastrojejunostomy (preferred) or Billroth II | Oesophagojejunostomy (Roux-en-Y, 40–60cm limb)
Proximal margin requirement | ≥5cm diffuse-type; ≥3cm intestinal-type | ≥2–3cm above GOJ; frozen section mandatory
Lymphadenectomy | D2 (stations 1–9, 11p, 12a for distal tumours) | D2 (stations 1–12, including 10/11d for total)
Spleen preservation | Standard | Standard (resect only if station 10 direct infiltration)
Nutritional consequence | Reduced acid/pepsin; altered gastric emptying | Complete absence of gastric function; B12 deficiency universal
Anastomotic leak risk | 5–8% (gastrojejunostomy) | 3–10% (oesophagojejunostomy — higher consequences)

> **Common Pitfall — Proximal Margin** — For diffuse-type (signet ring) gastric cancer, a **5cm proximal margin** is required due to submucosal spread. For intestinal-type gastric cancer, 3cm is accepted. Intraoperative frozen section of the proximal margin is mandatory if doubt exists. Positive proximal margin is R1 regardless of the distance from the gross tumour.

> **Minimally Invasive Gastrectomy — Current Evidence** — Laparoscopic distal gastrectomy (LDG) is now standard for early gastric cancer at high-volume Asian centres, with equivalent oncological outcomes and reduced short-term morbidity compared to open surgery (KLASS-01 trial: 5-year OS 94.2% LDG vs 93.3% open). For advanced gastric cancer, laparoscopic total gastrectomy (KLASS-02) shows non-inferior short-term surgical outcomes. Robotic gastrectomy has increasing adoption but lacks definitive survival data over laparoscopic approaches.


## Post-Gastrectomy Syndromes

Post-gastrectomy syndromes are functional and nutritional sequelae of altered gastric anatomy. They are more frequent after total than after distal gastrectomy and more common after Billroth II than Roux-en-Y reconstruction. Dumping syndrome (early and late) is the most common functional complication, affecting up to 20% of patients after total gastrectomy. Nutritional deficiencies — particularly vitamin B12, iron, and calcium — require lifelong monitoring and supplementation. Understanding the timing and mechanism of each syndrome is essential for accurate diagnosis and targeted management.

*Post-Gastrectomy Syndromes — Recognition and Management*
Syndrome | Mechanism | Timing | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Early dumping | Rapid gastric emptying → osmotic shift + gut hormone release | 15–30 min post-meal | Small meals, low simple carbohydrate, octreotide for severe
Late dumping (reactive hypoglycaemia) | Rapid glucose absorption → insulin surge → hypoglycaemia | 1–3 hours post-meal | Low GI diet, acarbose, octreotide
Bile reflux gastritis | Duodenal bile into gastric remnant or oesophagus | Chronic | Roux-en-Y conversion; ursodeoxycholic acid
Vitamin B12 deficiency | Loss of intrinsic factor from gastric parietal cells | Months–years | IM B12 monthly (lifelong after total gastrectomy)
Iron deficiency anaemia | Reduced acid for iron absorption; reduced dietary intake | Months–years | Oral iron; IV iron if malabsorption
Calcium/Vitamin D deficiency | Reduced gastric acid impairs calcium absorption; reduced dietary intake | Months–years | Calcium + Vitamin D3 supplementation; DEXA at 2 years
Roux stasis syndrome | Disrupted migrating motor complex in Roux limb | Weeks–months | Prokinetics; dietary modification; rarely revision surgery
Afferent loop syndrome | Afferent limb obstruction after Billroth II | Acute or chronic | Surgical revision to Roux-en-Y or drainage procedure

> **Board Pearl** — After total gastrectomy, **vitamin B12 must be supplemented lifelong** by intramuscular injection (hydroxocobalamin 1mg IM every 3 months) — there is no intrinsic factor and oral supplementation is unreliable. Failure to supplement causes irreversible subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.

> **Annual Nutritional Surveillance After Gastrectomy** — Annual blood panel is mandatory for all post-gastrectomy patients: **FBC** (iron deficiency, megaloblastic anaemia), **serum B12 and folate**, **ferritin and transferrin saturation**, **calcium, phosphate, and 25-OH vitamin D**, **albumin** (nutritional marker). DEXA scan at 2 years to assess for post-gastrectomy osteoporosis (reduced calcium absorption). Register patients with a dietitian at discharge — structured nutritional counselling reduces post-gastrectomy malnutrition.


## Gastric Cancer Staging and Lymphadenectomy

Accurate staging of gastric cancer is the cornerstone of treatment planning. The AJCC/UICC TNM 8th edition (2017) provides the current staging framework. Clinical staging (cTNM) is based on CT thorax/abdomen/pelvis, EUS for T and N staging, and staging laparoscopy for cT3-T4 or node-positive disease. Pathological staging (pTNM) requires a minimum of 16 lymph nodes examined for adequate N-staging. The distinction between N1 (1–2 positive nodes), N2 (3–6), and N3 (≥7: N3a = 7–15, N3b = ≥16) is critical for adjuvant therapy decisions and prognosis.

*TNM 8th Edition Gastric Cancer Staging — Key Categories*
Category | Definition | Survival Implication
--- | --- | ---
T1a | Tumour confined to mucosa (lamina propria/muscularis mucosae) | ESD eligible if differentiated, no LVI
T1b | Tumour invades submucosa | ESD if SM1 ≤500μm, differentiated, no LVI; otherwise gastrectomy + D1+
T2 | Tumour invades muscularis propria | Gastrectomy + D2; perioperative chemotherapy if ≥T2 in Western practice
T3 | Tumour penetrates subserosa (without peritoneal or adjacent organ invasion) | Perioperative FLOT standard; staging laparoscopy mandatory
T4a | Tumour perforates serosa (visceral peritoneum) | High peritoneal recurrence risk; staging laparoscopy mandatory
T4b | Tumour invades adjacent structures (aorta, liver, pancreas, colon) | Extended resection if R0 achievable; neoadjuvant chemotherapy recommended
N1 | 1–2 positive regional lymph nodes | Stage II (with T2-3)
N2 | 3–6 positive regional lymph nodes | Stage IIIA
N3a | 7–15 positive regional lymph nodes | Stage IIIB/C depending on T
N3b | ≥16 positive regional lymph nodes | Stage IIIC — very high recurrence risk; all adjuvant therapy
M1 | Distant metastases (including station 16, peritoneal cytology +, peritoneal implants) | Incurable; systemic chemotherapy standard

> **Staging Laparoscopy — When It Is Mandatory** — Staging laparoscopy with peritoneal washings (cytology) is recommended by NCCN for all cT3-T4 or node-positive gastric cancers before laparotomy. It detects occult peritoneal disease in **20–30%** of clinically resectable cases — preventing futile major surgery. Positive peritoneal cytology alone (P0 CY1) upgrades disease to M1 in JGCA staging (though AJCC 8th does not classify CY1 as M1 if no macroscopic peritoneal implants — know your guideline framework).

**Ensayo de referencia — Dutch D1D2 Trial — 15-Year Follow-Up:**

> **Minimum Lymph Node Requirements for Adequate Staging** — **AJCC TNM 8th edition:** ≥16 lymph nodes examined required for valid N-staging. **JGCA 5th edition:** ≥16 nodes required; recommends ≥25 nodes at expert centres. If fewer than 16 nodes are examined, pN0 is classified as pNx (inadequate staging) — this affects both prognosis and adjuvant therapy decisions. Low lymph node yield is a quality marker for gastrectomy and should be audited.


## Perioperative Chemotherapy — FLOT and Beyond

Perioperative chemotherapy is the standard of care for resectable gastric and gastroesophageal junction (GEJ) adenocarcinoma in Western practice. The FLOT4 trial (Al-Batran, Lancet 2019) established FLOT (docetaxel 50mg/m², oxaliplatin 85mg/m², leucovorin 200mg/m², 5-FU 2600mg/m² continuous infusion) given every 2 weeks as the preferred perioperative regimen, replacing the previously used ECF/ECX based on the MAGIC trial (Cunningham, NEJM 2006). The regimen consists of 4 pre-operative cycles followed by 4 post-operative cycles, with surgery performed 4–6 weeks after the last pre-operative cycle.

*Perioperative Chemotherapy Regimens — Comparison of Key Trials*
Trial | Regimen | Population | Key Result | Current Role
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
MAGIC (2006) | ECF/ECX peri-operative × 3 cycles each side | Resectable gastric/GEJ adenocarcinoma (n=503) | 5-year OS 36% vs 23% surgery alone (HR 0.75) | Superseded by FLOT in Western practice
FLOT4 (2019) | FLOT × 4 cycles each side | Resectable gastric/GEJ (n=716) | Median OS 50 vs 35 months (HR 0.77); pCR 16% | Western standard — Category 1 (NCCN/ESMO)
ACTS-GC (2007) | S-1 adjuvant × 1 year after D2 | Stage II-III gastric cancer, East Asian (n=1059) | 5-year OS 72% vs 61% (HR 0.67) | East Asian adjuvant standard after D2
CLASSIC (2012) | CAPOX adjuvant × 6 months after D2 | Stage II-III gastric cancer, East Asian (n=1035) | 5-year DFS 68% vs 53% (HR 0.58) | East Asian adjuvant alternative to S-1
INT-0116 (2001) | 5-FU/leucovorin + chemoradiotherapy adjuvant | Resected gastric cancer (n=556) | Median OS 36 vs 27 months (HR 0.74) | Adjuvant CRT: US historical standard; now used for R1 and high-risk cases
FLOT7-AIO | FLOT ± trastuzumab/pertuzumab peri-op (HER2+) | HER2+ resectable gastric/GEJ (n=~200) | pCR 34% vs 12% (p<0.001) — survival pending | Under investigation; not yet approved standard

> **FLOT Regimen — Practical Considerations** — Key practical points for FLOT management: **(1) Toxicity profile:** Neutropenia (G3/4 in 51%), peripheral neuropathy (oxaliplatin), fatigue, nausea. G-CSF prophylaxis recommended. **(2) Response assessment:** CT restaging after 4 pre-operative cycles — if progressive disease, reassess operability. pCR or major pathological response is a favourable prognostic marker. **(3) Post-operative completion:** 4 post-operative cycles should be completed as planned; delays due to surgical complications require MDT review. **(4) Nutritional monitoring:** Weight and nutritional status must be tracked throughout — malnutrition delays surgery and reduces chemotherapy tolerance.

**Ensayo de referencia — FLOT4 — Perioperative FLOT vs ECF/ECX:**

> **Mandatory Biomarker Panel — NCCN 2025 / ESMO 2024** — A comprehensive biomarker panel must be performed on ALL newly diagnosed gastric/GEJ adenocarcinomas: **(1) HER2** (IHC ± ISH): HER2+ (IHC 3+ or 2+/ISH+) — first-line trastuzumab + pembrolizumab + chemotherapy (KEYNOTE-811; Category 1, NCCN 2025/ESMO 2024). **(2) PD-L1 CPS** (combined positive score): CPS ≥5 → nivolumab + FOLFOX first-line (CheckMate 649; Category 1). **(3) MSI/MMR**: dMMR patients benefit from immunotherapy; consider omitting perioperative chemotherapy. **(4) CLDN18.2** (claudin 18 isoform 2): CLDN18.2+ (IHC ≥2+ in ≥75% tumour cells), RAS-WT, HER2-negative → zolbetuximab + FOLFOX/XELOX first-line (SPOTLIGHT/GLOW trials; FDA approved May 2024). **(5) FGFR2b**: emerging biomarker for bemarituzumab eligibility. HER2 expression may be lost at progression — **retesting at recurrence is mandatory**.


## Post-Gastrectomy Complications and Surgical Emergencies

Post-gastrectomy surgical complications are classified by the Clavien-Dindo system and, for anastomotic leaks, by the International Study Group of Gastric Cancer (ISGC) leak grading (Grades A–C based on clinical impact and intervention required). The most feared early complications are anastomotic leak (especially oesophagojejunostomy after total gastrectomy) and duodenal stump leak after distal gastrectomy. Pancreatic fistula and chylous ascites are important delayed complications following D2 lymphadenectomy involving the pancreatic region and thoracic duct territory.

*Post-Gastrectomy Surgical Complications — Diagnosis and Management*
Complication | Timing | Diagnosis | Key Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Oesophagojejunostomy leak | Day 4–7 | Drain fluid character; CT; upper GI water-soluble study | Grade A: conservative. Grade B: endoscopic stent/vacuum. Grade C: re-operation + diversion
Duodenal stump leak | Day 3–7 (Billroth II / subtotal gastrectomy) | High amylase drain fluid; CT showing right upper quadrant collection | Drain, antibiotics, octreotide; surgical revision if uncontrolled
Pancreatic fistula/leak | Day 3–10 | Drain fluid amylase >3× serum ULN on day 3+ | Grade A: expectant. Grade B: octreotide, drain, NPO. Grade C: surgical drainage or pancreatectomy
Chylous ascites | Day 3–14 | Milky drain fluid; triglycerides >200 mg/dL | MCT diet or NPO + TPN; octreotide; VATS duct ligation if refractory
Post-gastrectomy haemorrhage | Immediate (intraluminal) or delayed (pseudoaneurysm) | FBC, endoscopy, CT angiography | Endoscopic haemostasis; IR embolisation; re-operation if haemodynamic instability
Anastomotic stricture | 3–8 weeks | Dysphagia; OGD showing stenotic anastomosis | Endoscopic balloon dilation (1–3 sessions); stent for refractory cases; surgical revision rarely
Delayed gastric emptying/Roux stasis | Weeks–months | Early satiety, nausea; OGD showing food residue; gastric emptying study | Prokinetics (metoclopramide, domperidone); dietary modification; rarely revision

> **Oesophagojejunostomy Leak — Red Flags Requiring Urgent Action** — Suspect oesophagojejunostomy leak when: fever >38.5°C beyond day 3; tachycardia without other cause; drain output becomes bilious or haematic; pain out of proportion; CRP fails to trend down after day 3 (expected peak day 2–3, CRP >170 mg/L on day 4 is a validated leak predictor). **Pneumomediastinum on CT = Grade C leak = surgical or interventional emergency.** Covered oesophageal stent is first-line for Grade B leaks — success rate 76–85%; surgical revision reserved for haemodynamically unstable patients.

> **Duodenal Stump — The Lateral Blow-Out** — After Billroth II subtotal gastrectomy, the duodenal stump is the site of highest risk for catastrophic leak ('lateral blow-out'). Risk factors: poor blood supply, tension, staple line ischaemia, distal obstruction. If drain amylase >3× ULN on day 3, leak is confirmed. Management: controlled drainage with wide-bore drain, NPO, octreotide, antibiotics. Surgical revision involves oversewing the stump or Roux-en-Y drainage. Uncontrolled duodenal stump leak carries a mortality of 15–30%.


## Gastroesophageal Junction Tumours — Siewert Classification

Adenocarcinoma of the gastroesophageal junction (GOJ) has increased in incidence in Western populations and represents a distinct oncological entity at the intersection of oesophageal and gastric cancer surgery. The Siewert classification (1998) categorises GOJ tumours anatomically relative to the Z-line and determines the appropriate surgical approach. This classification has been validated across international series and guides the choice of resection (oesophagectomy vs gastrectomy), extent of lymphadenectomy, and perioperative treatment strategy.

*Siewert Classification of GEJ Adenocarcinoma — Surgical Approach*
Type | Tumour Location | Typical Origin | Preferred Resection | Perioperative Treatment
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Siewert I | 1–5cm above GOJ (distal oesophagus) | Barrett's oesophagus/columnar metaplasia | Ivor Lewis (subtotal) oesophagectomy — 2-field lymphadenectomy (mediastinal + upper abdominal) | FLOT (NCCN) or CROSS (carboplatin/paclitaxel + RT) — CROSS preferred if oesophageal >3cm
Siewert II | 1cm above to 2cm below GOJ (true cardia) | Cardiac mucosa or short-segment Barrett's | Transhiatal total gastrectomy + distal oesophagectomy OR Ivor Lewis (if significant oesophageal involvement) | FLOT perioperative (NCCN Category 1)
Siewert III | 2–5cm below GOJ (subcardial) | Gastric cardia mucosa | Extended total gastrectomy (proximal to ensure R0) — upper abdominal lymphadenectomy | Perioperative FLOT (as for gastric cancer)

> **Siewert Classification — How to Determine the Type** — Measure the tumour centre relative to the anatomical GOJ (Z-line). This requires careful review of CT, upper GI endoscopy, and EUS reports together: **Siewert I** = tumour centre 1–5cm above GOJ; **Siewert II** = tumour centre within 1cm above to 2cm below GOJ; **Siewert III** = tumour centre 2–5cm below GOJ. When CT and endoscopy reports conflict, the endoscopic measurement (from incisors to GOJ and tumour lower/upper extent) is most accurate. In practice, Siewert I and II overlap — MDT discussion with the operating surgeon, radiologist, and gastroenterologist is essential.

The **CROSS trial** (van Hagen, NEJM 2012) demonstrated that neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy (carboplatin/paclitaxel + 41.4Gy radiation) followed by surgery improved median OS from 24 to 49 months compared to surgery alone in oesophageal and GEJ cancer (predominantly Siewert I). The **pCR rate was 29% overall** (49% for squamous cell carcinoma, 23% for adenocarcinoma). For Siewert II tumours with significant oesophageal involvement, CROSS may be preferred over FLOT by some centres, especially in North America. For Siewert III tumours, FLOT is standard. This represents an area of active clinical equipoise (ESOPEC trial: FLOT vs CROSS for resectable oesophageal/GEJ — results showed FLOT superior for adenocarcinoma, Hoeppner Lancet 2024).

**Ensayo de referencia — ESOPEC Trial — FLOT vs CROSS for Oesophageal/GEJ Cancer:**

> **Frozen Section at the Proximal Oesophageal Margin — Non-Negotiable** — For all Siewert II resections and any GOJ tumour with oesophageal involvement, **intraoperative frozen section of the proximal oesophageal margin is mandatory**. The submucosal spread of diffuse-type GOJ tumours makes gross margin assessment unreliable. If the first frozen section is positive (R1), the resection must be extended — additional oesophageal resection is required before closing. Discovering R1 status on permanent pathology after recovery forecloses the option of immediate re-resection.
