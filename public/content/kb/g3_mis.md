## Principles of Laparoscopy

Laparoscopic surgery relies on the creation of a pneumoperitoneum — insufflation of the abdominal cavity with carbon dioxide (CO2) gas to create a working space. CO2 is used because it is: non-combustible (safe with energy devices), highly soluble in blood (rapidly absorbed and excreted via respiration), readily available, and inexpensive. However, CO2 insufflation has important physiological effects.

Physiological effects of pneumoperitoneum: (1) CARDIOVASCULAR: initial peritoneal insufflation causes a vagal bradycardic response (direct peritoneal stretch → vagal stimulation). Sustained pressure (12–15 mmHg) → increased intra-abdominal pressure → increased systemic vascular resistance (SVR — compression of abdominal aorta/vessels), increased venous compression (IVC compression reduces preload, but venous capacitance reduction may transiently increase venous return). Net effect: increased afterload + increased MAP; this is poorly tolerated in patients with severe cardiac disease (LV dysfunction, severe AS). Trendelenburg positioning (used for pelvic surgery) further increases preload and venous return — potential for pulmonary oedema in cardiac patients. (2) RESPIRATORY: increased abdominal pressure elevates the diaphragm, reducing functional residual capacity (FRC) and increasing peak airway pressure. Requires increased ventilator pressures. CO2 absorbed across peritoneum → hypercarbia → increased minute ventilation required. Patients with severe COPD may not tolerate this. (3) RENAL: increased IAP (≥15 mmHg) reduces renal cortical blood flow and GFR → may precipitate AKI in susceptible patients. (4) CO2 EMBOLISM: rare but catastrophic — CO2 enters an open venous channel → gas embolism → mill-wheel murmur, haemodynamic collapse, right heart failure.

Contraindications to pneumoperitoneum: RELATIVE (not absolute — experienced laparoscopic surgeon may proceed with modifications): uncorrected coagulopathy (bleeding risk), severe cardiac disease (NYHA IV, severe LV dysfunction — consider open), severe COPD (inability to tolerate hypercarbia), previous multiple abdominal operations with dense adhesions (risk of trocar entry injury). ABSOLUTE contraindications are very few: patient refusal of operation, inability to achieve haemostasis.

Trocar hernia: port site hernias occur through fascial defects ≥10 mm in diameter. Risk: trocar type (radially expanding vs cutting — lower with radially expanding), location (periumbilical higher risk), obesity, age, wound infection. Prevention: fascial closure for ALL port sites ≥10 mm (J-needle, Carter-Thomason, or under direct vision). Port sites <10 mm: fascial closure NOT required. Richter's hernia (partial enterotomy at port site) is a specific risk — presents as subacute obstruction with no full-thickness bowel herniation on imaging.

> **Veress Needle Entry — Safety Tests** — Veress needle (closed technique): after skin incision (usually umbilical), the Veress needle is advanced through the fascia and peritoneum. Safety tests before insufflation: (1) Aspiration test — aspirate before insufflating; if blood/bowel contents → reposition; clear = safe. (2) Saline drop test — place saline drop in needle hub; release abdomen; drop should fall inward if peritoneal position (negative intra-abdominal pressure). (3) Initial insufflation pressure — should be <8 mmHg (if >10 mmHg immediately → likely not in peritoneal cavity; re-position). (4) Insufflation volume — steady rise in pressure with gas volume = correct position. Hasson (open) technique: minilaparotomy to fascia, direct peritoneal incision under vision, blunt trocar placed — eliminates entry injury risk; preferred in patients with previous abdominal surgery or obesity.


## Energy Devices

Understanding energy devices is essential for safe MIS — each modality has distinct mechanisms, tissue effects, and safety profiles.

Monopolar electrosurgery: current passes from the active electrode (instrument tip), through the patient's body, to the return/dispersive electrode (diathermy pad). Cutting mode: high frequency, low voltage continuous current → rapid cellular water vaporisation → 'cutting' effect. Coagulation mode: high voltage, interrupted current → slower heating → protein denaturation → coagulation. Combined (blend). Safety concerns: (1) Capacitative coupling — current can be induced in adjacent conductive objects (e.g., a metal trocar touching a non-grounded instrument) even without direct contact, causing burns to bowel or other structures. Minimised by: all-metal trocar systems (provide conduction path to abdominal wall) and avoiding hybrid trocar systems (metal sleeve + plastic anchor — concentrate current). (2) Direct coupling — accidental activation of instrument touching adjacent structure. (3) Insulation failure — current escapes through damaged instrument insulation. (4) Lateral thermal spread: monopolar coagulation = 5–10 mm spread.

Bipolar electrosurgery: current passes between the two jaws of the instrument (not through the patient body) — more controlled, less arcing risk. Advanced bipolar systems: LigaSure (Medtronic) — uses electrical energy + pressure; seals vessels up to 7 mm diameter with lateral thermal spread 1–2 mm; the fusion technology creates a seal equivalent to three suture ligatures. EnSeal (J&J) — similar vessel sealing with nanoparticle-mediated tissue fusion; lateral thermal spread 1 mm. These devices do NOT cut automatically — a separate blade or scissors is used or they have an integrated blade.

Ultrasonic energy (Harmonic): ultrasonic vibration (55,500 Hz) of the blade → mechanical energy → frictional heat (80–100°C) → protein denaturation and coagulation. Key characteristics: no electrical current passes through the patient; lower temperatures (vs electrosurgery); reduced char and smoke; lateral thermal spread 3 mm. Can cut AND coagulate simultaneously. Slower sealing time than bipolar for large vessels. Safe for use near neural tissue (no electrical current). THUNDERBEAT (Olympus): combined advanced bipolar + ultrasonic in one instrument — combines speed of bipolar sealing with ultrasonic cutting.

Safety comparison: lateral thermal spread — monopolar coagulation 5–10 mm; LigaSure/EnSeal 1–2 mm; Harmonic 3 mm. Clinical pearl: near the bile duct, common bile duct, or bowel, use instruments with LOWEST lateral spread — LigaSure/EnSeal (1–2 mm) or Harmonic (3 mm) preferred over monopolar.

> **Capacitative Coupling — Monopolar Risk in MIS** — Capacitative coupling occurs when a monopolar instrument generates an electric field that induces current flow in nearby conductors (metal trocars, other instruments) WITHOUT direct contact. Risk scenario: a 5 mm plastic trocar (non-conducting anchor) with a metal sleeve — current concentrates at the junction (no path to ground) and may discharge to adjacent bowel. Prevention: (1) Use all-metal trocar systems (current disperses to abdominal wall). (2) Avoid using monopolar through a hybrid (plastic-anchored metal) trocar in isolation. (3) Use active electrode monitoring (AEM) systems. (4) Keep monopolar instrument in sight at all times when activated. (5) Use lowest effective power setting.


## Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy & Common Bile Duct

Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (LC) is the gold standard for symptomatic gallstone disease and is the most commonly performed laparoscopic operation globally. The Critical View of Safety (CVS — Strasberg, 1995) is the internationally accepted standard for safe cholecystectomy and the primary prevention strategy for bile duct injury (BDI). BDI occurs in ~0.3–0.5% of LCs and carries significant morbidity, mortality, and medicolegal consequence.

Critical View of Safety — THREE criteria (ALL THREE must be met): (1) The hepatocystic triangle must be completely dissected and cleared of fat and fibrous tissue. (2) The lowest part of the gallbladder must be separated from the cystic plate (liver). (3) Two and only two structures should be seen entering the gallbladder — the cystic duct and cystic artery. If CVS cannot be achieved → do NOT clip and divide structures → perform intraoperative cholangiography (IOC) for clarification OR convert to open OR subtotal cholecystectomy (for difficult inflammations).

Dome-down (fundus-first) dissection: alternative approach when the hepatocystic triangle is densely inflammatory. Begin dissection at the fundus and work downward to the infundibulum — maintains orientation when the triangle is obscured. Requires clear identification of cystic structures before clipping.

Intraoperative cholangiography (IOC): cannulation of the cystic duct with a cholangiogram catheter → injection of contrast → fluoroscopic imaging. Indicates: (1) CBD stones (filling defects); (2) Anatomical variations (low cystic duct insertion, short cystic duct, right posterior sectoral duct draining into cystic duct — 'the classic ductal injury scenario'); (3) Confirmation of CVS when uncertain. IOC interpretation: two main hepatic ducts should fill; contrast should flow into duodenum; no filling defects; cystic duct remnant should be short (<1 cm).

Laparoscopic common bile duct exploration (LCBDE): performed when intraoperative cholangiography reveals CBD stones. Techniques: (1) Transcystic LCBDE — wire-guided basket retrieval via the cystic duct (suitable for distal CBD stones, cystic duct diameter ≥4 mm); (2) Choledochotomy — direct incision of CBD (for stones >1 cm, proximal stones, multiple stones); requires T-tube or primary closure with or without a stent. Completion cholangiogram confirms clearance. Success rates for LCBDE: 80–90% for transcystic; >90% for choledochotomy.

Conversion criteria: (1) Inability to achieve CVS; (2) Suspected CBD injury; (3) Haemorrhage not controllable laparoscopically; (4) Unsuspected malignancy requiring wider resection; (5) Dense adhesions preventing safe access. Conversion is NOT failure — it is sound surgical judgment. Document indication for conversion.

*Critical View of Safety — Three Criteria*
Criterion | Technical Requirement | Common Error | Consequence of Non-compliance
--- | --- | --- | ---
1. Hepatocystic triangle cleared | Complete dissection — no fat or fibrous tissue; triangle fully opened | Incomplete posterior dissection — only anterior triangle cleared | Failure to identify CBD running into 'cystic duct' position
2. Lowest GB separated from cystic plate | Gallbladder bottom separated from liver (cystic plate visible) | Stopping dissection at infundibulum without separating GB from liver | Inadequate exposure — cannot confirm two-structure rule
3. Two and only two structures enter GB | Only cystic duct and cystic artery seen going into gallbladder | Mistaking CBD or right hepatic duct for 'cystic duct' | Bile duct injury — catastrophic complication

> **Bile Duct Injury — Prevention is Everything** — Bile duct injury (BDI) remains the most feared complication of cholecystectomy. The 'classic' injury: the CBD is mistaken for the cystic duct because the cystic duct and CBD run in parallel. Prevention: (1) ALWAYS achieve CVS before clipping. (2) If anatomy is unclear: call for help; convert; perform IOC. (3) Never clip a structure you cannot confidently identify. (4) Dome-down dissection for difficult cholecystitis. (5) If BDI suspected intraoperatively: stop, perform IOC, seek hepatobiliary surgical expertise (do NOT attempt laparoscopic repair unless experienced). (6) Subtotal cholecystectomy is safer than risking BDI in acute cholecystitis.


## Laparoscopic Colectomy & Rectal Surgery

Laparoscopic colorectal surgery has become the standard of care for most colonic and rectal resections. Oncological equivalence with open surgery has been established by multiple RCTs (CLASSIC, COLOR I/II, COST trials). Benefits: reduced wound complications, shorter hospital stay, faster recovery, fewer adhesional complications.

Right hemicolectomy — approaches: (1) Medial-to-lateral (M2L): the ileocolic vessels are divided first (medial dissection), the retroperitoneal plane is developed from medial to lateral (Toldt's fascia dissection), and the bowel is mobilised after vascular ligation. Advantages: oncological (high vascular ligation — clear mesocolic plane), reduced blood loss, cleaner plane. (2) Lateral-to-medial (L2M): traditional — mobilise the right colon by dividing lateral peritoneal attachments first, then proceed medially to vessels. Less common in modern practice — greater bleeding risk during vessel ligation. Most laparoscopic surgeons use M2L as the primary approach.

Intracorporeal vs extracorporeal anastomosis: RIGHT trial (2020) is the key evidence.

Left hemicolectomy/sigmoid: requires splenic flexure mobilisation (omental dissection from left transverse colon — entering the lesser sac, or retrocolic approach), high vessel ligation (IMA at origin), and mobilisation of descending/sigmoid colon. Laparoscopic stapled colorectal anastomosis with circular stapler.

Total mesorectal excision (TME) — robotic vs laparoscopic: the ROLARR trial directly compared robotic and laparoscopic TME for rectal cancer.

taTME (Transanal Total Mesorectal Excision): a hybrid technique combining a transabdominal laparoscopic component with a transanal approach (purse-string closure of rectum, transanal port insertion, working bottom-up in the TME plane). Advantages: may improve access in difficult pelves (obesity, narrow pelvis, bulky tumour), potentially better distal margin. Complications specific to taTME: (1) Urethral injury — the most severe unique complication; reported in 0.5–2% of cases; results from incorrect dissection plane anteriorly entering Denonvilliers' fascia space anterior to urethra. (2) CO2 gas embolism — from transanal gas insufflation. (3) Two-team synchronisation errors. Prevention of urethral injury: strict adherence to anatomical landmarks, always start posterior and lateral, ensure rectal mucosal closure is adequate.

## Robotic Surgery

Robotic-assisted surgery uses a surgeon console, patient-side cart (robot arm), and a vision system. The da Vinci Surgical System (Intuitive Surgical) is the dominant platform globally. Platforms: da Vinci Xi (current standard — four arms, advanced port positioning flexibility, improved instrument reach and rotation); da Vinci X (lower cost, fewer features than Xi); da Vinci SP (Single Port — single-arm, multi-articulating instruments for single-port access — indications: radical prostatectomy in narrow space, transoral, transvaginal).

Advantages of robotic surgery: (1) Three-dimensional (3D) high-definition magnified vision (vs 2D in standard laparoscopy — though 3D laparoscopes also exist). (2) Wristed instruments — 7 degrees of freedom (vs 4–5 in laparoscopic instruments) — enables intracorporeal suturing, fine dissection in confined spaces. (3) Tremor filtration — motion scaling reduces fine tremor. (4) Ergonomics — surgeon seated at console; reduced fatigue during long cases. (5) Ambidextrous — no 'fulcrum effect' of laparoscopy (instrument moves opposite to hand inside abdomen).

Disadvantages: (1) No haptic (tactile) feedback — cannot 'feel' tissue. (2) Cost: capital expenditure £1–2 million per robot; consumable instruments £800–2000 per case; service contracts substantial. (3) Setup time: docking the robot adds 15–30 minutes. (4) Limited instrument range compared to laparoscopy. (5) Large robot footprint — restricts theatre access. (6) Need for additional laparoscopic ports for retraction/assistance.

Current evidence by procedure: Radical prostatectomy (RALP): robotic is the GOLD STANDARD — near-universally performed robotically in UK/US; evidence for equivalent cancer control, reduced transfusion, shorter stay, possibly better continence/potency (NICE 2019 endorsement). Partial nephrectomy: robotic equivalent or superior to laparoscopic for complex tumours (R.E.N.A.L. score >7) — better intracorporeal suturing for renorrhaphy. Hysterectomy: robotic equivalent to laparoscopic for benign; cancer outcomes similar. Colorectal (rectal): ROLARR — no proven benefit over laparoscopic for TME. Colonic: ongoing RCTs (ROBOCOL). Bariatric: equivalent to laparoscopic for Roux-en-Y bypass; robotic sleeve gastrectomy adds no benefit.

Learning curve: robotic surgery has a significant learning curve — generally considered 25–50 cases for basic proficiency (procedure-dependent). Training frameworks: simulation, proctorship (supervised cases), stepwise skill progression. Most societies recommend structured training pathways before independent robotic practice.

> **RALP — Robotic Prostatectomy as Standard** — Robot-assisted laparoscopic prostatectomy (RALP) is the gold standard surgical approach for localised prostate cancer requiring radical prostatectomy. Advantages over open retropubic prostatectomy: reduced blood loss (mean 150 mL vs 500–1000 mL), fewer transfusions, shorter hospital stay (1–2 days vs 3–5 days), comparable oncological control (positive surgical margin rate). Wristed instruments enable precise nerve-sparing dissection (neurovascular bundles) — important for potency preservation. NICE 2019: recommends robotic as standard for radical prostatectomy in centres with appropriate volume and expertise.


## Natural Orifice & Single-Port Surgery

Natural Orifice Transluminal Endoscopic Surgery (NOTES): access to the peritoneal cavity via a natural body orifice (mouth, vagina, rectum, urethra) using a flexible endoscope. Potential advantage: no abdominal incisions → no wound complications or hernias → improved cosmesis. Practical challenges: loss of triangulation (all instruments enter from same point), difficulty achieving haemostasis, closure of entry site, risk of contamination, limited surgeon experience. Current applications: transvaginal cholecystectomy (most established NOTES procedure — performed in selected European centres); transgastric appendicectomy (research setting). Not widely adopted due to technical complexity and unclear benefit over standard laparoscopy.

Single-Incision Laparoscopic Surgery (SILS): all instruments (camera + working instruments) enter through a single umbilical incision (typically 2.5–3 cm). Technique: multi-channel single port device (e.g., SILS Port, GelPOINT). Advantage: single umbilical incision (superior cosmesis). Disadvantages: (1) Loss of triangulation — instruments conflict ('sword fighting'); (2) Limited instrument movement; (3) Higher trocar site hernia rate — large umbilical incision with significant fascial defect (>2 cm); (4) Longer operative time. Applications: cholecystectomy, appendicectomy, sleeve gastrectomy. Evidence: SILC trial (single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy) — equivalent safety to standard 4-port LC but longer operative time and higher hernia rate at the access site (risk ~2% vs <0.5% with standard LC).

Robotic Single Port (SP): da Vinci SP system allows multiple robotic instruments through a single 25 mm port with independently articulating arms. Specific indications: robotic-assisted transoral surgery (base of tongue, larynx), transvaginal natural orifice approach, robotic inguinal hernia repair in selected cases. Growing evidence for radical prostatectomy via perineal approach (SP-TRUP).

Reduced port surgery: 3-port technique (reducing from standard 4–5 ports) — minimises wound morbidity while maintaining triangulation. Increasingly used for appendicectomy, cholecystectomy, and hernia repair.

> **SILS Trocar Hernia Rate — Higher than Standard** — SILS (Single-Incision Laparoscopic Surgery): the single umbilical incision requires a large fascial defect (20–25 mm), creating a significantly higher risk of trocar site hernia (up to 2–3%) compared with standard multi-port laparoscopy (<0.5% for 5 mm ports; ~1% for 10–12 mm ports with fascial closure). This is the primary disadvantage of SILS — the cosmetic benefit (single scar) must be weighed against the hernia risk. Robotic SP (da Vinci SP) uses a 25 mm port but with fascial closure — hernia rates may be lower with proper closure technique.


## Complications of MIS & Conversion

Minimally invasive surgery has a distinct complication profile in addition to those shared with open surgery.

Gas embolism: CO2 enters an open venous channel during peritoneal insufflation → CO2 gas in right heart → 'mill-wheel' murmur on auscultation/precordial Doppler → right heart outflow obstruction → cardiovascular collapse. Risk: penetration of a major vessel with Veress needle or during dissection. Management: (1) Immediately STOP insufflation; (2) Position patient LEFT LATERAL DECUBITUS + Trendelenburg (Durant's manoeuvre — gas migrates away from pulmonary valve); (3) Aspirate via central venous catheter (right atrium); (4) CPR if arrested; (5) ECMO in extremis. Prevention: confirm Veress needle position before insufflation; use safety tests; low initial flow rate.

Trocar injury to vessels/bowel: entry injury is the most dangerous early laparoscopic complication. Vascular injury (aorta, inferior vena cava, iliac vessels) — immediate life-threatening haemorrhage; convert to open laparotomy immediately; compress with hand/packs. Bowel injury: small bowel or colon — may be missed initially; trocar misplacement into bowel; or thermal energy injury (delayed presentation 3–7 days with peritonitis — 'electric burn' injury).

Port site hernia (Richter's hernia): partial herniation of bowel wall through a trocar fascial defect — only a portion of the bowel circumference is caught; lumen remains patent initially → presents as subacute obstruction, not seen on CT (no complete herniation). Suspect in patients with subacute bowel symptoms, pain at port site 1–2 weeks post-op. Management: surgical repair of fascial defect + bowel assessment.

Subcutaneous emphysema: CO2 dissects into subcutaneous tissue during prolonged insufflation or from Veress needle in subcutaneous position → crepitus on palpation of chest/neck. Rarely significant. Usually resolves spontaneously. If severe (extending to neck, affecting airway): reduce insufflation pressure to 8–10 mmHg; may require conversion. Hypercarbia from subcutaneous CO2 absorption may require increased ventilation.

Shoulder tip pain: phrenic nerve irritation from residual CO2 in the peritoneal cavity (CO2 irritates the diaphragm → referred pain to shoulder via phrenic nerve C3–C5). Common post-laparoscopy — typically 24–48h. Management: reassure; NSAID analgesia; can be reduced by aspirating residual gas at end of procedure and placing patient Trendelenburg briefly to expel gas.

Conversion: the decision to convert to open is a safety intervention, NOT a failure. Indications: inability to achieve adequate visualisation, uncontrollable haemorrhage, iatrogenic injury requiring open repair, identified pathology requiring open approach. Always document indication for conversion clearly in the operative note. Pre-operative counselling must include conversion risk (typically 3–10% for elective laparoscopic colorectal).

> **Conversion is Sound Judgment, NOT Failure** — Converting from laparoscopic to open surgery is a sign of surgical wisdom, not failure. Common indications: inability to achieve CVS (cholecystectomy), dense adhesions, haemorrhage, suspected injury, inadequate exposure, unsuspected pathology. Document: reason for conversion, intraoperative events, any injuries. Consent for conversion must be obtained pre-operatively. Tell the patient post-operatively why conversion was necessary — clear communication reduces patient dissatisfaction. Conversion rates: laparoscopic cholecystectomy ~5%; laparoscopic colectomy ~3–10%; laparoscopic rectal (ROLARR) ~10–12%.
