## The IMRaD Structure

The IMRaD structure — Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion — is not an arbitrary convention imposed by journals. It reflects the logical architecture of scientific reasoning: why the study was needed, how it was conducted, what was found, and what it means. Understanding that each section answers a specific question — and only that question — is the most important conceptual step in learning to write scientifically. IMRaD emerged in the early 20th century and was formalised by the American National Standards Institute in 1972. Its widespread adoption by biomedical journals has transformed scientific communication by enforcing a shared logic structure that allows rapid extraction of specific information by different readers: clinicians looking for applicability read the abstract and conclusion; statisticians interrogate the Methods; meta-analysts mine the Results.

*IMRaD — The Question Each Section Must Answer*
Section | Core Question | Length (typical) | Common Error
--- | --- | --- | ---
Introduction | Why was this study needed? What gap does it fill? | 3–4 paragraphs / 400–600 words | Narrating all existing literature instead of identifying the gap
Methods | How was the study done? Could another centre replicate it? | Largest section — unlimited, limited only by necessity | Insufficient detail on eligibility criteria, exposures, and statistical analysis
Results | What did the study find? | Guided by figures/tables; text guides the reader | Interpretation or discussion of results in the Results section
Discussion | What do the findings mean in context? | 4–5 paragraphs / 600–900 words | Repeating results; overreaching conclusions; ignoring limitations

The **Introduction** has a funnel structure: begin broadly with the clinical problem and its burden, progressively narrow to the specific knowledge gap, and conclude with a precise statement of the study objective. The introduction ends with the study aim — not the results and not the conclusions. A classic three-paragraph introduction: (1) disease burden and unmet clinical need; (2) prior evidence and its limitations; (3) study rationale and objective. The Introduction should not exceed one printed page — reviewers are fatigued by lengthy preamble.

> **Board Pearl — The Introduction Gap Statement** — Every Introduction must contain an explicit **gap statement** — the precise articulation of what is unknown or insufficiently characterised. Examples: 'However, no randomised trial has compared X to Y in this population.' 'Existing cohort studies are limited by short follow-up and heterogeneous patient selection.' 'The impact of laparoscopic versus open resection on functional outcomes after rectal cancer surgery remains undefined.' The gap statement is the justification for your study's existence.

The **Discussion** should follow a four-paragraph architecture regardless of manuscript length: (1) Summary paragraph — restate the principal findings in 3–5 sentences without repeating numbers already in the Results; (2) Comparison with literature — how do your findings align with or differ from prior studies, and why?; (3) Strengths and limitations — an honest appraisal, written with the same critical rigour you would apply to someone else's work; (4) Conclusion — what should clinicians do differently as a result of this study, and what future research is needed? The Discussion should never introduce new data not already in the Results section.

> **Common Pitfall — Discussion as Results Redux** — The most common Discussion error is re-presenting results with slightly different wording. 'As shown in Table 2, the leak rate was 8% in the laparoscopic group vs 12% in the open group' belongs in Results. In the Discussion, you write: 'The lower anastomotic leak rate observed with laparoscopic surgery is consistent with the postulated benefits of reduced tissue trauma and improved visualisation of the anastomotic site, and aligns with the meta-analytic finding of Slim et al. (2014).' Interpretation, not repetition.


## Writing the Introduction — Funnel Structure

The Introduction is perhaps the most strategically important section of the manuscript from the perspective of peer review. It is the first full section a reviewer reads after the abstract, and it establishes the intellectual framework that justifies the entire study. A poorly constructed Introduction signals to reviewers that the authors may not have a clear grasp of the state of the literature or the novelty of their contribution. The funnel structure — broad to narrow — is not merely a stylistic convention; it mirrors the logical path from clinical importance to scientific necessity.

*Three-Paragraph Introduction Template*
Paragraph | Function | Content | Word Target
--- | --- | --- | ---
Paragraph 1 | Clinical context and burden | Disease epidemiology, clinical impact, unmet need. Start with the patient, not the biology. | 100–150 words
Paragraph 2 | State of the evidence and its limitations | What prior studies have shown and why they are insufficient (design limitations, population gaps, outcome gaps, conflicting results). | 150–200 words
Paragraph 3 | Gap statement and study aim | Explicit gap statement followed by: 'Therefore, the aim of this study was to...' or 'The objective of this prospective cohort study was to...' | 80–100 words

> **Clinical Example — Introduction for a Peritoneal Surgery Cohort** — **Paragraph 1:** 'Colorectal peritoneal metastases (CPM) occur in 10–15% of patients with colorectal cancer and historically carried a median survival of 5–7 months with systemic chemotherapy alone. The combination of cytoreductive surgery (CRS) and hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy (HIPEC) has transformed the oncological landscape for selected patients, with landmark trials demonstrating median overall survival of 22–63 months in optimally cytoreduced patients.'
**Paragraph 2:** 'However, patient selection for CRS+HIPEC remains heterogeneous and controversially debated. Most published series derive from single high-volume centres, limiting generalisability. Predictive models such as the Peritoneal Surface Disease Severity Score (PSDSS) and the Peritoneal Cancer Index (PCI) correlate with prognosis, but no validated algorithm integrates preoperative imaging, clinical, and biological factors to optimise patient selection prospectively.'
**Paragraph 3:** 'No prospective multicentre study has evaluated the utility of integrated PCI + PSDSS + CEA as a combined selection tool in a European surgical oncology centre network. Therefore, the aim of this study was to develop and internally validate a multivariate selection score for CRS+HIPEC candidacy using prospective data from five HIPEC centres in Spain.'

Common Introduction errors to avoid: (1) **Literature review without argument** — citing papers chronologically without synthesising what they collectively demonstrate and where they fall short; (2) **Premature Methods** — describing what you did in the Introduction ('we conducted a prospective study...') before the Methods section; (3) **Results in the Introduction** — announcing findings before presenting them (common in press releases; unacceptable in manuscripts); (4) **Overloaded opening sentence** — 'Colorectal cancer, the third most common malignancy worldwide, affecting X million individuals annually, with a global incidence of Y and a mortality of Z...' — readers lose the thread. Start with the clinical problem, not the epidemiological statistics.

> **Board Pearl — Active vs Passive Voice in the Introduction** — The Introduction is the section where active voice is most appropriate: 'Prior studies have failed to demonstrate...' (active) rather than 'It has not been demonstrated by prior studies...' (passive). Active voice is clearer, more direct, and signals confidence. However, the **Methods section** traditionally uses passive voice to emphasise the procedure rather than the actor: 'Patients were enrolled consecutively between January 2018 and December 2022' rather than 'We enrolled patients consecutively.' Most journals accept either; ICMJE does not mandate a voice preference, but consistency within sections is expected.


## Writing the Methods Section

The Methods section carries the heaviest intellectual burden in a scientific manuscript because it is the section against which the validity and reproducibility of the entire study rests. The reproducibility standard — could an independent team at another centre conduct this study from your Methods section alone and obtain comparable results? — is the single criterion by which Methods should be written. Every element that is not described is a flaw in reproducibility.

A complete Methods section for a surgical cohort study contains: (1) **Study design and setting** — observational/interventional, single/multi-centre, prospective/retrospective, dates of data collection; (2) **Participants** — explicit inclusion and exclusion criteria (not just 'consecutive patients'), how eligibility was verified, and the source population; (3) **Intervention/Exposure** — surgical procedure described to the level of technical detail that enables classification of cases, including surgeon volume and credentialling; (4) **Outcomes** — primary and secondary outcomes defined a priori with measurement method and timing; (5) **Data sources** — where data came from (electronic records, prospective database, operation notes), who extracted them, and quality checks performed; (6) **Statistical analysis** — all tests pre-specified with justification, handling of missing data, sensitivity analyses, and software used.

> **Clinical Application — Describing the Surgical Intervention** — Describing a surgical procedure in the Methods section requires a balance between completeness and brevity. Do NOT write: 'All patients underwent laparoscopic sigmoid resection.' DO write: 'All patients underwent elective laparoscopic-assisted sigmoid resection with high ligation of the inferior mesenteric artery (IMA). A double-stapled end-to-end colorectal anastomosis was fashioned using a 29mm circular stapler. All procedures were performed by one of three surgeons, each with >100 prior laparoscopic sigmoid resections and a centre volume of >40 laparoscopic colorectal resections per year. Defunctioning loop ileostomy was constructed at the surgeon's discretion for anastomoses ≤8cm from the anal verge or in the presence of technical concern.' This level of detail is the reproducibility standard.

Statistical analysis reporting is among the most poorly executed components of surgical manuscripts. CONSORT item 12a requires specification of the statistical methods used to compare primary and secondary outcomes. This includes: the statistical test chosen and justification (e.g., 'Kaplan-Meier survival analysis with log-rank comparison was used for time-to-event outcomes; proportional hazards assumption was verified by Schoenfeld residuals'); handling of missing data (complete case analysis, multiple imputation, or last observation carried forward — with justification); confounders included in multivariable analysis and method of selection (a priori clinical selection preferred over stepwise algorithmic selection); software and version.

> **CONSORT 2010 — Methods Checklist Items** — Key CONSORT items for Methods: Item 3a (eligibility criteria); Item 4a (settings and locations); Item 5 (interventions with sufficient detail for replication); Item 6a (primary and secondary outcomes, and how they were assessed); Item 7a (sample size calculation — target, assumptions, and power); Item 8a (sequence generation method for randomisation); Item 9 (allocation concealment mechanism); Item 11a (blinding — who was blinded and how); Item 12a (statistical methods for primary and secondary outcomes).

*SAMPL Guidelines — Statistical Reporting Standards for Methods*
Element | Required Information | Example
--- | --- | ---
Descriptive statistics | Specify distribution before choosing measure of central tendency | Normally distributed → mean ± SD; skewed → median (IQR)
Comparative tests | Name the test, state whether one- or two-tailed, and justify | Mann-Whitney U test (two-tailed) for non-normally distributed continuous variables
Regression models | Specify type, variable selection method, and goodness-of-fit | Binary logistic regression; a priori variable selection based on clinical plausibility; Hosmer-Lemeshow test
Survival analysis | State the method, censoring rules, and assumption verification | Kaplan-Meier with log-rank; censored at last known alive contact; PH assumption verified by Schoenfeld residuals (p=0.38)
Sample size | State primary outcome, α, power, expected effect, and calculated n | α=0.05, power=80%, expected difference in leak rate 8% vs 14%, n=186 per arm
Missing data | Report amount, pattern, and handling method | Missing data in 12/250 (4.8%) cases; multiple imputation with 20 datasets under MAR assumption
Software | Name and version | All analyses in R v4.3.1 (R Foundation, Vienna); survival package v3.5-7


## Results: Reporting Without Interpretation

The Results section presents the data. Nothing more. The discipline of reporting without interpretation — stating what was found without explaining why or what it means — is simultaneously the simplest principle in scientific writing and among the most commonly violated. Every sentence in Results should be verifiable by examining the tables and figures. Every table and figure should have a corresponding text narrative guiding the reader through the most important findings.

Effective Results organisation: (1) begin with participant flow — how many were assessed for eligibility, how many met criteria, how many were included (CONSORT flow diagram for RCTs, adapted flow for observational studies); (2) present baseline characteristics of the study population before any analysis; (3) report primary outcome first, then secondary outcomes, then subgroups and sensitivity analyses; (4) present absolute numbers alongside proportions (22/150 [14.7%], not just 14.7%); (5) report effect sizes with 95% confidence intervals before p-values — 'the anastomotic leak rate was lower in the laparoscopic group (8/120 [6.7%] vs 14/110 [12.7%]; OR 0.49, 95%CI 0.20–1.21; p=0.12)' provides far more information than 'p=0.12 (non-significant)'.

> **Board Pearl — Tables vs Figures** — **Tables** convey exact numerical values and are appropriate for baseline characteristics, complication data, regression model outputs, and comparison of multiple outcomes across groups. **Figures** convey patterns, trends, distributions, and relationships — Kaplan-Meier curves, box plots, scatter plots, forest plots. The decision rule: if the reader needs the exact number, use a table. If the reader needs to understand a trend or relationship, use a figure. Never duplicate the same data in both a table and a figure.

Reporting of p-values follows a consistent convention: report exact p-values to 2 decimal places when ≥0.01 (p=0.04, p=0.38), and as p<0.001 when below this threshold. Never write 'p=NS' without specifying the actual value — reviewers will request it, and it provides no information about the magnitude of the effect. The threshold for statistical significance must be pre-specified (α=0.05 unless otherwise justified) and reported in the Methods; results should never be reported as 'almost significant' (p=0.06) or 'trending toward significance' — these phrases are not statistically meaningful and are a red flag for reviewers.

> **Common Pitfall — Decimal Place Consistency** — Report all values to a consistent and clinically meaningful number of decimal places. Percentages: one decimal place for proportions >5% (14.7%), two decimal places for rare events <5% (0.34%). Continuous variables: match the precision of the measurement instrument (weight to one decimal place if measured in 0.1 kg increments). P-values: two decimal places when ≥0.01, then <0.001. Reporting an OR as 1.2349857 is scientifically absurd — round to 1.23. Statistical software outputs many decimal places; the author selects the appropriate precision. Inconsistency within a table (some values to 1 dp, others to 3 dp) signals careless proofreading.


## Discussion: The Four-Paragraph Architecture

A well-structured Discussion is the intellectual centrepiece of the manuscript — where the investigator demonstrates not just data literacy but clinical wisdom. The four-paragraph structure provides a scaffolding that prevents both over-reach and under-interpretation. Each paragraph has a specific function and a specific boundary.

*Four-Paragraph Discussion Architecture*
Paragraph | Function | Content | Do NOT
--- | --- | --- | ---
1 — Summary | Restate principal findings in clinical terms | 3–5 sentences synthesising the 1–2 most important results; include direction and magnitude | Re-present all results with numbers (that is the Results section)
2 — Contextualisation | Compare with existing literature | Where do your results agree/disagree with prior work and why? Explain discordances. | List prior studies without synthesising what they mean collectively
3 — Strengths & Limitations | Honest methodological appraisal | Name specific limitations with direction and likely magnitude of bias; match strengths to claims | Use vague language ('some limitations exist') or omit limitations entirely
4 — Conclusion | Clinical implication and future research | One sentence of implication; one sentence of future research needed | Introduce new data; overclaim causality; hedge excessively

> **Common Pitfall — Overreaching Conclusions** — The most serious Discussion error — the one most likely to attract major revision or rejection — is concluding beyond what the data support. A single-centre retrospective cohort cannot 'prove', 'demonstrate', or 'establish' causality. It can 'suggest', 'indicate', 'provide evidence', or 'support the hypothesis' that a relationship exists. Reserve causal language for RCTs with appropriate design controls. Reviewers are pattern-matching for overreach — every overclaim will be flagged.


## Abstract Writing — Structured vs Unstructured

The abstract is the most read section of any manuscript and frequently the only section read by busy clinicians making evidence-based decisions. It must stand completely alone — a reader who reads only the abstract should understand the study design, key findings, and clinical implications without accessing the full text. Two formats exist: structured and unstructured. Structured abstracts — with explicit headings (Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions) — are required by most surgical journals including BJS, EJSO, Annals of Surgery, and JAMA Surgery. Unstructured abstracts (a single prose paragraph) are used by some journals including NEJM and Lancet for original articles, and by most journals for review articles and editorials.

*Structured Abstract — Section-by-Section Requirements*
Heading | Content Required | Word Budget | Common Error
--- | --- | --- | ---
Background | Clinical problem + knowledge gap (2–3 sentences) | 40–60 words | Starting with generic epidemiology rather than the gap
Methods | Design, setting, population (eligibility), intervention/exposure, primary outcome | 60–80 words | Omitting sample size, date range, or primary outcome definition
Results | n analysed, primary outcome with effect size + 95%CI, 2–3 secondary outcomes | 80–100 words | Reporting only p-values without effect sizes; omitting CIs
Conclusions | One sentence of clinical implication; one sentence of scope/limitation | 30–40 words | Overreaching causality; repeating results rather than interpreting them

The **250-word discipline** is the most important technical constraint in abstract writing. Most journals impose a word limit of 250–300 words. Writing to this limit requires precise prioritisation: every sentence must earn its place. The Methods sentence 'This was a prospective cohort study conducted at a single tertiary referral centre specialising in peritoneal surface malignancy between January 2018 and December 2022, enrolling consecutive patients meeting predefined eligibility criteria including histologically confirmed colorectal peritoneal metastases, ECOG performance status 0–2, and absence of systemic metastases on preoperative imaging' contains the necessary information but uses 56 words. This is appropriate — the abstract Methods must be complete, not truncated.

> **Board Pearl — Writing Order: Abstract Last, Edited Most** — The abstract should be written **last** — after the complete manuscript is finalised — but edited **most**. Because the abstract will be read by the widest audience (database searches, social media, press summaries), every word choice matters disproportionately. The Results section of the abstract must include: the denominator (n analysed), the primary outcome event rate in each group (or mean difference for continuous outcomes), the effect size (OR, RR, HR, MD) with 95% CI, and the p-value. Abstracts that report only p-values ('p=0.03') without effect sizes will be returned for revision by most current editors.

**Visual abstracts** are increasingly required or encouraged by high-impact journals (JAMA Surgery, Annals of Surgery, BJS) as graphical summaries for social media dissemination. A visual abstract condenses the study into a single infographic: clinical question (left panel) and key result with effect size (right panel). Design standards: maximum 3–4 data elements; no paragraph text (bullet points or single-line labels only); colour-coded comparison groups; journal logo and DOI in footer. Studies with visual abstracts receive 2–3 times more social media engagement and up to 30% more article downloads.


## Figures & Tables — Design Standards for Publication

Publication-quality figures and tables are not merely aesthetic — they are functional communication tools that must convey complex data clearly, without distortion, and in a format reproducible at the journal's final print resolution. Most journals require figures at 300 DPI minimum (600 DPI preferred for line art) in TIFF or EPS format. Common file formats that are unacceptable: JPEG (lossy compression distorts numerical data), PowerPoint screenshots (inadequate resolution), screenshots from statistical software without annotation.

*Figure Design Standards by Type*
Figure Type | Recommended Format | Key Elements | Common Errors
--- | --- | --- | ---
Kaplan-Meier survival curve | Line graph with censoring marks | Both groups clearly labelled; at-risk table below x-axis; median survival ± 95%CI stated in figure or legend; log-rank p-value | No at-risk table; missing censoring marks; no confidence bands
Forest plot (meta-analysis) | Horizontal CI plot | Study names + weights; point estimates (squares scaled to weight); CI lines; diamond for pooled estimate; line of no effect; I² and Q-test p-value | Missing overall diamond; no heterogeneity statistic; unlabelled axes
Box plot | Box-and-whisker | Median line; IQR box; whiskers at 1.5×IQR; outliers as individual points; n per group labelled | Showing mean±SEM instead of median/IQR for non-normal data; no individual data points for small n
Flow diagram (CONSORT) | Structured flowchart | All exclusions numbered with reasons; n at each stage; parallel allocation arms shown | Omitting reasons for exclusion; non-standard format
Bar chart | Vertical bars | Error bars must be defined (SD, SEM, or 95%CI) in legend; y-axis starting at zero | Broken y-axis; undefined error bars; 3D effects that distort perception

> **Figure Legend Sufficiency Standard** — Every figure must be interpretable **without reading the main text**. The figure legend must contain: (1) a brief title (not a sentence — a noun phrase); (2) definition of all abbreviations used in the figure; (3) definition of error bars or shading; (4) explanation of any colour coding or line styles; (5) sample sizes per group; (6) statistical test used and p-value if displayed in the figure. A legend that reads 'Kaplan-Meier survival curves' is inadequate. A legend that reads 'Kaplan-Meier overall survival curves for patients undergoing CRS+HIPEC (blue) versus systemic chemotherapy alone (red) for colorectal peritoneal metastases. At-risk numbers are shown below the x-axis. Median survival was 34.2 months (95%CI 28.6–41.0) vs 12.7 months (95%CI 9.3–16.1) respectively. Log-rank p<0.001.' meets the sufficiency standard.

**Table design** follows the three-line rule in most surgical journals (consistent with APA and Vancouver styles): a horizontal rule above the column headers, a horizontal rule below the column headers, and a horizontal rule at the bottom of the table. No vertical rules within the table body. Column headers must be self-explanatory with units in parentheses (e.g., 'Age (years)', 'BMI (kg/m²)', 'Tumour size (cm)'). Footnotes below the table define abbreviations and provide statistical details. Baseline characteristics tables (Table 1) should report: n (%), mean±SD or median (IQR) as appropriate, and p-values from appropriate between-group tests — though many journals now discourage significance testing for Table 1 (baseline characteristics from RCTs should be similar by design; reporting p-values implies they 'should' differ).


## The Abstract

The abstract is the most read section of any manuscript and frequently the only section read by busy clinicians making evidence-based decisions. A structured abstract (Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions) is required by most surgical journals and enforces the same logical architecture as the full manuscript. The word limit is typically 250–300 words — every word must earn its place.

**Background:** 2–3 sentences identifying the clinical problem and knowledge gap. Do not start with 'X is a common condition affecting Y% of the population' — this is filler. State the gap directly: 'The impact of diabetes on anastomotic healing after laparoscopic colorectal surgery is poorly characterised in prospective data.' **Methods:** Specify design, setting, population, intervention, comparison, and primary outcome. Include date range and sample size. **Results:** Report the primary outcome with effect size and 95% CI; 2–3 secondary outcomes. Include the sample size analysed. **Conclusions:** One sentence of implication; one sentence of scope limitation. 'These findings suggest laparoscopic TME is associated with reduced anastomotic leak in diabetic patients and support its use as the preferred approach in this population. Prospective validation in a multicentre setting is warranted.'

> **Board Pearl — The 250-Word Discipline** — Writing a 250-word abstract that fully characterises a study is one of the hardest writing challenges in medicine. The discipline forces you to identify what actually matters. If you cannot summarise your Methods in 3 sentences, your Methods are probably unfocused. If your Results paragraph is 4 sentences with 3 effect sizes and p-values, you are providing the minimum essential information — not being sparse. The abstract should be written **last** but edited most.


## Reporting Guidelines

Reporting guidelines are minimum checklists of information that must be present in a manuscript for readers to critically appraise the study. They do not determine study quality — a study can follow all CONSORT items and still be methodologically poor — but they ensure that the information necessary for quality assessment is present. Journals increasingly mandate guideline compliance at submission, and EQUATOR Network maintains the definitive list of guidelines by study type.

*Reporting Guidelines — Surgical Research*
Guideline | Study Type | Key Items | Source
--- | --- | --- | ---
CONSORT 2010 | Randomised controlled trials | 25 items + flow diagram; requires allocation sequence, blinding details, ITT analysis | www.consort-statement.org
STROBE 2007 | Cohort, case-control, cross-sectional | 22 items; addresses selection bias, confounding, and missing data | www.strobe-statement.org
PRISMA 2020 | Systematic reviews and meta-analyses | 27 items + flow diagram; requires protocol registration, search strategy, risk of bias | www.prisma-statement.org
CARE 2013 | Case reports | 13 items; timeline, patient perspective, diagnostic reasoning, follow-up | www.care-statement.org
SCARE 2020 | Surgical case reports | Extension of CARE with surgical-specific items | www.scarecriteria.org
TRIPOD 2015 | Prediction models (diagnostic/prognostic) | 22 items; model development, validation, discrimination, calibration | www.tripod-statement.org
CHEERS 2022 | Health economic evaluations | 28 items; perspective, time horizon, discount rate, sensitivity analysis | www.equator-network.org
SAMPL 2015 | Statistical analysis and methods in literature | Covers descriptive stats, inferential tests, regression, survival, Bayesian | www.equator-network.org

**CONSORT** (Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials) is the most influential reporting guideline, demonstrating that trial reporting quality significantly improves when journals mandate its use. Key CONSORT items surgeons commonly under-report: item 4a (settings and locations — which hospitals, what volume?), item 5 (intervention description — at what level of technical detail?), item 9 (allocation concealment — numbered envelopes is no longer considered adequate), item 12a (statistical methods — which tests for which outcomes?), and item 19 (adverse events — must include all serious adverse events, not just those considered intervention-related).

> **STROBE — Observational Study Reporting** — STROBE item 16 requires reporting of the number of participants with missing data for each variable. Item 16b mandates description of how missing data were handled (complete case analysis, multiple imputation, etc.). Item 12b requires sensitivity analyses to assess robustness of results. Item 13a requires reporting of efforts to address confounding — not merely listing potential confounders but describing how they were measured and controlled. Most surgical observational studies are inadequately reported on these items.


## Cover Letter & Submission Strategy

The cover letter is the first document an editor reads — it determines whether a manuscript proceeds to peer review or receives a desk rejection within 24–48 hours. A well-crafted cover letter is not a summary of the manuscript; it is a structured argument for why this specific manuscript belongs in this specific journal, now. Editors at high-impact journals receive hundreds of submissions monthly; the cover letter must immediately establish novelty, fit, and quality.

*Cover Letter Structure*
Paragraph | Content | Length
--- | --- | ---
Opening | Manuscript title, study type, and direct statement of submission ('We submit for consideration...') | 2–3 sentences
Novelty and significance | What is new in this study and why does it matter? State the knowledge gap and how this manuscript fills it. | 3–4 sentences
Fit to journal | Why this journal? Reference the journal's readership, recent related publications, or scope — never generic ('EJSO is a leading journal'). Be specific. | 2–3 sentences
Ethical and compliance statements | Ethical approval reference number, patient consent, data availability, conflict of interest statement, no simultaneous submission | 3–5 sentences
Suggested reviewers | 2–3 names (optional but welcomed) with institution and reason for expertise — never competitors or collaborators | Optional, 2–3 lines

> **Common Pitfall — Generic Cover Letters** — Generic cover letters that begin 'We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled...' and contain no journal-specific content are immediately recognisable and signal a mass-submission strategy. Editors notice when the letter does not mention their journal by name, or worse, when a previous journal's name appears (a common error when recycling letters from prior rejections). The cover letter should also explicitly state: (1) that the manuscript has not been submitted elsewhere simultaneously; (2) that all authors have approved the submission; (3) the word count and figure/table count; and (4) any previous interactions with the editorial office (e.g., a prior submission that was revised).

**Submission strategy** begins with target journal selection before manuscript writing, not after. A cascading submission strategy — submitting to the highest-impact journal first and descending on rejection — is time-inefficient, takes 6–18 months per journal cycle, and results in a manuscript progressively aged in a competitive field. A more efficient strategy: honestly assess the manuscript against the journal's typical acceptance criteria (sample size, design, novelty, statistical sophistication) before submission; target the journal where the manuscript is competitive on its merits, not aspirationally; consider splitting the submission into a short communication (preliminary findings, rapid dissemination) versus a full paper (definitive analysis) if appropriate.

> **Board Pearl — Journal Instructions to Authors Compliance** — Before submitting, read the journal's Instructions to Authors (ITA) in full — every requirement. Common ITA-specific requirements that cause immediate return of manuscripts: word count limits (some journals impose 3,000-word main text limits for Original Articles); reference style (Vancouver numbered vs APA author-date); figure resolution and format (TIFF 300 DPI, not PNG); reference limit (some journals cap at 30–40 references); structured abstract format (required headings may differ from standard Background/Methods/Results/Conclusions); supplementary data policy; data sharing statement format; CRediT authorship contribution statement; mandatory declaration of AI assistance in writing or data analysis. Non-compliance is the single most common cause of desk rejection that is entirely avoidable.


## Responding to Reviewers — Structure & Tone

Receiving a decision of 'Major Revision' is a positive outcome — the manuscript is publishable in principle and the editor believes the authors can address the concerns raised. The response-to-reviewers document is a formal scientific correspondence that is evaluated as rigorously as the manuscript itself. A well-constructed response significantly increases the probability of acceptance at revision stage; a defensive or incomplete response frequently results in a second round of major revision or rejection.

*Response-to-Reviewers Document Structure*
Element | Content | Standard
--- | --- | ---
Cover page | Date, manuscript ID, title, and acknowledgement of reviewers | Professional, brief, non-effusive
General response | 1–2 paragraphs summarising the major changes made and the revised manuscript's improvements | Substantive, not generic
Reviewer 1, Comment 1 | Verbatim quote of reviewer comment, then author response, then revised text with page/line reference | Every comment addressed individually
Agreement responses | When reviewer is correct: 'The reviewer correctly identifies...We have revised the manuscript to...(page X, line Y)' | Concrete: show the change, state where
Disagreement responses | When reviewer is incorrect: 'We respectfully disagree. [Evidence/reference]. The original text has been retained but we have added clarification...' | Evidence-based; respectful; non-capitulating
Revised text excerpts | Paste the revised text in full within the response document | Allows reviewers to verify changes without re-reading entire manuscript

> **Clinical Example — Responding to a Statistical Critique** — **Reviewer Comment:** 'The authors used a multivariable logistic regression model but do not report whether the proportional odds assumption was verified. The variable selection process using stepwise regression is also methodologically problematic.'

**Author Response:** 'We thank Reviewer 2 for this important methodological observation. The reviewer is correct that stepwise variable selection is susceptible to overfitting and spurious model building. We have revised the statistical analysis accordingly. The multivariable logistic regression model now uses a priori variable selection based on clinical plausibility (as recommended by Harrell, 2001) rather than stepwise selection. Variables included were those identified by the clinical team as biologically plausible predictors of the primary outcome prior to data analysis: age, ASA grade, PCI, and neoadjuvant chemotherapy receipt. We have also verified the Hosmer-Lemeshow goodness-of-fit test (χ²=7.4, df=8, p=0.49, indicating adequate fit). These changes are detailed in the revised Methods section (page 6, lines 142–156) and the updated results are presented in the revised Table 3.'

Tone in the response document must remain consistently professional and respectful, regardless of the quality of the reviewer comments. Reviewers donate their time without compensation; even poorly articulated comments deserve a courteous response. Never use language that implies reviewer incompetence, even obliquely. If a reviewer has clearly misunderstood a key aspect of the study, address the misunderstanding by clarifying the manuscript rather than correcting the reviewer: 'We recognise that the original text may have been unclear on this point. We have revised the Methods section to explicitly state...' This approach both addresses the concern and demonstrates good faith.


## Supplementary Material & Data Availability

Supplementary material allows authors to present information that is important for reproducibility and completeness but would disrupt the narrative flow of the main manuscript or exceed journal word/page limits. The decision of what belongs in the main text versus supplementary material requires careful judgement: supplementary material is widely read by systematic reviewers and methodologically sophisticated readers, but rarely accessed by busy clinicians. The core clinical findings and their direct interpretation must always appear in the main text.

*Main Text vs Supplementary Material — Decision Framework*
Content Type | Main Text | Supplementary
--- | --- | ---
Primary outcome results | Always main text | Never supplementary-only
Full database search strategy | Abbreviated in Methods | Full Boolean string in supplement
Sensitivity analyses | If primary, main text; if secondary, supplement acceptable | Supplementary acceptable for exploratory analyses
Additional patient subgroup analyses | If pre-specified and clinically important, main text | Post-hoc subgroups → supplementary
Operative technique description | Key technical steps in Methods | Step-by-step protocol or video → supplementary
Extended baseline characteristics | Core demographics in Table 1 | Extended comorbidity lists → supplementary table
Risk of bias assessments (meta-analysis) | Summary RoB figure in main text | Full domain-level RoB table in supplementary

**Data availability statements** are now mandatory at most journals and increasingly demanded by funders. A data availability statement specifies where the data underlying the study are stored, whether they are publicly accessible, and under what conditions they can be accessed. Three tiers: (1) **Open data** — 'The dataset supporting the conclusions of this article is available in [repository name] at [DOI/URL]' — increasingly required by funders (NIH, Wellcome, Spanish FECYT); (2) **Conditional access** — 'The dataset is available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request, subject to data transfer agreement and institutional ethical approval'; (3) **Data not available** — 'The dataset cannot be made publicly available due to patient privacy and institutional data governance restrictions; a fully anonymised subset is available upon request.' Privacy legislation (GDPR in Europe) may restrict data sharing for patient-level clinical data without explicit consent for data sharing.

> **ICMJE Data Sharing Policy (2018)** — Since 2018, ICMJE member journals (including Annals of Surgery, JAMA Surgery, BJS, NEJM, Lancet) require that RCT data sharing plans be registered prospectively (in ClinicalTrials.gov) as a condition of publication. For observational studies, most journals now require a data availability statement even if data are not publicly available. Systematic reviews must include the full electronic search strategy (PRISMA item 7) in the manuscript or supplement and register their protocol on PROSPERO before data extraction begins. Non-compliance with data availability requirements is increasingly a basis for rejection or retraction.


## Authorship

Authorship of a scientific publication confers both credit and responsibility — credit for intellectual contribution and responsibility for the integrity of the reported work. The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (ICMJE) defines authorship by four criteria, all of which must be met by each author. Failure to apply these criteria rigorously leads to two of the most prevalent forms of publication misconduct: ghost authorship and gift authorship.

*ICMJE Authorship Criteria (all four required)*
Criterion | Practical Standard | Common Misconception
--- | --- | ---
1. Substantial contribution to conception/design OR acquisition/analysis/interpretation | Significant intellectual input — not mere data collection | Collecting data without intellectual contribution does not qualify
2. Drafting OR critically revising the manuscript for intellectual content | Reading and approving the final draft is insufficient | Reviewing for typos is not critical revision for intellectual content
3. Final approval of the version to be published | Must read and agree to the submitted version | Cannot approve a version you have not read
4. Accountability for all aspects of the work | Willing to investigate and resolve accuracy/integrity concerns | Cannot be an author without accepting this responsibility

> **Board Pearl — The Corresponding Author** — The **corresponding author** takes primary responsibility for communication with the journal (submission, peer review, revision, proofing) and is the public point of contact after publication. The corresponding author does not need to be the first or last author. However, the corresponding author must have full access to the data, be able to vouch for its integrity, and be available for post-publication queries (including data sharing requests, which are now mandated by many journals and funders).


## Choosing a Journal

Journal selection is a strategic decision that affects turnaround time, readership, and ultimately the impact of your work. The correct approach is systematic — not submitting to the highest-impact journal first and descending in rejection-cascade, but matching the manuscript to the journal where it best fits and will reach the most relevant audience.

> **Common Pitfall — Predatory Journals** — Predatory journals charge article processing charges (APCs) without providing legitimate peer review, editorial standards, or indexing. Red flags: unsolicited email invitations, implausibly rapid peer review (<72 hours), journals not indexed in MEDLINE/PubMed or Scopus, missing editorial board, journal name designed to mimic legitimate journals. Check legitimacy: is the journal listed in MEDLINE via PubMed's journal search? Is the publisher a member of COPE? Beall's List (archived) provides historical guidance. Publishing in predatory journals damages career credibility and does not constitute legitimate scientific contribution.
