## Thyroid Cancer — Classification, Staging & Oncological Management

Thyroid nodules are extremely common — palpable in 5% of adults, detectable on ultrasound in 50–70%. The clinical priority is identifying the minority that are malignant (~5–15%). The ATA 2015 guidelines (updated 2023) stratify nodule risk by ultrasound pattern (TIRADS-equivalent): high suspicion (solid hypoechoic, irregular margins, microcalcifications, taller-than-wide, rim calcification, extrathyroidal extension) vs benign features (purely cystic, spongiform). FNA is indicated for nodules ≥1cm in the high-suspicion pattern; ≥1.5–2cm for intermediate suspicion. The Bethesda System for Reporting Thyroid Cytopathology (I–VI) guides surgical decision-making. Molecular testing (ThyroSeq v3, Afirma GSC) has clinical utility for Bethesda III–IV nodules: BRAF V600E detected in a Bethesda III nodule dramatically raises malignancy probability and may guide the extent of initial surgery.

*Bethesda Classification — Surgical Decision Guide*
Bethesda | Category | Malignancy Risk | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
I | Non-diagnostic | 1–4% | Repeat FNA with US guidance
II | Benign | 0–3% | Clinical follow-up; no surgery
III | Atypia of undetermined significance (AUS/FLUS) | 10–30% | Repeat FNA / molecular testing (ThyroSeq v3) / diagnostic lobectomy
IV | Follicular neoplasm | 25–40% | Diagnostic hemithyroidectomy
V | Suspicious for malignancy | 50–75% | Hemithyroidectomy or total thyroidectomy
VI | Malignant | 97–99% | Total thyroidectomy ± central neck dissection

Thyroid cancer histological subtypes and their surgical implications: (1) Papillary thyroid cancer (PTC) — 80–85% of all thyroid cancers. Generally excellent prognosis. ATA 2015 risk stratification guides extent of surgery, radioiodine (RAI), and follow-up. Low-risk PTC: T1N0M0, classic or follicular variant, no vascular invasion — hemithyroidectomy acceptable. High-risk PTC: extrathyroidal extension, multifocal bilateral, lymph node metastasis, vascular invasion, BRAF+TERT comutation (worst prognosis) — total thyroidectomy + central dissection ± RAI. (2) Follicular thyroid cancer (FTC) — 10–15%. Capsular and/or vascular invasion determines risk (minimally invasive FTC vs widely invasive FTC). Haematogenous spread (lung, bone) > lymphatic. (3) Hürthle cell (oncocytic) carcinoma — variant of FTC; RAI-refractory; higher recurrence risk. (4) Medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) — 3–5%; arises from parafollicular C-cells; secretes calcitonin (tumour marker); 25% hereditary (MEN2A, MEN2B, FMTC). RAI does not work — surgery alone is curative. (5) Anaplastic thyroid cancer (ATC) — <2%; most lethal solid tumour (median survival 3–5 months). BRAF V600E in 25–45% — dabrafenib + trametinib combination achieves responses in 60–70% (ROAR basket trial). Secure the airway early; surgery rarely curative.

> **Board Pearl — RET Mutation and Medullary Thyroid Cancer** — Medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) arises from parafollicular C-cells and secretes calcitonin — a specific tumour marker. RET proto-oncogene mutations: hereditary MTC occurs in MEN2A (RET C634 codon mutation + pheochromocytoma + primary hyperparathyroidism) and MEN2B (M918T mutation + pheochromocytoma + mucosal neuromas; most aggressive). Prophylactic total thyroidectomy is recommended for RET mutation carriers — timing depends on specific codon and associated risk category. Calcitonin and CEA are post-operative surveillance markers. Selpercatinib (RET inhibitor) and cabozantinib are systemic therapy options for metastatic/progressive MTC (LIBRETTO-001 trial).

> **Common Pitfall — Anaplastic Thyroid Cancer** — Anaplastic thyroid cancer (ATC) is the most lethal solid tumour — median survival 3–5 months. It typically presents as a rapidly enlarging neck mass with tracheal invasion, dysphagia, and dysphonia. BRAF V600E mutation present in 25–45% — combined BRAF inhibitor (dabrafenib) + MEK inhibitor (trametinib) achieves 60–70% response rates and is now first-line (ROAR basket trial, 2022). Surgery is rarely curative — do not attempt radical resection without multidisciplinary neoadjuvant intent plan. Secure the airway early. Immunotherapy (pembrolizumab) and multimodal approaches are under active investigation.


## Thyroidectomy — Technique & Nerve Monitoring

Total thyroidectomy is the standard surgical procedure for thyroid cancer, bilateral multinodular goitre, and Graves' disease refractory to medical therapy. Hemithyroidectomy (thyroid lobectomy) is appropriate for benign unilateral disease, diagnostic resection (Bethesda IV), and low-risk differentiated thyroid cancer ≤1cm (ATA low-risk). Operative steps for total thyroidectomy: (1) Patient positioning: supine with neck extended (shoulder roll); Kocher collar incision 2–3cm above the clavicle in a skin crease. (2) Subplatysmal flaps raised; strap muscles divided in the midline (not transected). (3) Superior thyroid pole: ligate the superior thyroid artery and vein as close to the thyroid as possible — critical to identify and preserve the external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve (EBSLN) which runs medial to the superior thyroid vessels and can be damaged during ligation of the superior pole. (4) Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) identification: the RLN runs in the tracheoesophageal groove on the left, entering the larynx at the inferior border of the cricothyroid muscle. On the right, the RLN loops around the subclavian artery and has a more oblique course. The RLN is identified before any tissue division — identification, not just 'working laterally,' is the standard. The nerve should be traced from the inferior pole of the thyroid to its entry into the larynx. (5) Parathyroid glands: identify all four parathyroid glands; devascularised glands should be autotransplanted (minced into 1mm³ pieces, placed in small pockets in the sternocleidomastoid muscle). (6) Haemostasis and drain (if significant dead space).

Intraoperative neuromonitoring (IONM) of the RLN is standard practice in many centres. Two systems: intermittent IONM (needle electrodes in vocalis muscle, probe stimulation of nerve) and continuous IONM (electromyographic endotracheal tube, continuous nerve signal during dissection). IONM identifies the RLN in the operative field, confirms nerve integrity before and after resection, and alerts the surgeon to traction injury. The European Laryngological Society recommends IONM for all bilateral thyroid surgery, reoperative surgery, and Graves' disease where the risk of RLN injury is highest. Loss of EMG signal during IONM should prompt cessation of dissection on the contralateral side if bilateral thyroidectomy is planned — completing the second side risks bilateral RLN injury and emergency tracheostomy.

*Recurrent vs Superior Laryngeal Nerve — Key Differences*
Feature | Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) | External Branch of SLN (EBSLN)
--- | --- | ---
Course | Tracheoesophageal groove; loops around subclavian (R) or aortic arch (L) | Medial to superior thyroid vessels, near upper pole
Function | All laryngeal muscles except cricothyroid; vocal cord abduction/adduction | Cricothyroid muscle — voice pitch modulation
Injury presentation | Hoarseness, weak voice, aspiration (bilateral: stridor, airway emergency) | Voice fatigue, high-pitched voice loss (can be subtle)
Identification point | Tracheoesophageal groove; at inferior pole of thyroid | During superior pole mobilisation — stay close to thyroid capsule
Risk factor for injury | Reoperative surgery, Graves', cancer invasion, non-recurrent (right, rare) | Ligation of superior thyroid vessels away from capsule


## Complications of Thyroid Surgery

The most important complications of thyroid surgery are: (1) Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury — incidence 0.5–1% permanent for total thyroidectomy in experienced hands; up to 5% in reoperative surgery. Unilateral RLN injury causes hoarseness and aspiration; bilateral injury causes stridor and may require emergency tracheostomy. Management: voice therapy for unilateral injury; vocal cord medialisation (injection or thyroplasty) for permanent paralysis. (2) Hypoparathyroidism — the most common complication of total thyroidectomy. Transient hypoparathyroidism (PTH suppression resolves within 6 months) occurs in 20–30% of total thyroidectomy patients. Permanent hypoparathyroidism (<1% in experienced hands) is a significant long-term morbidity. (3) Haemorrhage/haematoma — 1–2% incidence; can be life-threatening due to airway compression. Presents with neck swelling, tachycardia, and airway compromise within hours. Emergency management: immediate wound opening at the bedside (do not wait for theatre if patient is in respiratory distress). (4) Chyle leak — from thoracic duct injury during central/lateral neck dissection; managed conservatively (low-fat diet, octreotide) or with surgical ligation.

> **Post-thyroidectomy Hypocalcaemia — Recognition and Management** — Symptomatic hypocalcaemia after total thyroidectomy presents with perioral and digital paraesthesias, Chvostek's sign (facial muscle twitching on tapping the facial nerve anterior to the ear), Trousseau's sign (carpal spasm after 3 minutes of blood pressure cuff inflation above systolic pressure). Check serum calcium and PTH at 4–6h post-operatively. PTH <15 pg/mL at 4–6h predicts hypocalcaemia requiring supplementation with 96% sensitivity. Treatment: oral calcium carbonate 1–2g three times daily + calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, 0.25–0.5 mcg twice daily). IV calcium gluconate (10mL of 10%) for symptomatic tetany. Autotransplantation of devascularised parathyroid glands reduces permanent hypoparathyroidism rates.

> **Board Pearl — Chvostek vs Trousseau** — Chvostek's sign: tapping the facial nerve at the preauricular region causes ipsilateral facial muscle twitching — present in 10% of normocalcaemic individuals (low specificity). Trousseau's sign: inflating a blood pressure cuff to above systolic for 3 minutes causes carpal spasm (main d'accoucheur) — more specific for hypocalcaemia (99% specific, 94% sensitive). Trousseau's sign is more reliable clinically. Both signs indicate neuromuscular excitability from hypocalcaemia.


## Primary Hyperparathyroidism & Parathyroid Carcinoma

Primary hyperparathyroidism (PHPT) is the most common cause of hypercalcaemia in outpatients — caused by a single parathyroid adenoma in 85% of cases. Biochemistry: elevated serum calcium with inappropriately elevated or non-suppressed PTH. The majority of cases are asymptomatic, diagnosed incidentally. Symptomatic PHPT (nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis, osteoporosis with fragility fracture, neuromuscular symptoms, gastrointestinal symptoms) is an absolute indication for surgery. Asymptomatic PHPT surgical criteria (2022 revised guidelines): age <50, serum Ca >1mg/dL above ULN, creatinine clearance <60mL/min, T-score ≤−2.5, vertebral fracture on imaging, 24h urine calcium >400mg/day. Pre-operative localisation for minimally invasive parathyroidectomy (MIP): sestamibi scintigraphy (sensitivity 80%) ± 4D-CT (sensitivity 90%) ± ultrasound. Concordant localisation allows focused MIP with ioPTH monitoring rather than bilateral 4-gland exploration.

> **Board Pearl — Intraoperative PTH Monitoring (Miami Criteria)** — Intraoperative PTH (ioPTH) monitoring is the standard of care for targeted parathyroidectomy. The Miami criteria: ioPTH should fall >50% from the highest pre-excision level AND into the normal range within 10 minutes of gland removal. If these criteria are not met, additional gland exploration is required. ioPTH monitoring with pre-operative sestamibi ± 4D-CT localisation allows minimally invasive parathyroidectomy (MIP) — the operation of choice for single adenoma with concordant localisation.

Parathyroid carcinoma is rare (<1% of PHPT) but carries significant morbidity and mortality from uncontrolled hypercalcaemia. Clinical suspicion is raised by: (1) markedly elevated PTH (often >3–5× ULN); (2) serum calcium >14 mg/dL; (3) palpable neck mass (in a disease where adenomas are rarely palpable); (4) concurrent renal and bone disease. Pathological diagnosis requires evidence of vascular invasion, perineural invasion, or capsular invasion with fibrous bands — cytology and core biopsy cannot reliably distinguish carcinoma from adenoma. The HRPT2/CDC73 gene (parafibromin) mutation is present in 70–80% of parathyroid carcinomas and is a useful ancillary stain (loss of parafibromin immunohistochemistry). Surgical management: en bloc resection of the parathyroid tumour with ipsilateral thyroid lobe and isthmus and any adherent tissue — do not rupture the capsule (seeding causes local recurrence). Central neck dissection if nodes are involved. Recurrence occurs in 50% — re-resection is the only potentially curative option. Cinacalcet and denosumab for hypercalcaemia control in unresectable disease. Adjuvant radiotherapy has limited evidence but is used in high-risk cases.

> **Parathyroid Carcinoma — Red Flags and Surgical Principles** — Suspect parathyroid carcinoma when: PTH >3× ULN, Ca >14 mg/dL, palpable neck mass, concurrent nephropathy and bone disease. At operation: gland adheres to surrounding structures, grey-white colour, dense fibrous bands, firm consistency. Do NOT disrupt the capsule — en bloc resection with ipsilateral thyroid lobe mandatory. If carcinoma not suspected pre-operatively and capsule is ruptured: high risk of parathyromatosis (multiple implants in the neck). Molecular: HRPT2/CDC73 mutation in 70–80%; check in all patients with family history or recurrent PHPT.


## Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia Syndromes

Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) syndromes are autosomal dominant hereditary disorders characterised by neoplasia in multiple endocrine glands. Three classic syndromes: MEN1, MEN2A, and MEN2B. MEN1 (Wermer syndrome): mutation in the MEN1 gene (chromosome 11q13, encoding menin — a tumour suppressor). The '3 Ps': (1) Parathyroid: primary hyperparathyroidism in 90–100% (multigland hyperplasia, not single adenoma — 3.5-gland resection + autotransplantation recommended); (2) Pituitary: anterior pituitary adenoma (prolactinoma most common) in 30–40%; (3) Pancreas/duodenum: gastrinoma (ZES — Zollinger-Ellison syndrome) most common, insulinoma second. Gastrinoma in MEN1 is frequently multiple, in the 'gastrinoma triangle' (between porta hepatis, junction of cystic and common bile duct, and 2nd/3rd portion of duodenum). MEN2A (Sipple syndrome): RET proto-oncogene mutation. (1) Medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) — 100% penetrance; earliest manifestation; prophylactic thyroidectomy recommended; (2) Phaeochromocytoma — 50%; bilateral; must be excluded (24h urine metanephrines/catecholamines or plasma metanephrines) BEFORE thyroidectomy; (3) Primary hyperparathyroidism — 20–30%; typically single adenoma. MEN2B: RET M918T mutation — most aggressive MTC; mucosal neuromas (lips, tongue), marfanoid habitus, phaeochromocytoma; no hyperparathyroidism. MTC is more aggressive and develops earlier in MEN2B — prophylactic thyroidectomy in the first 6 months of life recommended.

> **Critical Rule — Phaeochromocytoma Before Thyroidectomy in MEN2** — In MEN2A/2B patients, ALWAYS screen for and TREAT phaeochromocytoma BEFORE performing thyroidectomy (or any elective surgery). Unrecognised phaeochromocytoma during surgical manipulation causes life-threatening hypertensive crisis. Biochemical screening: plasma free metanephrines (sensitivity >97%) or 24h urine fractionated metanephrines. If phaeochromocytoma identified: alpha-blockade (phenoxybenzamine) for 10–14 days → adrenalectomy → then thyroidectomy.

> **Board Pearl — MEN1 Parathyroid Surgery** — In MEN1-associated PHPT, the parathyroid disease is multiglandular hyperplasia — NOT a single adenoma. Standard parathyroidectomy (MIP targeting a single gland) will fail. The appropriate operation is 3.5-gland resection (subtotal parathyroidectomy) leaving a well-vascularised remnant of approximately 50mg, OR total parathyroidectomy with autotransplantation into the non-dominant forearm (allows easy access for reoperation by excising a forearm piece under local anaesthesia if recurrence occurs).


## Adrenal Tumours — Incidentaloma, Pheochromocytoma & ACC

The adrenal glands are paired retroperitoneal organs situated superomedially to each kidney. Right adrenal: pyramidal, closely associated with the right liver lobe and the IVC — the right adrenal vein drains directly into the IVC (short, wide, must be carefully ligated). Left adrenal: semilunar, medial to the left kidney — the left adrenal vein drains into the left renal vein. The adrenal cortex (yellow, derived from mesoderm) has three zones: (1) zona glomerulosa — outermost — produces aldosterone (regulated by angiotensin II and potassium); (2) zona fasciculata — middle — produces cortisol (regulated by ACTH); (3) zona reticularis — innermost — produces adrenal androgens (DHEA, androstenedione). The adrenal medulla (brown, derived from neural crest) produces catecholamines: adrenaline (80%), noradrenaline (20%), and small amounts of dopamine. During adrenalectomy, early ligation of the adrenal vein (before tumour manipulation) is critical for phaeochromocytoma to prevent catecholamine surge — 'no-touch' technique.

*Adrenal Incidentaloma — Evaluation and Management*
Condition | Biochemical Test | Imaging Feature | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Pheochromocytoma | 24h urine catecholamines/metanephrines; plasma metanephrines | Heterogeneous, high Hounsfield units, rich vascularity | Laparoscopic adrenalectomy; alpha-blockade first (phenoxybenzamine)
Cortisol-secreting adenoma (Cushing) | 1mg overnight DST; 24h urine cortisol | Homogeneous, <10 HU (lipid-rich) | Laparoscopic adrenalectomy; steroid cover post-op
Aldosteronoma (Conn) | Aldosterone:renin ratio >30; AVS | Unilateral adenoma on CT | Laparoscopic adrenalectomy (lateralised); spironolactone if bilateral
Non-functioning adenoma | None elevated | >10 HU or >4cm or growing | Resect if >4cm or growing >0.8cm/yr; observe <4cm stable
Adrenocortical carcinoma | Mixed hormonal excess | Heterogeneous, >6cm, calcification | Open adrenalectomy — laparoscopic contraindicated for ACC

Adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC) is a rare but highly aggressive malignancy — incidence 1–2 per million per year. ESE/ENSAT 2023 Guidelines: staging (I–IV) based on tumour size, local invasion, lymph node involvement, and distant metastasis. Key surgical principles: (1) Open adrenalectomy is MANDATORY for suspected ACC — laparoscopic approach contraindicated due to risk of capsule disruption and peritoneal seeding (Brix et al., Ann Surg 2010: laparoscopic ACC resection associated with 50% local recurrence at 2 years vs 23% with open). (2) R0 resection is the primary determinant of survival — ipsilateral nephrectomy or splenectomy if required for R0. (3) Lymph node dissection: ipsilateral retroperitoneal lymph node dissection recommended. (4) Adjuvant mitotane: for Stage II–III ACC after R0 resection — reduces recurrence rate (FIRM-ACT trial). Platinum-based chemotherapy + mitotane for metastatic disease. (5) Hereditary ACC associations: Li-Fraumeni syndrome (TP53), Lynch syndrome (MMR), MEN1 — genetic counselling in young patients (<40 years).

> **Board Pearl — Aldosterone:Renin Ratio (ARR)** — The aldosterone:renin ratio (ARR) is the primary screening test for primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn syndrome). ARR >30 (with aldosterone >15 ng/dL) has sensitivity 80% and specificity 74% for primary aldosteronism. After biochemical confirmation (saline suppression test or fludrocortisone suppression), CT is performed. Adrenal vein sampling (AVS) is the gold standard for lateralisation — unilateral excess (lateralisation index >4:1) = adrenalectomy; bilateral = medical management with mineralocorticoid antagonists (spironolactone or eplerenone). AVS is mandatory in patients >35 years as CT can miss small adenomas or microadenomas and incorrectly characterise bilateral hyperplasia as unilateral.


## Minimally Invasive Adrenalectomy

Laparoscopic adrenalectomy is the standard of care for most adrenal lesions ≤6–8cm (excluding adrenocortical carcinoma). Two main approaches: (1) Transabdominal lateral approach (most common): patient in lateral decubitus position, 3–4 ports placed below the costal margin. The liver (right) or spleen (left) is mobilised medially to expose the adrenal. Advantage: excellent visualisation, familiar anatomy. (2) Posterior retroperitoneoscopic adrenalectomy (PRA): patient in prone jack-knife position; 3 ports in the retroperitoneum posteriorly. Does not enter the peritoneal cavity. Advantages: faster, avoids adhesions in re-operative cases, lower complication rates in high-volume centres; preferred for bilateral adrenalectomy (patient stays prone for both sides). Indications for open adrenalectomy: (a) adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC) — laparoscopic approach risks capsule disruption and tumour seeding; (b) tumours >8–10cm; (c) extensive local invasion on imaging; (d) prior abdominal surgery preventing safe laparoscopic access. Bilateral adrenalectomy (for ACTH-independent Cushing's or phaeochromocytoma in MEN2): Nelson's syndrome can develop after bilateral adrenalectomy for Cushing's disease if the pituitary adenoma is not subsequently treated — loss of cortisol feedback leads to extreme ACTH elevation and pituitary tumour expansion (hyperpigmentation, visual field loss).

> **Common Pitfall — Pheochromocytoma: Alpha-Block Before Surgery** — Failure to administer adequate pre-operative alpha-adrenergic blockade before phaeochromocytoma surgery causes life-threatening hypertensive crises during tumour manipulation. Protocol: phenoxybenzamine (non-competitive alpha blocker) for minimum 10–14 days pre-operatively; beta-blockade only AFTER alpha-blockade is established (never before — unopposed alpha causes severe hypertension). High sodium + fluid intake to expand contracted intravascular volume. Anaesthesia team must be briefed. Target pre-operative blood pressure <130/80 with no episode of SBP >160.

> **Board Pearl — Adrenal Myelolipoma and Incidentaloma Surveillance** — Adrenal myelolipoma is a benign, non-functioning tumour containing fat and haematopoietic tissue — pathognomonic CT appearance of macroscopic fat (<-30 Hounsfield units). No biochemical workup is needed after CT confirms fat content — it is benign. Observation is appropriate unless symptomatic or >6cm (risk of rupture). Incidentaloma surveillance: for non-functioning adenomas <4cm: repeat CT at 6–12 months and 2 years; repeat biochemical evaluation (1mg DST, plasma metanephrines) at 12 months. If no growth and biochemically stable after 2 years: no further follow-up required per Endocrine Society 2021 guidelines.


## Gastroenteropancreatic Neuroendocrine Tumours (GEP-NETs)

Neuroendocrine tumours (NETs) arise from enterochromaffin and neuroendocrine cells distributed throughout the gastrointestinal tract and pancreas. Incidence is increasing (5–7 per 100,000/year) due to improved imaging detection. GEP-NETs are classified by WHO 2022/ENETS 2023 criteria: Grade 1 (G1): Ki-67 <3%, mitotic count <2/2mm²; Grade 2 (G2): Ki-67 3–20%, mitotic count 2–20/2mm²; Grade 3 (G3): Ki-67 >20%, mitotic count >20/2mm²; Neuroendocrine Carcinoma (NEC): poorly differentiated, Ki-67 often >55%, small-cell or large-cell type. G1/G2 NETs have indolent biology and are amenable to curative resection even in the presence of liver metastasis. NEC behaves like small-cell carcinoma — chemotherapy (platinum-etoposide) is the primary treatment.

*GEP-NET Classification — WHO 2022 / ENETS 2023*
Grade | Ki-67 Index | Mitotic Count | Behaviour | Primary Treatment
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
G1 NET | <3% | <2/2mm² | Indolent; low metastatic potential | Surgical resection ± somatostatin analogues (SSA)
G2 NET | 3–20% | 2–20/2mm² | Intermediate; metastasis common for pancreatic | Surgical resection; SSA; PRRT if progressive
G3 NET | >20% | >20/2mm² | Aggressive; well-differentiated morphology still | Surgical resection if feasible; consider PRRT vs chemo
NEC (small-cell) | >55% | Variable | Very aggressive; like small-cell lung cancer | Platinum-etoposide chemotherapy; surgery rarely indicated

Small intestinal NETs (SI-NETs / carcinoid tumours): Arise from enterochromaffin cells in the ileum and proximal jejunum. Most common GEP-NET. Produce serotonin (5-HT), tachykinins, and other vasoactive substances. Carcinoid syndrome (flushing, diarrhoea, bronchospasm) occurs ONLY when vasoactive substances reach the systemic circulation — i.e., when hepatic metastases are present (bypassing first-pass liver metabolism) or in primary tumours with direct systemic drainage (lung, ovary). Carcinoid crisis during anaesthesia (life-threatening flushing, hypotension, bronchospasm): treated with IV octreotide 500mcg bolus. Carcinoid heart disease: right-sided valvular disease (tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonary stenosis) from serotonin-induced fibrosis — assess with echocardiography pre-operatively in metastatic disease. ENETS 2023 staging for SI-NETs: T1 (tumour ≤1cm, confined to submucosa/muscularis); T2 (>1cm or invades muscularis); T3 (invades subserosa or mesentery ≤3mm); T4 (perforates peritoneum, mesentery >3mm, other organs). Surgical management: right hemicolectomy for appendiceal and distal ileal tumours; formal oncological resection with wide mesenteric dissection and lymphadenectomy (desmoplastic mesenteric reaction from SI-NETs causes intestinal ischaemia — a key reason to resect even in metastatic disease). Appendiceal carcinoids <2cm: appendicectomy alone is curative (recurrence <2%). Appendiceal NETs 1–2cm: right hemicolectomy if: mesoappendix invasion, lymphovascular invasion, or G2 grade.

Pancreatic NETs (PanNETs): May be functioning (hormone-producing, causing specific syndromes) or non-functioning (majority). Functioning PanNETs: (1) Insulinoma — most common functioning PanNET; produces insulin causing hypoglycaemia (Whipple's triad: symptoms during fasting, blood glucose <40mg/dL, symptoms relieved by glucose); 90% benign; 90% solitary; equally distributed throughout the pancreas. Diagnosis: 72-hour fast with glucose, insulin, and C-peptide sampling. Localisation: EUS (sensitivity 90%), CT, somatostatin-receptor scintigraphy (insulinomas are often SSTR-negative; 68Ga-DOTATATE PET less reliable). Treatment: enucleation or distal pancreatectomy. (2) Gastrinoma — second most common; produces gastrin causing ZES (recurrent peptic ulceration, diarrhoea, oesophagitis); 60% malignant; 80% in the gastrinoma triangle; 25% associated with MEN1 (then multiple, duodenal). Diagnosis: fasting gastrin >10× ULN + gastric pH <2. Localisation: EUS + CT + somatostatin scintigraphy (SRS). Treatment: proton pump inhibitors (symptom control); resection for sporadic solitary gastrinomas; MEN1 gastrinomas often not cured by surgery (multiple/duodenal). (3) VIPoma: Verner-Morrison syndrome (WDHA: watery diarrhoea, hypokalaemia, achlorhydria); resect if localised. (4) Glucagonoma: necrolytic migratory erythema, diabetes, weight loss; large tumours at presentation, usually malignant.

*Functioning PanNETs — Clinical Syndrome Summary*
Tumour | Hormone | Syndrome | Malignancy | Surgery
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Insulinoma | Insulin | Hypoglycaemia, Whipple's triad | 10% malignant | Enucleation or distal pancreatectomy
Gastrinoma | Gastrin | Zollinger-Ellison — recurrent ulceration | 60% malignant | Resection if sporadic/solitary; PPI for MEN1
VIPoma | VIP | WDHA syndrome (pancreatic cholera) | 50–60% | Resect if localised; octreotide + IVF pre-op

Advanced NETs — systemic and locoregional therapies: (1) Somatostatin analogues (SSA): octreotide LAR and lanreotide — control carcinoid syndrome AND have antiproliferative effect (PROMID and CLARINET trials — significant improvement in PFS for G1/G2 SI-NETs and PanNETs). First-line for G1/G2 metastatic NETs. (2) Peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT): 177Lutetium-DOTATATE — for SSTR-positive G1/G2 progressive metastatic NETs. NETTER-1 trial (NEJM 2017): 177Lu-DOTATATE vs octreotide LAR — significant improvement in PFS (NR vs 8.4 months, HR 0.21). PRRT requires positive 68Ga-DOTATATE PET scan (SSTR expression). (3) Everolimus (mTOR inhibitor): RADIANT-3 (PanNETs) and RADIANT-4 (lung/GI NETs) trials — significant PFS improvement. (4) Sunitinib (TKI): SUN1111 trial — for progressive PanNETs, PFS 11.4 vs 5.5 months vs placebo. (5) Liver-directed therapy: hepatic artery embolisation/chemoembolisation/radioembolisation (TARE) for hepatic-dominant metastatic disease. Liver resection/ablation where feasible (debulking — 90% of liver tumour burden removal improves symptoms and survival in G1/G2). (6) NEC (poorly differentiated): platinum-etoposide chemotherapy — ORR 50% but short-lived. Temozolomide + capecitabine (TEMCAP) for G2/G3 well-differentiated PanNETs.

> **Board Pearl — 68Ga-DOTATATE PET and PRRT Eligibility** — 68Ga-DOTATATE PET-CT (somatostatin receptor scintigraphy) is the staging investigation of choice for G1/G2 NETs — superior to conventional CT/MRI and traditional 111In-pentetreotide (Octreoscan) in sensitivity and spatial resolution. Positive DOTATATE scan = SSTR-positive tumour = eligible for SSA therapy AND potentially PRRT (177Lu-DOTATATE, Lutathera). PRRT should only be used in patients with SSTR-positive lesions AND adequate renal function (GFR >30–50 mL/min) — 177Lu has renal tubular uptake. PanNETs and insulinomas are often SSTR-negative — check DOTATATE before planning PRRT.

> **Carcinoid Crisis — Anaesthetic Emergency** — Carcinoid crisis (life-threatening release of serotonin and vasoactive substances during anaesthesia/tumour manipulation) presents as severe hypotension or hypertension, bronchospasm, tachycardia, and flushing. Prevention: IV octreotide infusion (50–100 mcg/h) throughout the peri-operative period for metastatic carcinoid. Treatment of crisis: IV octreotide 500mcg bolus (not adrenaline — can paradoxically worsen vasoactive release). Avoid catecholamines, succinylcholine, histamine-releasing drugs, and morphine. Pre-operative echo to exclude carcinoid heart disease (right-sided valvular disease); consider cardiac surgical consultation if severe tricuspid/pulmonary valve disease.
