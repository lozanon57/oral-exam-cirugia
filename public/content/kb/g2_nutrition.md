## Nutritional Assessment

Malnutrition affects 30–50% of surgical patients on admission and is independently associated with increased surgical morbidity, mortality, and length of stay. Systematic nutritional screening identifies at-risk patients who require nutritional support before and after surgery.

Screening tools: (1) NRS-2002 (Nutritional Risk Screening 2002 — Kondrup et al.): validated for hospitalised adults. Combines nutritional status score (0–3: weight loss, BMI, reduced food intake) with disease severity score (0–3: severity of metabolic stress — minor/moderate/severe illness). An age factor (+1 for age ≥70 years) is added. Total score ≥3 = at nutritional risk → nutritional support plan required. Score <3 = weekly rescreening. (2) MUST (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool): primarily for community and primary care. BMI <20 = 2 points; BMI 20–20.9 = 1 point; unplanned weight loss >10% = 2 points; 5–10% = 1 point; acute disease effect (nil by mouth or likely for >5 days) = 2 points. Score 0 = low risk; 1 = medium risk (monitor); ≥2 = high risk (nutritional support). (3) MNA-SF (Mini Nutritional Assessment — Short Form): validated for elderly patients.

Body composition and sarcopenia: BMI has significant limitations as a nutritional marker — obese patients can be sarcopenic (reduced muscle mass, 'sarcopenic obesity'). Body composition assessment: DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry — gold standard for muscle/fat mass), CT muscle mass quantification at the level of the 3rd lumbar vertebra (L3). Sarcopenia is defined by CT as: L3 Skeletal Muscle Index (SMI) <52.4 cm²/m² in men, <38.9 cm²/m² in women. Low L3 SMI is associated with worse surgical outcomes, higher complication rates, and reduced chemotherapy tolerance in cancer patients.

Biochemical markers: albumin (normal 35–50 g/L) — IMPORTANT CAVEAT: albumin is an ACUTE PHASE REACTANT, not a direct nutritional marker. In inflammation/infection/surgery, albumin falls rapidly due to: (1) increased capillary permeability (transudation to extravascular space), (2) reduced hepatic synthesis (acute-phase proteins preferentially synthesised), (3) dilutional effect. Low albumin reflects severity of inflammatory response, not nutritional depletion per se. Pre-albumin (transthyretin, normal 200–400 mg/L) has a shorter half-life (2 days vs 20 days for albumin) and is a better short-term nutritional marker, but also falls in inflammation. Hand-grip strength (dynamometry): functional marker of nutritional status; reduced strength predicts poor surgical outcomes and is a component of frailty assessments.

> **Albumin is NOT a Nutritional Marker in Acute Settings** — Commonly misinterpreted: low albumin in an acutely ill patient does NOT mean the patient is malnourished — it means they have an acute inflammatory response. Albumin half-life is 20 days; acute surgical inflammation causes rapid fall within 24–48 hours even in normally nourished patients. Use albumin as a marker of inflammatory severity (and as prognostic tool for overall illness severity and mortality risk) — NOT as a target for nutritional correction. Providing albumin infusions to correct low albumin in critically ill patients has no evidence of benefit (SAFE trial).


## Metabolic Response to Surgery

The metabolic response to surgery involves two distinct phases, described by Cuthbertson (1932): (1) Ebb phase: immediately post-injury/surgery — hypometabolism, reduced cardiac output, hypothermia, decreased oxygen consumption. Duration: hours. Purpose: shunt resources to vital organs. (2) Flow phase: subsequent hypermetabolic response — increased metabolic rate, catabolism, protein breakdown, hyperglycaemia. Duration: days to weeks depending on insult magnitude. The flow phase is mediated by counter-regulatory hormones (cortisol, glucagon, catecholamines) and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) released from the surgical wound.

Protein catabolism: major surgery causes protein breakdown of 75–150 g/day (equivalent to 300–600 g lean muscle mass). Adequate protein supplementation (1.5–2.0 g/kg/day) is required to limit catabolism. Insufficient protein → impaired wound healing, immune dysfunction, diaphragm weakness (prolongs ventilation), skin breakdown.

Hyperglycaemia management: surgical stress causes insulin resistance and hyperglycaemia (even in non-diabetic patients). NICE-SUGAR trial (2009, NEJM): intensive insulin therapy targeting glucose 4.5–6.0 mmol/L INCREASED mortality compared to conventional management (target 6–10 mmol/L) in ICU patients — largely due to hypoglycaemic events (severe hypoglycaemia more than doubled in intensive arm: 6.8% vs 0.5%). Current consensus: target blood glucose 6–10 mmol/L in ICU/perioperative setting. Avoid hypoglycaemia (<4 mmol/L) — more harmful than moderate hyperglycaemia. Continuous insulin infusion monitored hourly in ICU.

Immunonutrition: specific nutrients that modulate immune function. Arginine: promotes T-cell function, wound healing, nitric oxide synthesis. Omega-3 fatty acids: reduce pro-inflammatory eicosanoids, downregulate IL-1/TNF. Glutamine: primary fuel for enterocytes and immune cells. Evidence: ESPEN 2017 surgical guidelines — oral immunonutrition (arginine + omega-3 + nucleotides) for 5–7 days preoperatively recommended for malnourished patients undergoing major upper GI surgery (Level IB). Controversy exists for ICU glutamine — see Section 6.

## Enteral Nutrition

The fundamental principle: 'if the gut works, use it.' Enteral nutrition (EN) is preferred over parenteral nutrition (PN) when the gastrointestinal tract is functional. EN preserves gut mucosal integrity, prevents bacterial translocation, maintains gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), avoids catheter-related complications, and is significantly cheaper. ESPEN 2019 ICU guidelines: if the gut is functional, start EN within 24–48 hours of ICU admission (after haemodynamic stabilisation).

Enteral access routes: (1) Nasogastric tube (NGT): for short-term (<4–6 weeks) EN in patients with intact gag reflex and low aspiration risk. Confirm position by pH testing (aspirate pH <5.5) and/or CXR (if pH equivocal). (2) Nasojejunal tube (NJT): for patients with high aspiration risk or gastroparesis — post-pyloric feeding. Requires fluoroscopic or endoscopic placement. (3) Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy (PEG): for long-term (>4–6 weeks) EN in patients who cannot swallow (neurological, head/neck cancer). Contraindications: inability to safely transilluminate (obese, prior gastric surgery), coagulopathy (INR >1.5, Plt <50), ascites, active GI tract disease at insertion site, peritoneal dialysis. (4) PEJ (Percutaneous Endoscopic Jejunostomy) or surgical jejunostomy: for patients with gastric outlet obstruction, gastroparesis, or post-oesophagectomy/gastrectomy.

EN formula choice: standard polymeric (1 kcal/mL, 40g protein/L — for general use); high-protein (1.5 kcal/mL, 75g protein/L — post-surgical, ICU catabolism); semi-elemental/elemental (hydrolysed protein — for short bowel syndrome, severe pancreatitis, malabsorption); disease-specific (renal formula — low K+, low phosphate; hepatic formula; diabetic formula — low glycaemic index). Standard formula is appropriate for most surgical patients.

EN complications: (1) Aspiration: prevent by 30–45° head-up positioning, monitoring gastric residual volumes (GRVs — stop EN if GRV >200–500 mL depending on protocol). (2) Tube misplacement: into the bronchial tree — ALWAYS confirm tube position before starting EN (pH <5.5 or CXR confirmation). (3) Refeeding syndrome: start EN at low rate in at-risk patients. (4) Diarrhoea: common (≥3 loose stools/day) — check for C. diff; consider semi-elemental formula; review medications (sorbitol-containing liquid meds, prokinetics). (5) Constipation: increase fluid, fibre-containing formula.

Route | Duration | Indications | Contraindications | Complications
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---

> **PEG Contraindications — Board Points** — Absolute contraindications to PEG: (1) Inability to safely transilluminate the anterior abdominal wall (obesity, previous surgery, liver enlargement — no 'safe window'). (2) Peritoneal dialysis — risk of peritoneal contamination. (3) Active systemic infection/sepsis at time of procedure. (4) Uncorrectable coagulopathy (INR >1.5 or Plt <50). (5) Obstruction or severe oesophageal disease preventing endoscope passage. Relative contraindications: ascites, bowel overlying the stomach on CT, prior gastric surgery. If PEG is not feasible: consider PEG-J (PEG with jejunal extension), surgical jejunostomy, or long-term NJT.


## Parenteral Nutrition

Parenteral nutrition (PN) delivers nutrients intravenously, bypassing the GI tract. PN is indicated when the gut is non-functional or inaccessible for a sufficient duration. Indications: (1) Intestinal failure (short bowel syndrome, enterocutaneous fistula with high output, small bowel obstruction without resolution, radiation enteritis); (2) Non-functional gut for >7 days post-operatively (prolonged ileus, GI malabsorption); (3) Pre-operative nutritional support when gut is unavailable and surgery cannot be delayed >7 days; (4) Supplemental PN when EN alone cannot meet ≥60% of requirements after 3–7 days of EN attempts.

Central vs peripheral PN: Central PN (CPN) — osmolality >900 mOsm/L (standard PN); requires central venous catheter (CVC) — PICC, tunnelled line (Hickman), or non-tunnelled (Groshong). Peripheral PN — osmolality ≤900 mOsm/L (diluted formulation); administered via peripheral venous cannula; shorter duration (7–10 days maximum — risk of thrombophlebitis); lower caloric density. Most hospital PN is central.

PN components: (1) Dextrose (glucose) — primary energy substrate (3.4 kcal/g); provides 50–60% of non-protein calories. (2) Amino acids — protein source (4 kcal/g); 1.5–2.0 g/kg/day for surgical/ICU patients. (3) Lipid emulsion (IVFE) — provides essential fatty acids + energy (9 kcal/g; fish oil-enriched emulsions [SMOFlipid] preferred to reduce hepatotoxicity). (4) Electrolytes — Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, phosphate. (5) Vitamins — water-soluble (B1, B2, B6, B12, folate, C) and fat-soluble (A, D, E, K). (6) Trace elements — zinc, selenium, copper, manganese, chromium. Deficiency of any component causes specific complications (B1 deficiency → Wernicke's encephalopathy in the refeeding syndrome context; selenium deficiency → cardiomyopathy).

PN-associated liver disease (PNALD / IFALD — intestinal failure-associated liver disease): cholestasis, steatosis, and eventually cirrhosis with long-term PN. Risk factors: excess glucose (overfeeding), soybean oil-based lipid emulsions (phytosterols), taurine and choline deficiency, small bowel bacterial overgrowth. Prevention: fish oil-based IVFE (SMOFlipid — reduced hepatotoxicity in RCT); cycling PN (16–18h on, 6–8h off — allows hepatic lipid clearance); avoid overfeeding; trace elements; treat bacterial overgrowth; maximise EN even small volumes.

Central Line-Associated Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI) prevention bundle: hand hygiene, chlorhexidine skin antisepsis, maximal sterile barrier precautions during insertion, optimal catheter site selection (subclavian preferred over femoral), daily review of line necessity (remove when no longer needed), alcohol port scrubbing before access. CLABSI rate target: <1 per 1000 catheter-days.

> **PN-Associated Liver Disease — Prevention and Recognition** — PNALD/IFALD: suspect when ALT/AST rises >3× ULN or ALP/GGT rises progressively on PN. Early: fatty infiltration (reversible); late: fibrosis/cirrhosis (irreversible). Prevention: (1) Fish oil-enriched lipid emulsion (SMOFlipid or Omegaven); (2) Avoid overfeeding — target 25–30 kcal/kg/day; (3) Cycle PN 16–18h/day with 6–8h rest; (4) Maximise enteral intake even small volumes (stimulates bile flow, CCK); (5) Treat SBBO (small bowel bacterial overgrowth) with antibiotics; (6) Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) — liver-protective; (7) Liver transplantation in end-stage IFALD (combined intestinal-liver transplant for intestinal failure).


## Refeeding Syndrome

Refeeding syndrome (RFS) is a potentially life-threatening metabolic complication of recommencing nutrition after a period of prolonged starvation or severe underfeeding. Pathophysiology: during starvation, cells deplete intracellular phosphate (and potassium, magnesium) — these are maintained in the normal range in the blood by homeostatic mechanisms. When carbohydrates are reintroduced, insulin surges → drives glucose + phosphate + potassium + magnesium INTO cells → RAPID DROP in plasma levels → electrolyte derangements.

Hypophosphataemia is the hallmark of RFS (phosphate <0.50 mmol/L = severe). Clinical consequences: severe hypophosphataemia → muscle weakness (diaphragmatic failure, respiratory arrest), haemolytic anaemia, platelet dysfunction, cardiac arrhythmias (fatal), seizures, delirium. Hypokalaemia → cardiac arrhythmias. Hypomagnesaemia → arrhythmias, tetany. Thiamine (B1) deficiency → Wernicke's encephalopathy (ataxia, ophthalmoplegia, confusion) + Korsakoff syndrome.

At-risk criteria (NICE CG32 — one or more major risk factors, or two or more minor factors): Major: BMI <16 kg/m², unintentional weight loss >15% in 3–6 months, little or no nutritional intake >10 days, low levels of K+, phosphate, or Mg2+ before feeding. Minor: BMI 16–18.5, unintentional weight loss 10–15% in 3–6 months, little or no nutritional intake >5 days, history of alcohol abuse, drug therapies (insulin, antacids, diuretics, chemotherapy).

NICE refeeding guidelines: 'Start LOW, go SLOW.' (1) Begin feeding at 10 kcal/kg/day (or 5 kcal/kg/day in extreme risk — BMI <14 or negligible intake >15 days). (2) Increase by 33% every 2–3 days over 4–7 days to reach full energy target. (3) MANDATORY thiamine replacement: Pabrinex IV × 30 minutes BEFORE starting any carbohydrate-containing fluids (even glucose for IV drugs). Continue B-complex vitamins daily for minimum 10 days. (4) Monitor electrolytes: phosphate, potassium, magnesium — check daily for first week; correct deficits BEFORE restarting nutrition if levels drop below threshold. (5) Restrict sodium and fluid (risk of fluid overload from aldosterone activation). (6) Monitor cardiac rhythm (ECG) in high-risk patients.

*Refeeding Syndrome — At-Risk Criteria (NICE CG32)*
Risk Level | Criteria | Action
--- | --- | ---
HIGH RISK (≥1 major factor) | BMI <16; weight loss >15% in 3–6 months; intake negligible >10 days; low K+/phosphate/Mg2+ before feeding | Start at 5–10 kcal/kg/day; thiamine before feeding; daily electrolytes; cardiac monitoring
AT RISK (≥2 minor factors) | BMI 16–18.5; weight loss 10–15%; intake negligible >5 days; history of alcohol; drugs (insulin/diuretics/chemotherapy) | Start at 10 kcal/kg/day; thiamine before feeding; electrolytes 3×/week

> **Thiamine BEFORE Carbohydrates — Non-Negotiable** — Give Pabrinex IV (thiamine + B vitamins) BEFORE any glucose/carbohydrate — including IV glucose for drugs. Glucose metabolism requires thiamine (B1) as a cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase. In a thiamine-deficient patient, glucose administration causes pyruvate to accumulate → lactic acidosis + Wernicke's encephalopathy (classic triad: confusion, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia — often incomplete). Pabrinex 2 pairs IV infused over 30 minutes before any nutrition is started. Oral thiamine (e.g., 100 mg TDS) is not absorbed reliably in malnourished patients — use IV Pabrinex.


## Nutrition in Specific Surgical Conditions

Upper GI surgery: post-oesophagectomy nutrition is a complex challenge. Standard ERAS after oesophagectomy includes: surgical feeding jejunostomy placed intraoperatively (standard practice at most centres — provides reliable early post-op EN route). EN via jejunostomy starts within 6–12 hours of surgery. Oral diet trialled from Day 3–5 (after anastomotic contrast swallow confirming no leak). If oral diet tolerated, jejunostomy feeding continues until oral intake >60% of requirements (usually Day 7–10), then jejunostomy can be removed at 6 weeks if nutritional intake is adequate. Water-soluble swallow study (gastrografin) on Day 5–7 to confirm anastomotic integrity before introducing free oral fluids.

Colorectal surgery (ERAS): oral diet starts within 4 hours of laparoscopic colectomy. Nasogastric tubes are NOT routinely used post-operatively and should be removed on awakening. Routine feeding jejunostomy is NOT indicated for colonic resection — reserved for: re-operative cases with poor anastomosis healing risk, high-output fistula risk, or when prolonged post-op ileus is expected. Encourage oral diet as key ERAS element — high compliance reduces ileus.

Pancreatic surgery (ERAS): ERAS Society guideline — early oral diet by postoperative Day 1 is recommended and safe after pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple) and distal pancreatectomy. Nasojejunal tube feeding or oral nutritional supplements used if oral intake insufficient. Routine pre-op jejunostomy is NOT recommended — reserve for malnourished patients (NRS-2002 ≥5 or weight loss >10%) or re-operative cases.

Liver surgery: post-hepatectomy energy requirements are increased due to glycogen loss and regenerative demands. Early oral diet (ERAS Day 1–2). Branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) supplementation may benefit patients with Child B/C cirrhosis post-resection (BCAA protects hepatic encephalopathy risk). Glucose monitoring essential after major hepatectomy (risk of hypoglycaemia from loss of glycogen stores).

Critical illness — permissive underfeeding: the PERMIT trial and SPN-ICU data support 'permissive underfeeding' (feeding 40–60% of calculated energy target) in early ICU admission (Days 1–3) — targeting full nutrition from Day 4–7. Reason: early overfeeding causes hyperglycaemia, increased CO2 production, and PN-related complications; the acute phase of illness is characterised by auto-catabolism (endogenous substrate mobilisation) which may actually be beneficial. Full protein target (1.5–2.0 g/kg) from Day 1 is recommended despite underfeeding of energy. The PERMIT trial (2015): low vs standard caloric targets in ICU — low caloric group had similar 90-day mortality but LESS hyperglycaemia and hypoglycaemia.

**Ensayo de referencia — CALORIES Trial — Enteral vs Parenteral Nutrition in ICU:**

> **Glutamine in ICU — Evidence Reversal (ESPEN 2023 Confirmed)** — Glutamine was the archetypal immunonutrient: primary fuel for enterocytes and immune cells; conditionally essential in critical illness (endogenous synthesis insufficient). Early RCTs showed benefit. However: REDOXS trial (NEJM 2013 — Heyland): high-dose enteral + parenteral glutamine (0.35 g/kg/day IV) in critically ill patients with multi-organ failure INCREASED 28-day mortality (32.4% vs 27.2%; p=0.05). METAPLUS trial: similar findings. **ESPEN 2023 position (confirmed)**: glutamine should NOT be given routinely to critically ill patients with organ failure or haemodynamic instability. Parenteral glutamine 0.2–0.4 g/kg/day may be appropriate in stable ICU patients on PN without organ failure. **ESPEN 2023 key updates for surgical patients:** (1) **Immunonutrition** (arginine + omega-3 + RNA): Grade A for malnourished patients undergoing major GI cancer surgery (5–7 days preoperatively + postoperative); Grade B for well-nourished patients undergoing major upper GI surgery; NOT recommended for well-nourished colorectal surgery patients. (2) **NRS-2002 screening** is mandatory for ALL surgical patients at admission — NRS-2002 ≥3 triggers mandatory nutritional assessment and may justify delaying elective surgery for 7–14 days of nutritional optimisation (Grade A). (3) **IV omega-3 enriched lipid emulsions** (fish oil-based) preferred over pure soybean oil when parenteral nutrition required perioperatively (Grade A — reduced infectious complications).
