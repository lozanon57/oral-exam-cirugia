## Neonatal Surgical Emergencies

Neonatal surgical emergencies require rapid diagnosis and a coordinated multidisciplinary approach. The most important neonatal intestinal obstructions are: duodenal atresia, jejunoileal atresia, malrotation/volvulus, Hirschsprung's disease, and anorectal malformations.

Duodenal atresia: failure of recanalisation of the duodenal lumen (8th–10th week). Associated with Down's syndrome (trisomy 21) in 30% of cases. Classic radiographic finding: 'double bubble sign' — gas in stomach and first part of duodenum, absent distal gas. Presentation: bilious vomiting within first 24 hours of life (obstruction is usually post-ampullary — 80% distal to ampulla of Vater). Diagnosis: AXR (double bubble). Management: NGT decompression, IV fluids, correct electrolytes, echocardiogram (25% have cardiac defects), then elective duodenoduodenostomy (diamond anastomosis) once stabilised. Annular pancreas is a differential causing duodenal obstruction — same surgical management.

Malrotation and volvulus: the midgut normally rotates 270° counterclockwise around the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) between weeks 4–10. Malrotation = incomplete rotation, leaving the caecum in the right upper quadrant and Ladd's bands crossing the duodenum. Volvulus = clockwise twist of the midgut around the narrow SMA pedicle — the most urgent neonatal surgical emergency. Presentation: bilious vomiting (bilious vomiting in a neonate = emergency until proven otherwise), upper abdominal distension. Upper GI contrast study (gold standard): corkscrew appearance of duodenum/jejunum; failure of DJ flexure to cross midline. Ultrasound: SMA/SMV inversion ('whirlpool sign'). Management: immediate resuscitation, IV antibiotics, urgent laparotomy (Ladd's procedure — do not delay for hydration if haemodynamically stable).

*Ladd's Procedure — Steps*
Step | Action | Purpose
--- | --- | ---
1. Eviscerate bowel | Exteriorise entire small bowel to expose the SMA pedicle | Assess viability and extent of volvulus
2. Detorse volvulus | Rotate bowel COUNTERCLOCKWISE (anticlockwise) to detorse | Restore SMA flow; allow reperfusion — assess bowel viability
3. Ladd's bands division | Divide peritoneal bands (Ladd's bands) crossing duodenum from caecum/right colon to retroperitoneum | Relieve duodenal obstruction
4. Broaden the mesenteric base | Divide peritoneum at base of mesentery to widen the SMA pedicle | Prevent recurrent volvulus
5. Appendicectomy | Remove appendix (incidental) | Prevents future diagnostic confusion (appendix now in LEFT iliac fossa)
6. Reposition bowel | Small bowel to RIGHT, large bowel to LEFT — no attempt to achieve normal rotation | Stable anatomical positioning

> **Bilious Vomiting in a Neonate = Emergency** — Any neonate with bilious (green) vomiting must be assumed to have malrotation with midgut volvulus until proven otherwise. Time from volvulus to bowel infarction can be <6 hours. Delay for investigations beyond essential resuscitation is inappropriate. Immediate upper GI contrast study (if haemodynamically stable) or direct to theatre (if in extremis). The entire midgut can become non-viable, condemning the child to short bowel syndrome or death.

Hirschsprung's disease: failure of neural crest cell migration to the distal colon, resulting in an aganglionic segment of variable length (80% rectosigmoid; 5% total colonic). Presents as delayed passage of meconium (>48h after birth), abdominal distension, bilious vomiting. Diagnosis: suction rectal biopsy (gold standard) — shows ABSENCE of ganglion cells in the submucosal and myenteric plexuses, with hypertrophied nerve bundles. AXR: dilated proximal colon with narrow aganglionic segment. Contrast enema: 'transition zone' between dilated proximal bowel and narrow distal aganglionic segment. Management: bowel rest, rectal washouts, colostomy if obstructed → definitive pull-through procedure (Swenson, Soave, or Duhamel — laparoscopic approaches now standard). Enterocolitis (Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis — HAEC) is the most dangerous complication: fever, distension, explosive diarrhoea → rectal washouts, antibiotics, decompression.

NEC (Necrotising Enterocolitis): predominantly affects premature infants (<32 weeks). Multifactorial: ischaemia, bacterial translocation, immature gut immunity. Bell's staging: Stage I (suspected) = apnoea, temperature instability, ileus; Stage IIA (proven) = pneumatosis intestinalis on AXR, blood in stools; Stage IIB = metabolic acidosis, thrombocytopenia; Stage IIIA (advanced) = deteriorating, peritonitis but no perforation; Stage IIIB = perforation (pneumoperitoneum on AXR). Medical management (Stages I–IIA): NBM, NGT, broad-spectrum antibiotics (48–72h), TPN. Surgical indications: pneumoperitoneum (IIIB), fixed dilated bowel loop (>24h unchanged), clinical deterioration despite maximum medical therapy, abdominal wall erythema. Surgical options: peritoneal drain (primary for very small premature infants <1000g) vs laparotomy + resection (preferred if stable enough). The NECTAR trial (ongoing) is comparing these approaches.

Abdominal wall defects: gastroschisis = herniation of bowel through a RIGHT paraumbilical wall defect (umbilical cord is normal, to the LEFT of the defect); no peritoneal sac; no major associated anomalies; bowel is thickened/matted from amniotic fluid exposure. Omphalocele (exomphalos) = herniation through the umbilical ring, covered by a sac (amnion + peritoneum); umbilical cord inserts into the apex of the sac; ASSOCIATED with major anomalies in 60–70% (cardiac, chromosomal — Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome, Down's, Patau's). Management of gastroschisis: emergency silo (spring-loaded or sutured) with gradual daily reduction → primary closure or staged closure; intestinal function recovery may take weeks. Omphalocele: avoid sac rupture, staged closure; treat associated anomalies first.

*Bell's Staging — Necrotising Enterocolitis*
Stage | Systemic | Intestinal | Radiological | Management
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
IA — Suspected NEC | Temperature instability, apnoea, bradycardia | Distension, vomiting, blood in stools | Normal or mild ileus | NBM 48h, antibiotics, TPN, frequent AXR
IIA — Proven NEC (mild) | As above | Absent bowel sounds, abdominal tenderness | Pneumatosis intestinalis | NBM 7–10 days, antibiotics, TPN
IIB — Proven NEC (moderate) | Mild metabolic acidosis, mild thrombocytopenia | Abdominal wall oedema/cellulitis | Pneumatosis, portal venous gas | As IIA + ICU monitoring
IIIA — Advanced NEC (no perforation) | Mixed acidosis, DIC, oliguria, hypotension | Peritonitis, marked tenderness | Marked pneumatosis, ascites | ICU maximum support; surgical consultation; may require urgent operation
IIIB — Advanced NEC (perforation) | Shock, DIC | Peritonitis, erythema | Pneumoperitoneum | SURGICAL — peritoneal drain vs laparotomy

> **Gastroschisis vs Omphalocele — Key Distinction** — Gastroschisis: PARA-umbilical defect (right side), NO sac, bowel exposed directly (thickened/foreshortened), umbilical cord SEPARATE and normal, NO associated anomalies (isolated defect). Omphalocele: UMBILICAL ring defect, sac PRESENT (amnion-peritoneum), cord inserts into sac apex, ASSOCIATED anomalies in 60–70% (cardiac, chromosomal, Beckwith-Wiedemann). Mnemonic: Gastroschisis = Gap in abdominal wall, Guts exposed, no covering. Omphalocele = sac (O = covered/round).


## Paediatric Inguinal Hernia & Hydrocele

Paediatric inguinal hernia is an indirect hernia caused by failure of closure of the processus vaginalis (the peritoneal diverticulum that accompanies the testis/round ligament as it descends into the scrotum/labia). Unlike adult hernia repair, children do NOT require mesh — the fundamental procedure is HIGH LIGATION OF THE PROCESSUS VAGINALIS AT THE INTERNAL INGUINAL RING, with no mesh reinforcement. The floor of the inguinal canal does not need repair in children. Incidence: 1–3% of infants; males >>females (5:1); more common on the right (60%).

Incarceration risk: highest in infants <1 year (up to 30% risk of incarceration). Irreducible hernia in an infant is an emergency — manual reduction under analgesia/sedation, then repair within 24–48h (allows oedema to resolve). Emergency surgery required if reduction fails or ischaemia is suspected. Bilateral exploration: previously routine; now laparoscopic inspection of the contralateral processus vaginalis (patent processus vaginalis 40% contralateral) guides selective repair.

Hydrocele: accumulation of fluid in the tunica vaginalis secondary to a patent processus vaginalis. Communicating hydrocele: varies in size (larger after activity, smaller in morning) — persists due to open processus vaginalis. Non-communicating hydrocele: constant size — trapped fluid, processus vaginalis closed. Management: communicating hydrocele → observe until age 2 (90% close spontaneously); if persistent at 2 years → surgical ligation of processus vaginalis (same as hernia repair). Non-communicating hydrocele → observe until 12–18 months (most resolve); persistent/large → hydrocelectomy. There is no role for aspiration in children (high recurrence, infection risk).

Laparoscopic vs open inguinal hernia repair in infants: laparoscopic (70° scope through umbilicus, peritoneal suture ligation of internal ring) offers advantages of simultaneous contralateral assessment and shorter recovery; evidence shows comparable recurrence rates. Both approaches are accepted. IPEG guidelines (2018): either approach acceptable; preference for laparoscopic in bilateral/bilateral assessment.

> **NO MESH in Paediatric Inguinal Hernia Repair** — The processus vaginalis is simply ligated at the internal ring — NO mesh is required or appropriate in children. The posterior inguinal canal wall is structurally normal and does not require reinforcement. Mesh in children carries risks of foreign body reaction, interference with growth, and testicular ischaemia. Always document on board exams: paediatric inguinal hernia = high ligation at internal ring only.


## Pyloric Stenosis

Hypertrophic pyloric stenosis (HPS) affects 2–4 per 1000 live births; males > females (4–5:1); first-born males most commonly. Pathophysiology: progressive hypertrophy of the pyloric muscle, causing gastric outlet obstruction. Presentation: non-bilious projectile vomiting at 3–6 weeks of age; hunger after vomiting (unlike the unwell child with sepsis); palpable pyloric 'olive mass' in right epigastrium (pathognomonic — present in 60–70% if examined carefully during test feed). Metabolic consequence of prolonged vomiting of HCl: hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis. Urine: paradoxical aciduria (kidneys excrete H+ to preserve Na+ in exchange for K+ when K+ depletion is severe — 'paradoxical aciduria'). Biochemistry: Na+ low/normal, K+ low, Cl− very low, pH high, HCO3− high.

Diagnosis: ultrasound is the diagnostic investigation of choice (avoids radiation). Pyloric muscle thickness ≥4 mm and/or pyloric channel length ≥16 mm confirms diagnosis. Upper GI contrast study (barium meal — 'string sign', 'double track sign', 'shoulder sign') if US inconclusive.

Pre-operative resuscitation (CRITICAL): HPS is a medical emergency before surgery — DO NOT rush to theatre. Correct the metabolic alkalosis first. 0.9% NaCl + 20 mmol/L KCl at appropriate maintenance rate; target Cl− >100 mmol/L, K+ >3.5 mmol/L, BE <3.5 before surgery. This typically requires 24–48 hours. GA induction in uncorrected alkalosis risks apnoea post-extubation.

Ramstedt pyloromyotomy: the definitive surgical treatment. The pyloric muscle is split longitudinally down to but NOT through the mucosa; the mucosa should bulge through the split; no suturing of muscle is required. Laparoscopic approach (3-port or umbilical single-incision) is equivalent to open in experienced hands — GUTS trial (UK, 2009): laparoscopic vs open — equivalent outcomes (mucosal perforation rate, operative time, length of stay), but laparoscopic associated with fewer wound complications. Most centres now use laparoscopic as primary approach.

*Metabolic Disturbance in Pyloric Stenosis*
Parameter | Finding | Mechanism
--- | --- | ---
pH | Raised (>7.45) — metabolic alkalosis | Loss of HCl in vomit raises serum HCO3−
Chloride | Low (<95 mmol/L) — hypochloraemia | Vomit contains high HCl; renal HCO3− retention at expense of Cl−
Potassium | Low (<3.5 mmol/L) — hypokalaemia | Aldosterone-driven K+ loss in exchange for Na+ (volume depletion); HCO3−/K+ exchange
Sodium | Low/normal | Volume depletion + water retention
Urine pH | Acidic (paradoxical aciduria) | Severe K+ depletion → H+ secreted instead of K+ to reclaim Na+

> **GUTS Trial — Laparoscopic vs Open Pyloromyotomy** — GUTS RCT (UK, 2009, Hall et al.): laparoscopic vs open Ramstedt pyloromyotomy in 180 infants. No significant difference in operative complications (mucosal perforation ~3% both groups), time to full feeds, or hospital stay. Laparoscopic had fewer wound infections/wound complications. Both approaches are safe and effective. Laparoscopic is now the preferred approach at most paediatric surgical centres.


## Appendicitis in Children

Paediatric appendicitis: different from adults — perforation rate higher (30–45%, vs 15–20% in adults) due to delayed presentation and thinner appendiceal wall; omentum is less developed in young children, limiting local containment. The Paediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS — Samuel 2002) components: (1) migration of pain to RIF (1 point); (2) anorexia (1); (3) nausea/vomiting (1); (4) RIF tenderness on coughing/hopping/percussion (2); (5) fever ≥38°C (1); (6) leukocytosis ≥10,000 (1); (7) RIF tenderness on palpation (2). Maximum 10 points. PAS ≥6 = high probability; PAS ≤2 = low probability; PAS 3–5 = equivocal → imaging.

Imaging: ultrasound is FIRST-LINE in children (no radiation, identifies appendix ≥75% of cases, also evaluates ovary/other causes in girls). MRI if ultrasound inconclusive (sensitivity 97%, specificity 96%) — preferred over CT in children to avoid ionising radiation. CT is reserved for diagnostic uncertainty when MRI unavailable or in obese children where US is limited.

Non-operative management (NOM) for uncomplicated appendicitis: APSA (American Pediatric Surgery Association) guidelines and multiple RCTs demonstrate that NOM with IV antibiotics is safe for uncomplicated appendicitis (no perforation, abscess, or appendicolith) in children. NOM success rate: ~70–80% at 1 year. Recurrence within 1 year: ~20–30%. NOM should be offered as an alternative after careful counselling about recurrence risk. Requires: confirmed uncomplicated appendicitis (CT or MRI), clinically improving with antibiotics, no appendicolith (fecolith), no peritonitis.

Perforated appendicitis: laparoscopic appendicectomy is the standard for perforated appendicitis. Benefits: lower wound infection rate, shorter hospital stay, reduced adhesion formation. Post-perforation abscess: if well-contained pericaecal abscess + no peritonitis → IV antibiotics ± CT-guided drainage; interval appendicectomy at 6–12 weeks (optional — recurrence risk ~10%). Immediate appendicectomy for perforated appendicitis with peritonitis.

*Paediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS)*
Component | Points | Notes
--- | --- | ---
Migration of pain to RIF | 1 | History of periumbilical → RIF migration
Anorexia | 1 | Clinically significant loss of appetite
Nausea/vomiting | 1 | Either present
RIF tenderness on coughing/hopping/percussion | 2 | Higher weight — indicates localised peritonism
Fever ≥38°C | 1 | Rectal or tympanic
Leukocytosis ≥10,000/µL | 1 | WBC threshold
RIF tenderness on direct palpation | 2 | Higher weight — most specific sign
TOTAL | 10 | ≤2 low risk; 3–5 equivocal (image); ≥6 high risk (operate)

> **APSA Guidelines — Non-Operative Management of Uncomplicated Appendicitis** — APSA 2022 guidelines: NOM with IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone + metronidazole, or ampicillin-sulbactam) is a safe alternative to immediate appendicectomy in children with UNCOMPLICATED appendicitis (no perforation, no appendicolith, no free fluid beyond the pelvis). Success rate 70–80% at 1 year. Families must be counselled about 20–30% recurrence risk requiring delayed appendicectomy. NOM SHOULD NOT be offered if: CT/MRI shows perforation, appendicolith (fecolith), phlegmon, or abscess >3 cm.


## Paediatric Trauma

Paediatric trauma management follows ATLS principles but with important modifications reflecting physiological and anatomical differences. Children have: larger head-to-body ratio (higher head injury risk), more pliable rib cage (internal organ injury with fewer rib fractures), smaller circulating blood volume (70 mL/kg), higher metabolic rate and temperature loss risk, limited physiological reserve but better initial compensation.

ATLS paediatric modifications: Fluid resuscitation: initial bolus 10 mL/kg IV crystalloid (NOT 20 mL/kg as in adults); reassess; repeat if no response; 3rd bolus → consider blood products. Blood transfusion: packed red cells 10 mL/kg. Weight estimation: Broselow tape or formula: weight (kg) = (age + 4) × 2. Intraosseous (IO) access: mandatory skill — if IV access cannot be established within 60 seconds in a shocked child → IO access. Site: proximal tibia (2 cm below tibial tuberosity, anteromedial surface) — first choice; distal femur, humeral head as alternatives. IO can deliver all drugs and fluids including blood products.

Solid organ injury — NOM: Children have higher success rates for non-operative management of solid organ injuries than adults. Spleen NOM success rate: >90% in haemodynamically stable children (vs ~70–80% in adults). Liver NOM: similar high success rates. APSA guidelines (solid organ injury): haemodynamically stable + no peritonitis = attempt NOM regardless of injury grade. CT grading guides admission/observation protocol (Grade I–II = 2-day admission; Grade III = ICU admission; Grade IV–V = ICU + angioembolisation consideration). Blood transfusion requirement (>40 mL/kg/24h) or haemodynamic instability = operative intervention.

Burns in children: Lund-Browder chart is the accurate method for burns assessment in children — accounts for changing body surface area proportions with age (head is larger relative proportion in infants: 19% at birth vs 9% in adults; legs are smaller: 14% at birth vs 18% in adults). Rule of nines is INACCURATE in children <10 years. Parkland formula applies: 3–4 mL/kg/% TBSA in first 24h (half in first 8h, half in next 16h). Child <30 kg: add 100% maintenance fluids to Parkland calculation.

> **Child Abuse — NICE NG76 Mandatory Considerations** — NICE NG76 red flags for non-accidental injury: any injury in non-ambulant child (<6–9 months); injury inconsistent with developmental stage or history; delay in seeking medical attention; multiple bruises in unusual locations (ears, neck, face in non-mobile child); retinal haemorrhages (shaken baby); posterior rib fractures. Safeguarding process: mandatory documentation, referral to safeguarding team, skeletal survey (full-body X-ray series), ophthalmology, social services involvement. Do NOT discharge without senior review and safeguarding plan.


## Paediatric Oncology for Surgeons

Wilms' tumour (nephroblastoma): most common renal malignancy in children (peak age 3–4 years). Associated syndromes: WAGR (Wilms', Aniridia, Genitourinary anomalies, intellectual disability — WT1 deletion); Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome; Denys-Drash syndrome. Bilateral in 5–7% (synchronous or metachronous — must screen contralateral kidney). Tumour marker: AFP is NOT elevated (use AFP for hepatoblastoma). Staging: I (confined to kidney, resected); II (extends beyond kidney, resected); III (residual non-haematogenous — lymph nodes, positive margin); IV (haematogenous metastases — lung most common); V (bilateral).

Treatment controversy — SIOP vs COG approach: SIOP (European, International Society of Paediatric Oncology) protocol: PREOPERATIVE chemotherapy (vincristine + actinomycin D × 4 weeks for localised disease; + doxorubicin × 6 weeks for metastatic) → nephrectomy → adjuvant based on histology. Advantages: tumour shrinkage (reduces surgical complications), avoids tumour rupture, identifies blastemal-predominant histology (worse prognosis). COG (Children's Oncology Group — North America): IMMEDIATE nephrectomy → adjuvant chemotherapy based on surgical stage and histology. Advantage: accurate pre-treatment staging and histology without downstaging bias. Both approaches achieve similar long-term survival (~90% 5-year OS for favourable histology). UK uses SIOP protocol.

Neuroblastoma: most common extracranial solid tumour in children (peak age 18 months). Arises from neural crest cells (adrenal medulla most common site — 40%; paraspinal ganglia). Key markers: urinary catecholamines (VMA, HVA), LDH, ferritin, NSE. Imaging: CT + MIBG (meta-iodobenzylguanidine) scan — highly specific for neuroblastoma. Risk stratification: low (Stage I–II, no MYCN amp), intermediate, high-risk (Stage IV, >18 months, MYCN amplification). MYCN amplification: present in 20% — strongly associated with rapid progression, chemotherapy resistance, poor prognosis (5-year OS ~30% in high-risk). Image-Defined Risk Factors (IDRFs): anatomical criteria defining resectability risk (encasement of vessels, intraspinal extension, etc.) — if IDRFs present, induction chemotherapy before surgery reduces surgical risk.

Hepatoblastoma: most common primary liver tumour in children (peak age <3 years; 90% <5 years). Tumour marker: AFP — markedly elevated (can be >100,000 µg/L); AFP is used for diagnosis, treatment monitoring, and recurrence detection. Staging: PRETEXT (Pretreatment Extent of Disease — SIOPEL) staging system — divides liver into 4 sectors; PRETEXT I (1 sector) to PRETEXT IV (all 4 sectors). Treatment: SIOPEL protocol (SIOP European Liver Tumour Study Group) — cisplatin-based neoadjuvant chemotherapy (PLADO or SIOPEL-4) → surgical resection → post-operative chemotherapy. Resection is achievable in ~80% after chemotherapy. Liver transplantation: for PRETEXT IV or unresectable disease after chemotherapy (excellent outcomes, 5-year OS >80%).

> **MYCN Amplification in Neuroblastoma** — MYCN (N-Myc) proto-oncogene amplification (>10 copies) in neuroblastoma is the single most important adverse prognostic factor: associated with rapid tumour growth, treatment resistance, and poor survival (5-year OS <30% in high-risk MYCN-amplified vs >90% in low-risk non-amplified). Identification by FISH or PCR on tumour tissue. High-risk treatment: induction chemotherapy → surgery → high-dose chemo/stem cell transplant → radiotherapy → isotretinoin (differentiation therapy) → dinutuximab (anti-GD2 immunotherapy — improves OS in high-risk). Always test for MYCN on biopsy.
