## IBD — Pathophysiology & Classification

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) comprises two main entities: **Ulcerative Colitis (UC)** and **Crohn's Disease (CD)**. Both are immune-mediated conditions characterized by chronic, relapsing gastrointestinal inflammation. Global IBD prevalence: ~0.3% in Western countries. Peak incidence: 20–30 years (bimodal peak at 50–60 years for UC). Pathophysiology: dysregulated mucosal immune response to gut microbiota in genetically susceptible individuals → chronic intestinal inflammation.

*Ulcerative Colitis vs Crohn's Disease — Histopathological & Clinical Differences*
Feature | Ulcerative Colitis | Crohn's Disease
--- | --- | ---
Distribution | Continuous, starts at rectum, extends proximally; colon only | Discontinuous (skip lesions); any segment of GI tract (mouth to anus)
Depth of inflammation | Mucosal and submucosal only | Transmural (all layers) — allows fistula, abscess, stricture formation
Histology | Crypt abscesses, goblet cell depletion, mucosal pseudopolyps | Transmural inflammation, granulomas (pathognomonic, found in 30–50%), skip lesions
Granulomas | Absent | Present (non-caseating, 30–50%) — differentiates from UC
Rectal involvement | Always involved | Spared in ~50% of cases (rectal sparing)
Perianal disease | Rare | Present in 25–35% (fistulae, abscesses, skin tags)
Smoking | PROTECTIVE (paradoxically) | Risk factor (worsens disease)
Surgical cure | Yes — proctocolectomy curative | No — surgery does not cure; recurrence common

*Montreal Classification — Ulcerative Colitis (Extent)*
Class | Extent | Clinical Significance
--- | --- | ---
E1 (Proctitis) | Disease limited to rectum (distal to rectosigmoid junction) | Lowest risk of complications; topical treatment often sufficient
E2 (Left-sided/distal UC) | Disease up to but not beyond splenic flexure | Moderate disease extent; oral + topical combination therapy
E3 (Pancolitis/extensive UC) | Disease proximal to splenic flexure, including transverse colon | Highest complication risk (toxic megacolon, colorectal cancer); systemic therapy required

*Montreal Classification — Crohn's Disease (Location, Behaviour, Perianal)*
Category | Class | Definition
--- | --- | ---
Location (L) | L1 — Ileal | Terminal ileum ± caecum
Location (L) | L2 — Colonic | Colon only (any segment)
Location (L) | L3 — Ileocolonic | Terminal ileum + colon
Location (L) | L4 — Upper GI (modifier) | Oesophagus, stomach, duodenum (add to L1–L3)
Behaviour (B) | B1 — Non-stricturing, non-penetrating | Inflammatory phenotype (no stricture/fistula)
Behaviour (B) | B2 — Stricturing | Luminal narrowing causing obstruction symptoms
Behaviour (B) | B3 — Penetrating | Fistulae, abscesses, inflammatory masses
Perianal (p) | p modifier | Add '+p' if perianal disease present, regardless of B category

*Extraintestinal Manifestations of IBD*
System | Manifestation | Association
--- | --- | ---
Joint | Peripheral arthropathy Type 1 (pauciarticular, parallels disease activity); Type 2 (polyarticular, independent) | UC > CD
Joint | Axial arthropathy: sacroiliitis, ankylosing spondylitis | CD > UC; independent of bowel disease
Skin | Erythema nodosum (parallels disease activity) | UC and CD equally

Eye | Episcleritis (parallels activity), uveitis/iritis (independent) | UC and CD

Liver/Biliary | Cholelithiasis (increased in CD — terminal ileum disease → bile salt malabsorption) | CD (ileal involvement)
Renal | Oxalate nephrolithiasis (fat malabsorption → increased oxalate absorption) | CD (ileal disease or short bowel)


## Medical Management Overview

Medical management of IBD is stratified by disease severity and is guided by treat-to-target principles: targeting clinical remission (symptom control) AND endoscopic remission (mucosal healing). The step-up approach starts with less toxic agents and escalates; the top-down approach starts with biologics early in severe or high-risk disease.

*Medical Therapy in IBD — Drug Classes & Mechanisms*
Drug Class | Agents | Mechanism | Use
--- | --- | --- | ---
Aminosalicylates (5-ASA) | Mesalazine (oral, topical), sulfasalazine | Anti-inflammatory at mucosal level; inhibit NF-κB, prostaglandins | UC mild-moderate (induction + maintenance); NOT effective in CD
Corticosteroids | Prednisolone, budesonide (ileocaecal CD), IV hydrocortisone/methylprednisolone | Broad anti-inflammatory; suppress cytokine production | Induction of remission (not maintenance — steroid dependency is indication for escalation)
Immunomodulators | Azathioprine (AZA), 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP), methotrexate (MTX, CD only) | AZA/6-MP: thiopurine → inhibit purine synthesis → lymphocyte apoptosis. MTX: folate antagonist | Maintenance; used with biologics to prevent antibody formation; TPMT testing before AZA
Anti-TNF biologics | Infliximab (IV), adalimumab (SC), certolizumab pegol, golimumab (UC) | Neutralize tumour necrosis factor-α | Moderate-severe UC and CD; perianal CD; top-down in high-risk disease
Anti-integrin biologics | Vedolizumab (IV) | Blocks α4β7-MAdCAM-1 — gut-selective; prevents lymphocyte trafficking to intestine | Moderate-severe UC and CD; preferred in patients where systemic immunosuppression is a concern
Anti-IL-12/23 biologics | Ustekinumab (IV induction, SC maintenance) | Binds p40 subunit of IL-12 and IL-23 — inhibits Th1 and Th17 pathways | Moderate-severe CD (approved for UC from 2019); good safety profile
JAK Inhibitors (small molecules) | Tofacitinib (oral, UC), upadacitinib (oral, UC+CD), filgotinib | Inhibit Janus kinases → block multiple inflammatory cytokine signaling pathways | Moderate-severe UC; rapid onset; caution: VTE risk, cardiovascular risk (tofacitinib)

> **Drug-to-Surgery Intervals — ECCO 2023 Specific Guidance** — **ECCO Crohn's Surgery 3rd ed. 2023** provides specific drug-to-surgery intervals for elective surgery: **(1) Anti-TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab, certolizumab):** delay elective surgery ≥12 weeks after last infusion when clinically feasible — anti-TNF within 12 weeks is associated with significantly increased infectious complications. **(2) Vedolizumab:** no mandatory delay recommended — lower infectious complication signal due to gut-selective mechanism. **(3) Ustekinumab:** insufficient evidence for firm recommendation. If surgery cannot be delayed: diverting ileostomy over primary anastomosis in patients on anti-TNF with poor nutrition (albumin <30 g/L) or high-dose steroids (>40 mg/day prednisolone). **ASUC algorithm (ECCO-ESCP 2023):** Day 3 assessment is mandatory — if no objective response to IV methylprednisolone → rescue therapy (IFX or cyclosporine); no response by Day 7 → colectomy within 24–48h; second rescue agent after failure of first is explicitly discouraged.


## Surgical Indications in Ulcerative Colitis

Surgery is required in approximately 20–30% of UC patients during their lifetime. UC is the only IBD where surgery (total proctocolectomy) is **curative**. Surgical urgency determines the approach: emergency (colectomy without reconstruction), urgent (subacute), or elective (restorative proctocolectomy with IPAA).

*Indications for Surgery in Ulcerative Colitis*
Category | Indication | Urgency
--- | --- | ---
Emergency | Toxic megacolon unresponsive to 24–48h medical therapy | Emergency
Emergency | Free perforation | Emergency
Emergency | Massive hemorrhage | Emergency
Acute | Severe UC (Truelove & Witts) failing 3–5 days IV corticosteroids; failure of rescue therapy (infliximab, ciclosporin) | Urgent (within days)
Elective | Medically refractory UC — steroid-dependent or -refractory disease; intolerable medical side effects | Elective (planned)
Elective | Low-grade dysplasia (multifocal or recurring); high-grade dysplasia; cancer | Semi-urgent to elective depending on stage
Elective | Growth retardation in children/adolescents unresponsive to medical therapy | Elective

**Restorative proctocolectomy with ileal pouch-anal anastomosis (IPAA, J-pouch)** is the gold standard elective operation for UC. Performed in 2 or 3 stages. Standard 2-stage: (1) total proctocolectomy + IPAA construction + diverting loop ileostomy; (2) ileostomy closure at 8–12 weeks after confirming anastomotic integrity (pouch-ogram). 3-stage for high-risk or emergency: (1) subtotal colectomy + end ileostomy; (2) completion proctectomy + IPAA + diverting ileostomy; (3) ileostomy reversal.

*IPAA (J-Pouch) — Pouch Configurations*
Configuration | Construction | Advantages/Disadvantages
--- | --- | ---
J-pouch (most common, 90%) | Two 15 cm ileal limbs folded, stapled. Apex anastomosed to anal transition zone (±mucosectomy) | Easiest construction; good function; 4–8 stools/day (functional result)
W-pouch (4-loop) | Four ileal limbs — larger reservoir volume | Higher volume; technically complex; used rarely for specific indications
S-pouch (3-loop) | Three ileal limbs; efferent spout to anus | Historically used; efferent limb can cause evacuation difficulty (catheterization sometimes needed); largely replaced by J-pouch
Kock pouch (continent ileostomy) | Intra-abdominal reservoir with continent valve | Used when IPAA failed or anus non-functional; requires intubation to empty

> **IPAA Contraindications** — Absolute: (1) Crohn's disease (risk of pouch Crohn's — anastomotic breakdown, pelvic sepsis; pouch failure rate 30–45% vs 5% in UC); (2) sphincter dysfunction (faecal incontinence — IPAA will worsen); (3) low rectal cancer requiring APR. Relative: Obesity (BMI >40, pouch reach to anus may be inadequate), prior extensive pelvic surgery, indeterminate colitis (discuss carefully — may develop Crohn's post-IPAA).

**Pouchitis** is the most common long-term complication after IPAA: incidence 30–50% at 5 years. Symptoms: increased stool frequency, liquid stools, urgency, bleeding, fever. Diagnosis: clinical + endoscopic (mucosal erythema, erosions, ulceration) ± histological (neutrophil infiltration). Treatment: metronidazole 400 mg TID or ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID for 2 weeks (first-line). Chronic antibiotic-refractory pouchitis: VSL#3 probiotic (multi-strain), budesonide, biologics (vedolizumab shows efficacy). Pouch failure requiring pouch excision or permanent ileostomy: ~5–10% at 10 years.


## Surgical Indications in Crohn's Disease

Unlike UC, surgery in Crohn's disease is NOT curative — disease recurs in the neo-terminal ileum in 60–80% at 10 years after ileocaecal resection. Surgical strategy: **bowel-sparing** — use the least amount of bowel resection necessary, preserve as much small bowel as possible to avoid short bowel syndrome. Surgery addresses **complications** (obstruction, fistula, abscess, cancer) rather than curing the underlying disease.

*Surgical Indications in Crohn's Disease*
Indication | Operation | Notes
--- | --- | ---
Ileocaecal CD — obstruction or medically refractory | Laparoscopic ileocaecal resection (ICR) | Most common CD operation; first episode of terminal ileum disease — surgery as valid alternative to medical therapy (LIR!C trial)
Colonic CD — medically refractory | Segmental colectomy OR subtotal colectomy depending on extent | Preserve bowel; total colectomy if pancolonic disease; rectal preservation if possible
Stricture (B2 CD) | Strictureplasty (if <10 cm, non-inflamed) OR resection | Bowel-sparing when possible; multiple strictureplasties in one operation acceptable
Penetrating CD — abscess (B3) | Percutaneous drainage (acute); resection of offending segment (elective) | Drain acute abscess; resect diseased segment when inflammation resolved
Enterocutaneous fistula | Resection of diseased bowel segment + fistula tract excision | Medical therapy first (anti-TNF); surgery for refractory fistulae
Enteroenteric fistula | Resect diseased segment; repair healthy recipient bowel | Short segment affected; functioning bowel segment usually only needs primary repair
Rectal CD — severe, refractory (proctitis) | Proctectomy ± colectomy | IPAA contraindicated in CD — end ileostomy; Hartmann's pouch or proctocolectomy
Cancer in CD | Resection per oncological principles | Colorectal cancer in CD managed as for sporadic CRC

**Ensayo de referencia — LIR!C Trial:**

> **Short Bowel Syndrome Risk in Crohn's Disease** — Short bowel syndrome (SBS) occurs when remaining small bowel length falls below 100–150 cm (with colon) or 50 cm (with end-jejunostomy). Crohn's patients are at risk from: repeated small bowel resections over years. Each resection should preserve maximum bowel — this is why strictureplasty is preferred over resection for B2 disease when feasible. Cumulative lifetime resection length should be monitored. Parenteral nutrition may be required for end-stage SBS.


## Perianal Crohn's Disease

Perianal Crohn's disease (PACD) affects 25–35% of CD patients and significantly impairs quality of life. Manifestations: skin tags (most common), anal fissures (often painless, off-midline — distinguishing from idiopathic), anal ulcers, abscesses, fistulae. Perianal fistulae are classified using the **Parks classification** (based on relationship to sphincters) and the **Van Assche MRI score** for monitoring response to therapy.

*Parks Classification of Perianal Fistulae*
Type | Course | Prevalence in PACD | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
Superficial | Below both sphincters, below dentate line | Rare in PACD | Lay-open fistulotomy
Intersphincteric | Between internal and external sphincters; exits at perineum | Most common (45%) | Seton or lay-open (assess sphincter involvement)
Transsphincteric | Crosses external sphincter at variable height; exits ischioanal fossa | 40% of PACD fistulae | Non-cutting seton (draining seton); anti-TNF; LIFT procedure; advancement flap
Suprasphincteric | Over puborectalis, through levators, to ischioanal fossa | 3–5% | Seton; complex repair; sphincter-preserving approach
Extrasphincteric | Above levators, through ischioanal fossa to skin; bypasses entire sphincter | Rare (<1%) | Faecal diversion; staged repair; high morbidity

**Ensayo de referencia — ACCENT II Trial:**

*Complex Perianal Fistula Management in Crohn's — Surgical Options*
Procedure | Mechanism | Success Rate | Best Indication
--- | --- | --- | ---
Draining (loose) seton | Non-cutting seton through fistula tract maintains drainage; prevents abscess | Symptom control; not curative | First step for all complex fistulae; always place before anti-TNF
Cutting seton | Progressively tightens through sphincter — gradual division | 50–60% closure | Low transsphincteric (risk of incontinence with high fistulae); AVOIDED in CD due to incontinence risk
LIFT procedure (Ligation of Intersphincteric Fistula Tract) | Ligates and divides tract in intersphincteric plane | 60–75% (non-CD); 40–60% (CD) | Simple transsphincteric fistulae in CD; sphincter-preserving
Endorectal advancement flap | Mucosal-submucosal flap from rectal wall covers internal opening | 40–60% in CD | Complex transsphincteric; requires quiescent luminal CD; failure more common in CD
Faecal diversion (loop ileostomy) | Defunction bowel; reduces contamination; allows fistula healing | Partial/temporary remission 60%; fistula closure ~30% | Severe PACD refractory to all other measures; bridge to definitive repair
Proctectomy | Remove rectum; permanent end colostomy/ileostomy | Definitive; 10–15% of PACD patients ultimately require | Severe refractory proctitis + perianal disease; last resort

> **Draining Seton BEFORE Anti-TNF — Critical Principle** — In complex perianal Crohn's fistula: ALWAYS place a draining (loose) seton BEFORE starting infliximab. Rationale: if anti-TNF therapy closes the external opening while active infection remains in the tract, an undrained abscess forms → pelvic sepsis. Draining seton keeps the tract open, allows continued drainage, and prevents abscess during biological treatment. Remove seton only after confirming fistula closure on MRI AND complete clinical assessment.


## Toxic Megacolon — Diagnosis & Emergency Management

**Toxic megacolon** is a life-threatening complication of severe colitis (UC most common; also CD, infectious colitis — C. difficile, CMV). It represents transmural inflammation with colonic ileus and dilation, leading to impending perforation. Mortality: untreated ~50%; with appropriate management ~2–5%.

*Jalan Criteria for Toxic Megacolon — Diagnosis Requires 3 of 4 Minor + 1 Major*
Category | Criteria
--- | ---
Major (radiological) | Colonic dilatation >6 cm on AXR (transverse colon most commonly measured)
Minor (3 of 4 required) | Fever >38.6°C OR HR >120/min OR WBC >10,500/μL OR anemia (Hb <6 g/dL below normal)
Supportive features | Dehydration, altered consciousness, hypotension, electrolyte disturbances

> **Emergency Management of Toxic Megacolon — Medical vs Surgical** — IMMEDIATE: (1) NBM; (2) NG suction; (3) IV fluid resuscitation; (4) IV corticosteroids (hydrocortisone 100 mg QID or methylprednisolone 60 mg/day); (5) IV antibiotics (cover anaerobes and Gram-negatives: piperacillin-tazobactam or metronidazole + ciprofloxacin); (6) correct electrolytes (especially hypokalaemia — worsens ileus); (7) avoid antidiarrheals, opioids, anticholinergics (worsen dilation); (8) VTE prophylaxis; (9) SERIAL AXR every 12–24h. SURGICAL: emergency colectomy if: no improvement at 24–48h (repeated AXR showing progressive dilation or no improvement), perforation, increasing instability, colonic diameter >10 cm. The operation is SUBTOTAL COLECTOMY + END ILEOSTOMY — NOT total proctocolectomy acutely (rectal stump left as Hartmann's pouch or exteriorized as mucus fistula).

> **Why SUBTOTAL Colectomy (Not Total Proctocolectomy) in Acute Toxic Megacolon** — In the emergency setting with severely ill, malnourished, immunosuppressed (corticosteroids) UC patient, total proctocolectomy with IPAA is associated with prohibitive pelvic sepsis, anastomotic leak, and pouch failure rates. Subtotal colectomy + end ileostomy: (1) faster operation — reduces time under anaesthesia; (2) avoids pelvic dissection in a toxic patient; (3) preserves rectum for future IPAA construction after patient recovers (typically 3–6 months later — 3-stage approach); (4) rectal stump sent for histology — confirms UC vs CD. Patient should recover, optimize nutrition, taper steroids, then undergo elective completion proctectomy + IPAA.


## Crohn's Strictureplasty — Techniques & Indications

Strictureplasty is a bowel-sparing technique to widen fibrotic Crohn's strictures without resection. It is preferred over resection when: multiple small bowel strictures, prior small bowel resections (SBS risk), strictures in a functionally important segment, or when resection would leave <100 cm of small bowel. Strictureplasty does NOT worsen recurrence risk compared to resection and is oncologically safe when malignancy is excluded.

*Strictureplasty Techniques by Stricture Length*
Technique | Stricture Length | Principle | Outcome
--- | --- | --- | ---
Heineke-Mikulicz (HM) | <10 cm (short strictures) | Longitudinal full-thickness enterotomy along stricture → transverse closure (similar to pyloplasty); widening without resection | Technically simple; success rate 85–90%; recurrence at strictureplasty site ~3–5%
Finney | 10–25 cm (intermediate strictures) | Side-to-side isoperistaltic enterotomy; loop formed and anastomosed — creates larger side-to-side reservoir | Good for medium strictures; more complex than HM
Michelassi (side-to-side isoperistaltic strictureplasty) | >25 cm (long/multiple strictures) | Two long parallel segments of diseased bowel anastomosed side-to-side in isoperistaltic fashion; up to 100 cm | For diffuse jejunoileitis; excellent bowel preservation; most complex technique

> **Strictureplasty Contraindications** — ABSOLUTE contraindications to strictureplasty: (1) Suspected malignancy at stricture site (biopsy mandatory; resect if cancer suspected); (2) Phlegmon or abscess at stricture (active sepsis — resect + drain); (3) Free perforation through stricture; (4) Nutritional status very poor. RELATIVE: Multiple strictures in same bowel segment (may be better to resect one segment containing multiple strictures rather than multiple Heineke-Mikulicz); active inflammation (prefer resection over strictureplasty in acutely inflamed tissue).

**Recurrence at strictureplasty site**: 3–5% at 5 years — significantly lower than feared. This was the key concern preventing wider adoption of strictureplasty, but long-term studies confirm it is safe and does not represent a cancer risk. Luminal biopsy at time of strictureplasty is essential — any suspicion of malignancy → resect the segment, do not strictureplasty.


## Surveillance & Cancer Risk in IBD

**IBD-associated colorectal cancer (IBD-CRC)**: patients with long-standing UC and extensive colonic CD have a significantly elevated CRC risk. The risk is approximately **2× population risk** in all UC patients; **8× population risk** in extensive UC (E3 pancolitis) of long duration (>20 years). Key risk factors: disease extent (pancolitis > left-sided > proctitis); disease duration (risk increases from ~2% at 10 years to ~8% at 20 years to ~18% at 30 years in extensive UC); coexisting PSC (highest risk — consider annual surveillance); family history of CRC; post-inflammatory polyps (pseudopolyps); active inflammation.

*ECCO 2023 / BSG 2019 — IBD Colorectal Cancer Surveillance Intervals*
Risk Category | Criteria | Surveillance Interval
--- | --- | ---
Low risk | Extensive UC or left-sided UC in remission; no PSC; no family history CRC; no stricture/post-inflammatory polyps | Every 5 years starting at 8–10 years from symptom onset
Intermediate risk | Extensive UC with mildly active inflammation; left-sided UC + family history CRC; post-inflammatory polyps; stricture (exclude cancer) | Every 3 years
High risk | Pancolitis + active inflammation; stricture that cannot exclude cancer; PSC (UC or CD); family history CRC age <50; previous dysplasia managed endoscopically | Annual colonoscopy
PSC + UC | ANY extent of UC with PSC | Annual colonoscopy (from diagnosis of PSC, regardless of disease duration)

**Chromoendoscopy** (spray of dilute methylene blue or indigo carmine) is recommended for IBD surveillance — significantly improves dysplasia detection compared to white-light endoscopy (relative risk 1.8 for detecting dysplasia). ECCO 2023 recommends chromoendoscopy or virtual chromoendoscopy (narrow-band imaging, FICE) as the standard for IBD surveillance colonoscopy in expert centres. If chromoendoscopy unavailable, take targeted biopsies from any visible lesions + random quadrantic biopsies every 10 cm.

> **Dysplasia Management in IBD** — VISIBLE dysplastic lesion (DALM — dysplasia-associated lesion or mass): if completely resectable endoscopically (polypoid DALM) → endoscopic resection with clear margins + annual surveillance. If non-polypoid or not resectable endoscopically → surgery (proctocolectomy). Invisible dysplasia on random biopsy: Low-grade dysplasia (LGD) — multifocal or confirmed on repeat colonoscopy → surgery. Single focus LGD — intensive surveillance at 3–6 months. High-grade dysplasia (HGD) — surgery (proctocolectomy) regardless of visibility.

**Faecal calprotectin (FC)** is a biomarker of intestinal inflammation (released by neutrophils in inflamed mucosa). FC >250 μg/g predicts active endoscopic inflammation with 90% sensitivity. FC is valuable for: (1) monitoring disease activity non-invasively; (2) predicting relapse (rising FC → upcoming flare); (3) assessing mucosal healing in clinical remission; (4) differentiating IBD relapse from IBS in patients with established IBD (FC normal → IBS likely; FC elevated → IBD activity).
