# Bariatric & Metabolic Surgery — General Surgery — Obesity, RYGB, Sleeve Gastrectomy & Metabolic Outcomes

*Block B · General Surgery · PGY2-3*

*Guidelines: IFSO-EASO Interdisciplinary European Guidelines 2022 · ASMBS/IFSO Updated Indications 2022 · IFSO 2023 Position Statement on Metabolic Surgery for T2DM*


## Indications and Patient Selection

Bariatric surgery is the most effective long-term treatment for severe obesity and associated metabolic comorbidities. The updated ASMBS/IFSO 2022 position statement broadened indications: surgery is recommended for BMI ≥35 kg/m² regardless of comorbidities, and for BMI 30–34.9 kg/m² with at least one obesity-related comorbidity (T2DM, hypertension, sleep apnoea, NAFLD). The 40-year BMI threshold is no longer the universal standard — metabolic disease burden now drives the indication. Contraindications to bariatric surgery: untreated psychiatric disorders preventing long-term compliance, active substance abuse, inability to comply with post-operative nutritional requirements, and medical fitness precluding anaesthesia (though these are relative). Age: bariatric surgery is increasingly offered to adolescents (aged 14–17 with severe obesity and completed bone growth) and to patients >65 years with appropriate selection. Pregnancy should be delayed for minimum 12–18 months after surgery.

*Bariatric Surgery Indications — ASMBS/IFSO 2022*
BMI | Indication | Key Comorbidities | Notes
--- | --- | --- | ---
≥40 kg/m² | Surgery recommended | Any or none | Class III obesity
35–39.9 kg/m² | Surgery recommended | Any or none | Previously required comorbidity — now without
30–34.9 kg/m² | Surgery recommended with comorbidity | T2DM, OSA, HTN, NAFLD, GERD | Updated 2022
<30 kg/m² | Not standard indication | Investigational in Asian populations with metabolic disease | Requires IRB-approved protocol

> **Board Pearl — Metabolic Surgery for T2DM** — Bariatric/metabolic surgery achieves T2DM remission (HbA1c <6.5% without medication) in 50–80% of patients at 1 year. The mechanism is multifactorial: early (hormonal — GLP-1 surge, ghrelin reduction) AND late (sustained weight loss, improved insulin sensitivity). The STAMPEDE trial (Schauer, NEJM 2017, 5-year data) demonstrated T2DM remission in 23% with RYGB vs 5% with medical therapy alone — an 18% absolute difference.


## Pre-operative Assessment & Multidisciplinary Team

Pre-operative bariatric assessment requires a multidisciplinary team (MDT) comprising: surgeon, physician/endocrinologist, dietitian, psychologist/psychiatrist, and anaesthetist. The pre-operative MDT evaluation serves to: (1) confirm the surgical indication, (2) identify and treat obesity-related comorbidities (optimise T2DM, hypertension, sleep apnoea, NAFLD), (3) screen for and treat psychological conditions that may compromise surgical outcomes (active depression, binge eating disorder, unrealistic expectations), (4) identify contraindications, (5) educate the patient on nutritional requirements and post-operative lifestyle changes. Mandatory pre-operative investigations: fasting glucose/HbA1c, lipid profile, thyroid function (exclude hypothyroidism as cause of obesity), FBC, LFTs, renal function, iron studies, folate, B12, vitamin D, PTH, coagulation screen, chest X-ray, ECG, upper GI endoscopy or barium meal (particularly for sleeve to exclude GERD, Helicobacter pylori), sleep study (for OSA evaluation and CPAP initiation). H. pylori eradication is mandatory pre-operatively, particularly for RYGB (inability to access the excluded stomach endoscopically post-operatively). Pre-operative weight loss (low-calorie diet or VLCD for 2–4 weeks) reduces liver volume by 20–30%, facilitates intraoperative exposure, and may reduce conversion rates.

*Pre-operative Optimisation — Key Targets*
Parameter | Target Before Surgery | Rationale
--- | --- | ---
HbA1c | <8–9% (ideally <7.5%) | Reduces wound complication and anastomotic leak risk
Sleep apnoea | CPAP initiated pre-op | Reduces post-operative respiratory complications
H. pylori | Eradicate before RYGB | Cannot access excluded stomach post-RYGB for endoscopy
Liver volume | VLCD 2–4 weeks pre-op | Reduces hepatomegaly; improves intraoperative exposure
Smoking | Cessation ≥6 weeks pre-op | Reduces marginal ulcer risk post-RYGB; reduces wound complications
NSAID use | Discontinue pre-op | NSAID = strongest risk factor for marginal ulcer after RYGB

> **Bariatric Surgery in Adolescents** — Bariatric surgery in adolescents aged 14–17 is appropriate for severe obesity (BMI ≥35 with significant comorbidities or BMI ≥40) when conservative measures have failed and skeletal maturity is confirmed. Sleeve gastrectomy is preferred (lower risk, reversible if needed) over RYGB in adolescents due to superior nutritional risk profile. Mandatory MDT assessment including adolescent psychiatry and family/guardian involvement. Outcomes equivalent to adult surgery with higher compliance when family support is strong.


## RYGB — Operative Steps & Anatomy

Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB) creates a small gastric pouch (15–30 mL) connected to a Roux limb of small bowel, bypassing the majority of the stomach, the entire duodenum, and a variable length of proximal jejunum. Operative steps (laparoscopic): (1) Patient positioning: supine/split-leg; surgeon between legs or to the left. Ports: 5 trocars (12mm optical, 12mm right, 5mm left upper, 5mm liver retractor, 5mm assistant). (2) Gastric pouch creation: divide the lesser omentum to expose the lesser curve. Create a horizontal division at the angle of His using a linear stapler (45mm blue load). Create the pouch (~25mL) by vertical division along the lesser curve, keeping the pouch small and excluding the fundus entirely. Pouch too large is the most common cause of weight regain. (3) Division of small bowel: measure the biliopancreatic limb (BP limb) 30–75cm from the ligament of Treitz; divide the jejunum. Measure the Roux (alimentary) limb 100–150cm from the division point distally and create the jejunojejunostomy. (4) Gastrojejunostomy: the Roux limb is brought up to the gastric pouch (antecolic or retrocolic, antegastric). Anastomosis performed by circular stapler (21mm, transoral anvil technique), linear stapler, or hand-sewn. Anterior row reinforced with absorbable sutures. (5) Mesenteric defect closure: ALL mesenteric defects must be closed (jejunojejunostomy mesentery + Petersen's space + mesocolic window if retrocolic). (6) Leak test: methylene blue or endoscopic air insufflation before closure.

*RYGB — Limb Lengths and Their Significance*
Limb | Standard Length | Modified for Super-obesity | Consequence if Too Long
--- | --- | --- | ---
Biliopancreatic (BP) limb | 30–75cm from Treitz | 150cm ('distal bypass') | Protein malnutrition if too long
Roux (alimentary) limb | 100–150cm | 150–200cm | Nutritional deficiency; diarrhoea
Common channel | Remainder (typically 250–400cm) | Minimum 100cm | Protein malabsorption if <100cm

> **Board Pearl — Antecolic vs Retrocolic RYGB** — Antecolic RYGB (Roux limb passes anterior to the transverse colon) creates only two mesenteric defects (jejunojejunostomy + Petersen's space). Retrocolic RYGB creates three defects (+ mesocolic window). Retrocolic technically allows a shorter Roux limb to reach without tension, but the additional mesocolic defect creates an extra site for internal hernia. Most current practice is antecolic to reduce internal hernia risk. Petersen's space must be closed in both configurations.


## Sleeve Gastrectomy — Operative Steps & Bougie Size

Laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy (LSG) resects 75–80% of the stomach along the greater curvature, creating a narrow tubular ('sleeve') gastric remnant. The mechanism of weight loss: restriction (small gastric volume) + ghrelin reduction (fundus — the primary ghrelin-producing tissue — is resected) + accelerated gastric emptying (improved satiety signalling). LSG operative steps: (1) Ports: 5–6 trocars; liver retractor; 12mm optical port. (2) Division of greater omentum from the greater curvature starting 4–6cm from the pylorus (preserve pylorus and antrum to reduce GERD and dumping). (3) Mobilisation continues to the angle of His — complete mobilisation of the fundus including the posterior adhesions is critical; fundus remnant is the most common cause of weight regain after sleeve. (4) Bougie/calibration tube inserted orally — size 34–40Fr (most evidence supports 36Fr as optimal balance of restriction vs leak risk). A smaller bougie (32Fr) creates a tighter sleeve with marginally lower weight regain but higher leak risk. A larger bougie (42Fr+) reduces restriction and increases weight regain. (5) Stapling: divide the stomach along the bougie starting 4–6cm from the pylorus, firing multiple loads of stapler (60mm blue/green loads). The staple line is reinforced with buttress material (pericardial strips, seamguard, or oversewing) — evidence is mixed on whether reinforcement reduces leak rates but is widely practised. (6) Staple line checked for haemostasis; leak tested with methylene blue or endoscopy.

*Sleeve Gastrectomy — Key Technical Points*
Step | Detail | Error to Avoid
--- | --- | ---
Antrum preservation | Start stapling 4–6cm from pylorus | Starting too close to pylorus → GERD + delayed emptying
Fundus mobilisation | Complete mobilisation of posterior fundus | Residual fundus → weight regain
Bougie size | 34–40Fr (36Fr most common) | Too small → higher leak risk; too large → insufficient restriction
Staple line | Parallel to lesser curve, following the bougie | Narrowing at the incisura → stricture; twist → obstruction
Angulation (incisura) | Avoid acute angulation at incisura angularis | Acute angle → outlet obstruction, dysphagia, leak

> **Sleeve Gastrectomy Leak — Recognition and Management** — Staple line leak after sleeve gastrectomy occurs in 1–3% of cases. It typically occurs near the angle of His (proximal staple line) — the most vulnerable point due to mechanical tension, poor perfusion, and angulation. Presentation: day 3–7, tachycardia, fever, increasing abdominal pain, rising CRP. CT with oral contrast is diagnostic (leak + perigastric collection). Management: (1) haemodynamically stable with small contained leak: NGT, NPO, IV antibiotics, CT-guided drainage or ERCP/EGD with covered stent or endoclip; (2) unstable or peritonitis: laparoscopy/laparotomy, washout, drain placement (cannot oversew a sleeve leak — tissue too friable). Persistent sleeve leaks (>4 weeks) may require Roux limb fistulojejunostomy or conversion to RYGB.

> **Common Pitfall — Post-RYGB Nutritional Deficiencies** — All RYGB patients require lifelong supplementation: multivitamin, iron (especially menstruating women), vitamin B12 (IM or high-dose oral), calcium with vitamin D. Thiamine deficiency (Wernicke's encephalopathy) can occur within weeks of excessive vomiting post-operatively — IV thiamine should be given to all bariatric patients with persistent vomiting before any IV glucose administration.

> **Board Pearl — Internal Hernia** — Internal hernia after RYGB occurs through mesenteric defects created during the operation (Petersen's space, mesocolic defect, jejunojejunostomy mesentery). Presentation is intermittent epigastric/periumbilical pain, often non-diagnostic CT between episodes. Any post-RYGB patient with unexplained abdominal pain should be evaluated for internal hernia — diagnostic laparoscopy if CT is inconclusive and clinical suspicion is high.

## Nutritional Deficiencies & Long-term Surveillance

Long-term nutritional surveillance is mandatory after bariatric surgery — deficiencies are common, progressive, and potentially life-threatening if untreated. Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency after bariatric surgery — occurring in 30–50% of RYGB patients, particularly premenopausal women. Iron absorption requires an acidic environment and gastric acid (which is absent or reduced after RYGB as the duodenum — the primary iron absorption site — is bypassed). Supplementation: oral ferrous sulphate or ferrous gluconate; IV iron sucrose or ferric carboxymaltose for severe deficiency or oral intolerance. Vitamin B12 absorption requires intrinsic factor (secreted by gastric parietal cells) and absorption in the terminal ileum. After sleeve gastrectomy (parietal cell mass reduced) and RYGB (gastric acid and IF production reduced), B12 deficiency develops in 30–40% within 5 years. Supplementation: sublingual or intramuscular B12 (bypasses intrinsic factor dependence); high-dose oral B12 (500–1000 mcg/day) is adequate if compliance assured. Vitamin D and calcium: calcium absorption requires vitamin D and an acidic gastric pH. After RYGB, both conditions are impaired. Calcium citrate (not calcium carbonate — requires acid for absorption) 1500–2000mg/day with vitamin D3 1500–3000 IU/day is the recommended supplementation. Failure leads to secondary hyperparathyroidism and metabolic bone disease. Thiamine (B1) deficiency can cause Wernicke's encephalopathy within weeks of surgery — particularly with persistent vomiting. NEVER give IV glucose without thiamine first in a post-bariatric patient with vomiting. Folate: supplement in all patients, especially women of childbearing age — crucial in pregnancy planning after surgery.

Nutrient | Prevalence after RYGB | Clinical Consequence | Supplementation
--- | --- | --- | ---

Vitamin B12 | 30–40% at 5 years | Megaloblastic anaemia, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment | IM B12 monthly or sublingual 1000mcg/day or high-dose oral 500–1000mcg

> **ASMBS Nutritional Surveillance Schedule** — Minimum post-operative nutritional follow-up: blood tests at 3, 6, 12 months, then annually. Tests: FBC, iron studies, ferritin, B12, folate, vitamin D, PTH, calcium, albumin, zinc, copper (for SADI-S/DS). DEXA scan: baseline before surgery, then every 2 years post-operatively to monitor bone density. Weight monitoring: BMI and % total weight loss at each visit. Protein intake: assess at every visit — target 60–80g/day for RYGB, higher for SADI-S.


## Metabolic Mechanisms of T2DM Remission

The mechanisms of T2DM remission after bariatric surgery are incompletely understood but involve both weight-dependent and weight-independent pathways. Weight-independent mechanisms explain why T2DM can improve dramatically within days of surgery, before significant weight loss has occurred. (1) GLP-1 surge (foregut/hindgut hypothesis): after RYGB and sleeve, rapid delivery of nutrients to the distal gut (L-cells in ileum) causes a massive postprandial GLP-1 surge — 5–10 times normal. GLP-1 stimulates insulin secretion (incretin effect), suppresses glucagon, and improves beta-cell function. (2) Ghrelin reduction: the gastric fundus (primary ghrelin source) is resected in sleeve gastrectomy and excluded in RYGB. Ghrelin suppression reduces appetite, and some data suggest ghrelin directly impairs insulin sensitivity — its reduction may contribute to early T2DM remission. (3) Bile acid signalling: RYGB alters bile acid composition and delivery. Bile acids activate TGR5 receptors in the gut, stimulating GLP-1 release. Altered bile acid profiles post-bypass contribute to improved glucose metabolism through FXR and TGR5 receptor pathways. (4) Changes in gut microbiome: bariatric surgery dramatically alters the gut microbiome composition, increasing bacteria associated with improved metabolic function. Microbiome changes occur within days and may mediate some early metabolic effects. (5) Caloric restriction (weight-dependent): after 3–6 months, sustained weight loss improves hepatic insulin resistance, reduces intracellular lipid accumulation in pancreatic beta-cells, and improves peripheral insulin sensitivity. STAMPEDE trial (5-year data): 23% T2DM remission after RYGB vs 5% medical therapy; RYGB superior to sleeve at 5 years (23% vs 18% complete remission).

**Landmark trial — STAMPEDE Trial:**

> **Board Pearl — ABCD Score Predicts T2DM Remission** — The ABCD score predicts likelihood of T2DM remission after bariatric surgery: Age <40y (1pt), BMI >35 (1pt), C-peptide level >2.9 ng/mL (2pts), Duration of T2DM <4 years (3pts) — maximum 10 points. Score >8 = 90%+ remission likelihood; score <3 = <50% likelihood. Short duration of T2DM (preserved beta-cell function) is the strongest predictor of remission — surgery is most effective before beta-cell exhaustion occurs.


## Revisional Bariatric Surgery

Revisional bariatric surgery is performed in 5–15% of bariatric patients and is indicated for: (1) inadequate weight loss or weight regain; (2) surgical complications not manageable conservatively (GERD after sleeve, marginal ulcer after RYGB, band erosion/slippage); (3) metabolic failure (T2DM recurrence); (4) patient preference or nutritional complications. Revisional surgery carries higher morbidity and mortality than primary surgery — complication rates are 2–3 times higher. The most common revision is conversion of sleeve gastrectomy to RYGB for: (a) severe GERD or Barrett's oesophagus (sleeve worsens GERD in 20–30%), (b) inadequate weight loss, or (c) weight regain. Conversion to RYGB resolves GERD in 85–90% of cases. Conversion of adjustable gastric band (LAGB): band erosion requires surgical/endoscopic removal; after removal, conversion to sleeve or RYGB after 3–6 months. Single-anastomosis duodenal switch (SADI-S) is an increasingly used option for revision after sleeve for inadequate weight loss — adds a malabsorptive component without creating the complexity of full RYGB. Endoscopic revisional options: transoral outlet reduction (TORe) for dilated gastrojejunostomy (RYGB weight regain), endoscopic sleeve gastroplasty (ESG) for weight regain after sleeve.

*Common Revision Scenarios*
Primary Procedure | Problem | Revision Options | Notes
--- | --- | --- | ---
Sleeve gastrectomy | GERD / Barrett's | Conversion to RYGB | 85–90% GERD resolution post-conversion
Sleeve gastrectomy | Weight regain | Conversion to RYGB or SADI-S | SADI-S preferred for high BMI
RYGB | Weight regain — dilated pouch/anastomosis | TORe (endoscopic); pouch revision | Endoscopic first; surgery if fails
RYGB | Marginal ulcer refractory to PPI | Revisional surgery: narrow pouch, remove foreign material | Exclude excluded stomach perforation/fistula first
Adjustable gastric band | Band erosion | Band removal; conversion to sleeve/RYGB after 3–6 months | Staged approach — single stage has higher complication rate

> **Board Pearl — Portal Vein Thrombosis After Bariatric Surgery** — Portal vein thrombosis (PVT) is a rare but serious complication of bariatric surgery — incidence 1–2% after sleeve (higher than RYGB). Presentation: fever, abdominal pain, elevated D-dimer, and nausea in the first 2–4 weeks. Diagnosis: CT with IV contrast (filling defect in portal or mesenteric veins). Treatment: immediate anticoagulation (LMWH or UFH, then warfarin for 3–6 months). PVT is associated with dehydration and hypercoagulable state — adequate hydration and early ambulation post-operatively are essential prevention strategies. Some centres routinely anticoagulate high-risk bariatric patients (BMI>50, prior VTE, Factor V Leiden) peri-operatively.
