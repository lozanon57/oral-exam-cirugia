# Colorectal Cancer — Surgical Oncology — Operative Principles & Evidence

*Block A · Surgical Oncology · PGY2-3*

*Guidelines: NCCN v2.2026 · ESMO Living Guideline 2024*


## Surgical Anatomy

**Case:** A 62-year-old man is referred with a 2-week history of rectal bleeding and change in bowel habit. Colonoscopy identifies a 3 cm mass at 8 cm from the anal verge. Biopsy confirms adenocarcinoma. He has no significant comorbidities (ECOG 0). His wife is present and very anxious about whether he will 'need a bag'.

The large bowel is invested by the **mesocolon**, a bilobed peritoneal reflection containing the vascular pedicles, lymphatics, and autonomic nerves. Understanding the embryological fusion planes is the foundation of oncological colonic surgery. The mesocolon is separated from the retroperitoneum by an avascular embryological fusion plane — the correct dissection plane in complete mesocolic excision (CME), analogous to the holy plane in TME.

The arterial supply of the colon derives from the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) and inferior mesenteric artery (IMA). The right colon (caecum, ascending colon, hepatic flexure, proximal transverse colon) is supplied by the ileocolic, right colic, and middle colic arteries from the SMA. The left colon (splenic flexure, descending colon, sigmoid) is supplied by the left colic and sigmoid branches of the IMA. The marginal artery of Drummond provides collateral flow along the mesenteric border — this continuity is critical when IMA ligation is planned in sigmoid and rectal resection.

The rectum extends from the rectosigmoid junction (approximately at the level of S3) to the anorectal ring, a total length of 12–15 cm. It is conventionally divided into three thirds: upper (10–15 cm from anal verge), middle (5–10 cm), and lower (0–5 cm). This classification has clinical importance: upper rectal cancers may be treated with anterior resection without full TME; middle and lower rectal cancers mandate complete TME with preservation of the entire mesorectum to the pelvic floor.

> **Board Pearl — Mesorectal Fascia** — The **mesorectal fascia propria** is the key anatomical structure in TME. Intact mesorectum on pathology correlates with <5% local recurrence at 5 years vs >20% with mesorectal defects (Quirke, Lancet 2009). Quality of the mesorectal excision is graded as: mesorectal (complete, smooth fascia), intramesorectal (moderate, irregularities not reaching muscularis propria), and muscularis (defects reaching muscularis propria — highest local recurrence risk).

The autonomic nerve supply to the pelvis is at risk during rectal surgery. The hypogastric nerves (sympathetic) descend from the superior hypogastric plexus at the level of the aortic bifurcation, coursing lateral to the presacral fascia. Injury during posterior mobilisation causes retrograde ejaculation in men. The pelvic splanchnic nerves (parasympathetic, S2–S4) emerge lateral to the rectum and are at risk during lateral dissection. Injury causes erectile dysfunction and bladder dysfunction. Bilateral nerve-sparing TME is achievable in most cases through meticulous dissection in the holy plane.

*Colorectal Blood Supply — Operative Implications*
Segment | Arterial Supply | Venous Drainage | Ligation Level for Cancer
--- | --- | --- | ---
Caecum / ascending colon | Ileocolic artery (SMA) | Ileocolic vein → SMV | High ligation at SMA origin
Hepatic flexure / proximal transverse | Middle colic artery (SMA) | Middle colic vein → SMV | Extended right hemicolectomy; middle colic at origin
Descending colon / sigmoid | Left colic + sigmoid branches (IMA) | IMA vein → portal vein | High tie IMA at aortic origin
Upper rectum | Superior rectal artery (IMA terminal) | Superior rectal vein → IMA vein | IMA ligation; preserve left colic if technically feasible
Mid/lower rectum | Middle + inferior rectal (internal iliac) | Middle rectal veins → internal iliac | IMA high tie; lateral vessels divided during lateral TME dissection

> **Common Pitfall — Presacral Venous Plexus** — Do not confuse the **presacral fascia** (Waldeyer's fascia) with the mesorectal fascia. The correct plane in TME is the areolar holy plane **anterior** to the presacral fascia — entry into the presacral venous plexus posterior to this fascia causes catastrophic haemorrhage, sometimes requiring thumbtack haemostasis. Always identify the correct tissue plane before committing to posterior dissection.


## Staging and Preoperative Workup

Colorectal cancer is staged according to the AJCC 8th edition TNM system, which provides the framework for prognosis estimation and treatment planning. Clinical staging (prefix c) precedes treatment; pathological staging (prefix p) follows resection. The distinction between clinical and pathological stage is fundamental — neoadjuvant therapy may achieve downstaging (ypTNM) that dramatically alters prognosis and treatment decisions.

*TNM Staging — Colorectal Cancer (AJCC 8th ed.)*
Stage | T | N | M | 5-yr OS (approx.)
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
I | T1–T2 | N0 | M0 | ~90%
IIA | T3 | N0 | M0 | ~75%
IIB | T4a | N0 | M0 | ~65%
IIC | T4b | N0 | M0 | ~55%
IIIA | T1–T2 | N1 | M0 | ~70%
IIIB | T3–T4a | N1–N2a | M0 | ~50%
IIIC | Any T | N2b | M0 | ~35%
IVA | Any T | Any N | M1a | ~20%
IVB | Any T | Any N | M1b | ~10%
IVC | Any T | Any N | M1c | <5%

Preoperative staging workup for colon cancer includes: CT thorax-abdomen-pelvis with IV contrast (primary staging and metastasis detection), colonoscopy with biopsy (histological confirmation, synchronous tumour exclusion — present in 2–8%), and CEA level (prognostic baseline; postoperative monitoring). For rectal cancer, additional mandatory investigations include high-resolution pelvic MRI (mesorectal staging, CRM assessment) and rigid sigmoidoscopy to precisely document distance from the anal verge.

> **MERCURY Study (2006, Lancet Oncology)** — **Population:** 408 patients with rectal cancer, preoperative MRI staging, prospective multicenter cohort. **Key finding:** MRI prediction of CRM involvement — sensitivity 77%, specificity 94%. MRI CRM distance of ≤1mm predicted histological CRM involvement with high accuracy. **Practice change:** High-resolution thin-slice MRI (3mm axial slices perpendicular to tumour axis) became the standard staging tool for rectal cancer, replacing ERUS for CRM assessment. ERUS retains utility for early T-stage (T1-T2) assessment.

MRI mesorectal staging reports should include: T-category (mrT), N-category (mrN), CRM distance (mm), extramural venous invasion (EMVI — associated with systemic relapse), relationship to peritoneal reflection, and distance from anal verge and sphincter complex. The MERCURY-II criteria for MRI-defined CRM threat (≤1mm) and the presence of EMVI on MRI are the two strongest imaging-based predictors of systemic recurrence and local recurrence respectively.

> **NCCN Rectal Cancer v2.2026 — Staging Algorithm** — All rectal cancer patients require: (1) complete colonoscopy; (2) CT chest/abdomen/pelvis with contrast; (3) pelvic MRI (preferred) or endorectal ultrasound; (4) CEA. PET-CT is not routinely indicated but may be considered for equivocal findings on CT or when staging is critical for management (e.g., borderline resectable liver metastases). **MSI/MMR status must be tested in ALL newly diagnosed colorectal cancers** — mandatory per NCCN 2026 — both for Lynch syndrome screening and to guide neoadjuvant immunotherapy eligibility. Reflexive biomarker panel (RAS/BRAF/HER2/MSI) should be requested at diagnosis anticipating metastatic course.

## Colon Cancer Surgery

Surgical resection of colon cancer requires complete removal of the primary tumour with adequate margins and an intact mesocolic envelope containing the regional lymph nodes. The operation is determined by tumour location, which dictates the vascular pedicle to be ligated and the extent of bowel to be removed. For all colonic resections, the fundamental oncological principles are: ligation of the feeding artery at its origin (high tie), en-bloc removal of the mesocolon within its fascial envelope, and harvest of ≥12 lymph nodes.

**Right hemicolectomy** is indicated for caecal, ascending colon, and hepatic flexure cancers. The ileocolic and right colic arteries (when present) are ligated at their origin from the SMA. An extended right hemicolectomy, including the middle colic artery, is performed for hepatic flexure and proximal transverse colon cancers. The resection includes 5–10 cm of terminal ileum, the caecum, ascending colon, and relevant transverse colon. Reconstruction is by ileotransverse anastomosis. The embryological fusion plane between the right mesocolon and Gerota's fascia (renal/retroperitoneal fascia) defines the posterior dissection boundary.

**Left hemicolectomy** for descending and sigmoid colon cancers requires ligation of the IMA. The level of IMA ligation is determined by tumour location: ligation at the origin from the aorta (high tie) maximises lymph node harvest and may be required for sigmoid cancers; ligation distal to the left colic artery origin (low tie) preserves the descending colon blood supply. The marginal artery continuity and sigmoid artery division must be carefully planned to ensure adequate perfusion of the anastomosis.

> **Board Pearl — Complete Mesocolic Excision (CME)** — CME, described by Hohenberger (2009), is the colonic equivalent of TME. It requires: (1) dissection in the correct embryological plane between the mesocolon and parietal retroperitoneum; (2) central vascular ligation at the arterial root; (3) removal of an intact mesocolon envelope. CME is associated with a higher lymph node yield (mean 32 vs 18 nodes) and improved 5-year cancer-specific survival (89.1% vs 81.9% in the Erlangen cohort). CME is now standard at high-volume centres.

**Case:** A 61-year-old woman undergoes laparoscopic right hemicolectomy for a pT3 N1b hepatic flexure adenocarcinoma. The operation is straightforward with high ligation of the ileocolic artery. Specimen pathology: 21 lymph nodes, 3 positive. Day 4 post-operatively she develops fever (38.9°C), tachycardia, and abdominal pain. CRP 280 mg/L. CT shows a 4cm pericolic fluid collection at the anastomosis with pneumatosis.


## Rectal Cancer Surgery

Total mesorectal excision (TME), described by Heald in 1982, is the standard surgical technique for mid and lower rectal cancer. TME involves sharp dissection in the holy plane between the mesorectal fascia propria and the presacral fascia, removing the entire mesorectum as an intact cylindrical specimen. Heald's original series reported 4% local recurrence at 5 years — a transformative result at a time when local recurrence rates of 25–40% were considered acceptable.

**Low anterior resection (LAR)** is indicated for tumours of the mid and upper rectum (>5 cm from the anal verge) where an adequate distal margin of ≥1–2 cm can be achieved with sphincter preservation. The operation requires: sigmoidectomy with high IMA ligation, complete TME to the pelvic floor, transection of the rectum with a linear stapler, and colorectal or coloanal anastomosis. A defunctioning loop ileostomy is typically fashioned for anastomoses ≤6 cm from the anal verge or when technical factors increase leak risk.

> **Common Pitfall — Distal Margin** — A distal margin of **≥1cm** is acceptable for mid-rectal cancers. For low rectal cancers undergoing intersphincteric resection, tumour-free distal margin of ≥5mm may be oncologically adequate. Do **not** sacrifice sphincter preservation for an unnecessarily wide distal margin when <5mm distal margin means the difference between LAR and APR — consult the MDT for borderline cases.

**Abdominoperineal resection (APR)** is indicated when sphincter preservation is not achievable — tumours involving the external sphincter, puborectalis, or levator ani, or when a tumour-free distal margin cannot be obtained. Standard APR includes: sigmoidectomy with IMA ligation, complete TME, extralevator abdominoperineal excision (ELAPE) for T3–T4 tumours at or below the anorectal junction, and end colostomy formation. ELAPE, described by Holm (2007), removes the levators en-bloc with the mesorectum and anal canal, reducing CRM positivity from ~30% (cylindrical APR) to ~10% in T3-T4 low tumours.

**Transanal TME (taTME)** is a hybrid approach in which the distal rectal dissection is performed from below (transanally) while the abdominal portion is completed laparoscopically. TaTME improves visualisation in the narrow male pelvis, potentially reducing CRM positivity and incomplete mesorectal excision rates in this challenging anatomy. The multicentre COLOR III trial (van der Pas, Lancet 2022) demonstrated non-inferiority of taTME to laparoscopic TME for CRM positivity (3.9% vs 5.8%) and equivalent short-term outcomes. TaTME requires specific training — a defined learning curve of approximately 40 cases — and should not be adopted without structured credentialling.

*Rectal Cancer Surgery — Procedure Selection*
Tumour Level | Procedure | Stoma | Key Technical Point
--- | --- | --- | ---
Upper rectum (>10cm) | High anterior resection ± partial mesorectal excision | Optional diversion | 5cm distal mesorectal margin beyond tumour
Mid rectum (5–10cm) | Low anterior resection + complete TME | Defunctioning ileostomy if <6cm anastomosis | Distal margin ≥1cm; protect hypogastric nerves
Low rectum (1–5cm), sphincter-preserving | LAR + complete TME ± intersphincteric resection | Mandatory ileostomy | Distal margin ≥5mm; assess levator involvement
Low rectum, sphincter-involved | ELAPE / APR | End colostomy permanent | Remove levators en-bloc; ELAPE reduces CRM+
T1 N0 favourable histology, <3cm | Transanal local excision (TEM/TAMIS) | None | Full-thickness excision; pathology review mandatory

**Key points:**
- TME (total mesorectal excision) is the oncological gold standard for rectal cancer — sharp dissection in the embryological plane between visceral and parietal pelvic fascia.
- The mesorectal fascia (MRF) is the critical margin in rectal cancer: CRM positive = tumour ≤1 mm from the inked MRF on pathology (UK definition).
- Surgical procedures: LAR (high/mid rectum, >5 cm from AV); ULAR + colo-anal anastomosis (low rectum, 3–5 cm); APR (tumour invading sphincter or <1–2 cm from AV); ELAPE for fixed/low T4 disease.
- Diverting ileostomy is mandatory for any anastomosis at risk (within 5 cm of AV, after neoadjuvant CRT, technically challenging) to reduce the clinical consequences of anastomotic leak.
- APR specimen quality (Quirke planes): plane 3 (mesorectal) = best oncological outcome; plane 1 (muscularis propria) = worse oncological quality.
- Lateral lymph node dissection (LLND) for lateral pelvic N+ disease is standard in Japan; in the West, neoadjuvant CRT followed by selective LLND for persistent lateral nodes >7 mm post-CRT is emerging practice.


## Neoadjuvant Strategies

The neoadjuvant treatment of locally advanced rectal cancer has undergone a revolution over the past decade, evolving from single-modality short-course radiotherapy or long-course chemoradiotherapy to complex total neoadjuvant therapy (TNT) regimens integrating full systemic chemotherapy before surgery. This shift is driven by two goals: improving local disease control (reducing CRM positivity and local recurrence) and addressing systemic disease earlier (reducing distant metastasis as the dominant cause of death).

*Neoadjuvant Options for Rectal Cancer — NCCN v2.2026*
Approach | Regimen | Indication | pCR Rate
--- | --- | --- | ---
Short-course RT (SCRT) | 25 Gy in 5 fractions | T3 N0, non-threatened CRM, immediate surgery | 5–15%
Long-course CRT | 45–50.4 Gy + capecitabine, 6 wks | cT3 with threatened CRM, cT4, N+ | 15–20%
TNT: SCRT + consolidation chemo (PREFERRED) | 25 Gy → FOLFOX/CAPOX → surgery (RAPIDO) | High-risk: T4, N2, EMVI+, mrCRM+ | 25–30%
TNT: Induction chemo + CRT (PREFERRED) | FOLFOX → CRT → surgery (PRODIGE-23) | cT3-T4 Nx, planned radical surgery | 26–28%
Checkpoint inhibitor — MSI-H (PREFERRED for dMMR) | Pembrolizumab or dostarlimab monotherapy | MSI-H/dMMR locally advanced rectal — Category 2A | Up to 100% cCR; surgery may be avoided
Watch-and-wait after cCR | Non-operative management | cCR on MRI + endoscopy after TNT or CKI — Category 2A (NCCN 2026) | N/A — 25% local regrowth at 2 years; 80–90% salvageable

> **Dostarlimab in MSI-H Rectal Cancer (Cercek, NEJM 2022)** — **Population:** 12 patients with MSI-H/dMMR locally advanced rectal cancer, neoadjuvant dostarlimab (PD-1 inhibitor) for 6 months. **Result:** 100% clinical complete response; all 12 patients entered watch-and-wait without surgery at median follow-up 12 months. **Practice change:** Neoadjuvant checkpoint inhibition is now being systematically explored for MSI-H rectal cancer — potentially eliminating the need for surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy in this molecularly defined subgroup. Testing MSI/MMR status in ALL colorectal cancers is now mandatory.

Watch-and-wait (W&W) — non-operative management after complete clinical response (cCR) to neoadjuvant therapy — has emerged as a validated management strategy for complete responders. The International Watch & Wait Database (IWWD, Renehan, Lancet 2016) demonstrated that patients achieving cCR after CRT have excellent organ-preservation rates (73% at 3 years) with oncological outcomes comparable to surgery in complete responders. cCR is defined by: absence of residual tumour on MRI, normalisation of previously pathological lymph nodes, and endoscopic mucosal whitening/flattening with negative biopsy.

> **Clinical Application — Watch-and-Wait Selection** — W&W requires extremely careful patient selection and intensive surveillance. Candidates must have: (1) confirmed cCR on MRI and endoscopy 8–12 weeks post-CRT; (2) willingness to comply with 3-monthly surveillance for 2 years; (3) anatomy amenable to endoscopic and MRI assessment; (4) histological confirmation of absence of residual disease. Approximately 25% of cCR patients experience local regrowth within 2 years — 80–90% of these are still salvageable with surgery. W&W should only be offered at experienced centres with structured protocols.

**Key points:**
- Long-course CRT (50.4 Gy + capecitabine/5-FU) achieves pCR in 15–20% and is standard for cT3 CRM-threatened or cT4/N+ rectal cancer (Sauer, NEJM 2004).
- Short-course RT (25 Gy in 5 fractions) achieves equivalent local control to long-course CRT (Swedish Rectal Cancer Trial, MRC CR07) — reserved for good-prognosis cT3 tumours not requiring tumour shrinkage.
- RAPIDO trial (Bahadoer, Lancet Oncology 2021): short-course RT → FOLFIRINOX → surgery (TNT) reduced distant metastases by 7% vs standard CRT; no OS benefit.
- PRODIGE 23 trial: FOLFIRINOX neoadjuvant → CRT → surgery improved 3-year DFS 76% vs 69% vs standard CRT.
- pCR (ypT0N0) after neoadjuvant therapy: prognostic marker, enables Watch-and-Wait NOM in select patients (GREC0-REG/OPRA trial criteria).
- Watch-and-wait (NOM): reserved for cCR confirmed by MRI + proctoscopy + CEA; 25% regrow within 2 years (salvageable in >90% of cases).


## Complications: Anastomotic Leak

Anastomotic leak is the most feared complication of colorectal surgery, occurring in 2–5% of colonic anastomoses and 5–15% of colorectal or coloanal anastomoses. The incidence is highest for low pelvic anastomoses — within 5 cm of the anal verge. Risk factors can be divided into patient-related (malnutrition, steroid use, diabetes, smoking, neoadjuvant CRT, ASA ≥3, male sex) and technical (inadequate blood supply, anastomotic tension, stapler malfunction, inadequate defunctioning).

*Anastomotic Leak — International Study Group Grading*
Grade | Definition | Clinical Features | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
A | Radiological/biochemical only | Rising CRP, small collection on CT, no systemic sepsis | Conservative — antibiotics, NPO, monitoring
B | Clinical deviation, no re-laparotomy | Fever, peritoneal signs, CT collection ± fistula | Percutaneous drain ± endoscopic vacuum therapy (EVT)
C | Re-laparotomy required | Systemic sepsis, peritonitis, free perforation on CT | Surgical drainage ± washout; Hartmann's or defunctioning

Endoscopic vacuum therapy (EVT) — a negative pressure device placed endoscopically into the presacral cavity — has emerged as a highly effective treatment for Grade B anastomotic leaks after LAR. EVT achieves cavity closure in 80–90% of cases, preserving the anastomosis and potentially avoiding permanent stoma. It requires weekly or twice-weekly endoscopic changes under sedation until the cavity is obliterated. EVT has largely replaced defunctioning loop ileostomy (without addressing the leak itself) as the management of choice for contained post-LAR anastomotic leaks at centres with EVT expertise.

> **Board Pearl — Anastomotic Leak and Local Recurrence** — Anastomotic leak after rectal cancer surgery is associated with a **doubling of local recurrence risk** — independent of tumour stage. The proposed mechanism is peritoneal tumour cell seeding from luminal contamination at the time of leak. This underscores the importance of defunctioning ileostomy for high-risk anastomoses and aggressive management of established leaks.


## Metastatic Colorectal Cancer — Liver Metastases

The liver is the most common site of colorectal metastasis, affected in up to 50% of patients during the course of their disease. Hepatic resection of colorectal liver metastases (CRLM) is the only potentially curative treatment — 5-year survival of 40–60% in optimally selected patients, compared to <5% with chemotherapy alone. The paradigm shift in CRLM management over the past two decades has been the expansion of resectability criteria rather than their restriction.

Contemporary resectability criteria for CRLM have moved from anatomical criteria (number, size, unilobar) to functional criteria: **achievability of R0 resection with adequate future liver remnant (FLR)**. The minimum FLR required for safe hepatic resection is ≥25% of total liver volume in a normal liver, ≥30% in patients who have received significant chemotherapy, and ≥40% in patients with underlying liver disease. Portal vein embolisation (PVE) of the liver to be resected induces compensatory hypertrophy of the FLR (30–40% volume gain in 4–6 weeks) and is the standard technique to increase FLR before major hepatectomy.

> **ESMO Metastatic Colorectal Cancer — Living Guideline 2024** — All patients with CRLM should be discussed at an **HPB-experienced MDT**. Resectability must be defined by a surgeon capable of performing the planned resection. The ESMO/EORTC categories: **Resectable** (upfront R0 achievable with adequate FLR); **Potentially resectable** (R0 achievable after downsizing with systemic therapy — typically 4–6 cycles FOLFOX/FOLFIRI ± targeted agent); **Unresectable** (no chance of R0 regardless of systemic therapy response). **2024 update**: For MSI-H/dMMR mCRC, pembrolizumab monotherapy is the Category I first-line standard (KEYNOTE-177); chemotherapy is avoided in MSI-H first-line. For BRAF V600E mutant: encorafenib + cetuximab preferred in second-line. ctDNA-guided anti-EGFR rechallenge is an emerging validated strategy for RAS/RAF wild-type patients. Comprehensive reflexive biomarker panel (RAS/BRAF/MSI/HER2/NTRK) mandatory at metastatic diagnosis.

Synchronous CRLM (present at time of primary diagnosis) occurs in 15–25% of colorectal cancer patients. The optimal surgical sequencing remains debated: primary first (most common in symptomatic primary), liver first (for large bilobar liver disease requiring multiple-stage surgery), or simultaneous resection (for selected patients with limited liver disease and uncomplicated primary). The choice is driven by symptom burden, oncological risk, and surgical complexity. Large RCTs addressing this question remain ongoing (e.g., SYNCHRONOUS trial).


## Colon Cancer — Operative Decisions

Operative decision-making in colon cancer surgery requires integration of tumour biology, anatomical location, patient fitness, and surgical expertise. While the principles of oncological resection (high vascular ligation, mesocolic excision, adequate lymphadenectomy) are universal, the specific decisions about approach (laparoscopic vs open), extent of resection, stoma formation, and reconstruction vary by case. High-volume surgical centres applying CME with laparoscopic technique achieve significantly better oncological outcomes than lower-volume centres using conventional approaches.

The management of obstructing colon cancer deserves particular attention. Left-sided obstruction (sigmoid, descending colon) in a haemodynamically stable patient is best managed by endoscopic self-expanding metallic stent (SEMS) placement as a bridge to elective surgery. SEMS decompresses the colon, permits staging workup, and allows optimisation of nutritional and functional status before planned laparoscopic resection. In the ESCO trial, SEMS-bridged elective surgery achieved primary anastomosis in 67% vs 43% in emergency surgery, with 8.4% vs 43% permanent stoma rate. Emergency surgery is reserved for perforation, peritonitis, or caecal ischaemia.

*Right vs Left Hemicolectomy — Key Technical Distinctions*
Element | Right Hemicolectomy | Left Hemicolectomy / Sigmoid
--- | --- | ---
Arterial ligation | Ileocolic ± right colic at SMA origin | IMA at aortic origin (high tie)
Embryological plane | Right mesocolon / Gerota's fascia junction | Left mesocolon / Toldt's fascia
Key structure at risk | Right ureter, duodenum, right gonadal vessels | Left ureter, IMA bifurcation, inferior mesenteric vein
Reconstruction | Ileotransverse anastomosis (handsewn or stapled) | Colorectal or sigmoid anastomosis (circular stapler)
Defunctioning stoma | Not routinely required | Consider if anastomosis <15cm from anal verge or high risk

> **Board Pearl — IMA Ligation Level** — High tie of the IMA (at aortic origin) maximises lymph node yield at the apical node (station 253 in Japanese classification). Low tie (distal to left colic artery) preserves left colic circulation. Current evidence does not show OS benefit for high vs low tie in stage II-III disease, but high tie is required for adequate lymphadenectomy when tumour is within 10cm of the IMA origin. Always document the IMA ligation level in the operative note.

Transverse colon cancer presents a technical challenge due to variable anatomy of the middle colic artery. Options include extended right hemicolectomy (for right-sided transverse tumours with middle colic ligation), extended left hemicolectomy (for left-sided transverse tumours), or transverse colectomy with anastomosis. Transverse colectomy is technically demanding due to anastomosis under tension and is generally avoided at high-volume centres in favour of extended hemicolectomy, which provides better lymphatic clearance and a tension-free anastomosis.

> **Common Pitfall — Ureteric Identification** — The left ureter is at particular risk during IMA ligation in left hemicolectomy and sigmoid resection. It crosses anterior to the left iliac vessels at the pelvic brim and runs immediately medial to the gonadal vessels. Failure to identify the ureter before IMA division is the most common cause of iatrogenic ureteric injury in colorectal surgery. If you cannot identify the left ureter clearly, do not divide the IMA until you have done so. Prophylactic ureteric stent placement should be considered for re-operative surgery, bulky tumours, or extensive adhesions.


## Lynch Syndrome & Hereditary CRC

Lynch syndrome (hereditary non-polyposis colorectal cancer, HNPCC) is the most common hereditary colorectal cancer syndrome, caused by germline mutations in DNA mismatch repair (MMR) genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2. Constitutional MMR deficiency (CMMRD) is a rare biallelic form. Lynch syndrome accounts for 3–5% of all colorectal cancers and 15–20% of CRC in patients under 50. The lifetime risk of CRC is 40–80% for MLH1/MSH2 carriers and 10–40% for MSH6/PMS2 carriers.

*Lynch Syndrome — Gene-Specific Cancer Risks and Surveillance*
Gene | CRC Lifetime Risk | Other Major Cancers | CRC Surveillance Start | Colonoscopy Interval
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
MLH1 | 40–70% | Endometrial (40–60%), ovarian, gastric, biliary | Age 20–25 or 5 yr before earliest family CRC | Every 1–2 years
MSH2 | 40–70% | Endometrial (40–60%), urinary tract, small bowel | Age 20–25 or 5 yr before earliest family CRC | Every 1–2 years
MSH6 | 10–40% | Endometrial (15–70%), ovarian | Age 25–30 | Every 1–2 years
PMS2 | 10–20% | Endometrial (15–25%) | Age 25–30 | Every 1–2 years

Surgical decision-making in Lynch syndrome is profoundly influenced by the high lifetime risk of metachronous CRC. For a patient with Lynch syndrome presenting with their first CRC, the choice between segmental colectomy and extended (subtotal or total) colectomy must balance: (1) the risk of metachronous CRC in the remaining colon (15–20% at 10 years with segmental resection); (2) functional outcomes after extended resection; and (3) the patient's age, gender, family planning, and compliance with surveillance. NCCN recommends discussing extended colectomy with Lynch syndrome patients presenting with their first CRC, particularly MLH1/MSH2 carriers under 60 years of age.

> **CAPP2 Trial (Burn, Lancet 2011)** — **Population:** 861 Lynch syndrome mutation carriers, randomised to aspirin 600mg daily vs placebo for 2 years. **Result:** Post hoc analysis at 4.5 years showed 63% reduction in CRC incidence (HR 0.37, p=0.02) in the aspirin group, with no benefit during active treatment. **Practice change:** Aspirin chemoprevention is now discussed with Lynch syndrome carriers — CAPP3 trial is evaluating optimal dose (100mg vs 300mg vs 600mg). NCCN notes aspirin 100–300mg daily as a reasonable option for Lynch syndrome carriers.

Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) is caused by APC germline mutations and is characterised by hundreds to thousands of colorectal adenomas. Without prophylactic surgery, CRC develops in virtually all FAP patients by age 40. Prophylactic colectomy is standard — options include total colectomy with ileorectal anastomosis (IRA) if the rectum has few polyps, or proctocolectomy with ileal pouch-anal anastomosis (IPAA). The choice depends on polyp burden in the rectum, rectal cancer risk, and patient preference. All first-degree relatives of FAP patients should undergo APC genetic testing.

> **Board Pearl — Universal Tumour Testing** — Universal MMR/MSI testing of all colorectal cancers at diagnosis is now recommended by NCCN, ESMO, and ASCO — not only when Lynch syndrome is clinically suspected. MMR testing (IHC for MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) costs <$200 and identifies: (1) Lynch syndrome carriers requiring family testing; (2) sporadic MSI-H tumours with poor 5-FU response; (3) MSI-H tumours eligible for pembrolizumab in metastatic disease. IHC loss of MLH1 should trigger reflexive BRAF V600E and MLH1 promoter methylation testing to distinguish Lynch from sporadic MSI-H.

**Key points:**
- Lynch syndrome: most common hereditary CRC syndrome (3% of all CRC); autosomal dominant mutation in MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, or PMS2 mismatch repair genes.
- Revised Amsterdam II criteria: 3-2-1 rule — 3 affected relatives, 2 generations, 1 <50 years old. But 30–40% of Lynch cases fail Amsterdam — use Bethesda/revised Bethesda for broader screening.
- Universal tumour MMR/MSI testing is now mandated for ALL newly diagnosed colorectal cancers (NCCN, ESMO 2023).
- Lynch CRC management: surgical options include segmental colectomy (routine surveillance) vs subtotal colectomy (Lynch confirmed, acceptable risk profile) — shared decision-making.
- Extracolonic surveillance for Lynch: annual endometrial sampling (women), annual urinalysis for urothelial cancer, upper GI endoscopy every 2–3 years.
- Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP): APC mutation; >100 polyps; prophylactic colectomy typically at age 15–25 before malignant transformation. AFAP: attenuated variant, <100 polyps, later presentation.


## Rectal Cancer — Neoadjuvant & Watch-and-Wait

The shift toward organ preservation in rectal cancer — maintaining the rectum and avoiding a permanent stoma — represents one of the most significant paradigm changes in colorectal oncology. This paradigm requires precision in: identifying complete clinical responders after neoadjuvant therapy, applying structured surveillance protocols, and managing the minority who develop local regrowth with timely salvage surgery. The evidence base for watch-and-wait (W&W) has matured rapidly, moving from expert-only practice to a guideline-endorsed management option.

The definition of clinical complete response (cCR) requires all four criteria to be simultaneously met: (1) MRI — normalisation of tumour signal intensity, no residual tumour mass, no pathological lymph nodes, tumour replaced by fibrosis; (2) endoscopy — flat white scar at the tumour site, no mucosal irregularity or mass, ulceration absent or completely fibrotic; (3) digital rectal examination (DRE) — no palpable abnormality; (4) biopsies from the scar site — negative for malignancy (at least 3–4 biopsies from the scar). Partial response does not qualify for W&W — these patients proceed to surgery.

*Watch-and-Wait — Surveillance Protocol (IWWD Standard)*
Timepoint | Assessment | Frequency | Action if Regrowth
--- | --- | --- | ---
Months 1–24 | MRI pelvis + flexible sigmoidoscopy + CEA | Every 3 months | Immediate salvage surgery referral
Months 25–60 | MRI pelvis + flexible sigmoidoscopy + CEA | Every 6 months | Salvage surgery if technically feasible
Annual | CT chest/abdomen/pelvis | Annually × 5 years | Systemic workup for distant recurrence
First 2 years | DRE at every visit | Every 3 months | Any palpable abnormality → re-biopsy + MRI

> **OPRA Trial (Garcia-Aguilar, JCO 2022)** — **Population:** 324 patients with locally advanced rectal cancer (cT2-T4, N0-N2), TNT (induction FOLFOX → CRT or CRT → consolidation FOLFOX). **Result:** Overall organ preservation rate 53%; induction FOLFOX → CRT arm achieved 59% organ preservation vs 47% for CRT → consolidation arm. pCR 28% in induction arm. **Practice change:** TNT with induction FOLFOX first maximises organ preservation rates compared to consolidation-first approaches.

Local regrowth after cCR occurs in approximately 25% of W&W patients, predominantly within the first 2 years. Critically, 80–90% of regrowths are detected at an early stage when the tumour remains endoluminally accessible and surgically salvageable — this is the justification for the intensive 3-monthly surveillance protocol. In the IWWD analysis, there was no significant difference in disease-specific survival between W&W patients with eventual salvage surgery and patients who underwent primary surgery, provided regrowth was detected early.

> **Common Pitfall — Non-Operative Management Eligibility** — W&W is only appropriate for patients who achieve a TRUE complete clinical response — not partial responders, not 'near-complete' responses. Accepting a partial response for non-operative management results in leaving viable cancer in situ with unacceptably high rates of local progression. When in doubt, discuss at MDT and obtain specialist endoscopy review before declaring cCR. Patient willingness and ability to comply with 3-monthly surveillance is equally important — patients unwilling or unable to comply should proceed to surgery.


## Metachronous & Synchronous Liver Metastases

Colorectal liver metastases (CRLM) are classified as synchronous (present at time of primary diagnosis, occurring in 15–25% of patients) or metachronous (developing after primary resection, occurring in 20–25% of patients during surveillance). This distinction influences management approach, surgical timing, and prognosis. Metachronous CRLM generally carries a slightly better prognosis than synchronous disease, though modern series with bilobar disease and multiple metastases have blurred this historical distinction.

For synchronous CRLM, three surgical sequencing strategies exist: (1) **Primary-first** — most appropriate when the primary is symptomatic (bleeding, obstruction), technically challenging to resect, or when liver disease is limited and does not require immediate treatment. Surgery is staged: colorectal resection first, then liver resection 6–8 weeks later. (2) **Liver-first** — appropriate when the liver disease is the dominant oncological risk (extensive bilateral disease requiring staged hepatectomy, borderline resectability) and the primary is asymptomatic. (3) **Simultaneous** — for carefully selected patients with uncomplicated primary and limited liver resection (≤3 segments), where combined morbidity is acceptable.

*CRLM Surgical Sequencing — Decision Framework*
Scenario | Recommended Sequence | Rationale | Key Criteria
--- | --- | --- | ---
Symptomatic primary + limited liver disease | Primary-first | Relieve obstruction/bleeding; CRLM resectable electively | Asymptomatic liver disease
Asymptomatic primary + dominant bilobar liver disease | Liver-first | Liver disease is the life-limiting problem | Asymptomatic primary; adequate staging
Asymptomatic primary + ≤3 liver metastases, same operation feasible | Simultaneous | Single anaesthetic; no oncological delay | Minor hepatectomy only; fit patient
Bilobar disease requiring PVE | Staged hepatectomy (two-stage) | FLR inadequate; PVE needed | First stage: clear contralateral lobe; PVE → second stage

> **Board Pearl — Two-Stage Hepatectomy** — Two-stage hepatectomy for bilobar CRLM: Stage 1 = clear the left lobe (or future remnant lobe) of metastases + right portal vein embolisation (PVE). Wait 4–6 weeks for left lobe (FLR) hypertrophy. Stage 2 = right hepatectomy to remove the remaining metastases. Overall 5-year OS 25–35%. Key failure mode: 30% of patients fail to reach stage 2 due to disease progression — this reinforces the importance of initial response to systemic chemotherapy as a selection criterion for this complex approach.

Ablative techniques — radiofrequency ablation (RFA), microwave ablation (MWA), and stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) — complement surgical resection in CRLM management. Ablation is appropriate for: (1) CRLM not amenable to resection due to proximity to major vessels or bile ducts; (2) patients with inadequate FLR who cannot tolerate further resection; (3) patients with recurrent disease after prior hepatectomy in whom further resection is technically impossible. Ablation should not routinely replace resection when resection is technically feasible — long-term local recurrence rates after ablation (20–30%) exceed those after resection (5–10%).
