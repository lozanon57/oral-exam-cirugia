*Block A · Surgical Oncology · PGY3-4*

*Guidelines: TARPSWG Retroperitoneal Sarcoma Guidelines 2023 · ESMO Soft Tissue Sarcoma 2021 · STRASS (Lancet Oncol 2020) · PRODIGE 7 (Lancet Oncol 2021) · OVHIPEC-1 (NEJM 2018) · ESMO Peritoneal Metastases 2023 · PSOGI HIPEC Consensus 2022*


## Retroperitoneal Sarcoma

Retroperitoneal sarcomas (RPS) represent a heterogeneous group of mesenchymal malignancies arising in the retroperitoneal space. The two most common histological subtypes — well-differentiated liposarcoma (WDLPS) and dedifferentiated liposarcoma (DDLPS), together comprising 40–50% of RPS, and leiomyosarcoma (LMS, 20%) — have markedly different biological behaviours, recurrence patterns, and responses to systemic therapy. Histological subtype is the dominant prognostic factor and should drive surgical strategy.

*Retroperitoneal Sarcoma — Key Histological Subtypes*
Subtype | Frequency | Recurrence Pattern | Chemo/RT Response | Surgery Strategy
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
WDLPS | 30% | Local recurrence dominant | Poor | Extended resection; preserve FLR and major vessels
DDLPS | 18% | Local + distant | Partial (trabectedin) | Extended resection; consider adjuvant RT
LMS | 20% | Distant (lung, liver) dominant | Moderate (gemcitabine/docetaxel) | Compartmental resection; systemic therapy priority
Solitary fibrous tumour | 5% | Late local/distant | Limited | R0 primary goal; long surveillance
MPNST | 5% | Local + distant | Limited | Wide resection; adjuvant RT for R1

> **TARPSWG Surgical Principles for RPS** — The Trans-Atlantic RPS Working Group (TARPSWG) recommends: (1) **Extended resection** for primary RPS — en-bloc removal of the tumour with adherent organs (ipsilateral kidney, colon, psoas muscle) accepted to achieve R0/R1 margins, even at the cost of significant morbidity; (2) Compartmental resection — removing the entire anatomical compartment occupied by the tumour; (3) Surgery at **high-volume expert centres** — outcomes are volume-dependent; (4) Multidisciplinary planning for complex vascular involvement (IVC, aorta).

> **Common Pitfall** — Do not attempt en-bloc resection of RPS without preoperative cross-sectional imaging reviewed by an experienced HPB/sarcoma radiologist. Vascular involvement (IVC, renal vessels, aorta) requires vascular surgery collaboration. Operating without a plan for major vessel reconstruction is dangerous.


## Cytoreductive Surgery + HIPEC

Cytoreductive surgery (CRS) combined with hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy (HIPEC) is the standard of care for selected patients with peritoneal metastases from colorectal cancer. The procedure achieves two goals: CRS removes all visible peritoneal disease (peritonectomy of affected surfaces); HIPEC delivers high-concentration chemotherapy (oxaliplatin 460 mg/m² or mitomycin C) directly to the peritoneal cavity at 41–43°C, exploiting the dose-intensity and heat-sensitisation effect to eliminate microscopic residual disease.

Patient selection is the most critical determinant of CRS+HIPEC outcome. The Peritoneal Cancer Index (PCI) quantifies disease burden — the abdomen is divided into 13 regions, each scored 0–3 based on the largest implant visible. PCI 0–20 is generally associated with acceptable outcomes for CRC-origin disease; PCI >20 predicts low R0 achievement and higher morbidity without survival benefit. The Completeness of Cytoreduction (CC) score grades residual disease: CC-0 (no visible residual), CC-1 (≤2.5mm residual), CC-2 (>2.5mm, any surface), CC-3 (>25mm, or unresectable). Only CC-0 and CC-1 resections are associated with survival benefit.

> **Verwaal RCT (Sugarbaker, NEJM 2003 / JNCI 2008)** — **Population:** 105 patients with peritoneal carcinomatosis from colorectal cancer, randomised CRS+HIPEC (mitomycin C) vs systemic 5-FU/leucovorin. **Result:** Median OS 22.3 vs 12.6 months (p<0.001); 5-year OS 45% vs 0%. **Practice change:** Established CRS+HIPEC as the only potentially curative treatment for selected patients with colorectal peritoneal metastases, and as the standard of care in experienced centres.

> **Board Pearl — HIPEC Patient Selection** — Optimal candidates for CRS+HIPEC: (1) Colorectal peritoneal metastases (no extra-abdominal disease); (2) PCI ≤15–20; (3) ECOG PS 0–1; (4) No hepatic parenchymal metastases (or limited, resectable); (5) Adequate cardiorespiratory reserve for 6–10h surgery; (6) Response or stability on systemic chemotherapy (excludes rapidly progressive disease). Synchronous peritoneal + liver metastases: case-by-case — combined CRS+HIPEC + hepatectomy carries high morbidity.

> **PRODIGE 7 — The Oxaliplatin HIPEC Controversy (Quenet, Lancet Oncol 2021)** — **Design:** 265 patients with colorectal peritoneal metastases, randomised to CRS + oxaliplatin HIPEC (460 mg/m², 30 min, 43°C) vs CRS alone. All patients received perioperative systemic chemotherapy (FOLFOX/FOLFIRI ± bevacizumab). **Result:** No significant difference in OS (41.2 vs 41.7 months; HR 1.00). No DFS benefit. Higher grade 3–4 morbidity with HIPEC (24.1% vs 13.6%). **Key criticisms:** (1) Short HIPEC duration — 30 min may be insufficient for adequate oxaliplatin exposure (Verwaal used mitomycin C with longer contact time); (2) High rate of systemic chemotherapy use equalises arms; (3) Very selected patient population (high-quality surgical centres). **Practice implication:** PRODIGE 7 challenged oxaliplatin-based HIPEC but did NOT invalidate mitomycin C-based HIPEC (Verwaal RCT). ESMO now recommends CRS+HIPEC 'only within clinical trials' for CRC peritoneal metastases; PSOGI and most expert centres continue CRS+HIPEC (mitomycin C preferred) for selected patients. The field remains active — CAIRO6 (mitomycin C HIPEC) results are awaited.

**Gastric Cancer Peritoneal Metastases and CRS+HIPEC.** Peritoneal dissemination is the most common pattern of metastatic gastric cancer (30–40% of stage IV cases) and carries a median OS of 3–6 months with systemic chemotherapy alone. CRS+HIPEC for gastric peritoneal metastases remains **investigational** — it is not the standard of care and should be offered exclusively within clinical trials. Patient selection is stringent: PCI ≤6 is the generally accepted threshold; signet ring cell (diffuse-type) histology is a relative contraindication (median OS <18 months even after CC-0 resection); response to perioperative chemotherapy (FLOT or FOLFOX) is mandatory to exclude biologically aggressive disease before surgery. HIPEC regimen for gastric cancer uses cisplatin (75–100 mg/m²) with or without mitomycin C at 42°C for 60–90 minutes. The GASTRICHIP trial (France) failed to complete enrolment. PERISCOPE-II (Netherlands, NCT02810784) is the key ongoing RCT evaluating CRS+HIPEC in stage IV gastric cancer with peritoneal metastases after systemic chemotherapy.

> **Common Pitfall — Gastric CRS+HIPEC Outside Trials** — Offering CRS+HIPEC to a gastric cancer patient with peritoneal metastases outside a structured clinical trial is not appropriate. The procedure carries 10–15% grade 3–4 morbidity and 1–3% mortality, and survival benefit has not been demonstrated in an RCT for this indication. Signet ring cell histology (diffuse-type gastric cancer), PCI >6, and progression on systemic chemotherapy are red flags that should prompt discussion at a multidisciplinary peritoneal oncology team meeting before considering any surgical intervention beyond palliative intent.

**Ovarian Cancer Peritoneal Metastases and CRS+HIPEC.** Unlike gastric and colorectal peritoneal metastases, CRS+HIPEC for ovarian cancer has prospective RCT evidence from the OVHIPEC-1 trial and a substantial evidence base supporting its use both at initial surgery and at first platinum-sensitive relapse. Ovarian cancer is, by nature, a disease of the peritoneal cavity — primary debulking surgery (PDS) or interval debulking surgery (IDS) after neoadjuvant platinum-taxane chemotherapy is the established surgical approach, with CC-0 (R0) cytoreduction as the primary surgical goal. The integration of HIPEC into ovarian cancer surgery has been evaluated at both the primary (OVHIPEC-2, ongoing) and relapsed (OVHIPEC-1, 2018) settings.

> **OVHIPEC-1 — Cisplatin HIPEC at Interval Debulking Surgery for Ovarian Cancer (van Driel, NEJM 2018)** — **Design:** 245 patients with stage III epithelial ovarian cancer after neoadjuvant platinum-taxane chemotherapy (3 cycles), randomised to IDS + cisplatin HIPEC (100 mg/m², 90 min, 40°C) vs IDS alone. **Results:** Recurrence-free survival 14.2 vs 10.7 months (HR 0.66; p<0.001). Overall survival 45.7 vs 33.9 months (HR 0.67; p=0.02). Adverse events grade 3–4 not significantly different between arms. **Interpretation:** OVHIPEC-1 established cisplatin HIPEC at IDS as a valid treatment strategy for stage III epithelial ovarian cancer, providing an OS benefit of nearly 12 months with acceptable toxicity. **Caveats:** (1) All patients had CC-0 or CC-1 cytoreduction; (2) Investigators were specifically trained; (3) Benefit may differ by histological subtype (high-grade serous benefited most). **OVHIPEC-2** (ongoing, NCT02681432) evaluates HIPEC at primary debulking surgery (no neoadjuvant chemotherapy). The trial supports routine use of cisplatin HIPEC at IDS for stage III ovarian cancer achieving CC-0/CC-1 cytoreduction.

*CRS+HIPEC Indications by Tumour Type — Evidence Level and Preferred Regimen*
Tumour Type | Evidence Level | HIPEC Regimen | PCI Threshold | Notes
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
CRC peritoneal metastases | RCT (Verwaal 2003; PRODIGE 7 controversial) | Mitomycin C 30 mg/m² or oxaliplatin 460 mg/m² | ≤20 | Mitomycin C preferred after PRODIGE 7; ESMO recommends 'trials only'
Pseudomyxoma peritonei (LGMCP) | Prospective registries (Moran 2019) | Mitomycin C 30 mg/m² ± cisplatin | No absolute PCI limit | High PCI no contraindication in LGMCP; CRS is primary determinant
Peritoneal mesothelioma (epithelioid) | Prospective registries; no RCT | Cisplatin 200 mg/m² + doxorubicin 15 mg/m² | ≤20 (selected <30) | Sarcomatoid histology: contraindication
Ovarian cancer (stage III) | OVHIPEC-1 RCT (van Driel 2018) | Cisplatin 100 mg/m², 90 min, 40°C | CC-0/CC-1 required | At IDS after 3 cycles NACT; OS benefit 12 months
Gastric cancer peritoneal metastases | Investigational — no RCT | Cisplatin 75–100 mg/m² ± mitomycin C | ≤6 (very selective) | Offer within clinical trials only; SRC histology: relative CI


## PIPAC — Pressurised Intraperitoneal Aerosol Chemotherapy

PIPAC (Pressurised Intraperitoneal Aerosol Chemotherapy) is a novel palliative treatment modality for unresectable peritoneal malignancy. Under laparoscopic access, chemotherapy is aerosolised and delivered under pressure (12 mmHg) into the peritoneal cavity. The aerosol creates a drug concentration gradient that penetrates peritoneal tissue to a depth of 3–5mm — substantially deeper than conventional intraperitoneal instillation. PIPAC is typically repeated every 4–6 weeks and can be combined with concurrent systemic chemotherapy.

PIPAC is not curative — it aims to control peritoneal disease and palliate symptoms (ascites, abdominal pain) in patients with unresectable peritoneal metastases from gastric, colorectal, ovarian, and mesothelioma origin. Histological response (tumour regression grading on sequential biopsies) is achieved in 50–75% of patients across tumour types. PIPAC is currently investigated in Phase II/III trials and should be offered within structured protocols at centres with PIPAC expertise.


## Pseudomyxoma Peritonei — Histological Subtypes & CRS+HIPEC

Pseudomyxoma peritonei (PMP) is a clinical syndrome characterised by mucinous tumour implants and gelatinous ascites resulting from rupture of a mucinous neoplasm, most commonly arising from the appendix. The term 'pseudomyxoma peritonei' describes the clinical presentation, not a specific pathological entity — histological subtype is the dominant prognostic factor. The revised pathological classification (Murphy/PSOGI 2016 consensus) stratifies PMP into three main groups: acellular mucin (AM), low-grade mucinous carcinoma peritonei (LGMCP), and high-grade mucinous carcinoma peritonei (HGMCP), with or without signet ring cells.

CRS+HIPEC is the standard of care for PMP. The PCI in PMP is typically high — often 25–39 — because mucinous disease spreads in a characteristic redistribution pattern to dependent areas (right iliac fossa, pelvis, greater omentum, right subphrenic space). Paradoxically, despite high PCI, the relative ease of complete cytoreduction (mucinous material is often amenable to stripping without extensive bowel resection in LGMCP) means that CRS+HIPEC remains feasible in most LGMCP patients even at high PCI, with excellent long-term outcomes. HIPEC for PMP most commonly uses mitomycin C (30 mg/m² over 90 minutes at 42°C), sometimes combined with cisplatin.

*PMP Histological Subtypes — Prognosis and Management*
Subtype | Murphy/PSOGI Term | Ki-67 | 10yr OS | HIPEC Rationale
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Acellular mucin | Acellular mucin (AM) | <1% | ~90% | Surgical cytoreduction may suffice; HIPEC for sterilisation of micro-deposits
Low-grade mucinous carcinoma | LGMCP (low-grade PMP) | <10% | ~63% | CRS+HIPEC standard; mitomycin C protocol; excellent long-term outcomes
High-grade mucinous carcinoma | HGMCP (high-grade PMP) | >10% | ~20% | CRS+HIPEC indicated if CC-0/1 achievable; systemic chemotherapy for non-cytoreducible
HGMCP with signet ring cells | HGMCP-SRC | Variable | <10% | Poor prognosis; systemic therapy; CRS+HIPEC in selected cases only

> **Board Pearl — Appendiceal Origin and Murphy Criteria** — PMP is almost invariably of appendiceal origin (>95% of cases). When peritoneal mucinous disease is identified, the appendix must always be examined. The Murphy criteria (2001) require: (1) appendiceal mucinous neoplasm as the primary tumour (LAMN or MACA); (2) peritoneal mucinous disease; (3) characteristic redistribution pattern. PMP from primary ovarian mucinous tumours is now considered exceptional — most cases previously attributed to ovarian origin were metastatic appendiceal disease. Bilateral ovarian 'Krukenberg-type' mucinous deposits in PMP represent secondary involvement and do not indicate ovarian primary.

> **Long-term CRS+HIPEC Outcomes in PMP (Moran, Lancet Oncol 2019)** — **Data:** Multi-centre registry study of 2200+ patients with PMP undergoing CRS+HIPEC. **Result:** LGMCP — 10-year OS 63%, median OS not reached; HGMCP — 10-year OS 20%; signet ring — median OS <36 months. **Practice relevance:** Long-term survival is achievable in LGMCP with CRS+HIPEC, validating the aggressive surgical approach even for high PCI disease. Completeness of cytoreduction (CC-0) is the strongest predictor of survival in all subtypes.


## Peritoneal Mesothelioma — Diagnosis & CRS+HIPEC Outcomes

Malignant peritoneal mesothelioma (MPeM) is a rare, aggressive malignancy arising from the mesothelial lining of the peritoneal cavity. It is distinct from pleural mesothelioma in aetiology, presentation, management, and prognosis. Asbestos exposure is an aetiological factor in approximately 30–40% of cases (lower than pleural mesothelioma). BAP1 (BRCA-associated protein 1) tumour suppressor gene mutation is found in approximately 20–25% of cases and is associated with germline susceptibility to mesothelioma — genetic counselling is warranted. BAP1-mutated MPeM is paradoxically associated with better survival (epithelioid histology more common).

Histological subtypes of MPeM are: epithelioid (most common, best prognosis), biphasic (mixed epithelioid and sarcomatoid), and sarcomatoid (worst prognosis, very rare in peritoneal). Diagnosis requires histological confirmation — the key differential is adenocarcinoma, which requires specific immunohistochemistry (IHC) to distinguish. Calretinin, WT1, CK5/6, D2-40 (mesothelial markers) are positive in mesothelioma; CEA, MOC-31, Ber-EP4 (adenocarcinoma markers) are negative. This IHC panel is mandatory on all core biopsies or diagnostic laparoscopy biopsies.

CRS+HIPEC is the standard of care for epithelioid MPeM with adequate performance status. PCI threshold for CRS+HIPEC in MPeM is generally considered <20, though selected patients with PCI up to 30 have been treated at expert centres. HIPEC regimen: cisplatin 200–250 mg/m² + doxorubicin 15–25 mg/m² (or cisplatin + pemetrexed) at 41–43°C for 60–90 minutes. Five-year OS after CRS+HIPEC for epithelioid MPeM is 40–60% at expert centres — significantly better than the historical median OS of 12 months with systemic therapy alone. Sarcomatoid histology is a contraindication to CRS+HIPEC due to futility.

*Peritoneal Mesothelioma vs Adenocarcinoma — Immunohistochemistry Panel*
Marker | Mesothelioma | Adenocarcinoma | Utility
--- | --- | --- | ---
Calretinin | Positive (nuclear + cytoplasmic) | Negative | Most specific positive mesothelioma marker
WT1 | Positive (nuclear) | Negative (except ovarian) | Strong positive in mesothelioma
CK5/6 | Positive | Negative (usually) | Squamous/mesothelial marker
D2-40 (podoplanin) | Positive | Negative | Lymphatic/mesothelial marker
CEA (carcinoembryonic antigen) | Negative | Positive | Key negative marker for mesothelioma
MOC-31 / Ber-EP4 | Negative | Positive | Adenocarcinoma markers; negative in mesothelioma
BAP1 (IHC loss) | Loss in ~60% | Retained | BAP1 nuclear loss supports mesothelioma diagnosis

> **Board Pearl — MARS2 Trial Context** — The MARS2 trial (Treasure, Lancet 2023) evaluated extended pleurectomy/decortication vs no surgery in pleural mesothelioma and showed no benefit from surgery. This is a **pleural mesothelioma** trial and does NOT apply to peritoneal mesothelioma — CRS+HIPEC for epithelioid MPeM has consistently demonstrated survival benefit in prospective registries. Do not extrapolate MARS2 to peritoneal disease.

> **Common Pitfall — IHC Differentiation from Adenocarcinoma** — Peritoneal mesothelioma can mimic adenocarcinoma histologically and should always be considered in the differential for peritoneal malignancy, particularly in the absence of a known primary. Request the full mesothelioma IHC panel on ALL peritoneal biopsies. Misdiagnosis as adenocarcinoma results in treatment with systemic chemotherapy regimens that are inappropriate for mesothelioma, delaying CRS+HIPEC evaluation at a specialist centre.


## Systemic Therapy in Retroperitoneal Sarcoma — By Histotype

Chemotherapy for retroperitoneal sarcoma follows histotype-specific principles — there is no universal 'sarcoma chemotherapy.' Sensitivity to standard regimens (doxorubicin ± ifosfamide) varies dramatically by histotype. Systemic therapy is generally reserved for unresectable, metastatic, or recurrent RPS, or as neoadjuvant therapy in specific scenarios. The fundamental principle is: histotype drives treatment selection — biopsy and molecular characterisation are mandatory before systemic therapy.

*Retroperitoneal Sarcoma Systemic Therapy — By Histotype*
Histotype | Chemosensitivity | First-Line Agents | Adjuvant Role | Notes
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
WDLPS | Very poor | Not typically chemotherapy (low-grade) | No established role | CDK4/MDM2 amplified; palbociclib investigational; surgery remains primary treatment
DDLPS | Partial | Doxorubicin ± ifosfamide; trabectedin (2nd line) | No established adjuvant chemotherapy benefit (STRASS was a radiotherapy trial — see STRASS callout below) | Trabectedin targets FUS/DDIT3 — activity in DDLPS; Eribulin shows OS benefit in liposarcoma
LMS | Moderate | Doxorubicin + dacarbazine; gemcitabine/docetaxel | No clear role for adjuvant | Gemcitabine/docetaxel active in uterine LMS; retroperitoneal LMS: doxorubicin-based first-line
Synovial sarcoma | High (SYT-SSX) | Ifosfamide-based regimens | Ifosfamide-based adjuvant considered | Highest sensitivity to ifosfamide; trabectedin 2nd line
SFT (solitary fibrous tumour) | Limited | Bevacizumab + temozolomide (Stacchiotti regimen) | No standard | VEGF pathway; risk stratification by Demicco criteria
MPNST | Limited | Doxorubicin + ifosfamide; MEK inhibitors (NF1-associated) | No clear role | NF1-associated MPNST may respond to MEK inhibition (selumetinib)

> **Board Pearl — Trabectedin in DDLPS** — Trabectedin (Yondelis) is a marine-derived alkylating agent with specific activity in liposarcomas and leiomyosarcoma through its interaction with minor groove DNA binding and RNA splicing factors. In DDLPS, trabectedin targets the FUS-DDIT3 fusion (created by the 12q13-15 MDM2/CDK4 amplification pathway). It is used as second-line therapy after doxorubicin failure. The phase III L-Sarcoma trial demonstrated improved progression-free survival (5.6 vs 1.5 months) vs best supportive care in liposarcoma and LMS. Eribulin also showed OS benefit in liposarcoma (L-sarcoma subgroup analysis).

> **Common Pitfall — One Size Does Not Fit All** — Standard empirical doxorubicin + ifosfamide is appropriate for some RPS histotypes (synovial sarcoma, some DDLPS, LMS) but is largely ineffective and potentially harmful in WDLPS (very low-grade, not chemo-sensitive) and SFT. Administering doxorubicin-based chemotherapy to a WDLPS patient exposes them to cardiotoxicity without benefit. Always confirm histotype before initiating systemic therapy. Molecular profiling (MDM2 amplification for WDLPS/DDLPS; SYT-SSX for synovial sarcoma; STAT6 for SFT) is essential.

> **STRASS Trial — The Only RCT in Retroperitoneal Sarcoma (Bonvalot, Lancet Oncol 2020)** — **STRASS (EORTC 62092)** is the only randomised controlled trial ever conducted in retroperitoneal sarcoma, and it evaluated **preoperative radiotherapy**, NOT chemotherapy. **Design:** 266 patients with primary RPS randomised to preoperative RT (50.4 Gy in 28 fractions) + surgery vs surgery alone. **Primary endpoint:** Abdominal recurrence-free survival (ARFS). **Result:** No difference in ARFS overall (HR 0.95, 95%CI 0.68–1.33; p=0.76). **Pre-specified subgroup analysis:** Significant benefit in WDLPS/DDLPS (HR 0.67; 3yr ARFS 60.4% vs 46.4%), but no benefit in LMS (local recurrence not the dominant failure pattern). **Interpretation:** Preoperative RT is not standard for all RPS; it may be considered in WDLPS/DDLPS at high risk of local recurrence (en-bloc resection not feasible, retroperitoneal location adjacent to structures at risk). **STRASS2** (ongoing) evaluates preoperative chemotherapy (doxorubicin + ifosfamide) in high-grade RPS.


## Quality of Life & Long-term Follow-up After CRS+HIPEC

CRS+HIPEC is a major surgical procedure with significant short- and long-term impacts on patient quality of life (QoL). The EORTC QLQ-C30 questionnaire, supplemented by the QLQ-CR29 (colorectal) or QLQ-OV28 (ovarian) modules, is the standard patient-reported outcome tool in CRS+HIPEC research. Understanding the trajectory of QoL recovery is essential for patient counselling and shared decision-making.

In the immediate postoperative period (weeks 1–8), patients typically experience a substantial decline across all QoL domains — global health status, physical functioning, fatigue, and pain — below baseline. Recovery follows a predictable trajectory: by 3 months, most patients regain pre-operative QoL; by 6 months, the majority return to baseline or exceed it (particularly in symptom-free patients). By 12 months, functional recovery is generally complete in patients without major complications or recurrence. Importantly, patients who achieve CC-0 cytoreduction and remain recurrence-free report QoL equivalent to age-matched healthy controls at 12 months.

Long-term sequelae after CRS+HIPEC include: nutritional impairment (bowel resection, short bowel syndrome in extensive small bowel resection; managed with dietary modification, enzyme supplementation, parenteral nutrition in severe cases); stoma formation (permanent or temporary ostomy in 15–30% of CRS+HIPEC patients); adhesion-related complications; and — specific to platinum-based HIPEC regimens — mild cognitive impairment and peripheral neuropathy from cisplatin exposure. Surveillance imaging (CT thorax/abdomen/pelvis with contrast) is recommended every 3–6 months for the first 2 years, then 6-monthly for years 3–5, then annually.

*Surveillance Schedule After CRS+HIPEC by Tumour Type*
Tumour Type | Year 1–2 | Year 3–5 | Year >5 | Imaging Modality
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Colorectal peritoneal metastases | CT every 3 months | CT every 6 months | Annual CT | CT TAP + CEA/CA19-9
PMP (LGMCP) | CT every 6 months | CT annually | CT every 2 years | CT TAP + tumour markers (CEA, CA125, CA19-9)
Peritoneal mesothelioma | CT every 3–4 months | CT every 6 months | Annual CT | CT TAP ± PET-CT at 2yr
Ovarian peritoneal metastases | CT every 3 months + CA125 | CT every 6 months | Annual CA125 + imaging | CT TAP + CA125

> **Board Pearl — Functional Recovery Timeline** — Patients undergoing CRS+HIPEC should be counselled to expect: (1) **Immediate postoperative** (weeks 1–4): fatigue, pain, poor appetite — worst QoL period; (2) **3 months**: significant improvement — most return to baseline physical function; (3) **6 months**: return to near-normal daily activities; (4) **12 months**: full functional recovery in uncomplicated cases. The 6–12 month functional recovery window is important for surgical planning discussions and return-to-work counselling.

> **Board Pearl — Recurrence Pattern by Tumour Type** — Recurrence pattern after CRS+HIPEC varies by tumour type and guides surveillance strategy: **CRC peritoneal**: peritoneal recurrence (40–60%) + hepatic (25%); **PMP LGMCP**: peritoneal recurrence dominant (local), very late (10+ years); **MPeM epithelioid**: peritoneal recurrence, occasionally pleural; **RPS recurrence**: local dominant (WDLPS/DDLPS) vs distant (LMS). Peritoneal recurrence after CRS+HIPEC can be re-treated with repeat CRS+HIPEC in selected cases — particularly PMP and MPeM with long disease-free intervals.
