# Hepatopancreatic-Biliary Surgery — Surgical Oncology — Liver, Pancreas, and Biliary Tract

*Block A · Surgical Oncology · PGY3-4*

*Guidelines: NCCN Hepatocellular v2.2025 · NCCN Pancreatic v2.2025 · ESMO 2024 · ISGPS 2023*


## Liver: Couinaud Anatomy and Resection Planning

The Couinaud classification divides the liver into 8 functionally independent segments based on portal pedicles and hepatic vein drainage. Each segment has its own portal triad (portal vein, hepatic artery, bile duct) and drains into one of the three hepatic veins. This functional independence allows individual segments to be resected while maintaining viability of adjacent segments — the anatomical basis of modern liver surgery.

*(Figure: Figure 1. Couinaud liver segmentation. The main portal scissura (Cantlie's line, midplane of middle hepatic vein) divides the functional left from the right lobe. Segments I–IV constitute the left liver; V–VIII the right liver.)*

> **Board Pearl — FLR Requirements** — Minimum **Future Liver Remnant (FLR)** for safe major hepatectomy: ≥25% in normal liver; ≥30% after significant chemotherapy (>6 cycles); ≥40% in underlying liver disease (cirrhosis, steatohepatitis). When FLR is insufficient, portal vein embolisation (PVE) of the resection side induces 30–40% compensatory hypertrophy in 4–6 weeks.

> **EORTC 40983 (Nordlinger, Lancet 2008)** — **Population:** 364 patients with resectable CRLM, perioperative FOLFOX4 vs surgery alone. **Result:** 3-year PFS 35.4% vs 28.1% (p=0.058, marginal). **Practice change:** Perioperative chemotherapy became standard for resectable CRLM in many European centres, though debate continues regarding upfront surgery for clearly resectable low-risk disease.


## Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple Procedure)

Pancreaticoduodenectomy (PD) is indicated for pancreatic head, periampullary (ampullary, duodenal, distal bile duct) adenocarcinoma and appropriate benign or low-grade malignant disease (IPMN with high-grade dysplasia, solid pseudopapillary neoplasm). The resection includes: head, neck, uncinate of pancreas; duodenum; proximal 10–15 cm jejunum; distal common bile duct; gallbladder. Pylorus-preserving PD (PPPD) avoids antrectomy and is equivalent oncologically with potentially better nutrition.

*Post-Pancreatectomy Complications — ISGPS Definitions*
Complication | Definition (ISGPS) | Grade | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
POPF (Post-op pancreatic fistula) | Drain amylase >3× upper limit normal on Day 3+ | A (biochemical), B (clinical), C (re-op) | Octreotide, NPO; drain; re-operation for C
DGE (Delayed gastric emptying) | Unable to return to solid oral diet by POD 7 | A (POD 7–14), B (POD 14–21), C (>21) | NG tube, prokinetics, TPN for severe
PPH (Post-pancreatectomy haemorrhage) | Haemorrhage >24h post-op from surgical site | Early (<24h) / Late; Grade B,C require intervention | Angioembolisation; re-operation

> **Common Pitfall — Vascular Resectability** — Pancreatic cancer is **borderline resectable** when the tumour contacts the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) or common hepatic artery (CHA) without distortion, or the superior mesenteric-portal vein (SMPV) confluence with ≤180° contact. Neoadjuvant FOLFIRINOX or gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel is standard for borderline-resectable disease. Arterial involvement >180° or >1cm length renders disease **locally advanced** (unresectable without vascular reconstruction expertise).


## Cholangiocarcinoma

Hilar cholangiocarcinoma (Klatskin tumour) is classified by the Bismuth-Corlette system (Types I–IVb) based on ductal involvement at the confluence. Resectability requires: uninvolved hepatic artery and portal vein on the side of remnant liver; uninvolved second-order biliary radicles in the remnant lobe; adequate FLR (typically right or left trisectionectomy). Preoperative biliary drainage of the FLR side is required when FLR bilirubin is elevated. R0 resection is the strongest prognostic factor — R1 5-year OS is <10% vs 30–40% for R0.

> **Board Pearl** — The **Bismuth-Corlette classification** for hilar cholangiocarcinoma: Type I — below confluence; Type II — at confluence, both hepatic ducts patent; Type IIIa — right hepatic duct occluded; Type IIIb — left hepatic duct occluded; Type IV — both hepatic ducts occluded. Types I-IIIa/b are potentially resectable; Type IV requires complex reconstruction or is unresectable.


## Pancreatic Cancer — Resectability Criteria & Borderline Disease

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) resectability is determined by the relationship of the tumour to the major mesenteric and hepatic vessels. NCCN 2024 and the International Study Group for Pancreatic Surgery (ISGPS) define four categories based on cross-sectional imaging. Resectable disease is managed with upfront surgery; borderline-resectable disease requires neoadjuvant therapy before reassessment; locally advanced disease is typically unresectable without major vascular reconstruction at expert centres; metastatic disease is managed with palliative systemic therapy. The distinction between borderline-resectable and locally advanced is critical because it determines whether the patient enters a neoadjuvant pathway aimed at conversion to resection.

*NCCN/ISGPS Resectability Criteria for Pancreatic Adenocarcinoma*
Category | SMA / Celiac Axis | SMV / Portal Vein | CHA | Approach
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Resectable | No contact | No contact or ≤180° without vein deformity | No contact | Upfront surgery (Whipple or distal pancreatectomy)
Borderline Resectable | Contact ≤180° (SMA) or any contact (CA) without involvement of aorta | >180° contact OR occlusion reconstructible | Short segment contact or encasement at GDA origin; reconstructible | Neoadjuvant FOLFIRINOX ± RT, then re-stage
Locally Advanced | Contact >180° (SMA); any CA involvement encasing aorta | Occlusion not reconstructible | Long-segment encasement | Systemic therapy; consider conversion surgery at expert centres
Metastatic | Any | Any | Any | Palliative systemic therapy (FOLFIRINOX or gem/nab-P)

Neoadjuvant therapy for borderline-resectable PDAC typically consists of FOLFIRINOX (oxaliplatin, irinotecan, leucovorin, 5-FU) for fit patients (ECOG 0–1, bilirubin <1.5× ULN), followed by stereotactic body RT (SBRT) or hypofractionated RT (50.4 Gy in 28 fractions) targeting the primary tumour and regional nodes. After 4–6 months, re-staging CT assesses response; CA 19-9 normalisation is a favourable prognostic marker. Conversion surgery — resection of a previously locally advanced tumour that has responded — is feasible in 10–20% of initially locally advanced cases and confers a survival benefit similar to upfront resectable disease in selected patients.

> **Board Pearl — Venous Reconstruction** — Segment resection and reconstruction of the superior mesenteric-portal vein confluence is oncologically acceptable in pancreatic cancer if R0 resection is achievable and the patient can tolerate the added operative complexity. Lateral venorrhaphy, patch repair, segmental resection with primary anastomosis, or interposition graft (autologous or prosthetic) are all described. Venous involvement per se is not a contraindication to resection at experienced centres — arterial involvement (SMA, CA) is the true barrier to R0.

> **Common Pitfall — Borderline Disease on CT** — Borderline-resectable criteria are frequently missed on standard CT reports. Always review cross-sectional imaging personally using a dedicated pancreatic protocol (triple-phase CT, 3mm cuts with MPR reconstruction). Contact between tumour and SMA measuring ≤180° on axial images is easily overlooked by non-specialist radiologists and may lead to inappropriate upfront surgery in borderline-resectable patients.


## Hepatocellular Carcinoma — BCLC Staging & Surgical Indications

The Barcelona Clinic Liver Cancer (BCLC) staging system integrates tumour characteristics, liver function (Child-Pugh class, MELD score), and performance status to guide treatment allocation in HCC. It is endorsed by AASLD, EASL, and ESMO as the standard staging and treatment framework. Each BCLC stage is linked to a specific treatment recommendation based on the best available evidence.

*BCLC Staging System for Hepatocellular Carcinoma*
Stage | Tumour | Liver Function | PS | Recommended Treatment
--- | --- | --- | --- | ---
0 — Very early | Single ≤2 cm | Child-Pugh A | 0 | Resection or ablation (RFA) — 5yr OS ~70%
A — Early | Single any size OR up to 3 nodules ≤3 cm | Child-Pugh A–B | 0 | Resection (if adequate FLR) / ablation / transplant (Milan criteria)
B — Intermediate | Multinodular, no vascular invasion, no extra-hepatic | Child-Pugh A–B | 0 | TACE (transarterial chemoembolisation)
C — Advanced | Vascular invasion OR extra-hepatic spread | Child-Pugh A–B | 1–2 | Sorafenib / lenvatinib / atezolizumab+bevacizumab
D — End-stage | Any | Child-Pugh C | 3–4 | Best supportive care; consider transplant listing if <10pts Child-Pugh C

Surgical resection for HCC requires: single tumour (no satellite nodules or vascular invasion on imaging); Child-Pugh A (MELD <10 preferred); adequate FLR (≥40% for cirrhotic liver — higher threshold than non-cirrhotic). Laparoscopic hepatectomy for HCC in Child-Pugh A cirrhosis is oncologically equivalent to open resection and associated with less post-operative liver failure due to reduced hepatic ischaemia and shorter hospital stay. The OSLO-COMET trial (2016) demonstrated laparoscopic superiority for minor resections in overall morbidity.

> **Board Pearl — Milan Criteria for Transplant** — The **Milan Criteria** for liver transplant in HCC: single tumour ≤5 cm OR up to 3 tumours all ≤3 cm; no vascular invasion; no extrahepatic spread. Patients meeting Milan criteria achieve 5-year post-transplant OS ~70% — equivalent to transplant for non-malignant indications. The UCSF criteria (single ≤6.5 cm or ≤3 tumours with largest ≤4.5 cm and total ≤8 cm) have been adopted at some centres without convincing survival disadvantage. Bridging therapy (TACE, ablation) is used during the wait period to prevent drop-off.

> **Common Pitfall — Child-Pugh and Resection Risk** — Child-Pugh B is a relative contraindication to major hepatectomy (segments ≥3) due to risk of post-hepatectomy liver failure (PHLF). Child-Pugh C is an absolute contraindication to resection. Even in Child-Pugh A, bilirubin >1 mg/dL and albumin <3.5 g/dL substantially increase PHLF risk. The ALBI (Albumin-Bilirubin) grade provides a more discriminating assessment than Child-Pugh and is increasingly used for surgical risk stratification in HCC.


## Biliary Tract — Cholangiocarcinoma Types & Management

Cholangiocarcinoma (CCA) arises from bile duct epithelium and is classified anatomically into intrahepatic (iCCA, 10–15%), perihilar/hilar (pCCA — Klatskin tumour, 50–60%), and distal (dCCA, 20–30%). Each anatomical type has distinct surgical management, prognostic profile, and adjuvant therapy considerations. Risk factors include primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC), choledochal cysts, hepatolithiasis, liver fluke infestation (Opisthorchis viverrini in Southeast Asia), and cirrhosis.

*Bismuth-Corlette Classification — Resectability and Operative Approach*
Type | Bile Duct Involvement | Typical Operation | Resectability
--- | --- | --- | ---
I | Below the confluence | Bile duct excision + hepaticojejunostomy | Resectable — liver-sparing
II | At the confluence; right and left hepatic ducts patent | Hilar resection + hepaticojejunostomy ± caudate lobe | Resectable
IIIa | Right hepatic duct second-order radicles involved | Right hepatectomy (±extended) + caudate + bile duct excision | Resectable
IIIb | Left hepatic duct second-order radicles involved | Left hepatectomy (±extended) + caudate + bile duct excision | Resectable
IV | Both right and left second-order radicles involved | Hepatectomy (typically right trisectionectomy) + caudate or palliation | Often unresectable; selected cases at expert centres
Distal CCA | Distal CBD (below cystic duct) | Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple) | Resectable in ~70%

Distal cholangiocarcinoma (dCCA) is managed with pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple procedure), which provides the best oncological result for lesions of the distal bile duct. Five-year OS after R0 resection is 25–40% for dCCA — superior to pCCA. For pCCA, caudate lobe resection (segment I) is mandatory because bile ducts from segment I drain directly into the hepatic duct confluence and are invariably involved in Bismuth II–IV tumours. Preoperative portal vein embolisation (PVE) of the future resection side is indicated when FLR is <40% (cirrhotic or cholestatic liver) or <25% (normal liver) to reduce the risk of post-hepatectomy liver failure.

> **BILCAP Trial (Primrose, Lancet Oncol 2019)** — **Population:** 447 patients with resected biliary tract cancer (CCA or gallbladder cancer), adjuvant capecitabine vs observation. **Result:** Median OS 51.1 vs 36.4 months (p=0.028 per-protocol analysis); HR 0.75. **Practice change:** Adjuvant capecitabine for 6 months became the standard of care after R0/R1 resection of biliary tract cancers. This is the only positive adjuvant trial in CCA and replaced the previous watch-and-wait standard.

> **Board Pearl — Portal Vein Embolisation Before Major Hepatectomy** — In hilar CCA requiring right trisectionectomy (segments I, IV–VIII), the FLR is often only segments II–III (~25% total volume). In a cholestatic liver (elevated bilirubin), the safe FLR threshold rises to ≥40%. PVE of the right portal branches (±right anterior sector) induces hypertrophy of segments II–III. PVE should be performed after biliary drainage of the FLR side (left lobe) to ensure the hypertrophying remnant is functional. Biliary drainage before PVE of the contralateral side is the critical sequence to remember.


## Post-Hepatectomy Complications — ISGPS Definitions

Major hepatic resections carry a risk of specific life-threatening complications beyond standard surgical morbidity. The International Study Group of Liver Surgery (ISGLS/ISGPS) has defined standardised grading systems for the most important post-hepatectomy complications, enabling uniform reporting and comparison across centres. These complications — post-hepatectomy liver failure (PHLF), post-pancreatectomy complications after combined resections, bile leak, and haemorrhage — must be recognised early and managed in a structured stepwise fashion.

*ISGPS/ISGLS Post-Hepatectomy Complications — Grading and Management*
Complication | ISGLS Definition | Grade A | Grade B | Grade C | Key Management
--- | --- | --- | --- | --- | ---
Post-Hepatectomy Liver Failure (PHLF) | Peak bilirubin >50 µmol/L on Day 5+ OR 50% rise from preop with INR >1.5 | Biochemical only; no change in clinical management | Requires change in clinical management; non-invasive interventions | Requires invasive interventions (dialysis, transplant evaluation) | Avoid nephrotoxins; nutritional support; plasma exchange in severe; transplant evaluation for grade C
Bile Leak | Bilirubin in drain >3× serum on Day 3+ OR bile peritonitis requiring intervention | Managed conservatively | Requires interventional radiology or ERCP | Requires re-operation | ERCP + stent for anastomotic leak; IR-guided drain for collections
POPF (after hepato-pancreatectomy) | Drain amylase >3× ULN on Day 3+ | Biochemical leak only | Clinical impact; drain maintained; antibiotics; TPN | Re-operation or percutaneous drainage | NPO, octreotide, drain management; IR drainage
PPH (Post-pancreatectomy haemorrhage) | Haemorrhage from surgical site >24h | Grade B: haemodynamically stable; angiography/transfusion | Grade C: haemodynamic instability; re-operation | — | Early: surgical haemostasis; Late: angioembolisation preferred over re-operation

> **Board Pearl — ALPPS for Small Future Liver Remnant** — **ALPPS (Associating Liver Partition and Portal Vein Ligation for Staged Hepatectomy)** achieves rapid FLR hypertrophy (7–14 days vs 4–6 weeks for PVE) by combining right portal vein ligation with in situ liver splitting in stage 1, followed by completion right hepatectomy in stage 2. ALPPS is associated with higher morbidity (30–40% major complications) and should be reserved for patients in whom PVE has failed or is not technically feasible, particularly those with rapidly progressing disease who cannot wait 4–6 weeks. The LIGRO trial (2022) showed improved R0 rates vs TSH (two-stage hepatectomy).

> **Common Pitfall — Post-Hepatectomy Liver Failure** — PHLF carries a mortality of 30–90% in Grade C. Early recognition requires daily LFTs and INR in the first postoperative week. The '50-50 rule' (bilirubin >50 µmol/L AND prothrombin time >50 seconds on Day 5) predicts 50% 90-day mortality. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast), ensure adequate nutrition, and involve hepatology early. Liver transplant for PHLF is possible at some centres but controversial given organ scarcity.


## Pancreatic Cystic Neoplasms — IPMN & Mucinous

Pancreatic cystic lesions are increasingly detected incidentally on cross-sectional imaging. The most common cystic neoplasms with malignant potential are intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms (IPMN) and mucinous cystic neoplasms (MCN). Serous cystadenomas and pseudocysts lack malignant potential and can be managed conservatively. Accurate classification is critical because management — from surveillance to surgical resection — depends entirely on the correct diagnosis and risk stratification.

IPMN arises from the pancreatic ductal epithelium and is classified as main-duct (MD-IPMN), branch-duct (BD-IPMN), or mixed-type. MD-IPMN has a high malignancy rate (40–70%) and surgical resection is recommended for all patients who are fit. BD-IPMN has a lower malignancy risk (15–25%) and is managed according to the 2017 revised Fukuoka Guidelines, which distinguish 'worrisome features' from 'high-risk stigmata'. Mucinous cystic neoplasm (MCN) occurs predominantly in women (>90%), in the body/tail, does not communicate with the duct, has an ovarian-type stroma, and is resected electively when symptomatic or increasing in size, given malignant transformation risk of ~10–15%.

*Fukuoka 2017 Criteria for Branch-Duct IPMN Management*
Feature Category | Features | Action
--- | --- | ---
High-Risk Stigmata (resect) | Obstructive jaundice with pancreatic cyst; enhancing solid component; main duct ≥10 mm | Surgical resection recommended in all operable patients
Worrisome Features (EUS ± resect) | Cyst ≥3 cm; enhancing cyst wall; main duct 5–9 mm; abrupt MPD calibre change with upstream atrophy; lymphadenopathy; elevated CA 19-9; cyst growth ≥5mm/2yr; new-onset DM | EUS-FNA; resection if concerning cytology/EUS features
Low-risk (surveillance) | BD-IPMN <3 cm; no worrisome features or high-risk stigmata | MRI surveillance: 6m, 1yr, then annually if stable; indefinite for young patients

*Surveillance Intervals for Low-Risk BD-IPMN (Fukuoka 2017)*
Cyst Size | Initial Surveillance Interval | Subsequent Interval
--- | --- | ---
<1 cm | CT/MRI in 2–3 years | Every 2 years
1–2 cm | CT/MRI annually for 2 years | Every 1–2 years if stable
2–3 cm | EUS in 3–6 months, then MRI every 6–12 months | Alternate MRI and EUS every 6 months; resection if equivocal
>3 cm (no worrisome features) | EUS alternate with MRI every 3–6 months | Consider resection in young fit patients given cumulative risk

> **Board Pearl — MCN vs IPMN Distinction** — **Key distinction for boards:** MCN occurs almost exclusively in women; does NOT communicate with the pancreatic duct (confirmed by MRCP or EUS); has ovarian-type stroma on pathology; located in body/tail; typically unilocular or oligolocular. IPMN communicates with the duct (demonstrates mucin extruding from papilla at ERCP = pathognomonic), occurs in either sex, any age, any pancreatic location. On MRI: communication with main duct differentiates IPMN from MCN. This distinction matters because all resectable MCNs should be resected (elective); IPMN follow risk stratification.
