## Acute Appendicitis

Acute appendicitis is the most common cause of emergency surgery worldwide, with a lifetime risk of 7–8%. The diagnosis is clinical, supported by imaging (CT with IV contrast has sensitivity >95%, specificity >95% in adults). The Alvarado score (≥7 = high probability) and AAAS (Adult Appendicitis Score) stratify diagnostic probability and guide imaging decisions. CT is preferred in adults; ultrasound is first-line in children and pregnant women (to avoid radiation). The Alvarado score allocates points as follows: migration of pain to RIF (1), anorexia (1), nausea/vomiting (1), RIF tenderness (2), rebound tenderness (1), elevated temperature (1), leukocytosis (2) — maximum 10 points. A score ≥7 predicts appendicitis with high probability; 4–6 is equivocal and warrants CT; ≤3 is low risk. CRP adds independent diagnostic value, particularly when elevated >80 mg/L in combination with leukocytosis.

*WSES Classification — Acute Appendicitis*
Grade | Pathology | CT Findings | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
WSES I (uncomplicated) | Acute non-perforated appendicitis | Enlarged appendix, periappendiceal fat stranding, no perforation | Laparoscopic appendicectomy (or antibiotics alone in selected pts)
WSES II (complicated) | Perforated appendicitis, local peritonitis | Perforation, localised free fluid, no abscess | Emergency laparoscopic appendicectomy
WSES III (complicated) | Periappendiceal abscess | Walled-off collection >3cm | Non-operative: IV antibiotics + CT-guided drainage; interval appendicectomy optional
WSES IV (complicated) | Diffuse peritonitis | Free intraperitoneal fluid and air, diffuse contamination | Emergency surgery — laparoscopic or open; peritoneal toilet

> **Board Pearl — Antibiotic-First for Uncomplicated Appendicitis (WSES 2025)** — The **WSES Jerusalem Guidelines 2025 edition** (JAMA Surgery, PMID 41604201) formally endorses non-operative management (NOM) with antibiotics as a safe, evidence-based alternative to appendicectomy for uncomplicated appendicitis across all populations (adults, children, elderly, pregnant women, obese patients — with population-specific guidance). The DIAMOND (2021), APPAC (2015), and CODA (2020) trials support this: ~70% success at 1 year, ~30% recurrence requiring eventual appendicectomy. New in 2025 WSES: (1) Appendicectomy can be safely **delayed up to 24 hours** without adverse outcomes. (2) Interval colonoscopy/imaging mandatory after NOM of complicated appendicitis with abscess to exclude underlying neoplasm. (3) Short-course post-operative antibiotics (2–3 days maximum) for complicated appendicitis. Shared decision-making is the cornerstone — document the conversation.

> **Common Pitfall** — Never delay surgery for imaging if the patient has peritonitis, haemodynamic instability, or septic shock — clinical diagnosis alone is sufficient for immediate theatre in this setting.


## Perforated Peptic Ulcer

Perforated peptic ulcer (PPU) carries 20–30% mortality in elderly and high-risk patients. Clinical diagnosis: sudden-onset severe epigastric pain, board-like rigidity, free air on erect CXR or CT. CT (sensitivity >95%) identifies perforation site, amount of free gas/fluid, and adjacent organ involvement. Initial resuscitation: IV access, crystalloid, NGT, urinary catheter, antibiotics, PPI. Boey score (1981) risk-stratifies operative mortality: shock on admission (SBP<90), serious medical illness, delayed presentation >24h — each adds 1 point; Boey 3 = >90% mortality. The surgical standard of care is omental patch repair (Graham patch) — either open or laparoscopic. H. pylori eradication is mandatory post-operatively; NSAIDs should be stopped and PPI started long-term. Definitive ulcer surgery (highly selective vagotomy) is rarely performed in the modern era given effective acid suppression.

*Operative vs Non-Operative Management — Perforated Peptic Ulcer*
Factor | Favours Surgery | Favours Non-operative (Taylor)
--- | --- | ---
Clinical status | Peritonitis, septic shock | Stable; sealed perforation on CT
CT findings | Large perforation, free fluid | No free fluid; contained perforation
Duration | Any (peritonitis) | <24h from onset; no peritonitis
Age/comorbidity | Any age with peritonitis | High surgical risk; no peritonitis
Response to resuscitation | Non-responder | Rapid improvement with IV fluids/antibiotics

> **Board Pearl — Laparoscopic vs Open Repair** — Laparoscopic omental patch repair (Graham patch) is equivalent to open repair for PPU in stable patients — meta-analysis (Søreide, Br J Surg 2015): equivalent leak rate, shorter hospital stay. Key requirements: surgeon laparoscopic experience, adequate suturing capability, low estimated contamination. Convert to open if: dense adhesions, large perforation (>10mm), haemodynamic instability, or technically unsafe.


## Small Bowel Obstruction

Adhesive small bowel obstruction (ASBO) accounts for 75% of SBO in adults. Causes: adhesions (70%), hernias (10%), neoplasm (5%), Crohn's (3%), volvulus (2%). Diagnosis: clinical (colicky pain, nausea, vomiting, obstipation) + imaging (CT is preferred — identifies level, cause, and signs of strangulation: mesenteric oedema, free fluid, absent mucosal enhancement). Water-soluble contrast follow-through (Gastrografin challenge) has both diagnostic and therapeutic utility — passage to colon at 24h predicts non-operative resolution with 97% sensitivity. Non-operative management with NGT decompression, IV fluids, and nil by mouth succeeds in 70–80% of ASBO without strangulation. Failure criteria: no improvement at 48–72h, clinical deterioration, or signs of strangulation. A closed-loop obstruction (two points of obstruction creating a sealed segment) carries high risk of rapid ischaemia and demands emergency operation — CT typically shows a U-shaped or C-shaped bowel loop with mesenteric twist ('whirlpool sign').

> **Signs of Strangulation — Operate Immediately** — CT signs mandating emergency surgery: absent mucosal enhancement (ischaemia), pneumatosis intestinalis, mesenteric vessel occlusion, closed-loop obstruction, portal venous gas. Clinical signs: persistent localised tenderness, fever, tachycardia unresponsive to resuscitation, rising lactate. Do not delay for serial imaging if strangulation is clinically suspected.

> **EAST Guidelines — ASBO Management** — EAST 2013 guidelines: operative intervention required if (1) strangulation present or suspected, (2) complete obstruction without prior abdominal surgery (hernia or de novo cause likely), (3) failure of non-operative management at 3–5 days. Water-soluble contrast challenge is recommended when non-operative management is pursued — passage to colon within 24h has 97% sensitivity for predicting success.


## Damage Control Surgery

Damage control surgery (DCS) is the staged surgical approach for haemodynamically unstable patients (abdominal trauma, surgical emergency) who cannot tolerate definitive surgery. Phase 1 (Haemostasis and contamination control): pack bleeding, control gross contamination, temporary abdominal closure (TAC). Phase 2 (ICU resuscitation): correct the 'lethal triad' — hypothermia (<35°C), acidosis (pH<7.2), coagulopathy (INR>1.5). Phase 3 (Definitive surgery): re-laparotomy within 24–48h when physiology corrected; remove packs, restore anatomy, close abdomen. DCS has reduced mortality for damage control scenarios by 20–30% compared to primary definitive surgery in unstable patients. Temporary abdominal closure (TAC) techniques include the Bogota bag, ABThera negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT), and Wittmann patch. NPWT (Vacuum-assisted closure) is the preferred TAC method — it removes fluid, reduces oedema, and facilitates fascial closure. Primary fascial closure is achieved in >70% of patients managed with NPWT-based TAC when closed within 72–96h.

*Damage Control Surgery — Three Phases*
Phase | Timing | Goal | Key Actions
--- | --- | --- | ---
Phase 1 — Abbreviated surgery | Intraoperative | Survival: stop bleeding, stop contamination | Packing, vascular shunts, staple bowel, TAC, leave abdomen open
Phase 2 — ICU resuscitation | Hours to days | Correct lethal triad | Correct hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy; massive transfusion protocol; vasopressors
Phase 3 — Definitive surgery | 24–48h after physiology corrected | Restore anatomy | Remove packs, bowel anastomosis, fascial closure, washout

> **Board Pearl — Negative Pressure Wound Therapy for Open Abdomen** — NPWT (ABThera system) is the gold standard for temporary abdominal closure after damage control laparotomy. It removes peritoneal exudate (up to 1L/day), prevents fascial retraction, and reduces risk of enteroatmospheric fistula compared to Bogota bag. Achieve primary fascial closure as early as physiology allows (ideally within 72–96h) — delay beyond 7–10 days dramatically reduces closure rate and increases fistula risk.


## Acute Diverticulitis — WSES Classification & Management

Acute colonic diverticulitis is the most common complication of diverticular disease, affecting 10–25% of patients with diverticulosis during their lifetime. The sigmoid colon is involved in 90% of cases. Diagnosis: CT with IV contrast is the imaging of choice (sensitivity 93–97%, specificity 100%) — demonstrates pericolic fat stranding, thickened bowel wall, and complications (abscess, perforation, fistula). CRP >150 mg/L correlates strongly with complicated disease. The WSES classification (2020) stratifies severity and guides management decisions. Uncomplicated diverticulitis (WSES Ia/Ib) can often be managed as an outpatient with oral antibiotics or, in selected immunocompetent patients, without antibiotics at all (AVOD trial, Chabok, Br J Surg 2012 — no antibiotic equivalent to antibiotics for mild uncomplicated diverticulitis). Complicated diverticulitis (WSES II–IV) requires hospitalisation, IV antibiotics, and may require drainage or surgery.

*WSES Classification — Acute Diverticulitis*
Grade | Hinchey | Description | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
WSES Ia (uncomplicated) | 0 | Mild clinical diverticulitis; CT: pericolic fat stranding only | Outpatient; oral antibiotics or observation
WSES Ib (uncomplicated) | 0 | Moderate diverticulitis; CT: pericolic fat stranding + wall thickening | Oral antibiotics; outpatient if tolerating PO
WSES II (complicated) | Ia/Ib | Pericolic/mesocolic abscess <4cm or >4cm | IV antibiotics; CT-guided drainage if >4cm
WSES III (complicated) | II | Purulent peritonitis — perforated diverticulitis with generalised peritoneum | Emergency surgery — laparoscopic lavage OR resection
WSES IV (complicated) | III/IV | Faecal peritonitis — free perforation | Emergency surgery — Hartmann's procedure or primary anastomosis with loop ileostomy

> **Hartmann's Procedure vs Primary Anastomosis — WSES 2020** — For perforated diverticulitis (Hinchey III/IV), both Hartmann's procedure and primary anastomosis with loop ileostomy (PA+LI) are acceptable options. Meta-analyses and the LADIES trial (Vennix, Lancet 2019) show equivalent mortality but higher reversal rates with PA+LI (94% vs 57% for Hartmann's). PA+LI is preferred in haemodynamically stable, non-immunocompromised patients operated by experienced laparoscopic surgeons. Hartmann's remains appropriate for haemodynamically unstable patients, faecal contamination, or inexperienced surgeons.

**Ensayo de referencia — LADIES Trial:**

> **Board Pearl — Elective Sigmoid Resection After Diverticulitis** — Elective sigmoid colectomy after uncomplicated diverticulitis is no longer routinely recommended after a single episode. WSES 2020 guidelines recommend individualised decision-making based on: patient age (<50 has higher recurrence risk), immunosuppression, persistence of symptoms, and patient preference. After complicated diverticulitis (abscess, fistula), elective resection is generally recommended 6–8 weeks after recovery to reduce risk of recurrence with complications.


## Lower GI Bleeding — Diagnosis & Intervention

Lower gastrointestinal bleeding (LGIB) is defined as bleeding distal to the ligament of Treitz. Common causes: diverticulosis (30–40%), anorectal causes (haemorrhoids, fissure — 15%), ischaemic colitis (10%), colitis (IBD, infectious — 10%), angiodysplasia/angioectasia (5–10%), neoplasm (5%). Haemodynamic stability determines the urgency of investigation. Initial assessment: haemodynamic resuscitation (IV access, type and screen, transfusion threshold Hb<7g/dL in stable patients), exclude upper GI source (NGT or upper endoscopy if haematemesis or haematochezia with haemodynamic instability — 15% of apparent LGIB is actually UGIB). CT angiography (CTA) is the diagnostic investigation of choice in active LGIB — sensitivity 85–90% for active bleeding (>0.3–0.5mL/min); identifies the bleeding vessel and guides selective mesenteric angiography. Colonoscopy is the definitive diagnostic and therapeutic tool for LGIB — should be performed after adequate bowel preparation within 24h of presentation in haemodynamically stable patients (urgent colonoscopy within 8–12h in high-risk patients per ESGE 2021). Endoscopic haemostasis (adrenaline injection, thermal contact, clips) achieves haemostasis in 85% of colonic causes.

*LGIB Management Algorithm*
Patient Status | Initial Step | Definitive Investigation | Intervention
--- | --- | --- | ---
Haemodynamically stable, bleeding settled | Resuscitate; outpatient if stable | Elective colonoscopy within 24h | Endoscopic haemostasis if source identified
Active bleeding, stable | Resuscitate; IV access; transfuse if Hb<7 | CTA → colonoscopy OR angiography | Endoscopic or angiographic embolisation
Active bleeding, unstable | Massive resuscitation; consider ICU | CTA → interventional radiology (embolisation) | Superselective embolisation; surgery if failed
Refractory/recurrent, source unknown | Multidisciplinary (IR + endoscopy + surgery) | Tagged RBC scan; capsule endoscopy | Surgery (segmental colectomy if localised; subtotal colectomy if diffuse)

> **Pitfall — Blind Subtotal Colectomy** — Empirical subtotal colectomy without source localisation carries 30-day mortality up to 30% and has a 40% risk of ongoing bleeding if the source is the small bowel or right colon misidentified. Always attempt to localise the bleeding source before surgery using CTA, tagged RBC scan, or colonoscopy. Reserve blind subtotal colectomy for life-threatening haemorrhage where all other modalities have failed or are unavailable.

> **Board Pearl — Diverticular Bleeding** — Diverticular bleeding typically causes massive painless haematochezia in the elderly. It is RIGHT-sided in 50–60% despite the predominance of diverticulosis on the left. Bleeding stops spontaneously in 80% of episodes but recurs in 20–35%. After two episodes, elective resection of the involved segment should be considered after source confirmation with colonoscopy or tagged RBC scan.


## Acute Mesenteric Ischaemia

Acute mesenteric ischaemia (AMI) is a surgical emergency with overall mortality of 60–80% if not treated within hours. The superior mesenteric artery (SMA) supplies the small bowel from the duodenojejunal flexure to the mid-transverse colon. AMI has four aetiological subtypes: (1) SMA embolism — most common (50%), often cardioembolic (AF, recent MI, endocarditis); (2) SMA thrombosis — atherosclerotic, accounts for 15–25%, often on background of chronic mesenteric ischaemia ('intestinal angina'); (3) Non-occlusive mesenteric ischaemia (NOMI) — 20%, low-flow states (cardiogenic shock, sepsis, vasopressors), no mechanical occlusion; (4) Mesenteric venous thrombosis (MVT) — 5%, associated with hypercoagulable states (Factor V Leiden, PNH, malignancy), better prognosis. Classic presentation: acute onset severe periumbilical pain out of proportion to physical findings (early), progressing to peritonitis (late, implies transmural infarction). Lactate elevation is a late sign. CT angiography is diagnostic — identifies embolus/thrombus, extent of ischaemia, pneumatosis intestinalis, portal venous gas, and free air. Immediate management: IV heparin anticoagulation, IV broad-spectrum antibiotics, nasogastric tube, urinary catheter, vasopressor avoidance where possible.

*Acute Mesenteric Ischaemia — Aetiology and Management*
Aetiology | Frequency | CTA Finding | Management
--- | --- | --- | ---
SMA embolism | 50% | Filling defect in SMA (often at jejunal branches origin) | Emergency embolectomy (open or endovascular) + bowel assessment + anticoagulation
SMA thrombosis | 25% | Occlusion at SMA origin; atherosclerotic plaque | Surgical or endovascular revascularisation; often extensive bowel loss
NOMI | 20% | Normal vessels; reduced flow; intestinal oedema | ICU optimisation; vasodilators (papaverine); avoid vasopressors; avoid surgery unless perforation
Mesenteric venous thrombosis | 5% | Portal/mesenteric vein filling defect | Anticoagulation; surgery only for peritonitis; better prognosis

> **Second-Look Laparotomy in Mesenteric Ischaemia** — After revascularisation and bowel resection for AMI, a planned second-look laparotomy at 24–48h is mandatory to reassess bowel viability and identify additional necrotic segments. At first operation, preserve borderline viable bowel and leave stapled ends — do not anastomose until the bowel is clearly viable at re-look. This staged approach prevents anastomosis of ischaemic bowel and reduces the extent of unnecessary bowel resection.

> **Board Pearl — Pain Out of Proportion** — Acute mesenteric ischaemia classically presents with pain out of proportion to examination findings — this mismatch (severe pain + soft abdomen early on) should prompt urgent CTA even when the examination is initially benign. By the time peritonitis develops, transmural necrosis is established and mortality approaches 80%.


## Sigmoid Volvulus & Colonic Pseudo-obstruction

Sigmoid volvulus accounts for 80% of colonic volvulus in Western countries (caecal volvulus 15%, transverse colon 5%). It is caused by axial rotation of the sigmoid colon on its mesentery, producing a closed-loop obstruction. Risk factors: chronic constipation, high-fibre diet, institutionalised patients, neuropsychiatric disease, African/Asian origin. Clinical features: progressive abdominal distension, obstipation, minimal pain initially (closed-loop obstruction — ischaemia occurs later). Plain AXR shows the classic 'coffee bean' sign (inverted U-shaped sigmoid loop pointing to the RUQ). CT confirms the 'whirlpool' sign at the mesenteric twist. Management ladder: (1) Flexible sigmoidoscopy/rigid proctoscopy with decompression tube — first-line if no peritonitis or CT evidence of ischaemia. Success rate 60–90% but recurrence 40–90%. (2) Emergency sigmoid colectomy — if ischaemia, perforation, peritonitis, or failed endoscopic decompression. After successful endoscopic decompression, elective sigmoid colectomy within the same admission reduces recurrence from 40–90% to <5%. Caecal volvulus cannot be decompressed endoscopically — requires emergency right hemicolectomy or caecostomy.

*Sigmoid Volvulus — Treatment Ladder*
Step | Method | Indication | Success Rate
--- | --- | --- | ---
1st line | Endoscopic decompression (flexible sigmoidoscopy + rectal tube) | No peritonitis, no CT ischaemia | 60–90%
2nd line (same admission) | Elective sigmoid colectomy with primary anastomosis | After successful decompression | Definitive; recurrence <5%
Emergency | Hartmann's procedure or primary resection + anastomosis | Peritonitis, ischaemia, perforation, failed decompression | Required; Hartmann's preferred if contaminated

Colonic pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie syndrome) is a functional obstruction of the colon without a mechanical cause. It is associated with post-operative state, severe illness, electrolyte imbalance (hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia), opioid use, and neurological disease. The colon can dilate enormously (caecum >12cm carries high perforation risk). Management: correct electrolytes, stop opioids, mobilise patient, avoid antidiarrhoeal agents. Neostigmine (2mg IV) is the pharmacological treatment of choice — acetylcholinesterase inhibitor that stimulates colonic motor activity. Success rate 75–90% with single dose; repeat if needed. Contra-indications: bradycardia, bronchospasm, mechanical obstruction. Colonoscopic decompression if neostigmine fails. Surgery (caecostomy or colostomy) reserved for perforation or failed endoscopic decompression.

> **Board Pearl — Neostigmine for Ogilvie Syndrome** — Neostigmine 2mg IV is 90% effective for Ogilvie syndrome. Administer with cardiac monitoring (risk of bradycardia) and atropine at bedside. Contraindicated in true mechanical obstruction — confirm with CT before giving. The caecal diameter threshold of 12cm on AXR or CT identifies patients requiring urgent intervention to prevent perforation.


## Perioperative Management of the Emergency Patient

Emergency surgery carries 5–10 times higher mortality than elective surgery for comparable procedures. Perioperative optimisation encompasses pre-operative resuscitation, anaesthetic risk assessment, operative strategy, and post-operative intensive care. The National Emergency Laparotomy Audit (NELA, UK) has demonstrated that processes of care — including senior surgeon and anaesthetist involvement, pre-operative CT, 30-day mortality risk estimation, and post-operative HDU/ICU admission — significantly reduce mortality. The POSSUM score (Physiological and Operative Severity Score for the Enumeration of Mortality and Morbidity) and P-POSSUM are validated for emergency laparotomy risk estimation. The American College of Surgeons NSQIP Surgical Risk Calculator and the Emergency Laparotomy Risk Score (ELRS) provide rapid bedside risk stratification. Pre-operative resuscitation principles: IV fluid administration to optimise preload (guided by lactate clearance and urinary output), blood transfusion to maintain Hb >7–8g/dL (higher threshold in cardiac disease), correction of coagulopathy (FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate), electrolyte replacement, and warming. Source control is the fundamental principle in peritonitis — early antibiotic administration (within 1 hour of sepsis recognition per Surviving Sepsis Campaign) and surgical source control within 6 hours for peritonitis are associated with improved survival.

*Antibiotic Regimens for Surgical Infections*
Infection Type | Setting | First-line Regimen | Alternative
--- | --- | --- | ---
Community-acquired intra-abdominal infection (mild-moderate) | Non-ICU; no prior antibiotics | Amoxicillin/clavulanate IV or ciprofloxacin + metronidazole | Ertapenem (if penicillin allergy or ESBL risk)
Community-acquired intra-abdominal infection (severe/ICU) | Septic shock; organ dysfunction | Piperacillin/tazobactam or meropenem + antifungal cover | Imipenem/cilastatin
Healthcare-associated/post-operative peritonitis | Prior antibiotics; hospital flora | Meropenem ± vancomycin (MRSA cover) ± anidulafungin (Candida) | Consult microbiology
Cholangitis (Tokyo grade III) | Biliary sepsis with organ failure | Piperacillin/tazobactam or meropenem; biliary drainage within 24h | Ciprofloxacin + metronidazole if mild
Necrotising fasciitis | Skin/soft tissue emergency | Meropenem + clindamycin (toxin inhibition) + vancomycin | Piperacillin/tazobactam + clindamycin

> **NELA Quality Indicators — Emergency Laparotomy** — The National Emergency Laparotomy Audit (NELA) quality indicators associated with reduced mortality include: (1) CT scan performed before surgery, (2) risk assessment documented (NELA risk tool or POSSUM), (3) consultant surgeon and anaesthetist directly involved, (4) patient admitted to critical care post-operatively if predicted mortality >5%, (5) surgery commenced within 2 hours of decision for immediate cases. Hospitals meeting all NELA standards have 30-day mortality rates ~5% lower than those not meeting standards.

> **Board Pearl — Source Control Timing in Peritonitis** — For patients with secondary peritonitis (perforation, anastomotic leak), operative source control within 6 hours of diagnosis is associated with significantly improved survival compared to delayed surgery. Every hour of delay beyond 6 hours is associated with a 1–2% increase in mortality per hour in septic shock. Pre-operative resuscitation should be concurrent with surgical preparation, not sequential — do not delay surgery for >30–60 minutes of resuscitation in unstable patients.
